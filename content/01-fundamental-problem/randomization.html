Randomization

How random assignment eliminates selection bias

In this section, you will learn to

  • Explain how independence (Y(0), Y(1)) \perp\!\!\!\perp D sets selection bias to zero and makes the difference in means identify the ATE
  • Interpret the randomization distribution to see why the difference-in-means estimator is unbiased but variable in any single draw
  • Distinguish Fisher’s exact (sharp-null permutation) framework from Neyman’s repeated-sampling framework for inference
  • Compute an exact permutation p-value by shuffling treatment labels under the sharp null H_0: Y_i(1) = Y_i(0)
  • Explain why the standard variance estimator s_1^2/N_1 + s_0^2/N_0 is conservative and why balance checks cannot detect imbalance in unobserved confounders

Why Randomization Works

The previous two pages established a problem: we want E[Y(1) - Y(0)] but can only observe E[Y|D=1] - E[Y|D=0], and the two differ by the selection bias term E[Y(0)|D=1] - E[Y(0)|D=0].

Consider a concrete example. A Swedish municipality wants to evaluate a new fallprevention (fall prevention) program in its äldrevård (elderly care) facilities. The outcome is the number of fall-related injuries over six months. If the program is offered on a voluntary basis, the facilities that adopt it may be systematically different from those that do not — precisely the selection problem we have been studying. Sweden’s national kvalitetsregister (quality registries), which track patient outcomes across the entire healthcare system, provide an ideal infrastructure for running and analyzing randomized trials at scale.

Randomization solves the selection problem directly. If treatment D is assigned independently of potential outcomes — if a coin flip, a lottery, or a random number generator decides who gets treated — then treatment status carries no information about what would have happened absent treatment. Formally:

Random assignment guarantees (Y(0), Y(1)) \perp\!\!\!\perp D — the potential outcomes are statistically independent of (that is the double-perp symbol \perp\!\!\!\perp) the treatment indicator, so knowing whether a unit was treated tells you nothing about what it would have done either way. This independence condition implies:

E[Y(0) | D = 1] = E[Y(0) | D = 0] = E[Y(0)]

E[Y(1) | D = 1] = E[Y(1) | D = 0] = E[Y(1)]

Under independence, the selection bias is identically zero, and the naive difference in means identifies the ATE:

E[Y | D=1] - E[Y | D=0] = E[Y(1)] - E[Y(0)] = \text{ATE}

The power of randomization lies in its scope: it balances all pre-treatment characteristics between treated and untreated groups, including those we cannot observe or measure. No observational method can make this guarantee.

The two pictures below make the difference visible before you reach the Balance Scanner far down the page. On the left, facilities self-select into the program: a confounder U (say, baseline staff quality) drives both who adopts and the outcome, so a backdoor path D \leftarrow U \rightarrow Y stays open (red) and contaminates the naive comparison. On the right, a coin flip assigns D: the arrow into D from U is severed by randomization (grey, dashed), so the backdoor is cut. The effect U \rightarrow Y still exists (grey, solid) — staff quality still drives injuries — but with no path from U into D it can no longer confound, leaving the green causal arrow D \rightarrow Y as the only thing the comparison picks up.

The Difference-in-Means Estimator

Under random assignment, the natural estimator of the ATE is the sample difference in means:

\hat{\tau} = \bar{Y}_1 - \bar{Y}_0 = \frac{1}{N_1} \sum_{i: D_i = 1} Y_i - \frac{1}{N_0} \sum_{i: D_i = 0} Y_i

where N_1 and N_0 are the numbers of treated and untreated units, respectively. This estimator is unbiased: E[\hat{\tau}] = \text{ATE}.

The estimator is unbiased, but it is not precise in any single draw. Each random allocation produces a different \hat{\tau}, and some draws will produce estimates far from the true ATE by chance. The distribution of \hat{\tau} across all possible random allocations is the randomization distribution, and its spread determines how much uncertainty remains after the experiment.

See It: The Randomization Distribution

The simulation below makes the fall-prevention example concrete. The fixed population is a set of äldrevård facilities, each with a known pair of potential outcomes: the number of fall-related injuries it would record over six months with the program (Y_i(1)) and without it (Y_i(0)). The program helps on average, so the true ATE is negative — it lowers injuries. Each time you click “Randomize once,” half the facilities are randomly assigned to run the program, the difference in mean injuries (treated minus control) is computed, and the estimate is added to the histogram. Over many draws, the histogram centers on the true ATE.

// Generate fixed population of facilities (regenerates when N changes)
population = {
  const rng = mulberry32r(12345);
  const pop = [];
  for (let i = 0; i < sampleSize; i++) {
    // Injuries without the program (clamped at 0 — counts can't be negative)
    const y0 = Math.max(0, baselineFalls + 4 * randnr(rng));
    const tau = populationATE + 3 * randnr(rng);
    const y1 = Math.max(0, y0 + tau);
    pop.push({ y0: y0, y1: y1, tau: y1 - y0 });
  }
  return pop;
}

// The estimand the difference-in-means is actually unbiased for is the REALIZED
// finite-population ATE — the mean of tau_i over this fixed population. Because
// y1 is clamped at 0 (counts can't be negative) and N is finite, this differs
// from the nominal data-generating parameter (-4.0). The histogram of estimates
// centers on THIS value, so it is the honest reference for unbiasedness and the
// center of the Neyman Normal.
populationMeanTau = population.reduce((s, d) => s + d.tau, 0) / population.length

function mulberry32r(a) {
  return function() {
    a |= 0; a = a + 0x6D2B79F5 | 0;
    var t = Math.imul(a ^ a >>> 15, 1 | a);
    t = t + Math.imul(t ^ t >>> 7, 61 | t) ^ t;
    return ((t ^ t >>> 14) >>> 0) / 4294967296;
  }
}

function randnr(rng) {
  let u1 = rng();
  let u2 = rng();
  return Math.sqrt(-2 * Math.log(u1)) * Math.cos(2 * Math.PI * u2);
}

// Accumulate estimates across draws
estimates = {
  const count = drawButton;
  if (count === 0) return [];

  const results = [];
  for (let d = 0; d < count; d++) {
    // True random allocation (not seeded — different each click)
    const n1 = Math.floor(sampleSize / 2);
    const indices = Array.from({length: sampleSize}, (_, i) => i);
    // Fisher-Yates shuffle
    for (let i = indices.length - 1; i > 0; i--) {
      const j = Math.floor(Math.random() * (i + 1));
      [indices[i], indices[j]] = [indices[j], indices[i]];
    }
    const treated = new Set(indices.slice(0, n1));

    let sumT = 0, sumC = 0, nT = 0, nC = 0;
    for (let i = 0; i < sampleSize; i++) {
      if (treated.has(i)) {
        sumT += population[i].y1;
        nT++;
      } else {
        sumC += population[i].y0;
        nC++;
      }
    }
    results.push({ estimate: sumT / nT - sumC / nC, draw: d });
  }
  return results;
}

// Theoretical SDs computed from the (fully known) population.
// With N1 = N0 = floor(N/2). We have both potential outcomes here, so we
// can compute the true (Neyman) sampling SD and the conservative one the
// standard estimator targets.
neymanSDs = {
  const n1 = Math.floor(sampleSize / 2);
  const n0 = sampleSize - n1;
  const n = sampleSize;

  const varOf = (arr) => {
    const m = arr.reduce((s, x) => s + x, 0) / arr.length;
    return arr.reduce((s, x) => s + (x - m) ** 2, 0) / (arr.length - 1);
  };

  const S1sq = varOf(population.map(d => d.y1)); // Var(Y(1))
  const S0sq = varOf(population.map(d => d.y0)); // Var(Y(0))
  const Stau = varOf(population.map(d => d.tau)); // Var(tau_i)

  // Conservative variance: what s1^2/N1 + s0^2/N0 estimates (ignores Var(tau)/N)
  const conservativeVar = S1sq / n1 + S0sq / n0;
  // True (Neyman) finite-population variance: subtracts Var(tau_i)/N
  const trueVar = Math.max(0, conservativeVar - Stau / n);

  return {
    predicted: Math.sqrt(trueVar),
    conservative: Math.sqrt(conservativeVar)
  };
}

// Points tracing the Neyman-predicted Normal density, rescaled to the
// histogram's count axis. Only meaningful once we have a few draws.
normalCurve = {
  if (estimates.length < 2 || !(predictedSD > 0)) return [];

  // Match the histogram's binning to convert density -> expected count.
  const vals = estimates.map(d => d.estimate);
  const lo = d3.min(vals);
  const hi = d3.max(vals);
  const binWidth = (hi - lo) > 0 ? (hi - lo) / normalThresholds : predictedSD / 5;

  const mu = populationMeanTau;
  const sd = predictedSD;
  const pdf = (x) => Math.exp(-0.5 * ((x - mu) / sd) ** 2) / (sd * Math.sqrt(2 * Math.PI));

  // Draw the curve across +/- 4 SD around the true ATE.
  const pts = [];
  const steps = 120;
  const xmin = mu - 4 * sd;
  const xmax = mu + 4 * sd;
  for (let i = 0; i <= steps; i++) {
    const x = xmin + (i / steps) * (xmax - xmin);
    pts.push({ x: x, count: estimates.length * binWidth * pdf(x) });
  }
  return pts;

How to Read the Randomization Distribution

This first interactive shows what “unbiased but variable” means in practice. Each allocation gives one estimate; many allocations trace out the whole randomization distribution. Work through it in order:

Step 2: Build the distribution. Click Draw 500. The histogram fills in and centers on the ochre dashed line (the realized ATE for this population). This centering is unbiasedness: no single draw is exact, but the draws average to the truth.

Step 3: Tighten with sample size. Slide Number of facilities (N) upward and draw again. The histogram keeps its center but narrows — precision improves with N, at the \sqrt{N} rate. Shrink N to 20 and the spread widens noticeably.

Step 4: Compare the three SDs. Read the Empirical SD (spread of your draws), the Predicted SD (the true Neyman SD from the known population), and the Conservative SD (what the t-test reports). The empirical value converges to the predicted one, which always sits just below the conservative one.

Step 5: The slate curve is a prediction, not a fit. The Neyman Normal overlay is computed in advance from the population variances, not fitted to your histogram. Randomization eliminates selection bias by construction. It guarantees that treated and untreated groups are comparable on average across all characteristics — observed and unobserved. No amount of careful covariate adjustment in an observational study can replicate this guarantee, because we can never condition on variables we do not measure.

Two Frameworks for Inference

The randomization distribution illustrated above provides the basis for inference — quantifying the uncertainty in our estimate. There are two distinct frameworks for doing so, and the distinction matters for understanding what exactly is random.

The table below previews how the two frameworks differ before we work through each one. They answer different questions and lean on different assumptions, even though both are powered by the same randomization that produced the data.

Fisher’s Exact Framework

In Fisher’s framework, potential outcomes are fixed constants. The only source of randomness is the treatment assignment mechanism itself. Under the sharp null H_0: Y_i(1) = Y_i(0) for all i (treatment has zero effect on every unit), we can compute the exact distribution of any test statistic by enumerating all \binom{N}{N_1} possible assignments.

This is the framework underlying Fisher’s exact test and permutation tests. It makes no distributional assumptions but tests only the sharp null of no effect whatsoever.

The simulator below turns this idea into a picture. It fixes one trial of fall-prevention facilities, then repeatedly shuffles the treatment labels under the sharp null — each shuffle is a treatment assignment that could have happened but carries no real effect. The histogram those shuffles trace out is the randomization (reference) distribution of \hat{\tau}. Overlay the value you actually observed and the share of shuffles at least as extreme is the exact permutation p-value — no t-distribution, no normal approximation. Then dial in a true effect and watch the observed statistic march into the tail while the p-value collapses.

// The randomization (reference) distribution under the SHARP NULL.
// Outcomes y are now frozen constants. We repeatedly RE-LABEL: pick a random
// subset of size n1 to be "treated", recompute the difference in means. Under
// H0 (no effect on anyone), the labels are exchangeable, so this set of
// recomputed statistics is the exact null distribution of τ̂.
rndx_permDist = {
  rndx_newTrial;                      // redraw when the trial changes
  const units = rndx_trial;
  const N = units.length;
  const ys = units.map(u => u.y);
  const n1 = units.filter(u => u.d === 1).length;
  const total = ys.reduce((s, y) => s + y, 0);
  const B = rndx_nPerms;

  const idx = Array.from({ length: N }, (_, i) => i);
  const stats = [];
  for (let b = 0; b < B; b++) {
    // Partial Fisher-Yates: shuffle just enough to draw n1 "treated".
    for (let i = N - 1; i >= N - n1; i--) {
      const j = Math.floor(Math.random() * (i + 1));
      [idx[i], idx[j]] = [idx[j], idx[i]];

    let sumT = 0;
    for (let i = N - 1; i >= N - n1; i--) sumT += ys[idx[i]];
    const sumC = total - sumT;
    stats.push(sumT / n1 - sumC / (N - n1));

The permutation test makes no distributional assumptions at all; its validity comes entirely from the randomization that produced the data.

Neyman’s Repeated Sampling Framework

In Neyman’s framework, we assume the N units are a random sample from a superpopulation. Both the potential outcomes and the treatment assignment are random. The estimand is the superpopulation ATE: E[Y(1) - Y(0)].

Neyman showed that the variance of \hat{\tau} under repeated sampling is:

\text{Var}(\hat{\tau}) = \frac{S_1^2}{N_1} + \frac{S_0^2}{N_0}

where S_1^2 = \text{Var}(Y_i(1)) and S_0^2 = \text{Var}(Y_i(0)) are the population variances of the potential outcomes. Since we cannot observe both potential outcomes, we estimate this with the sample analog:

\widehat{\text{Var}}(\hat{\tau}) = \frac{s_1^2}{N_1} + \frac{s_0^2}{N_0}

This is the familiar formula behind the two-sample t-test. It is conservative: it overestimates the true variance because it omits a term equal to \text{Var}(\tau_i)/N — the variance of the individual treatment effects \tau_i = Y_i(1) - Y_i(0), divided by N. That term is non-negative and unobservable (it depends on both potential outcomes for the same unit), so dropping it can only inflate the estimated variance. The Math Details below derive this exactly.

Practical Considerations

Random assignment in principle solves the identification problem. In practice, several issues arise.

Balance checks. After randomization, researchers typically check whether treated and untreated groups are balanced on observable covariates. If the randomization worked, some imbalances are expected by chance — testing 20 covariates at the 5% level will produce about one significant difference under the null. Balance checks are useful for detecting procedural failures, but their absence should not be treated as evidence that the randomization failed.

The scanner below shows what balance checks can and cannot see — and why randomization is uniquely powerful. It tracks two covariates for each äldrevård facility: an observed one, X (say, the share of frail residents, which a researcher can measure), and a hidden confounder U (say, baseline staff quality, which never makes it into the data). Switch between random assignment and a self-selection rule and watch the standardized mean differences live: random assignment drives the imbalance in both X and U toward zero, while self-selection biases both — and you would only ever catch the imbalance in X.

The metric the bars report is the standardized mean difference (SMD): the gap between the treated and control group means, measured in standard-deviation units (so it is unit-free and comparable across variables). The convention is that |\text{SMD}| < 0.1 counts as “balanced” — a widely used rule of thumb, not a hard law.

  const rule = rndx_balRule;
  const sel = rndx_balSelStrength;

  const rndn = () => {
    const u1 = Math.random(), u2 = Math.random();
    return Math.sqrt(-2 * Math.log(u1 + 1e-12)) * Math.cos(2 * Math.PI * u2);
  };

  const units = [];
  for (let i = 0; i < N; i++) {
    const x = rndn();                 // observed covariate (frailty share)
    const u = rndn();                 // hidden confounder (staff quality)
    units.push({ id: i, x, u });
  }

  if (rule === "Random assignment") {
    // Coin flip independent of X and U: shuffle and split in half.
    const idx = units.map((_, i) => i);
    for (let i = idx.length - 1; i > 0; i--) {
      const j = Math.floor(Math.random() * (i + 1));
      [idx[i], idx[j]] = [idx[j], idx[i]];
    }
    const n1 = Math.floor(N / 2);
    const treated = new Set(idx.slice(0, n1));
    units.forEach((m, i) => { m.d = treated.has(i) ? 1 : 0; });
  } else {
    // Self-selection: opt-in score rises with BOTH X and U (plus noise).
    // Facilities above the median score enroll, so ~half are treated but the
    // treated are systematically higher on X and U.
    const scored = units.map(m => ({
      m,
      score: sel * (m.x + m.u) + (1 - sel) * rndn() + 0.4 * rndn()

    const cutoff = d3.median(scored, s => s.score);
    scored.forEach(s => { s.m.d = s.score >= cutoff ? 1 : 0; });

  const ct = rndx_balData.filter(m => m.d === 0);

How to Read the Balance Scanner

The whole promise of randomization is balance on everything, observed or not. This scanner lets you watch that promise hold — and watch self-selection break it on a variable you can never see. Work through it in order:

Step 1: Randomize and read the two bars. Start with Random assignment. Both standardized mean differences — for the observed covariate X and the hidden confounder U — sit inside the green \pm 0.1 “balanced zone,” and both cards read balanced in green. The coin flip did not know or care about either variable, so neither group is systematically different.

Step 2: See balance is on-average, not exact. Click Draw new randomization several times. The two SMDs jitter to new small values each draw — sometimes +0.04, sometimes -0.06 — but they bounce around zero rather than drifting away. This is the honest picture: a single randomization need not be perfectly balanced, but imbalances are small, random, and average out. Shrink Number of facilities to 40 and the jitter grows; push it to 600 and the bars pin tightly to zero (balance improves with N).

Step 3: Switch to self-selection. Choose Self-selection (facilities opt in). Now the facilities that enroll are the ones with both more frail residents (high X) and better baseline staff (high U). Both bars swing out of the green zone and both cards flip to imbalanced red. Crucially, the hidden U is just as biased as the observed X.

Step 4: Confront the blind spot. Here is the lesson. Under self-selection you could run a balance check, spot the imbalance in X, and statistically adjust for it. But U never appears in your data — there is no column for “baseline staff quality” — so its imbalance is invisible and uncorrectable. The naive comparison is contaminated by a confounder you cannot even name. Drag Self-selection strength from 0 to 1 and watch both bars fan outward together: stronger self-selection means worse hidden confounding.

Step 5: Tie it back to the guarantee. Flip back to Random assignment and click Draw new randomization once more. Both bars snap back inside the green band, including the one for U. This is what the Key Insight box above meant by “balanced on average across all characteristics — observed and unobserved.” Random assignment balances the variable you forgot to measure; self-selection cannot, and neither can any adjustment that conditions only on what you happened to record.

Stratification. When the sample is small or a particular covariate is strongly prognostic, stratified (or blocked) randomization — randomizing separately within strata defined by that covariate — can improve precision without introducing bias.

Compliance. Randomization determines treatment assignment, but subjects may not comply. Some assigned to treatment may refuse; some assigned to control may find a way to get treated. The intention-to-treat (ITT) analysis compares groups as randomized. Recovering the effect of actual treatment requires additional assumptions (monotonicity) and methods (instrumental variables), discussed in later modules.

Limitations

Randomization is the gold standard for internal validity, but it has real constraints:

  • Ethics. We cannot randomly assign smoking, poverty, or violence.
  • External validity. The population willing to participate in an experiment may differ from the policy-relevant population.
  • Cost and feasibility. Randomization at scale (e.g., across municipalities or countries) is expensive and logistically challenging.
  • Time horizon. Experiments run for finite periods; long-run effects may differ from short-run impacts.

These limitations are why the rest of this book exists. When randomization is not possible, we turn to quasi-experimental methods — designs that approximate the logic of random assignment using observational data and identifying assumptions.

Math Details: Unbiasedness Proof

Proof that E[\hat{\tau}] = \text{ATE} Under Random Assignment

Let treatment be randomly assigned with P(D_i = 1) = p independently across units. The difference in means is:

\hat{\tau} = \frac{1}{N_1} \sum_{i: D_i = 1} Y_i - \frac{1}{N_0} \sum_{i: D_i = 0} Y_i

By the switching equation and independence:

E[\hat{\tau}] = E\!\left[\frac{1}{N_1} \sum_{i: D_i = 1} Y_i(1)\right] - E\!\left[\frac{1}{N_0} \sum_{i: D_i = 0} Y_i(0)\right]

Because D \perp (Y(0), Y(1)), each treated observation is a random draw from the population distribution of Y(1), and each control observation from Y(0). Therefore:

E[\hat{\tau}] = E[Y_i(1)] - E[Y_i(0)] = \text{ATE}

Variance of \hat{\tau} (Neyman)

Under completely random assignment of N_1 units to treatment out of N total:

\text{Var}(\hat{\tau}) = \frac{S_1^2}{N_1} + \frac{S_0^2}{N_0} - \frac{S_{01}^2}{N}

where S_{01}^2 = \text{Var}(\tau_i) = \text{Var}(Y_i(1) - Y_i(0)) is the variance of individual treatment effects. The last term is non-negative and unobservable, so the standard estimator s_1^2/N_1 + s_0^2/N_0 is conservative.

Under constant treatment effects (\tau_i = \tau for all i), S_{01}^2 = 0 and the standard estimator is exact.

Exercise 1

Using the simulation above, set N = 20 and draw 500 allocations. Note the standard deviation of the estimates. Now set N = 200 and draw 500 again. By what factor did the standard deviation decrease? How does this relate to the \sqrt{N} convergence rate?

Show answer

Typical results: at N = 20, the standard deviation of estimates is around 1.4 (injuries per facility). At N = 200, it drops to about 0.6. (These magnitudes match the Predicted SD (Neyman) card, which is computed directly from the population, and the Empirical SD card converges to them once you have drawn a few hundred allocations.)

The ratio is roughly 1.4 / 0.58 \approx 2.4. The idealized \sqrt{N} law predicts \sqrt{200/20} = \sqrt{10} \approx 3.16. The observed ratio is somewhat smaller than 3.16 here because the fixed population is regenerated at each N, so the potential-outcome variances S_1^2, S_0^2 (and the clamping of injury counts at zero) shift slightly between the two sample sizes — the variance is not a clean constant divided by N. The qualitative lesson still holds firmly.

That lesson is the \sqrt{N} convergence rate: the standard error of \hat{\tau} scales roughly as \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}. Increasing the sample by a factor of 10 reduces the standard deviation by a factor near \sqrt{10} \approx 3.16. This means getting more precise estimates is expensive — to (approximately) halve the standard error, you need to quadruple the sample size.

Exercise 2

Explain why the standard variance estimator s_1^2/N_1 + s_0^2/N_0 is conservative (overestimates the true variance of \hat{\tau}). Under what conditions is it exactly correct? Why does this conservatism make hypothesis testing using this estimator slightly more trustworthy, not less?

Show answer

The true variance of \hat{\tau} under complete randomization is:

\text{Var}(\hat{\tau}) = \frac{S_1^2}{N_1} + \frac{S_0^2}{N_0} - \frac{S_{01}^2}{N}

where S_{01}^2 = \text{Var}(\tau_i) is the variance of individual treatment effects. The standard estimator s_1^2/N_1 + s_0^2/N_0 omits the third term S_{01}^2/N, which is always non-negative (it’s a variance). So the estimator overestimates the true variance.

Exactly correct when: S_{01}^2 = 0, which means \text{Var}(\tau_i) = 0 — i.e., treatment effects are constant (\tau_i = \tau for all i). Under constant effects, the omitted term vanishes.

Why conservatism is good for testing: If we overestimate the variance, we overestimate the standard error, which makes our t-statistic smaller and our p-value larger. This means we reject the null less often than we should. In other words, the test has actual size smaller than nominal size — if we set \alpha = 0.05, we reject under the null less than 5% of the time. The test is valid (controls Type I error) but slightly under-powered. This is far preferable to the reverse: an anti-conservative estimator would reject too often, leading to false positives.

Exercise 3

A researcher randomizes 100 patients to drug vs. placebo. She then tests 20 baseline covariates for balance and finds that age differs significantly (p = 0.03) between arms. She concludes the randomization failed. Evaluate her reasoning. What would you expect under a correct randomization?

Show answer

Her reasoning is flawed. Under a correct randomization with 20 independent balance tests at \alpha = 0.05, we expect about 20 \times 0.05 = 1 false positive. Finding one significant result out of 20 tests is entirely consistent with a successful randomization.

The probability of finding at least one significant result out of 20 independent tests is 1 - (1 - 0.05)^{20} = 1 - 0.95^{20} \approx 0.64. So there’s a 64% chance of finding at least one “significant” imbalance even when the randomization is perfect. This is the multiple testing problem.

A single p = 0.03 result among 20 tests provides essentially no evidence against the randomization. If the researcher is concerned, she could: (1) apply a Bonferroni correction (the threshold becomes 0.05/20 = 0.0025, and 0.03 is nowhere near that); (2) conduct a joint F-test of all covariates simultaneously; or (3) simply note the imbalance and control for age in the analysis as a robustness check.

Exercise 4

In an experiment, 30% of subjects assigned to treatment refuse to take the drug, while 5% of subjects assigned to control obtain the drug through other channels. The intention-to-treat estimate is \hat{\tau}^{ITT} = 2.1. If these are the only two compliance types, what can you say about the effect of actually taking the drug? What additional assumption would you need?

Show answer

The share who actually take the drug among those assigned to treatment is 1 - 0.30 = 0.70. Among those assigned to control, 0.05 take the drug anyway. The “first stage” — the difference in take-up rates between assigned-to-treatment and assigned-to-control — is 0.70 - 0.05 = 0.65.

Under the monotonicity assumption (no “defiers” — no one who would take the drug if assigned to control but refuse if assigned to treatment), we can use the Wald/IV estimator:

\hat{\tau}^{LATE} = \frac{\hat{\tau}^{ITT}}{\text{First stage}} = \frac{2.1}{0.65} \approx 3.23

This estimates the Local Average Treatment Effect (LATE): the average effect of actually taking the drug for the compliers — those whose drug-taking behavior is changed by the randomized assignment.

The additional assumption needed is monotonicity: assignment to treatment can only increase (never decrease) the probability of taking the drug. This rules out “defiers” who perversely take the drug only when told not to.

Note: the LATE (3.23) is larger than the ITT (2.1) because the ITT is “diluted” by non-compliance — it averages the drug’s effect over compliers and zero effect over non-compliers.

Key takeaways

  • Random assignment makes treatment status independent of potential outcomes, zeroing out selection bias so the naive difference in means identifies the ATE.
  • The difference-in-means estimator is unbiased but not precise in any one trial; its spread is the randomization distribution, which narrows at the \sqrt{N} rate.
  • Fisher’s permutation test gives an exact p-value for the sharp null by reshuffling labels — no t-distribution or normal approximation required.
  • Neyman’s variance estimator s_1^2/N_1 + s_0^2/N_0 is conservative because it drops the unobservable \text{Var}(\tau_i)/N term; it is exact only under constant treatment effects.
  • Randomization’s unique power is balance on unobserved confounders too; balance checks only see measured covariates, so they cannot rescue a self-selected comparison.
