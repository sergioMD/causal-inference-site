Overdispersion and the Negative Binomial

When the variance exceeds the mean

In this section, you will learn to

  • Define overdispersion and explain why it violates the Poisson model’s equidispersion restriction \operatorname{Var}(y\mid x) = E[y\mid x]
  • Trace overdispersion to unobserved heterogeneity and show why it leaves the Poisson coefficients consistent but the Poisson standard errors badly too small
  • Write the Negative Binomial as a Poisson–gamma mixture with variance function \operatorname{Var}(y\mid x) = \mu + \alpha\mu^2 and read off the dispersion parameter \alpha
  • Choose among three remedies — the Negative Binomial model, quasi-Poisson / robust (sandwich) standard errors, and a formal overdispersion test — and say what each fixes
  • Fit and compare the models in R (MASS::glm.nb, AER::dispersiontest) and Python (statsmodels)

The Question Behind the Counts

The vårdcentraler (primary care centers) of Region Uppsala are scored each year on a simple operational metric: the number of unplanned return visits a chronic-disease patient makes in the twelve months after enrolment in a diabetes-coordination program. A patient who is well controlled returns rarely; a fragile patient returns again and again. The verksamhetschef (clinic manager) wants to know whether an extra hour of distriktssköterska (district nurse) follow-up per quarter reduces these return visits, and — crucially — whether the estimated reduction is statistically distinguishable from noise.

The natural starting point is Poisson regression: visit counts are non-negative integers, so we model E[\text{visits}\mid x] = \exp(x'\beta). But the Poisson model carries a hidden, rigid promise. It insists that the variance of the counts equals their mean, observation by observation. Real patient counts almost never honour that promise. Two patients with identical recorded characteristics — same age, same HbA1c, same medication list — still differ in a hundred unrecorded ways (social support, health literacy, an unlisted comorbidity). That extra, unmodelled variation inflates the spread of the counts beyond what Poisson allows. The counts are overdispersed.

This page is about what overdispersion does to your conclusions. The short version: it leaves the point estimates roughly correct but makes the Poisson standard errors far too small, so coefficients look far more significant than the data warrant. We will see why, quantify it, and fix it three ways.

Equidispersion: The Promise Poisson Cannot Keep

Recall the defining feature of the Poisson distribution. If y \mid x \sim \text{Poisson}(\mu) with \mu = \exp(x'\beta), then

E[y \mid x] = \mu \qquad\text{and}\qquad \operatorname{Var}(y \mid x) = \mu .

Read this as: “given the covariates, both the expected count and its variance equal the same single number \mu.” The mean and the variance are not two free quantities — they are locked together. This is equidispersion, and it is not an assumption you add to Poisson; it is baked into the distribution itself. One parameter \mu governs both the centre and the spread.

Overdispersion is the condition in which the conditional variance of the outcome exceeds its conditional mean:

\operatorname{Var}(y \mid x) \;>\; E[y \mid x].

The ratio \phi = \operatorname{Var}(y\mid x) / E[y\mid x] is the dispersion. Equidispersion is \phi = 1 (the Poisson case); overdispersion is \phi > 1; underdispersion (\phi < 1) is rarer in health data but possible. Read \phi as: “how many times larger the spread is than the Poisson model would permit at the same mean.”

Where does the extra spread come from? Almost always from unobserved heterogeneity. Suppose each patient really is Poisson, but with their own rate \mu_i = \mu \cdot \nu_i, where \nu_i is an unobserved multiplier (a fragile patient has \nu_i > 1, a robust one \nu_i < 1) with E[\nu_i] = 1 and \operatorname{Var}(\nu_i) = \alpha. Within a patient the count is Poisson; across patients the rate itself varies. By the law of total variance the marginal variance picks up an extra term, and equidispersion breaks. The Math Details below carry out the algebra; the punchline is that mixing a Poisson over a varying rate produces exactly the variance function of the Negative Binomial.

Why Ignoring It Deflates Your Standard Errors

Here is the subtle part, and the reason overdispersion is dangerous rather than merely untidy. The Poisson mean model E[y\mid x] = \exp(x'\beta) can be correct even when the variance model is wrong. The Poisson maximum-likelihood estimator is a member of the quasi-MLE family: as long as the conditional mean is correctly specified, \hat\beta_{\text{Poisson}} stays consistent for \beta regardless of the true variance. Your coefficients are fine.

The damage lands on the uncertainty. The Poisson MLE computes its standard errors from the model-implied information matrix, which assumes \operatorname{Var}(y\mid x) = \mu. If the true variance is \phi\mu with \phi > 1, the reported variance of \hat\beta is too small by roughly a factor of \phi — so the standard errors are too small by a factor of about \sqrt{\phi}.

Spurious significance. With dispersion \phi, naive Poisson standard errors are deflated by about \sqrt{\phi}, so the z-statistics are inflated by about \sqrt{\phi}. At \phi = 4 — not unusual for visit counts — every z-statistic is roughly doubled, a true z = 1.4 (not significant) is reported as z \approx 2.8 (p < 0.01), and p-values collapse toward zero. You will declare a nursing intervention “highly significant” when the honest standard error says you cannot tell it apart from zero. The point estimate is right; the confidence in it is fabricated.

This is the worst kind of error in applied work: not a wrong number, but unwarranted certainty about a right one. Three fixes follow.

Fix 1: The Negative Binomial Model

The cleanest remedy models the overdispersion directly. Start from the heterogeneity story: let each patient’s rate be \mu_i \nu_i with \nu_i drawn from a gamma distribution with mean 1 and variance \alpha. A Poisson count whose rate is itself gamma-distributed is, after integrating out the unobserved \nu_i, distributed as Negative Binomial (specifically the NB2 form used in econometrics).

The Negative Binomial (NB2) model keeps the Poisson mean but adds a dispersion parameter \alpha \ge 0 that lets the variance grow quadratically with the mean:

E[y \mid x] = \mu = \exp(x'\beta), \qquad \boxed{\;\operatorname{Var}(y \mid x) = \mu + \alpha\,\mu^2\;}.

Read the variance function as: “the Poisson variance \mu, plus an extra term \alpha\mu^2 that captures unobserved heterogeneity.” The dispersion parameter \alpha is the variance of the unobserved rate multiplier. When \alpha = 0 the extra term vanishes and the model collapses exactly to Poisson — so Poisson is the special, nested case \alpha = 0.

The quadratic term \alpha\mu^2 is the workhorse. Because it scales with \mu^2, the gap between the NB variance and the Poisson identity line \operatorname{Var} = \mu widens as the mean grows: heavy-visiting patient groups are exactly where Poisson most badly underestimates the spread. The NB log-likelihood is maximised by the same machinery as any other maximum-likelihood model — \beta and \alpha are estimated jointly — and because NB nests Poisson at \alpha = 0, a likelihood-ratio test of H_0\!: \alpha = 0 is a direct test for overdispersion.

// Gamma(shape=k, scale=θ) sampler via the Marsaglia–Tsang method, built on
// d3.randomNormal / Math.random so randomness is library-driven (no own PRNG).
// Used to draw the unobserved rate multiplier ν ~ Gamma(mean 1, var α).
ctnb_randNorm = d3.randomNormal(0, 1)
ctnb_gammaSample = function (shape) {
  // Marsaglia & Tsang (2000). Returns a draw from Gamma(shape, scale=1).
  if (shape < 1) {
    // Boost: Gamma(shape) = Gamma(shape+1) * U^(1/shape).
    const u = Math.random();
    return ctnb_gammaSample(shape + 1) * Math.pow(u, 1 / shape);
  }
  const d = shape - 1 / 3;
  const c = 1 / Math.sqrt(9 * d);
  while (true) {
    let x, v;
    do {
      x = ctnb_randNorm();
      v = 1 + c * x;
    } while (v <= 0);
    v = v * v * v;
    const u = Math.random();
    if (u < 1 - 0.0331 * x * x * x * x) return d * v;
    if (Math.log(u) < 0.5 * x * x + d * (1 - v + Math.log(v))) return d * v;
  }
}
// Draw one Poisson count by inversion (Knuth) — fine for the modest means here.
ctnb_poissonSample = function (lambda) {
  const L = Math.exp(-lambda);
  let k = 0, p = 1;
  do { k++; p *= Math.random(); } while (p > L);
  return k - 1;
}

// ν ~ Gamma(mean 1, var α). We then report the subgroup's empirical mean and
// variance so the cloud traces the variance–mean curve.
ctnb_cohort = {
  ctnb_draw; // dependency: re-run on button press
  const G = ctnb_nGroups;
  const perGroup = 60;
  const alpha = ctnb_alpha;
  const rows = [];
  for (let g = 0; g < G; g++) {
    // Subgroup baseline mean: spread multiplicatively around μ̄.
    const mu = ctnb_meanVisits * Math.exp(ctnb_randNorm() * 0.45);
    const counts = [];
    for (let i = 0; i < perGroup; i++) {
      let lam = mu;
      if (alpha > 0) {
        // ν ~ Gamma(shape=1/α, scale=α) ⇒ E[ν]=1, Var[ν]=α.
        const nu = ctnb_gammaSample(1 / alpha) * alpha;
        lam = mu * nu;

      counts.push(ctnb_poissonSample(lam));
    }
    const m = d3.mean(counts);
    const v = d3.variance(counts);
    rows.push({ group: g, mean: m, varc: v });
  }
  return rows;
}

How to Read the Overdispersion Lab

Work through these in order; each isolates one idea.

Step 1: Establish the Poisson baseline. Set dispersion \alpha to 0. Every subgroup is pure Poisson, so the empirical variance equals the mean: the slate points scatter tightly along the green dashed identity line \operatorname{Var} = \mu. The “Empirical \hat\phi” tile reads near 1.0 and turns green. This is equidispersion — the promise Poisson keeps only when \alpha = 0.

Step 2: Inject heterogeneity. Drag dispersion \alpha upward. The points lift off the green line and rise toward the red NB curve \operatorname{Var} = \mu + \alpha\mu^2. The lift-off grows with the mean: at small \mu the two curves nearly coincide, but at large \mu the quadratic term \alpha\mu^2 pulls the variance far above the mean. The \hat\phi tile climbs past 1 and turns red.

Step 3: Watch the standard error inflate. As \hat\phi rises, read the “SE inflation \sqrt{\hat\phi}” tile. This is the factor by which honest standard errors exceed the naive Poisson ones. At \hat\phi = 4 it reads 2.0\times — the Poisson SEs are half their correct size.

Step 4: See the confidence intervals diverge. On the interval panel, the red bar is the Poisson 95% interval and the slate bar is the dispersion-corrected (NB / robust) interval. With \alpha = 0 they coincide. As \alpha rises, the slate interval grows visibly wider while the point estimate (black dot) stays put — the estimate is stable, the uncertainty is what was being understated.

Step 5: Catch the spurious significance. Compare the “Poisson z” and “Corrected z” tiles. For moderate \alpha the Poisson z can sit comfortably above 1.96 (apparently significant) while the corrected z drops below it (honestly not significant). That gap is the false confidence overdispersion manufactures. Press Draw a new cohort to confirm the pattern is structural, not a fluke of one draw.

Fix 2: Quasi-Poisson and Robust (Sandwich) Standard Errors

The Negative Binomial changes the likelihood. Sometimes you would rather keep the Poisson mean model — which you trust — and simply repair the standard errors. Two closely related options do this.

The quasi-Poisson estimator keeps the Poisson score equations (so the point estimates are identical to Poisson) but estimates a single dispersion scalar \phi from the data and multiplies every variance by it. Operationally, it computes \hat\phi from the Pearson residuals and rescales the Poisson covariance matrix by \hat\phi, inflating each standard error by \sqrt{\hat\phi}. It assumes the variance is \phi\mu — proportional to the mean (a linear variance function), which differs from NB’s quadratic \mu + \alpha\mu^2.

The robust (Huber–White “sandwich”) standard error goes further and assumes nothing about the variance function’s form. It estimates \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta) from the empirical outer product of the score contributions, so it is valid under any form of overdispersion (or clustering, with the cluster-robust version) as long as the mean is correctly specified.

The mean model is the load-bearing assumption. All three fixes — NB, quasi-Poisson, and robust SEs — rely on E[y\mid x] = \exp(x'\beta) being correct; they differ only in how they model the variance. NB assumes a quadratic variance and re-estimates the likelihood (most efficient if the NB form is right); quasi-Poisson assumes a linear variance and rescales; robust SEs assume nothing about the variance and are the most agnostic (and the safest default when you are unsure). When the mean model itself is wrong, none of them save you.

Fix 3: Testing for Overdispersion

Before reaching for a remedy, test whether you need one. The cleanest test sits inside the NB nesting. Because Poisson is NB with \alpha = 0, the null hypothesis H_0\!: \alpha = 0 is “no overdispersion.”

Cameron and Trivedi’s regression-based test makes this operational without even fitting the NB. Fit Poisson, form the auxiliary variable

z_i = \frac{(y_i - \hat\mu_i)^2 - y_i}{\hat\mu_i},

and regress z_i on a function of \hat\mu_i (just \hat\mu_i for the NB1/linear alternative, or \hat\mu_i^2/\hat\mu_i = \hat\mu_i scaled for NB2). Read z_i as: “the squared Poisson residual, debiased by the count itself and standardised by the fitted mean.” Under equidispersion E[z_i] = 0; under overdispersion it is positive. A one-sided t-test on the slope is the overdispersion test, and AER::dispersiontest in R implements it directly. The likelihood-ratio test comparing the fitted Poisson and NB log-likelihoods is the other standard route (with the technicality that \alpha = 0 lies on the boundary of the parameter space, so the LR statistic is a \tfrac12\chi^2_0 + \tfrac12\chi^2_1 mixture — in practice, halve the naive p-value).

What the overdispersion test assumes:

  1. The conditional mean is correctly specified, E[y\mid x] = \exp(x'\beta). The test detects variance–mean mismatch given a right mean; a wrong mean (omitted covariate, wrong link) can masquerade as overdispersion.
  2. The alternative has the assumed variance form. dispersiontest targets \operatorname{Var} = \mu + \alpha\, g(\mu) for a chosen g; the test is most powerful against that direction. Excess zeros specifically are a different failure — handled on the next page — and may show up here as apparent overdispersion.
Math Details: The Poisson–gamma mixture and why naive SEs deflate

The variance function from a Poisson–gamma mixture

Let the conditional count be Poisson with a random rate \lambda_i = \mu_i \nu_i, where \mu_i = \exp(x_i'\beta) is the systematic part and \nu_i is an unobserved multiplier with E[\nu_i] = 1 and \operatorname{Var}(\nu_i) = \alpha. Conditional on \nu_i,

E[y_i \mid x_i, \nu_i] = \mu_i \nu_i, \qquad \operatorname{Var}(y_i \mid x_i, \nu_i) = \mu_i \nu_i .

Apply the law of total expectation over \nu_i:

E[y_i \mid x_i] = E_{\nu}\!\left[\mu_i \nu_i\right] = \mu_i\, E[\nu_i] = \mu_i .

The mean is unchanged — this is why the Poisson coefficients stay consistent. Now apply the law of total variance:

\operatorname{Var}(y_i \mid x_i) = \underbrace{E_\nu\!\left[\operatorname{Var}(y_i\mid \nu_i)\right]}_{\text{within}} + \underbrace{\operatorname{Var}_\nu\!\left(E[y_i\mid \nu_i]\right)}_{\text{between}} .

The first term is E_\nu[\mu_i\nu_i] = \mu_i. The second is \operatorname{Var}_\nu(\mu_i\nu_i) = \mu_i^2\,\operatorname{Var}(\nu_i) = \alpha\,\mu_i^2. Adding,

\boxed{\;\operatorname{Var}(y_i\mid x_i) = \mu_i + \alpha\,\mu_i^2\;}.

This is exactly the NB2 variance function. If \nu_i \sim \text{Gamma}(\text{shape}=1/\alpha,\ \text{scale}=\alpha) (so E[\nu]=1, \operatorname{Var}[\nu]=\alpha), integrating the Poisson over this gamma gives the Negative Binomial probability mass function in closed form:

\Pr(y_i = k \mid x_i) = \frac{\Gamma(k + 1/\alpha)}{\Gamma(1/\alpha)\,k!} \left(\frac{1/\alpha}{1/\alpha + \mu_i}\right)^{1/\alpha} \left(\frac{\mu_i}{1/\alpha + \mu_i}\right)^{k}.

As \alpha \to 0 this converges to the Poisson PMF e^{-\mu_i}\mu_i^k/k! — the nesting made explicit.

Why the naive standard errors are too small

The Poisson MLE solves the score equations \sum_i (y_i - \mu_i)\,x_i = 0. By the standard sandwich (robust) variance for an estimating equation, the true variance of \hat\beta is

\operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta) = A^{-1} B\, A^{-1}, \qquad A = \sum_i \mu_i x_i x_i', \qquad B = \sum_i \operatorname{Var}(y_i\mid x_i)\, x_i x_i' .

The Poisson MLE assumes \operatorname{Var}(y_i\mid x_i) = \mu_i, which makes B = A and collapses the sandwich to the naive A^{-1}. But under overdispersion \operatorname{Var}(y_i\mid x_i) = \phi\mu_i (taking the linear case for clarity), so B = \phi A and

\operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta)_{\text{true}} = A^{-1}(\phi A)A^{-1} = \phi\, A^{-1} = \phi \cdot \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta)_{\text{naive}} .

The true variance is \phi times the reported one. Standard errors — the square roots of the diagonal — are therefore too small by a factor of \sqrt{\phi}, and every z = \hat\beta / \widehat{\operatorname{se}} is inflated by \sqrt{\phi}. With \phi = 4, a genuine z = 1.4 is reported as 2.8. The quasi-Poisson fix multiplies A^{-1} by \hat\phi; the robust fix estimates B directly from \sum_i (y_i-\hat\mu_i)^2 x_i x_i', valid for any variance form.

Fitting It: R and Python

Watch the nurse-hours standard error grow as you move from naive Poisson to the corrected models.

  • Python
# install.packages(c("MASS", "AER", "sandwich", "lmtest"))
library(MASS)      # glm.nb
library(AER)       # dispersiontest
library(sandwich)  # robust (sandwich) covariance
library(lmtest)    # coeftest

set.seed(2024)
n <- 1200
nurse_hours <- round(runif(n, 0, 6), 1)          # extra nurse-hours / quarter
hba1c_z     <- rnorm(n)                            # standardised baseline HbA1c

# True log-rate: more nurse-hours -> fewer return visits; worse HbA1c -> more.
mu <- exp(1.1 - 0.18 * nurse_hours + 0.35 * hba1c_z)

# Overdispersion via a gamma rate multiplier nu ~ Gamma(mean 1, var = alpha).
alpha_true <- 0.5
nu      <- rgamma(n, shape = 1 / alpha_true, scale = alpha_true)
visits  <- rpois(n, lambda = mu * nu)             # Poisson-gamma => NB counts

df <- data.frame(visits, nurse_hours, hba1c_z)

## 1. Poisson — point estimates are fine, the SEs are not -------------------
m_pois <- glm(visits ~ nurse_hours + hba1c_z, family = poisson, data = df)

## 2. Test for overdispersion (Cameron–Trivedi). H0: equidispersion --------
dispersiontest(m_pois, trafo = 2)   # trafo=2 targets Var = mu + alpha*mu^2
# -> estimated alpha ~0.5 (recovering the truth) and a tiny p-value: reject
#    equidispersion. (Omit trafo for the raw dispersion ratio phi > 1 instead.)

## 3. Negative Binomial — models the dispersion directly -------------------
m_nb <- glm.nb(visits ~ nurse_hours + hba1c_z, data = df)
summary(m_nb)                       # theta = 1/alpha; SEs are wider than Poisson
cat("Estimated alpha = ", 1 / m_nb$theta, "\n")   # ~ 0.5, recovering the truth

## 4. Robust (sandwich) SEs on the Poisson fit — assumption-free repair ----
coeftest(m_pois, vcov = sandwich)   # same betas as Poisson, honest SEs

# Compare the nurse_hours SE across the three: naive Poisson is far too small.
se_pois   <- summary(m_pois)$coefficients["nurse_hours", "Std. Error"]
se_robust <- sqrt(diag(sandwich(m_pois)))["nurse_hours"]
se_nb     <- summary(m_nb)$coefficients["nurse_hours", "Std. Error"]
cat(sprintf("SE(nurse_hours): Poisson=%.4f  Robust=%.4f  NB=%.4f\n",
            se_pois, se_robust, se_nb))

import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import statsmodels.api as sm
import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

rng = np.random.default_rng(2024)
n = 1200
nurse_hours = np.round(rng.uniform(0, 6, n), 1)   # extra nurse-hours / quarter
hba1c_z     = rng.standard_normal(n)               # standardised baseline HbA1c

# True log-rate: nurse-hours lower visits; worse HbA1c raises them.
mu = np.exp(1.1 - 0.18 * nurse_hours + 0.35 * hba1c_z)

# Overdispersion via a gamma rate multiplier nu ~ Gamma(mean 1, var = alpha).
alpha_true = 0.5
nu     = rng.gamma(shape=1 / alpha_true, scale=alpha_true, size=n)
visits = rng.poisson(mu * nu)                      # Poisson-gamma => NB counts

df = pd.DataFrame({"visits": visits,
                   "nurse_hours": nurse_hours,
                   "hba1c_z": hba1c_z})

# 1. Poisson — consistent betas, but SEs assume equidispersion --------------
m_pois = smf.glm("visits ~ nurse_hours + hba1c_z",
                 data=df, family=sm.families.Poisson()).fit()

# 2. Quick overdispersion check: Pearson chi2 / df should be ~1 under Poisson.
phi_hat = m_pois.pearson_chi2 / m_pois.df_resid
print(f"Pearson dispersion phi_hat = {phi_hat:.3f}")   # >1 => overdispersed

# 3. Negative Binomial (NB2) — estimates alpha jointly with beta ------------
m_nb = smf.glm("visits ~ nurse_hours + hba1c_z",
               data=df,
               family=sm.families.NegativeBinomial(alpha=alpha_true)).fit()
# For alpha estimated by ML, use the discrete model:
m_nb_ml = sm.NegativeBinomial.from_formula(
    "visits ~ nurse_hours + hba1c_z", data=df).fit(disp=0)
print(f"Estimated alpha = {m_nb_ml.params['alpha']:.3f}")  # ~0.5, recovered

# 4. Robust (sandwich) SEs on the Poisson fit — assumption-free repair ------
m_pois_robust = smf.glm("visits ~ nurse_hours + hba1c_z",
                        data=df, family=sm.families.Poisson()).fit(cov_type="HC0")

print("SE(nurse_hours):",
      f"Poisson={m_pois.bse['nurse_hours']:.4f}",
      f"Robust={m_pois_robust.bse['nurse_hours']:.4f}",
      f"NB={m_nb_ml.bse['nurse_hours']:.4f}")
# Naive Poisson SE is the smallest; robust and NB are markedly wider.

In both languages the three approaches agree on the coefficient (about -0.18 per nurse-hour) but disagree sharply on its standard error: the naive Poisson SE is the smallest, and the robust and NB SEs are roughly \sqrt{\hat\phi} times larger. The dispersion test confirms the inflation is real, and the recovered \hat\alpha \approx 0.5 matches the value we simulated.

Exercises

Exercise 1

A Region Uppsala study of post-discharge readmissions fits a Poisson model and reports a nurse-hours coefficient \hat\beta = -0.18 with Poisson standard error \widehat{\operatorname{se}}_{\text{Pois}} = 0.05. The Pearson dispersion is \hat\phi = 4.

  1. What is the Poisson z-statistic? (b) What is the dispersion-corrected standard error and the corrected z? (c) At the 5% two-sided level, does the conclusion change?
Show answer

(a) z_{\text{Pois}} = |{-0.18}| / 0.05 = 3.60 — comfortably “significant.”

(b) The corrected SE inflates by \sqrt{\hat\phi} = \sqrt{4} = 2: \widehat{\operatorname{se}}_{\text{corr}} = 0.05 \times 2 = 0.10. The corrected z = 0.18/0.10 = 1.80.

(c) Yes, the conclusion flips. The Poisson z = 3.60 exceeds the critical 1.96 (reject H_0, “significant”); the corrected z = 1.80 falls below 1.96 (fail to reject, not significant at 5%). The overdispersion had manufactured the significance — the honest analysis cannot distinguish the nursing effect from zero.

Exercise 2

Explain in your own words why overdispersion leaves the Poisson coefficients consistent but ruins the Poisson standard errors. Reference the law of total variance.

Show answer

Write the count as Poisson with a random rate \mu\nu, where \nu is an unobserved multiplier with mean 1 and variance \alpha. By the law of total expectation, E[y\mid x] = E_\nu[\mu\nu] = \mu\,E[\nu] = \mu — averaging over the heterogeneity leaves the mean exactly \mu = \exp(x'\beta). Since the Poisson estimating equations only require a correct mean, \hat\beta stays consistent.

By the law of total variance, the variance gains an extra “between” term: \operatorname{Var}(y\mid x) = E_\nu[\mu\nu] + \operatorname{Var}_\nu(\mu\nu) = \mu + \alpha\mu^2 > \mu. The Poisson likelihood computes its standard errors as if the variance were \mu, so it underestimates the true variance of \hat\beta by roughly a factor of \phi — standard errors come out too small by \sqrt{\phi}. The mean is right; the variance the model assumes for inference is wrong.

Exercise 3

For the NB2 variance function \operatorname{Var}(y\mid x) = \mu + \alpha\mu^2 with \alpha = 0.5, compute the dispersion ratio \phi = \operatorname{Var}/\mu at \mu = 2 and at \mu = 8 return visits. What does the comparison reveal about where Poisson fails worst, and what happens as \alpha \to 0?

Show answer

The dispersion ratio is \phi = \operatorname{Var}/\mu = (\mu + \alpha\mu^2)/\mu = 1 + \alpha\mu.

  • At \mu = 2: \phi = 1 + 0.5 \times 2 = 2.0 (variance twice the mean; SE inflation \sqrt{2} \approx 1.41).
  • At \mu = 8: \phi = 1 + 0.5 \times 8 = 5.0 (variance five times the mean; SE inflation \sqrt{5} \approx 2.24).

Because \phi = 1 + \alpha\mu grows linearly in \mu, overdispersion bites hardest at high means — the heavy-visiting patient subgroups, exactly where the policy stakes are largest. This matches the lab, where the points peeled off the Poisson line most dramatically at the right end of the x-axis.

As \alpha \to 0, \phi = 1 + \alpha\mu \to 1 for every \mu: the NB variance collapses to the Poisson identity and equidispersion is restored. Poisson is the \alpha = 0 special case.

Exercise 4

A colleague fits Poisson, gets a tiny p-value on a läkare-continuity covariate, and declares the effect “robust.” You suspect overdispersion. (a) Name two diagnostics you would run before trusting the p-value. (b) Your robust-SE and quasi-Poisson fixes give nearly identical, wide standard errors, but the NB SE is noticeably smaller than both. What does that pattern suggest, and which SE should you report?

Show answer

(a) (i) Compute the Pearson dispersion \hat\phi = \text{Pearson }\chi^2 / \text{df}; values well above 1 signal overdispersion. (ii) Run a formal overdispersion test — AER::dispersiontest (Cameron–Trivedi regression test of H_0\!: \alpha = 0) or a likelihood-ratio test comparing the Poisson and NB fits. A small p on either says the equidispersion-based Poisson SEs are not trustworthy.

(b) If the NB SE is meaningfully smaller than the agnostic robust/quasi-Poisson SEs, the NB variance assumption (\operatorname{Var} = \mu + \alpha\mu^2) is doing real work — it is more efficient precisely because it imposes a parametric variance form. That efficiency is only a free lunch if that form is correct. The robust SE makes no such assumption, so when you are not confident the NB2 variance function is right (e.g., the true variance might be linear, or there could be excess zeros), the safer report is the robust (sandwich) SE, accepting a small efficiency loss for protection against a misspecified variance. If diagnostics confirm the NB2 form fits well (e.g., NB residual checks, comparable AIC), reporting the more efficient NB SE is defensible.

Key takeaways

  • Poisson regression forces equidispersion, \operatorname{Var}(y\mid x) = E[y\mid x]; real counts are usually overdispersed (\operatorname{Var} > E), almost always because of unobserved heterogeneity.
  • Overdispersion leaves the Poisson coefficients consistent but deflates the standard errors by about \sqrt{\phi}, inflating z-statistics and manufacturing spurious significance.
  • The Negative Binomial (NB2) adds a dispersion parameter \alpha with variance \operatorname{Var} = \mu + \alpha\mu^2; it nests Poisson at \alpha = 0, so a test of \alpha = 0 is a test for overdispersion.
  • Quasi-Poisson rescales the Poisson covariance by \hat\phi (linear variance); robust (sandwich) SEs assume no variance form at all (safest default) — all three rely on a correct mean model.
  • Always test (Cameron–Trivedi dispersiontest or an NB-vs-Poisson LR test) and report dispersion-aware standard errors; the point estimate is rarely the problem, the confidence in it is.
