Fixed versus Random Effects

The bias-efficiency tradeoff and the Hausman test

In this section, you will learn to

  • State the single assumption — \operatorname{Cov}(\alpha_i, x_{it}) = 0 — that decides whether random effects (RE) is consistent, and explain why fixed effects (FE) never needs it
  • Frame the FE-versus-RE choice as a genuine bias-efficiency tradeoff rather than a contest with one right answer
  • Construct the Hausman statistic from the gap between the FE and RE coefficient vectors and read its \chi^2 rejection as evidence against RE’s orthogonality assumption
  • Explain why \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{FE}} - \hat\beta_{\text{RE}}) = \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{FE}}) - \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{RE}}) holds under the null (Hausman’s efficiency lemma)
  • Apply practical guidance — and its caveats — to a Swedish primary-care panel where region effects plausibly correlate with a policy regressor

One Assumption Decides Everything

On the previous page we met the random effects (RE) model. It treats the unit effect \alpha_i not as a fixed nuisance parameter to be swept away, but as a random draw from a distribution, \alpha_i \sim (0, \sigma_\alpha^2), that is rolled into a composite error v_{it} = \alpha_i + e_{it}. RE then runs feasible generalized least squares (GLS) — a precision-weighted blend of the within (over-time) and between (across-unit) variation. Because it uses both sources of variation, RE is more efficient than fixed effects whenever it is allowed to run; and because it does not difference \alpha_i away, it can even estimate the effects of time-invariant regressors, which FE cannot touch.

That all sounds strictly better than fixed effects. The catch is a single assumption hiding underneath the GLS machinery.

The random effects orthogonality assumption is that the unit effect is uncorrelated with the regressors in every period:

\operatorname{Cov}(\alpha_i,\, x_{it}) = 0 \quad \text{for all } t.

Read this as: “Whatever fixed, unobserved trait \alpha_i a region carries — its administrative culture, its capital stock, its baseline population health — that trait is uncorrelated with how the region scores on the regressor x_{it}.” Equivalently, in the composite-error form y_{it} = \mu + \beta x_{it} + (\alpha_i + e_{it}), the regressor must be uncorrelated with the whole composite error v_{it}, not just with the idiosyncratic part e_{it}.

Everything on this page flows from one yes-or-no question:

Is \operatorname{Cov}(\alpha_i, x_{it}) = 0?

The two answers lead to sharply different worlds, and it is worth stating them at the very top because the rest of the page is an elaboration of this table.

  • If yes (orthogonality holds): RE is consistent and more efficient than FE. It is the better estimator, and it can additionally identify time-invariant regressors. Choosing FE here would needlessly throw away precision.
  • If no (the unit effect is correlated with x): RE is biased and inconsistent, because the GLS transformation leaves a piece of \alpha_i correlated with the regressor. FE remains consistent regardless, because the within transformation deletes \alpha_i entirely — as we proved on the Fixed Effects page.

So the choice between fixed and random effects is not a matter of taste or software defaults. It is a bias-efficiency tradeoff governed by whether one covariance is zero. The Hausman test is the formal device for interrogating that covariance using the data themselves.

Why FE Is Robust and RE Is Fragile

It helps to see mechanically why the same correlation that is harmless for FE is fatal for RE.

Fixed effects estimates \beta from the demeaned equation \tilde{y}_{it} = \beta \tilde{x}_{it} + \tilde{e}_{it} (tilde = deviation from the unit’s own time-mean). The unit effect \alpha_i, being constant over t, is annihilated by demeaning, so its correlation with x_{it} has nothing left to act on. FE is consistent whether or not orthogonality holds — it never relies on it. The price: FE uses only within-unit variation, which makes it less efficient and blind to time-invariant covariates.

Random effects does not fully demean. The feasible-GLS transformation subtracts only a fraction \theta of each unit’s mean:

y_{it} - \theta\,\bar{y}_i = \mu(1-\theta) + \beta\,(x_{it} - \theta\,\bar{x}_i) + \big[(1-\theta)\alpha_i + (e_{it} - \theta\bar{e}_i)\big], \qquad \theta = 1 - \sqrt{\frac{\sigma_e^2}{\sigma_e^2 + T\sigma_\alpha^2}}.

Read this as: “RE quasi-demeans — it removes the share \theta of each unit’s average, where \theta slides from 0 (pooled OLS) toward 1 (fixed effects) as the unit variance \sigma_\alpha^2 grows or the panel gets longer.” Because \theta < 1 in general, a residual (1-\theta)\alpha_i survives in the transformed error. If \operatorname{Cov}(\alpha_i, x_{it}) \neq 0, then that surviving piece is correlated with the (quasi-demeaned) regressor, and RE inherits an omitted-variable bias. Only when \theta = 1 — the FE limit — does the bias vanish entirely.

For random effects GLS to be the best linear unbiased estimator, two conditions must hold together:

  1. Orthogonality: \operatorname{Cov}(\alpha_i, x_{it}) = 0 for all t — the unit effect is uncorrelated with the regressors. This is the assumption the Hausman test interrogates, and the one fixed effects does not need.
  2. Strict exogeneity of the idiosyncratic shock: E[e_{it} \mid x_{i1}, \dots, x_{iT}, \alpha_i] = 0 — the same condition fixed effects also requires. Both estimators fail under reverse causality or time-varying confounding; the Hausman test says nothing about this assumption, only about the first.

When both hold, RE attains the minimum variance among consistent estimators in its class — the property that makes the Hausman denominator valid. The entire FE-versus-RE debate is about condition 1; condition 2 is shared ground.

FE and RE are the same estimator viewed through different values of \theta. Pooled OLS sets \theta = 0 (keeps all of \alpha_i); fixed effects sets \theta = 1 (removes all of \alpha_i); random effects picks the \theta \in (0,1) that minimizes variance under the assumption that \alpha_i is orthogonal to x. RE’s efficiency and its fragility are two faces of the same coin: by keeping some between-unit variation it gains precision, but that retained variation is exactly what carries the bias when orthogonality fails.

A Swedish Example Where the Assumption Plausibly Fails

Consider the 21 Swedish regioner, each observed over several years. The regressor of interest is the per-capita budget a region devotes to its structured diabetes coordination program (Diabetessamordning), measured in SEK per resident, and the outcome is the regional average HbA1c (lower is better) among Type 2 diabetes patients. We want \beta: the effect of one more SEK per resident of program spending on glycemic control.

The unit effect \alpha_i bundles everything fixed about a region — the affluence of its tax base, the density of its vårdcentraler, the share of its population that is elderly, its long-standing administrative competence. Now ask the orthogonality question honestly. Are the regions that spend more on diabetes coordination a random subset, or are they systematically the wealthy, well-organized, urban regions whose patients would have had better HbA1c anyway?

In this setting \operatorname{Cov}(\alpha_i, x_{it}) = 0 is implausible. Program spending is a policy choice made by exactly the regions whose fixed traits also drive the outcome: affluent regions with deep tax bases both fund coordination generously and enjoy better baseline glycemic control for unrelated reasons. RE would credit the program with that pre-existing regional advantage and overstate the spending effect. FE, by comparing each region only to its own past, sidesteps the entire problem. This is the textbook case for defaulting to fixed effects — and, as we will see, the Hausman test is built to flag it.

The decisive question, then, is empirical: does the RE estimate differ from the FE estimate by more than sampling noise can explain? If the two estimators disagree systematically, the only explanation (under the model) is that RE’s orthogonality assumption is violated, since FE is consistent either way. That comparison is the Hausman test.

See It: The Bias-Efficiency Tradeoff

  • the RE estimate drifts away from the true \beta (its bias grows with the correlation),

viewof refevre_draw = Inputs.button("Draw new sample", { value: 0, reduce: v => v + 1 })
refevre_trueBeta   = -0.30   // true within effect (per 100 SEK/resident)
refevre_alphaSD    = 0.55    // SD of regional fixed effect α_i (HbA1c points)
refevre_xWithinSD  = 1.00    // within-region year-to-year spending variation
refevre_noiseSD    = 0.35    // idiosyncratic shock e_it (HbA1c points)
refevre_xBaseMean  = 5.0     // mean spending (100 SEK/resident)
refevre_yBaseMean  = 7.4
// ── Simulate one balanced panel. Reactive on slider, N, T, and the button. ──
// Each region i has a latent standard-normal z_i that drives BOTH its fixed
// effect α_i AND its average spending x̄_i, with target correlation = refevre_corr.
// Within-region spending then varies year to year around x̄_i. The structural
// model uses the SAME β for every region, so FE recovers it regardless of corr.
refevre_panel = {
  refevre_draw; // dependency: clicking the button forces a fresh draw
  const N    = refevre_nRegions;
  const T    = refevre_T;
  const rho  = refevre_corr;
  const nrm  = d3.randomNormal.source(Math.random)(0, 1);
  const rows = [];

  for (let i = 0; i < N; i++) {
    const z      = nrm();                                   // shared latent
    const indep  = nrm();                                   // independent part of α
    const alphaStd = rho * z + Math.sqrt(1 - rho * rho) * indep;
    const alpha_i  = refevre_alphaSD * alphaStd;            // fixed effect (centered 0)
    const xbar_i   = refevre_xBaseMean + 1.4 * z;           // region avg spending

    for (let t = 0; t < T; t++) {
      const x = xbar_i + refevre_xWithinSD * nrm();         // within variation
      const e = refevre_noiseSD * nrm();
      const y = refevre_yBaseMean + alpha_i
                + refevre_trueBeta * (x - refevre_xBaseMean) + e;
      rows.push({ region: i, t, x, y });
    }
  }
  return rows;
}
// ── Helpers: within-unit means, simple OLS slope, and grand means. ──
refevre_groupMeans = {
  const g = d3.group(refevre_panel, d => d.region);
  const m = new Map();
  for (const [r, rows] of g) {
    m.set(r, { xbar: d3.mean(rows, d => d.x), ybar: d3.mean(rows, d => d.y), n: rows.length });
  }
  return m;
}
// ── Fixed-effects estimate: OLS on within-demeaned data, with its variance. ──
// Returns {beta, varBeta}. varBeta uses the within residual variance s^2 / Sxx,
// with the proper FE degrees of freedom (NT − N − 1).
refevre_FE = {
  const N = refevre_nRegions, T = refevre_T;
  let Sxx = 0, Sxy = 0;
  const dm = [];
  for (const d of refevre_panel) {
    const m = refevre_groupMeans.get(d.region);
    const xt = d.x - m.xbar, yt = d.y - m.ybar;
    dm.push({ xt, yt });
    Sxx += xt * xt; Sxy += xt * yt;
  }
  const beta = Sxy / Sxx;
  let sse = 0;
  for (const p of dm) { const r = p.yt - beta * p.xt; sse += r * r; }
  const df = N * T - N - 1;                 // lose N unit means + 1 slope
  const s2 = sse / df;
  return { beta, varBeta: s2 / Sxx };
}
// ── Random-effects (feasible GLS) estimate, with its variance. ──
// 1. Estimate σ_e² from the within residuals and σ_α² from the between residuals
//    (standard one-way error-components / Swamy–Arora–style moment estimators).
// 2. Form θ = 1 − sqrt(σ_e² / (σ_e² + T σ_α²)) and quasi-demean.
// 3. OLS on the quasi-demeaned data gives β_RE and its variance.
refevre_RE = {
  const N = refevre_nRegions, T = refevre_T;

  // --- within (FE) residual variance estimates σ_e² ---
  let Sxx_w = 0, Sxy_w = 0;
  for (const d of refevre_panel) {
    const m = refevre_groupMeans.get(d.region);
    Sxx_w += (d.x - m.xbar) ** 2;
    Sxy_w += (d.x - m.xbar) * (d.y - m.ybar);
  }
  const bW = Sxy_w / Sxx_w;
  let sseW = 0;
  for (const d of refevre_panel) {
    const m = refevre_groupMeans.get(d.region);
    const r = (d.y - m.ybar) - bW * (d.x - m.xbar);
    sseW += r * r;
  }
  const sigma_e2 = sseW / (N * T - N - 1);

  // --- between regression of group means: σ_1² = σ_e² + T σ_α² ---
  const gm = Array.from(refevre_groupMeans.values());
  const xbarBar = d3.mean(gm, d => d.xbar);
  const ybarBar = d3.mean(gm, d => d.ybar);
  let Sxx_b = 0, Sxy_b = 0;
  for (const g of gm) {
    Sxx_b += (g.xbar - xbarBar) ** 2;
    Sxy_b += (g.xbar - xbarBar) * (g.ybar - ybarBar);
  }
  const bB = Sxy_b / Sxx_b;
  let sseB = 0;
  for (const g of gm) { const r = (g.ybar - ybarBar) - bB * (g.xbar - xbarBar); sseB += r * r; }
  const sigma1_2 = (sseB / (N - 2)) * T;            // between residual var × T
  const sigma_a2 = Math.max((sigma1_2 - sigma_e2) / T, 1e-6);

  // --- theta and quasi-demeaning ---
  const theta = 1 - Math.sqrt(sigma_e2 / (sigma_e2 + T * sigma_a2));

  let Sxx = 0, Sxy = 0, Sxxc = 0, n = 0;
  const star = [];
  // grand means for the constant term
  const xGrand = d3.mean(refevre_panel, d => d.x);
  const yGrand = d3.mean(refevre_panel, d => d.y);
  for (const d of refevre_panel) {
    const m = refevre_groupMeans.get(d.region);
    const xs = d.x - theta * m.xbar;
    const ys = d.y - theta * m.ybar;
    star.push({ xs, ys });
  }
  // GLS with an intercept: regress ys on (const*(1−θ), xs). Solve 2×2 normal eqs.
  const c = 1 - theta;
  let Scc = 0, Scx = 0, Scy = 0, Sxxs = 0, Sxys = 0;
  for (const p of star) {
    Scc += c * c; Scx += c * p.xs; Scy += c * p.ys;
    Sxxs += p.xs * p.xs; Sxys += p.xs * p.ys; n++;
  }
  const det = Scc * Sxxs - Scx * Scx;
  const beta = (Scc * Sxys - Scx * Scy) / det;
  const intercept = (Sxxs * Scy - Scx * Sxys) / det;
  // residual variance of the GLS-transformed regression → Var(β_RE)
  let sse = 0;
  for (const p of star) { const r = p.ys - intercept * c - beta * p.xs; sse += r * r; }
  const s2 = sse / (n - 2);
  const varBeta = s2 * Scc / det;
  return { beta, varBeta, theta };
}
// ── Hausman statistic for the single slope coefficient. ──
// H = (β_FE − β_RE)² / (Var(β_FE) − Var(β_RE)).  Under H0 (RE consistent &
// efficient) the variance difference is positive and H ~ χ²(1). We guard the
// denominator: in finite samples it can go slightly negative, which signals an
// "uninformative" Hausman test (a real-world caveat we discuss below).
refevre_hausman = {
  const diff = refevre_FE.beta - refevre_RE.beta;
  const vdiff = refevre_FE.varBeta - refevre_RE.varBeta;
  const informative = vdiff > 0;
  const H = informative ? (diff * diff) / vdiff : NaN;
  return { diff, vdiff, H, informative };
}
// χ²(1) critical value at 5% = 3.841. (1 restriction: the single slope.)
refevre_crit = 3.841

// Build a sampling-distribution sketch for each estimator: a normal curve
// centered at the point estimate with its own standard error, so the reader
// SEES the FE cloud stay on target while the RE cloud slides off it.
refevre_curves = {
  const seFE = Math.sqrt(refevre_FE.varBeta);
  const seRE = Math.sqrt(refevre_RE.varBeta);
  const lo = Math.min(refevre_trueBeta, refevre_FE.beta, refevre_RE.beta) - 4 * Math.max(seFE, seRE);
  const hi = Math.max(refevre_trueBeta, refevre_FE.beta, refevre_RE.beta) + 4 * Math.max(seFE, seRE);
  const npts = 121;
  const dens = (x, mu, s) => Math.exp(-0.5 * ((x - mu) / s) ** 2) / (s * Math.sqrt(2 * Math.PI));
  const pts = [];
  for (let k = 0; k < npts; k++) {
    const b = lo + (hi - lo) * k / (npts - 1);
    pts.push({ b, d: dens(b, refevre_FE.beta, seFE), est: "Fixed effects" });
    pts.push({ b, d: dens(b, refevre_RE.beta, seRE), est: "Random effects" });
  }
  return { pts, lo, hi };
}

How to Read This Visualization

Work through the steps in order; each isolates one idea in the bias-efficiency tradeoff.

Step 1: Start with orthogonality satisfied. Drag Corr(αᵢ, spending) to 0. The two density curves — red (fixed effects) and slate (random effects) — both sit on the dashed ochre true β line. Notice the slate RE curve is taller and narrower than the red FE curve: with the assumption satisfied, RE is unbiased and more efficient (smaller standard error). The Hausman gauge sits well below its dashed \chi^2_1 threshold and the verdict is green: RE is the better choice here.

Step 2: Introduce a violation. Slide the correlation toward +0.9. Watch the slate RE curve slide to the right, away from the true β, while the red FE curve stays anchored on it. This is the whole page in one motion: bias (RE drifts) versus consistency (FE holds). The RE point-estimate card climbs toward zero — RE is now crediting the program with the affluent regions’ pre-existing advantage, understating how much spending truly lowers HbA1c.

Step 3: Watch Hausman fire. As the RE curve separates from the FE curve, the Hausman bar grows and eventually crosses the dashed threshold; the bar turns red and the verdict flips to “Reject H₀ — prefer fixed effects.” The test is detecting exactly the gap your eye sees between the two curves, scaled by how precisely each is estimated.

Step 4: Feel the efficiency cost of FE. At every setting, compare the widths of the two curves. The red FE curve is always at least as wide as the slate RE curve — FE pays for its robustness with variance, because it uses only within-region variation. When orthogonality holds (Step 1), that extra width is pure waste; when it fails (Step 2), it is the price of staying unbiased. That is the tradeoff.

Step 5: Stress-test the test. Shrink N to 12 and T to 3, then click Draw new sample a few times. With little data, the curves are wide, the Hausman bar is jumpy, and it sometimes turns grey: “uninformative.” That grey state — when the estimated \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{FE}}) - \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{RE}}) comes out negative — is a genuine, common failure of the classic Hausman test in small samples, and a reason many applied economists do not lean on it alone.

The Hausman Test

We have been eyeballing the gap between \hat\beta_{\text{FE}} and \hat\beta_{\text{RE}}. Hausman (1978) turned that gap into a formal specification test.

The logic is a clean application of a general principle. We have two estimators:

  • \hat\beta_{\text{FE}} is consistent under both hypotheses — whether or not \alpha_i is correlated with x.
  • \hat\beta_{\text{RE}} is consistent and efficient only under the null H_0: \operatorname{Cov}(\alpha_i, x_{it}) = 0, and inconsistent under the alternative.

Under H_0 both estimators converge to the same true \beta, so their difference should be small — only sampling noise. Under the alternative, RE converges to the wrong value while FE still converges to the truth, so the difference does not vanish. A large difference is therefore evidence against H_0.

The Hausman test compares the two coefficient vectors. With \hat{q} = \hat\beta_{\text{FE}} - \hat\beta_{\text{RE}}, the statistic is

H = \hat{q}^{\prime}\,\big[\,\widehat{\operatorname{Var}}(\hat\beta_{\text{FE}}) - \widehat{\operatorname{Var}}(\hat\beta_{\text{RE}})\,\big]^{-1}\,\hat{q} \;\xrightarrow{\;d\;}\; \chi^2_{k}\quad \text{under } H_0,

where k is the number of time-varying coefficients being compared.

Read this as: “Take the gap between the FE and RE coefficient vectors, scale it by the inverse of the difference in their variance matrices, and you get a chi-squared statistic with one degree of freedom per compared coefficient.” A large H — exceeding the \chi^2_k critical value — rejects H_0 and says: the RE assumption is violated, use fixed effects.

For a single slope (k = 1), the matrix expression collapses to the simple scalar form the interactive computes:

H = \frac{(\hat\beta_{\text{FE}} - \hat\beta_{\text{RE}})^2}{\widehat{\operatorname{Var}}(\hat\beta_{\text{FE}}) - \widehat{\operatorname{Var}}(\hat\beta_{\text{RE}})}.

The numerator is the squared disagreement; the denominator is the difference in the estimators’ variances. The next section explains why that difference of variances — rather than the variance of the difference computed the usual way — is the right denominator.

Practical Guidance and Its Caveats

The Hausman test is a useful diagnostic, but it is not an oracle, and treating it as a mechanical decision rule has burned many applied researchers. A few hard-won points.

What a rejection does and does not mean. Rejecting H_0 tells you that FE and RE disagree by more than chance — which, within the assumed model, can only happen if \operatorname{Cov}(\alpha_i, x_{it}) \neq 0. It does not tell you the FE estimate is “correct” in any absolute sense; FE has its own assumptions (strict exogeneity of e_{it}, no time-varying confounding). The test discriminates between two specific estimators, not between right and wrong.

1. Failing to reject is weak evidence. A non-rejection can mean orthogonality genuinely holds — or that the test had no power (short panel, little within-unit variation, large standard errors). “We fail to reject, so we use random effects” is much weaker than it sounds: absence of evidence is not evidence of absence.

2. The classic test can be uninformative. The denominator \widehat{\operatorname{Var}}(\hat\beta_{\text{FE}}) - \widehat{\operatorname{Var}}(\hat\beta_{\text{RE}}) is guaranteed positive only asymptotically under the null. In finite samples it can come out negative (you saw this in the interactive at small N, T), leaving the statistic undefined. Modern practice replaces it with a regression-based Hausman test — an auxiliary regression that is robust to clustering and never produces a negative variance — or the Mundlak/Wooldridge approach of the next page. That robust version is also the fix when errors are clustered or heteroskedastic (the norm in panels), where the classic variance-difference denominator is no longer valid.

Why many applied economists default to fixed effects. In observational micro-panels, the orthogonality assumption \operatorname{Cov}(\alpha_i, x_{it}) = 0 is rarely defensible: the whole reason you have unobserved unit heterogeneity is usually that it is entangled with your regressors (regions that spend more are systematically different regions). The efficiency gain from RE is real but modest; the bias from a violated assumption can be large and is hard to sign. Faced with that asymmetry — small upside, large downside — many researchers simply report FE as their headline specification and use RE (or the Mundlak device) only when they can argue orthogonality on substantive grounds. The Hausman test then plays a supporting role: a rejection confirms the default; a non-rejection is treated cautiously, not as a green light.

When should you reach for random effects? When orthogonality is plausible by design (e.g., the unit “effect” is genuinely a random sampling artifact, as in many cluster-randomized trials), when you must estimate a time-invariant regressor’s effect (FE cannot), or when T is very small and the efficiency gain is decisive. Even then, the correlated random effects / Mundlak device of the next page often dominates: it keeps RE’s ability to handle time-invariant covariates while neutralizing the bias from correlated \alpha_i.

Math Details: Why Var(b_FE − b_RE) = Var(b_FE) − Var(b_RE) Under the Null

The puzzle

For two arbitrary random vectors a and b,

\operatorname{Var}(a - b) = \operatorname{Var}(a) + \operatorname{Var}(b) - 2\operatorname{Cov}(a, b).

So in general the variance of the difference of the two estimators is

\operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{FE}} - \hat\beta_{\text{RE}}) = \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{FE}}) + \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{RE}}) - 2\operatorname{Cov}(\hat\beta_{\text{FE}}, \hat\beta_{\text{RE}}).

Yet the Hausman denominator is the difference of the variances, \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{FE}}) - \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{RE}}). For these to agree we would need

\operatorname{Cov}(\hat\beta_{\text{FE}}, \hat\beta_{\text{RE}}) = \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{RE}}).

That is exactly what Hausman proved, and the engine is the efficiency of \hat\beta_{\text{RE}} under H_0.

Hausman’s efficiency lemma

Lemma. Let \hat\beta_{\text{eff}} be an efficient estimator under H_0 (it attains the smallest asymptotic variance in its class), and let \hat\beta_{\text{cons}} be any other consistent estimator. Then the efficient estimator is asymptotically uncorrelated with the difference \hat{q} = \hat\beta_{\text{cons}} - \hat\beta_{\text{eff}}:

\operatorname{Cov}(\hat\beta_{\text{eff}},\, \hat{q}) = 0.

Proof by contradiction. Suppose \operatorname{Cov}(\hat\beta_{\text{eff}}, \hat{q}) = C \neq 0. Construct a new estimator by combining the efficient estimator with a multiple of the (mean-zero, under H_0) difference:

\hat\beta_{\lambda} = \hat\beta_{\text{eff}} - \lambda\,\hat{q}, \qquad \lambda \in \mathbb{R}.

Since \hat{q} \xrightarrow{p} 0 under H_0 (both estimators are consistent for the same \beta), \hat\beta_\lambda is also consistent for \beta for any \lambda. Its variance is

\operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_\lambda) = \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{eff}}) - 2\lambda\,C + \lambda^2 \operatorname{Var}(\hat{q}).

Differentiate with respect to \lambda and set to zero: the variance is minimized at \lambda^\star = C / \operatorname{Var}(\hat{q}) \neq 0, giving

\operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\lambda^\star}) = \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{eff}}) - \frac{C^2}{\operatorname{Var}(\hat{q})} < \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{eff}}).

But this is a consistent estimator with strictly smaller variance than the efficient one — a contradiction, since \hat\beta_{\text{eff}} was assumed efficient. Hence C = 0. \;\blacksquare

From the lemma to the denominator

Apply the lemma with \hat\beta_{\text{eff}} = \hat\beta_{\text{RE}} (efficient under H_0) and \hat\beta_{\text{cons}} = \hat\beta_{\text{FE}} (consistent under H_0). The lemma gives \operatorname{Cov}(\hat\beta_{\text{RE}}, \hat{q}) = 0, i.e.

\operatorname{Cov}\!\big(\hat\beta_{\text{RE}},\, \hat\beta_{\text{FE}} - \hat\beta_{\text{RE}}\big) = 0 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; \operatorname{Cov}(\hat\beta_{\text{FE}}, \hat\beta_{\text{RE}}) = \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{RE}}).

Substitute this into the general variance-of-a-difference formula:

\begin{aligned} \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{FE}} - \hat\beta_{\text{RE}}) &= \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{FE}}) + \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{RE}}) - 2\operatorname{Cov}(\hat\beta_{\text{FE}}, \hat\beta_{\text{RE}}) \\[2pt] &= \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{FE}}) + \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{RE}}) - 2\operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{RE}}) \\[2pt] &= \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{FE}}) - \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{RE}}). \end{aligned}

This is the boxed identity that lets us compute the Hausman denominator from the two individual variances, without ever estimating the cross-covariance \operatorname{Cov}(\hat\beta_{\text{FE}}, \hat\beta_{\text{RE}}) — which would be awkward to obtain. The whole convenience of the classic Hausman test rests on this lemma.

The catch, restated

The identity holds only under H_0, because that is where the efficiency of \hat\beta_{\text{RE}} — the premise of the lemma — applies. Under the alternative, RE is no longer efficient (it is not even consistent), so the cross-covariance need not equal \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{RE}}), and the denominator \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{FE}}) - \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{RE}}) can fail to be positive in finite samples. That is precisely the “uninformative Hausman” pathology you triggered in the interactive at small N and T.

Code: FE, RE, and the Hausman Test

The code below builds a region-year panel in which the unit effect \alpha_i is deliberately correlated with program spending, then fits fixed effects, random effects, and the Hausman test. FE should land near the true \beta = -0.30; RE should be biased toward zero; Hausman should reject.

  • R
  • Python
library(plm)       # panel estimators + phtest (Hausman)
library(dplyr)

set.seed(2026)

# --- Simulate 21 regioner over 6 years, with Cov(alpha_i, spending) > 0 ---
N <- 21; T <- 6
true_beta <- -0.30                       # effect of +100 SEK/resident on HbA1c

regions <- tibble(
  region = 1:N,
  z      = rnorm(N)                       # latent driving BOTH alpha and spending
) |>
  mutate(
    alpha     = 0.55 * z,                 # fixed effect correlated with z
    staff_bar = 5.0 + 1.4 * z             # region-average spending (100 SEK/cap)
  )

panel <- expand.grid(region = 1:N, year = 1:T) |>
  left_join(regions, by = "region") |>
  mutate(
    spend = staff_bar + rnorm(n(), 0, 1.0),                 # within variation
    hba1c = 7.4 + alpha + true_beta * (spend - 5.0) + rnorm(n(), 0, 0.35)
  )

pdat <- pdata.frame(panel, index = c("region", "year"))

# --- Fixed effects (within) and random effects (GLS) ---
fe <- plm(hba1c ~ spend, data = pdat, model = "within")
re <- plm(hba1c ~ spend, data = pdat, model = "random")

cat("True beta :", true_beta, "\n")
cat("FE beta   :", round(coef(fe)["spend"], 3), "\n")   # ~ -0.30 (consistent)
cat("RE beta   :", round(coef(re)["spend"], 3), "\n")   # biased toward 0

# --- Hausman test: FE vs RE ---
h <- phtest(fe, re)
print(h)            # large chi-sq, tiny p-value => reject H0 => prefer FE

# --- Robust / regression-based Hausman (handles clustered errors) ---
# phtest with method = "aux" runs the auxiliary-regression version, which never
# returns a negative variance and accepts vcov for clustering.
phtest(hba1c ~ spend, data = pdat, method = "aux",
       vcov = function(x) vcovHC(x, method = "white1", cluster = "group"))
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
from linearmodels.panel import PanelOLS, RandomEffects

rng = np.random.default_rng(2026)

# --- Simulate 21 regioner over 6 years, with Cov(alpha_i, spending) > 0 ---
N, T, true_beta = 21, 6, -0.30
z         = rng.normal(0, 1, N)            # latent driving alpha AND spending
alpha     = 0.55 * z                       # fixed effect correlated with z
staff_bar = 5.0 + 1.4 * z                  # region-average spending (100 SEK/cap)

rows = []
for i in range(N):
    for t in range(1, T + 1):
        spend = staff_bar[i] + rng.normal(0, 1.0)              # within variation
        hba1c = 7.4 + alpha[i] + true_beta * (spend - 5.0) + rng.normal(0, 0.35)
        rows.append({"region": i + 1, "year": t, "spend": spend, "hba1c": hba1c})
panel = pd.DataFrame(rows).set_index(["region", "year"])

# --- Fixed effects (within) and random effects (GLS) ---
fe = PanelOLS.from_formula("hba1c ~ spend + EntityEffects", panel).fit()
re = RandomEffects.from_formula("hba1c ~ 1 + spend", panel).fit()

b_fe, b_re = fe.params["spend"], re.params["spend"]
v_fe, v_re = fe.cov.loc["spend", "spend"], re.cov.loc["spend", "spend"]

print(f"True beta : {true_beta:.3f}")
print(f"FE beta   : {b_fe:.3f}")   # ~ -0.30 (consistent)
print(f"RE beta   : {b_re:.3f}")   # biased toward 0

# --- Hausman test (single coefficient): H = (b_FE - b_RE)^2 / (V_FE - V_RE) ---
from scipy import stats
diff  = b_fe - b_re
vdiff = v_fe - v_re                  # positive under H0; can go negative in small samples
if vdiff > 0:
    H = diff**2 / vdiff
    p = 1 - stats.chi2.cdf(H, df=1)
    print(f"Hausman H : {H:.3f}   (chi2_1 5% crit = 3.841)")
    print(f"p-value   : {p:.4f}   => reject H0 (prefer FE)" if H > 3.841
          else f"p-value   : {p:.4f}   => fail to reject")
else:
    print("Hausman uninformative: Var(FE) - Var(RE) < 0 (finite-sample artifact).")

Both routes show the same story: FE recovers \beta \approx -0.30, RE is pulled toward zero by the correlated \alpha_i, and the Hausman statistic is large enough to reject orthogonality — the data themselves tell us to prefer fixed effects.

Exercises

Exercise 1

A panel study of vårdcentral waiting times reports a fixed-effects estimate \hat\beta_{\text{FE}} = -2.40 with \operatorname{SE} = 0.50, and a random-effects estimate \hat\beta_{\text{RE}} = -1.10 with \operatorname{SE} = 0.30, for a single time-varying regressor (nurse staffing).

  1. Compute the scalar Hausman statistic. (b) Compare it to the \chi^2_1 5% critical value of 3.841 and state the conclusion. (c) Which estimator should be reported, and why?
Show answer

(a) The variances are the squared standard errors: \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{FE}}) = 0.50^2 = 0.25 and \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{RE}}) = 0.30^2 = 0.09. The difference is 0.25 - 0.09 = 0.16. The coefficient gap is \hat\beta_{\text{FE}} - \hat\beta_{\text{RE}} = -2.40 - (-1.10) = -1.30. Then

H = \frac{(-1.30)^2}{0.16} = \frac{1.69}{0.16} = 10.56.

(b) Since H = 10.56 > 3.841, we reject H_0: \operatorname{Cov}(\alpha_i, x_{it}) = 0 at the 5% level (in fact well beyond it; the \chi^2_1 1% critical value is 6.63).

(c) Report fixed effects. The rejection says RE and FE disagree by more than sampling noise can explain, which — within the model — means RE’s orthogonality assumption is violated and RE is inconsistent. FE is consistent regardless, so it is the credible estimate. The RE estimate of -1.10 is biased toward zero, understating the benefit of staffing by more than half.

Exercise 2

Explain why the difference of variances, \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{FE}}) - \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{RE}}), must be non-negative under the null, and what it means substantively when a software package returns a negative value.

Show answer

Under H_0, random effects is the efficient estimator — it attains the minimum asymptotic variance among consistent estimators in its class — while fixed effects is consistent but not efficient (it discards the between-unit variation). An efficient estimator has, by definition, a variance no larger than any competitor’s, so \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{RE}}) \le \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{FE}}), making the difference \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{FE}}) - \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{RE}}) \ge 0. This non-negativity is exactly what makes the difference a valid variance for the denominator of H, and it relies on Hausman’s efficiency lemma (\operatorname{Cov}(\hat\beta_{\text{FE}}, \hat\beta_{\text{RE}}) = \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{RE}})).

A negative estimated difference is a finite-sample contradiction of an asymptotic result. It does not mean FE is more efficient than RE in the population; it means the sample estimates of the two variances are noisy enough (typically because N or T is small, or errors are non-spherical so the efficiency premise fails) that their estimated difference flipped sign. The classic Hausman statistic is then undefined. The right response is not to force a number but to use a regression-based / robust Hausman test (or the Mundlak approach), which is guaranteed well-defined and accommodates clustered errors.

Exercise 3

A researcher wants to estimate the effect of a region being coastal (a time-invariant indicator) on its average HbA1c, using a panel of the 21 regioner over 8 years. A colleague suggests fixed effects “to be safe.” (a) Why can fixed effects not estimate this effect at all? (b) Under what assumption could random effects estimate it, and how would you assess whether that assumption is credible here?

Show answer

(a) “Coastal” never changes within a region over the study window, so it is collinear with the unit fixed effect \alpha_i. The within (demeaning) transformation subtracts each region’s own mean; for a constant regressor z_i, z_{it} - \bar{z}_i = z_i - z_i = 0, a column of zeros. A zero column carries no information, so the coefficient is not identified under FE — software drops it or returns NA. FE deliberately throws away the between-region variation, which is the only variation a time-invariant regressor has.

(b) Random effects can estimate the coastal effect, because it retains between-unit variation (it only quasi-demeans, \theta < 1). But this requires the RE orthogonality assumption, \operatorname{Cov}(\alpha_i, \text{coastal}_i) = 0 — the unobserved fixed traits of a region must be uncorrelated with its being coastal. That is doubtful: coastal regions may systematically differ in industry mix, diet, age structure, and wealth, all of which load onto \alpha_i and onto HbA1c. To assess it, you cannot use a standard Hausman test on the coastal coefficient itself (FE provides no estimate to compare against). Instead you would (i) run Hausman on the time-varying regressors to check orthogonality there as a proxy for the model’s overall credibility, and (ii) use the Mundlak / correlated-random-effects device (next page), which lets the time-invariant coefficient be estimated while explicitly modeling and netting out the correlation between \alpha_i and the time-varying regressors — a far more honest route than naive RE.

Exercise 4

In the interactive, set N = 12 and T = 3, put the correlation slider at a moderate value (say 0.4), and click Draw new sample several times. You will see the Hausman verdict flicker between “reject,” “fail to reject,” and the grey “uninformative” state across draws — even though the true correlation never changes. Explain what this demonstrates about relying on the Hausman test as a mechanical decision rule.

Show answer

The data-generating process is fixed — there genuinely is a moderate correlation between \alpha_i and spending, so RE is genuinely (mildly) biased every time. Yet the test’s verdict swings from draw to draw. Three things are happening, all symptoms of low power and small-sample fragility:

  1. Low power. With only N = 12 regions and T = 3 years, the standard errors on both \hat\beta_{\text{FE}} and \hat\beta_{\text{RE}} are large, so a moderate true bias often fails to produce a statistically significant FE–RE gap. The test frequently “fails to reject” a violation that is really there — a Type II error.

  2. Unstable variance difference. The denominator \widehat{\operatorname{Var}}(\hat\beta_{\text{FE}}) - \widehat{\operatorname{Var}}(\hat\beta_{\text{RE}}) is itself estimated noisily; in some draws it comes out negative and the classic statistic is undefined (the grey state).

  3. Knife-edge rejections. When it is defined, H straddles the 3.841 threshold, so tiny sampling differences tip the verdict either way.

The lesson: the Hausman test is a diagnostic, not a verdict. Treating “fail to reject ⇒ use RE” as an automatic rule, especially in short panels, can lead you to adopt a biased estimator simply because the test lacked the power to catch the bias. This is a central reason many applied economists default to fixed effects (whose consistency does not hinge on a test passing) and turn to RE only when orthogonality is defensible on substantive grounds — or use the more stable Mundlak device of the next page.

Key takeaways

  • The entire FE-versus-RE choice hinges on one assumption, \operatorname{Cov}(\alpha_i, x_{it}) = 0: if it holds, RE is consistent and more efficient (and can estimate time-invariant regressors); if it fails, RE is biased while FE stays consistent.
  • FE and RE are the same estimator at different quasi-demeaning weights \theta; FE (\theta = 1) deletes \alpha_i and is robust, RE (\theta < 1) keeps some between variation, gaining efficiency but inheriting bias when \alpha_i is correlated with x.
  • The Hausman test turns the FE–RE coefficient gap into a \chi^2 statistic; a large value rejects orthogonality and points to fixed effects.
  • The classic denominator equals \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{FE}}) - \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{RE}}) because, under the null, the efficient RE estimator is uncorrelated with the FE–RE difference (Hausman’s lemma) — which is also why the difference can go negative and “uninformative” in small samples.
  • Treat Hausman as a diagnostic, not an oracle: failing to reject is weak evidence, the test can be uninformative or non-robust, and in observational panels many economists default to FE because the downside of a violated RE assumption dwarfs the modest efficiency it offers.

But the test leaves us with an uncomfortable either/or: take FE and lose all ability to estimate time-invariant regressors, or take RE and risk bias from a correlated \alpha_i. The next page, Correlated Random Effects: The Mundlak Device, dissolves that dilemma. Mundlak’s insight is to model the correlation directly — by adding the unit means \bar{x}_i as regressors, random effects can reproduce the FE slope on time-varying covariates while still estimating time-invariant ones, and the coefficients on those added means deliver a Hausman test as an ordinary regression output. It is the bridge between the two worlds we have been forced to choose between.
