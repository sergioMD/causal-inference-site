The Random Effects Model

Treating unit heterogeneity as random

In this section, you will learn to

  • Write the unobserved-effects model y_{it} = \mathbf{x}_{it}'\boldsymbol\beta + \alpha_i + \varepsilon_{it} and state the random-effects assumption that \alpha_i is a random draw uncorrelated with the regressors
  • Explain why the composite error u_{it} = \alpha_i + \varepsilon_{it} is serially correlated within a unit, so pooled OLS is unbiased but inefficient
  • Derive the quasi-demeaning parameter \theta = 1 - \sqrt{\sigma_\varepsilon^2 / (\sigma_\varepsilon^2 + T\sigma_\alpha^2)} and recognize the RE estimator as feasible GLS
  • Show that random effects nests pooled OLS (\theta = 0) and fixed effects (\theta = 1) as the two endpoints of a single dial
  • Judge when the efficiency gain of random effects is real — and when its uncorrelatedness assumption makes it inconsistent

When Heterogeneity Is Noise, Not Confounding

Sweden’s 21 regioner (regions) each run a network of vårdcentraler (primary care centers), and we observe each region over several years. On the previous panel pages we worried that the persistent, unmeasurable traits of a region — its administrative culture, its capital stock, its baseline population health — might be correlated with the policy variable we care about. That correlation is what makes pooled OLS biased, and it is what motivates the fixed-effects estimator: let each region be its own control.

But there is a second, gentler view of unit heterogeneity. Suppose a national agency randomly rotates a quality-improvement coaching program across vårdcentraler, and we ask whether the program lowers the share of diabetes patients with poorly controlled HbA1c. The regions still differ from one another in ways we cannot fully measure — but if the program was assigned without regard to those regional traits, then the region-specific intercept \alpha_i is just unexplained variation, not a confounder. It raises or lowers a region’s outcome, but it is unrelated to who got the program.

In that world, throwing away the between-region variation — as fixed effects does — is wasteful. The between-region comparisons are perfectly clean, because nothing systematic is hiding in them. The random effects model is built for exactly this situation: it treats \alpha_i not as a parameter to be swept out, but as a random draw from a distribution, and then uses both the within-region and the between-region variation to estimate \boldsymbol\beta as efficiently as possible.

This page is about that trade. Random effects buys precision by making a strong assumption — that \alpha_i is uncorrelated with the regressors. When the assumption holds, it is the most efficient linear estimator available. When it fails, it is biased, and you should be using fixed effects instead. The next page makes that choice formal; here we build the machinery and the intuition.

The Unobserved-Effects Model, Read Two Ways

Let y_{it} be the outcome for region i in year t — say, the percentage-point share of a region’s diabetes patients with HbA1c above the control threshold (lower is better). Let \mathbf{x}_{it} be a vector of regressors (the coaching-program indicator, nurse staffing, mean patient age). The unobserved-effects model is

y_{it} = \mathbf{x}_{it}'\boldsymbol\beta + \alpha_i + \varepsilon_{it}, \qquad i = 1,\dots,N, \quad t = 1,\dots,T.

This is the same equation that underlies fixed effects. What changes is the story we tell about \alpha_i.

In the model y_{it} = \mathbf{x}_{it}'\boldsymbol\beta + \alpha_i + \varepsilon_{it}:

  • Fixed effects treats \alpha_i as a parameter — one unknown number per region, estimated alongside \boldsymbol\beta (or differenced away). It makes no assumption about how \alpha_i relates to \mathbf{x}_{it}, so it permits \operatorname{Cov}(\alpha_i, \mathbf{x}_{it}) \neq 0.
  • Random effects treats \alpha_i as a random variable — a single draw, for region i, from a distribution with mean zero and variance \sigma_\alpha^2. The defining assumption is

\operatorname{Cov}(\alpha_i,\, \mathbf{x}_{it}) = 0 \quad\text{for all } t,

i.e. the region-specific draw is uncorrelated with every regressor in every period.

Read this as: “Each region’s baseline is a lottery ticket drawn from a common urn; fixed effects estimates each ticket, random effects only estimates the spread of the urn and assumes the draw does not depend on the things we are studying.”

Under the random-effects assumption, \alpha_i is no longer a confounder — it is structured noise. We do not need to estimate one intercept per region. We only need to estimate one extra number, \sigma_\alpha^2, the variance of the urn. That economy of parameters is the source of the efficiency gain, and it is the whole reason to consider random effects at all.

The random-effects exogeneity assumption. Beyond the usual idiosyncratic exogeneity E[\varepsilon_{it}\mid \mathbf{x}_{i1},\dots,\mathbf{x}_{iT}, \alpha_i] = 0, the random-effects estimator additionally requires

E[\alpha_i \mid \mathbf{x}_{i1},\dots,\mathbf{x}_{iT}] = 0.

Read this as: “Knowing a region’s entire history of regressors tells you nothing about its random intercept.” This is much stronger than what fixed effects needs. If high-\alpha_i regions (better baseline systems) systematically staff more nurses, this assumption fails, \hat{\boldsymbol\beta}_{\text{RE}} is biased, and fixed effects — which never makes this assumption — is the safe choice.

The Composite Error Has a Memory

Why can we not just run pooled OLS and be done? Pooled OLS is unbiased under the random-effects assumption — the regressors are uncorrelated with \alpha_i and with \varepsilon_{it}, so there is no omitted-variable bias. The problem is efficiency, and to see it we collapse \alpha_i and \varepsilon_{it} into a single composite error:

u_{it} = \alpha_i + \varepsilon_{it}.

The pooled regression y_{it} = \mathbf{x}_{it}'\boldsymbol\beta + u_{it} has an error that is not well-behaved. The region-specific component \alpha_i appears in every period for region i, so two observations from the same region share it. That induces within-unit serial correlation. Assuming \alpha_i and \varepsilon_{it} are mutually independent with variances \sigma_\alpha^2 and \sigma_\varepsilon^2:

\operatorname{Var}(u_{it}) = \sigma_\alpha^2 + \sigma_\varepsilon^2, \operatorname{Cov}(u_{it},\, u_{is}) = \sigma_\alpha^2 \quad \text{for } t \neq s \text{ (same region)}.

Read the second line aloud: “Any two observations from the same region have a covariance equal to the variance of the region effect — they are correlated simply because they share the same \alpha_i.” The implied within-region correlation between any two time periods is the intraclass correlation

\rho = \frac{\sigma_\alpha^2}{\sigma_\alpha^2 + \sigma_\varepsilon^2},

read as “the share of total error variance that comes from the persistent region effect.” When \rho is large, most of the noise is the region’s permanent level, and observations within a region are highly redundant.

Pooled OLS ignores this correlation. It is still unbiased under the RE assumption, but its conventional standard errors are wrong (they treat correlated region-years as independent) and it is inefficient (it weights redundant observations as if they carried independent information). The cure is generalized least squares (GLS): transform the data so the error becomes spherical again, then run OLS on the transformed data. The random-effects estimator is that GLS transform.

Random Effects Is Quasi-Demeaning

Here is the elegant payoff. Fixed effects removes \alpha_i by subtracting each region’s entire time mean — it demeans completely. Pooled OLS subtracts nothing. The GLS solution to the serially correlated error turns out to subtract a fraction of the unit mean:

The random-effects (feasible GLS) estimator is OLS applied to the quasi-demeaned data

y_{it} - \theta\,\bar{y}_i = (\mathbf{x}_{it} - \theta\,\bar{\mathbf{x}}_i)'\boldsymbol\beta + (u_{it} - \theta\,\bar{u}_i),

where \bar{y}_i = \tfrac{1}{T}\sum_t y_{it} is the unit time-average and the quasi-demeaning parameter is

\theta = 1 - \sqrt{\dfrac{\sigma_\varepsilon^2}{\sigma_\varepsilon^2 + T\,\sigma_\alpha^2}} \;\in\; [0,1].

Read this as: “Instead of subtracting each region’s full mean, subtract only the fraction \theta of it — and that fraction is dictated by how big the region effect is relative to the idiosyncratic noise, scaled by the number of periods T.”

The parameter \theta is the single dial that controls everything. Look at its two endpoints:

  • If \sigma_\alpha^2 = 0 (no region effect at all), then \theta = 1 - \sqrt{\sigma_\varepsilon^2/\sigma_\varepsilon^2} = 1 - 1 = 0. Quasi-demeaning subtracts nothing: random effects collapses to pooled OLS. There is no serial correlation to correct.

  • As \sigma_\alpha^2 \to \infty (the region effect dwarfs the noise), the fraction under the root \to 0, so \theta \to 1. Quasi-demeaning subtracts the full unit mean: random effects collapses to fixed effects (the within estimator). The persistent region level is so dominant that the only trustworthy variation is within-region.

Between these poles, \theta interpolates. The larger the intraclass correlation \rho — the more of the noise that is persistent region effect — the closer \theta is to 1 and the more random effects behaves like fixed effects. The more periods T we observe, the more precisely each region’s mean is pinned down, and again the closer \theta moves toward 1.

Pooled OLS, random effects, and fixed effects are not three rival philosophies — they are one estimator at three settings of \theta. Pooled OLS sets \theta = 0 and trusts all variation equally. Fixed effects sets \theta = 1 and trusts only within-unit variation. Random effects lets the data choose \theta from the variance ratio, optimally blending the between-unit and within-unit comparisons. When its assumption holds, that optimal blend is more efficient than either extreme.

In practice \sigma_\alpha^2 and \sigma_\varepsilon^2 are unknown, so we plug in consistent estimates (typically from the within and between residuals) to get \hat\theta. The two-step procedure — estimate the variances, then run OLS on the quasi-demeaned data — is feasible GLS, and it is what plm(model="random") and linearmodels.RandomEffects do under the hood.

See It: The Quasi-Demeaning Dial

The interactive below simulates a panel of Sweden’s 21 regioner observed over several years. There is a single regressor x_{it} (think nurse staffing density, centered) and a true slope \beta. Each region gets its own random intercept \alpha_i, drawn independently of x — so the random-effects assumption holds by construction, and all three estimators are unbiased. What differs is how they use the data.

The left panel plots every region-year point, colored by region, with each region’s cloud connected to its own mean. Three fitted lines are overlaid: pooled OLS (slate, \theta=0), fixed effects / within (ochre, \theta=1), and the random-effects fit (Uppsala red) at the current \theta. The right panel shows where \theta sits on the [0,1] dial and how the RE slope slides between the pooled and within slopes as you move it.

Drag the variance ratio slider to change \sigma_\alpha^2/\sigma_\varepsilon^2 and watch \theta — and the red RE line — travel from the pooled line toward the within line. Draw a fresh sample to confirm the pattern is not an artifact of one draw.

Random Effects as a Dial Between Pooled OLS and Fixed Effects

viewof remod_varRatio = Inputs.range([0, 6], {
  value: 1.0,
  step: 0.1,
  label: "Variance ratio σ²α / σ²ε"
})
viewof remod_T = Inputs.range([2, 12], {
  value: 6,
  step: 1,
  label: "Periods per region (T)"
})
viewof remod_betweenStrength = Inputs.range([0, 4], {
  value: 2.0,
  step: 0.1,
  label: "Spread of region intercepts (visual)"
})
viewof remod_draw = Inputs.button("Draw new sample", { value: 0, reduce: v => v + 1 })
// ── Simulate a panel of 21 regioner. ──
// True model:  y_it = beta0 + beta1 * x_it + alpha_i + eps_it
// alpha_i is drawn INDEPENDENTLY of x_it, so the random-effects assumption
// holds and all three estimators are unbiased. The slider only changes how
// the variance is split, which changes theta and the EFFICIENCY of RE.
remod_panel = {
  const seedBump = remod_draw;      // re-run AND re-seed on button press
  const N = 21;                     // Sweden's regioner
  const T = remod_T;
  const beta0 = 12;                 // baseline % poorly-controlled HbA1c
  const beta1 = -0.8;               // true slope: more staffing -> lower share
  // We keep sigma_eps fixed and let the slider scale sigma_alpha so the
  // displayed variance RATIO equals remod_varRatio exactly.
  const sigmaEps = 1.2;
  const sigmaAlpha = sigmaEps * Math.sqrt(remod_varRatio);
  // betweenStrength stretches the VISUAL spread of region means on the x-axis
  // without touching the alpha/eps split (region means of x differ, alpha ⟂ x).
  const xSpread = remod_betweenStrength;

  // Seed depends on the draw counter so each "Draw new sample" press yields a
  // genuinely different (but reproducible) panel; sliders alone keep the seed.
  // d3.randomLcg requires a seed in [0, 1) — anything outside that range makes
  // d3 silently fall back to Math.random() (non-reproducible). The golden-ratio
  // increment spreads successive presses across [0, 1) without ever leaving it.
  const seed = (0.137 + 0.6180339887 * seedBump) % 1;
  const rNorm = d3.randomNormal.source(d3.randomLcg(seed))(0, 1);
  const rows = [];
  for (let i = 0; i < N; i++) {
    const alpha_i = sigmaAlpha * rNorm();
    // Each region has its own x-center, but alpha_i is drawn separately,
    // so Cov(alpha, x) ≈ 0 in expectation.
    const xCenter = xSpread * rNorm();
    for (let t = 0; t < T; t++) {
      const x = xCenter + 1.0 * rNorm();        // within-region x variation
      const eps = sigmaEps * rNorm();
      const y = beta0 + beta1 * x + alpha_i + eps;
      rows.push({ region: i, t, x, y, alpha_i });
    }
  }
  return rows;
}
// Per-region means, used both for plotting and for the within/between split.
remod_regionMeans = {
  const byReg = d3.group(remod_panel, d => d.region);
  const out = new Map();
  for (const [r, rows] of byReg) {
    out.set(r, {
      xbar: d3.mean(rows, d => d.x),
      ybar: d3.mean(rows, d => d.y)
    });
  }
  return out;
}
// Simple OLS slope+intercept helper on arrays of {x, y}.
remod_ols = function(pts) {
  const n = pts.length;
  const mx = d3.mean(pts, d => d.x);
  const my = d3.mean(pts, d => d.y);
  let sxx = 0, sxy = 0;
  for (const p of pts) {
    sxx += (p.x - mx) * (p.x - mx);
    sxy += (p.x - mx) * (p.y - my);
  }
  const b1 = sxy / sxx;
  const b0 = my - b1 * mx;
  return { b0, b1 };
}
// θ from the CURRENT slider values, using the same variance ratio fed to the
// simulation, so the dial and the data agree exactly.
remod_theta = {
  const T = remod_T;
  const ratio = remod_varRatio;            // = sigma_alpha^2 / sigma_eps^2
  // theta = 1 - sqrt( sigma_eps^2 / (sigma_eps^2 + T sigma_alpha^2) )
  //       = 1 - sqrt( 1 / (1 + T * ratio) )
  return 1 - Math.sqrt(1 / (1 + T * ratio));
}
// The three estimators.
//  Pooled:  OLS on raw (x, y)                       -> theta = 0
//  Within:  OLS on fully demeaned data             -> theta = 1
//  RE:      OLS on quasi-demeaned data at theta     -> the dial
remod_fits = {
  const pooled = remod_ols(remod_panel.map(d => ({ x: d.x, y: d.y })));

  const within = remod_ols(remod_panel.map(d => {
    const m = remod_regionMeans.get(d.region);
    return { x: d.x - m.xbar, y: d.y - m.ybar };
  }));

  const th = remod_theta;
  const re = { b1: remod_reSlopeAt(th) };

  return { pooled, within, re, theta: th };
}
// RE slope as a function of the quasi-demeaning parameter theta. This is the
// OLS slope on the quasi-demeaned data z_it - theta * zbar_i. NOTE: this slope
// is NOT a linear blend of the pooled (theta=0) and within (theta=1) slopes —
// it is a ratio of theta-dependent sums, so it curves between the endpoints.
// The right-panel track below samples THIS function, so the red RE marker
// always lies exactly on the plotted curve.
remod_reSlopeAt = function(th) {
  return remod_ols(remod_panel.map(d => {
    const m = remod_regionMeans.get(d.region);
    return { x: d.x - th * m.xbar, y: d.y - th * m.ybar };
  })).b1;
}
// LEFT panel: scatter of all region-years + the three fitted lines.
// Lines are drawn on the RAW (x, y) scale. For the within and RE fits, which
// were estimated on (quasi-)demeaned data, we recover a plotting intercept by
// passing the fitted slope through the grand mean of (x, y), so all three lines
// are directly comparable in slope on one axis.
{
  const pts = remod_panel;
  const xs = pts.map(d => d.x);
  const xMin = d3.min(xs) - 0.5, xMax = d3.max(xs) + 0.5;
  const gx = d3.mean(pts, d => d.x);
  const gy = d3.mean(pts, d => d.y);

  const lineFor = (slope) => {
    const intercept = gy - slope * gx;   // anchor at the grand mean
    return [
      { x: xMin, y: intercept + slope * xMin },
      { x: xMax, y: intercept + slope * xMax }
    ];
  };

  const pooledLine = lineFor(remod_fits.pooled.b1);
  const withinLine = lineFor(remod_fits.within.b1);
  const reLine     = lineFor(remod_fits.re.b1);

  return Plot.plot({
    width: 460,
    height: 380,
    marginLeft: 56,
    marginBottom: 46,
    style: { fontFamily: "'Inter', system-ui, sans-serif", fontSize: "12px" },
    x: { label: "Nurse staffing (centered)  →", grid: true },
    y: { label: "% poorly-controlled HbA1c", grid: true },
    color: { legend: false, range: ["#79766e"] },
    marks: [
      // faint region clouds
      Plot.dot(pts, {
        x: "x", y: "y",
        r: 2.6, fill: "#79766e", fillOpacity: 0.35
      }),
      // the three fits
      Plot.line(pooledLine, { x: "x", y: "y", stroke: "#3d5a6c", strokeWidth: 2.5, strokeDasharray: "6,4" }),
      Plot.line(withinLine, { x: "x", y: "y", stroke: "#9c7a33", strokeWidth: 2.5, strokeDasharray: "2,3" }),
      Plot.line(reLine,     { x: "x", y: "y", stroke: "#990000", strokeWidth: 3.5 }),
      Plot.ruleY([d3.min(pts, d => d.y) - 0.5])
    ]
  });
}

    ${row("#9c7a33", "dotted", "Fixed effects / within  (θ = 1)", remod_fits.within.b1)}

// RIGHT panel idea, rendered as a horizontal θ dial: the grey track is the
// ACTUAL locus of RE slopes as θ sweeps 0→1 (sampled from remod_reSlopeAt), the
// pooled slope is pinned at θ=0, the within slope at θ=1, and a red marker shows
// where RE sits — both in θ-position AND in slope value. Because the track is the
// true RE-slope curve (not a straight chord), the red dot always lies on it.
{
  const th = remod_fits.theta;
  const bPool = remod_fits.pooled.b1;
  const bWith = remod_fits.within.b1;
  const bRE   = remod_fits.re.b1;

  // Sample the true RE-slope curve across θ ∈ [0, 1] (41 points). The RE slope
  // is a nonlinear function of θ, so this curve bows slightly off the straight
  // chord joining the two endpoints — and the red marker rides exactly on it.
  const track = d3.range(0, 41).map(k => {
    const t = k / 40;
    return { x: t, y: remod_reSlopeAt(t) };
  });

  // map slope -> a y in the chart by using slope directly on a value axis
  return Plot.plot({
    width: 460,
    height: 200,
    marginLeft: 58,
    marginBottom: 46,
    marginTop: 28,
    style: { fontFamily: "'Inter', system-ui, sans-serif", fontSize: "12px" },
    x: {
      label: "Quasi-demeaning parameter θ  →",
      domain: [-0.04, 1.04],
      ticks: [0, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1],
      grid: true
    },
    y: { label: "Estimated slope β̂", grid: true },
    marks: [
      // the true RE-slope curve from pooled (θ=0) to within (θ=1)
      Plot.line(track, {
        x: "x", y: "y", stroke: "#cfccc2", strokeWidth: 2, curve: "catmull-rom"
      }),
      // endpoints
      Plot.dot([{ x: 0, y: bPool }], { x: "x", y: "y", r: 6, fill: "#3d5a6c" }),
      Plot.dot([{ x: 1, y: bWith }], { x: "x", y: "y", r: 6, fill: "#9c7a33" }),

How to Read the Dial

Work through these steps in order; each isolates one idea.

Step 1: Find the three lines. In the left panel, the grey dots are all N \times T region-year observations. Three lines run through them: the slate dashed line is pooled OLS (\theta=0), the ochre dotted line is fixed effects / within (\theta=1), and the solid Uppsala-red line is random effects at the current \theta. The legend below prints each slope and the true value, \beta = -0.800.

Step 2: Confirm all three are unbiased. Because the simulation draws \alpha_i independently of x, every estimator should land near -0.8. They differ a little from draw to draw (that is sampling noise, not bias) — press Draw new sample a few times and watch all three scatter around the truth, never systematically off.

Step 3: Slide the variance ratio up. Drag \sigma_\alpha^2/\sigma_\varepsilon^2 from 0 toward 6. In the right panel, the red RE marker climbs the track from the slate “Pooled” endpoint toward the ochre “Within” endpoint, and the printed \theta rises from 0 toward 1. The intraclass correlation \rho in the verdict line rises with it. The lesson: the more of the noise that is persistent region effect, the more random effects discounts the between-region comparisons and leans on the within-region ones.

Step 4: Slide it back to zero. Set the variance ratio to 0. Now \theta = 0 exactly: the red RE line lies on top of the slate pooled line. With no region effect, there is no serial correlation to fix, and feasible GLS reduces to OLS.

Step 5: Add periods. Push Periods per region (T) higher with the variance ratio fixed at a middling value. Watch \theta creep upward even though the variance ratio did not change. More time periods pin down each region’s mean more precisely, so \theta = 1 - \sqrt{1/(1 + T\rho/(1-\rho))} rises toward 1 — with long panels, random effects and fixed effects converge.

Step 6: Watch the RE slope ride the curve. Keep one eye on the right panel as you sweep the variance ratio slowly. The grey track is the true path the RE slope follows as \theta runs from 0 to 1 — it bows gently between the slate “Pooled” endpoint and the ochre “Within” endpoint rather than running dead straight, because the quasi-demeaned slope is a nonlinear function of \theta (a ratio of \theta-dependent sums), not a simple linear blend of the two extremes. The red dot always rides exactly on that curve: a smooth, single-parameter journey from pooled OLS to fixed effects governed entirely by \theta.

Why GLS Beats OLS Here

Pooled OLS and random effects target the same \boldsymbol\beta and, under the RE assumption, both are unbiased. The difference is variance. The Gauss–Markov theorem guarantees OLS is the best linear unbiased estimator only when the errors are homoskedastic and uncorrelated. Here the composite error u_{it} has the block structure we computed — a constant \sigma_\alpha^2 covariance between any two periods of the same region. Generalized least squares is the estimator that is BLUE under that error structure: it premultiplies by the inverse square root of the error covariance matrix \boldsymbol\Omega, which restores spherical errors. The quasi-demeaning transform is exactly that premultiplication written out for the random-effects \boldsymbol\Omega.

Random effects is not “fixed effects with an extra assumption.” It is GLS for a panel error that remembers its unit. When the assumption \operatorname{Cov}(\alpha_i, \mathbf{x}_{it}) = 0 holds, RE is the efficient estimator — it has smaller standard errors than both pooled OLS (which ignores the correlation) and fixed effects (which throws away clean between-unit information). The efficiency gain is largest when T is small and the between-unit variation in \mathbf{x} is large, because that is precisely the information fixed effects discards.

Math Details: Deriving θ and the GLS Transformation

The error covariance matrix for one unit

Stack the T observations of region i into vectors \mathbf{y}_i, \mathbf{X}_i, and \mathbf{u}_i = \alpha_i \boldsymbol\iota_T + \boldsymbol\varepsilon_i, where \boldsymbol\iota_T is a T\times 1 vector of ones. With \alpha_i \perp \boldsymbol\varepsilon_i, \operatorname{Var}(\alpha_i) = \sigma_\alpha^2, and \operatorname{Var}(\boldsymbol\varepsilon_i) = \sigma_\varepsilon^2 \mathbf{I}_T, the T\times T covariance of the composite error within a unit is

\boldsymbol\Omega = \operatorname{Var}(\mathbf{u}_i) = \sigma_\varepsilon^2 \mathbf{I}_T + \sigma_\alpha^2 \boldsymbol\iota_T \boldsymbol\iota_T'.

Read this as: “a diagonal \sigma_\varepsilon^2 on every observation, plus a constant \sigma_\alpha^2 added to every entry — the off-diagonal \sigma_\alpha^2 terms are the shared region effect.” GLS needs \boldsymbol\Omega^{-1/2}.

Inverting the rank-one-plus-diagonal structure

Use the projection decomposition. Let \mathbf{P} = \tfrac{1}{T}\boldsymbol\iota_T\boldsymbol\iota_T' be the matrix that averages over time (so \mathbf{P}\mathbf{z} = \bar{z}\,\boldsymbol\iota_T), and let \mathbf{Q} = \mathbf{I}_T - \mathbf{P} be the within (demeaning) projection. These are orthogonal idempotents: \mathbf{P} + \mathbf{Q} = \mathbf{I}_T, \mathbf{P}\mathbf{Q} = \mathbf{0}. Because \boldsymbol\iota_T\boldsymbol\iota_T' = T\mathbf{P}, we can write

\boldsymbol\Omega = \sigma_\varepsilon^2(\mathbf{P} + \mathbf{Q}) + T\sigma_\alpha^2 \mathbf{P} = (\sigma_\varepsilon^2 + T\sigma_\alpha^2)\,\mathbf{P} + \sigma_\varepsilon^2\,\mathbf{Q}.

Since \mathbf{P} and \mathbf{Q} project onto orthogonal subspaces, \boldsymbol\Omega acts like the scalar (\sigma_\varepsilon^2 + T\sigma_\alpha^2) on the “between” subspace and like \sigma_\varepsilon^2 on the “within” subspace. Any power of \boldsymbol\Omega is therefore obtained by taking that power of each scalar:

\boldsymbol\Omega^{-1/2} = (\sigma_\varepsilon^2 + T\sigma_\alpha^2)^{-1/2}\,\mathbf{P} + \sigma_\varepsilon^{-1}\,\mathbf{Q}.

Factor out a scalar to reveal θ

Multiply through by \sigma_\varepsilon (a harmless rescaling that does not change the GLS estimator):

\sigma_\varepsilon\,\boldsymbol\Omega^{-1/2} = \sqrt{\dfrac{\sigma_\varepsilon^2}{\sigma_\varepsilon^2 + T\sigma_\alpha^2}}\;\mathbf{P} + \mathbf{Q}.

Define

\theta = 1 - \sqrt{\dfrac{\sigma_\varepsilon^2}{\sigma_\varepsilon^2 + T\sigma_\alpha^2}},

so that the coefficient on \mathbf{P} is (1-\theta). Then

\sigma_\varepsilon\,\boldsymbol\Omega^{-1/2} = (1-\theta)\mathbf{P} + \mathbf{Q} = (1-\theta)\mathbf{P} + (\mathbf{I}_T - \mathbf{P}) = \mathbf{I}_T - \theta\mathbf{P}.

The transform is quasi-demeaning

Apply this operator to a data vector \mathbf{z}_i. Because \mathbf{P}\mathbf{z}_i = \bar{z}_i\,\boldsymbol\iota_T,

(\mathbf{I}_T - \theta\mathbf{P})\mathbf{z}_i = \mathbf{z}_i - \theta\,\bar{z}_i\,\boldsymbol\iota_T,

which in scalar form is the quasi-demeaning z_{it} - \theta\,\bar{z}_i. Running OLS on the transformed y_{it} - \theta\bar{y}_i and \mathbf{x}_{it} - \theta\bar{\mathbf{x}}_i is the GLS estimator. \blacksquare

The two limits, algebraically

  • \sigma_\alpha^2 = 0 \Rightarrow the fraction under the root is 1 \Rightarrow \theta = 0 \Rightarrow transform is \mathbf{I}_T (pooled OLS).
  • \sigma_\alpha^2 \to \infty \Rightarrow the fraction \to 0 \Rightarrow \theta \to 1 \Rightarrow transform is \mathbf{I}_T - \mathbf{P} = \mathbf{Q} (the within/FE projection).

Feasibility

\sigma_\varepsilon^2 and \sigma_\alpha^2 are unknown. Consistent estimators: \hat\sigma_\varepsilon^2 from the within (fixed-effects) residual variance, and \hat\sigma_\alpha^2 = \hat\sigma_{\text{between}}^2 - \hat\sigma_\varepsilon^2/T from the between regression of unit means. Plugging \hat\theta in gives feasible GLS, which is asymptotically equivalent to the infeasible GLS that knows the true variances.

Both implementations simulate the same panel of 21 regioner, fit pooled OLS, fixed effects, and random effects, and print \hat\theta and the three slopes so you can see the interpolation in real estimator output.

library(plm)      # panel estimators: pooling, within, random
library(nlme)     # lme(): random effects as a mixed model (ML/REML)

set.seed(2026)

# --- Simulate a panel: 21 regioner over T = 6 years ---
N <- 21; T <- 6
beta0 <- 12; beta1 <- -0.8
sigma_eps <- 1.2; sigma_alpha <- 1.2   # variance ratio = 1

regions <- data.frame(region = 1:N,
                      alpha  = rnorm(N, 0, sigma_alpha),
                      xc     = rnorm(N, 0, 2))   # region x-center (⟂ alpha)

panel <- expand.grid(region = 1:N, year = 1:T)
panel <- merge(panel, regions, by = "region")
panel$x <- panel$xc + rnorm(nrow(panel), 0, 1)
panel$y <- beta0 + beta1 * panel$x + panel$alpha +
           rnorm(nrow(panel), 0, sigma_eps)

pdata <- pdata.frame(panel, index = c("region", "year"))

# --- Three estimators on identical data ---
m_pool <- plm(y ~ x, data = pdata, model = "pooling")  # theta = 0
m_with <- plm(y ~ x, data = pdata, model = "within")   # theta = 1
m_re   <- plm(y ~ x, data = pdata, model = "random")   # feasible GLS

# Recover the estimated quasi-demeaning parameter theta-hat
ercomp(m_re)              # variance components + theta
theta_hat <- ercomp(m_re)$theta
cat(sprintf("theta-hat = %.3f\n", as.numeric(theta_hat)))

cat(sprintf("Pooled  beta = %+.3f\n", coef(m_pool)["x"]))
cat(sprintf("Random  beta = %+.3f\n", coef(m_re)["x"]))
cat(sprintf("Within  beta = %+.3f\n", coef(m_with)["x"]))

Exercises

Exercise 1

A panel of vårdcentraler has idiosyncratic error variance \sigma_\varepsilon^2 = 4 and region-effect variance \sigma_\alpha^2 = 1, observed over T = 5 years.

  1. Compute the intraclass correlation \rho. (b) Compute the quasi-demeaning parameter \theta. (c) If the agency extends the panel to T = 20 years, what happens to \theta, and what does that imply about how random effects starts to behave?
Show answer

(a) \rho = \dfrac{\sigma_\alpha^2}{\sigma_\alpha^2 + \sigma_\varepsilon^2} = \dfrac{1}{1 + 4} = 0.20. Twenty percent of the error variance is the persistent region effect.

(b) \theta = 1 - \sqrt{\dfrac{\sigma_\varepsilon^2}{\sigma_\varepsilon^2 + T\sigma_\alpha^2}} = 1 - \sqrt{\dfrac{4}{4 + 5\cdot 1}} = 1 - \sqrt{4/9} = 1 - \tfrac{2}{3} = 0.333.

So random effects subtracts one-third of each region’s mean.

(c) At T = 20: \theta = 1 - \sqrt{\dfrac{4}{4 + 20}} = 1 - \sqrt{4/24} = 1 - \sqrt{1/6} \approx 1 - 0.408 = 0.592.

\theta rises from 0.333 to 0.592 even though the variance ratio is unchanged. With more periods, each region’s mean is estimated more precisely, so GLS leans more heavily on within-region variation — random effects drifts toward fixed effects as T grows.

Exercise 2

Show that pooled OLS is unbiased for \boldsymbol\beta under the random-effects assumptions, yet its conventional (OLS) standard errors are wrong. Identify the two distinct problems with pooled OLS in this setting.

Show answer

Unbiasedness. Pooled OLS regresses y_{it} on \mathbf{x}_{it} with composite error u_{it} = \alpha_i + \varepsilon_{it}. Its bias depends on \operatorname{Cov}(\mathbf{x}_{it}, u_{it}). Under the RE assumptions, \operatorname{Cov}(\mathbf{x}_{it}, \alpha_i) = 0 and \operatorname{Cov}(\mathbf{x}_{it}, \varepsilon_{it}) = 0, so \operatorname{Cov}(\mathbf{x}_{it}, u_{it}) = 0. Hence \hat{\boldsymbol\beta}_{\text{pooled}} is unbiased and consistent.

Two distinct problems.

  1. Wrong standard errors. Conventional OLS variance formulas assume \operatorname{Var}(\mathbf{u}) = \sigma^2 \mathbf{I}. But here observations within a region share \alpha_i, so \operatorname{Cov}(u_{it}, u_{is}) = \sigma_\alpha^2 \neq 0 for t \neq s. The off-diagonal terms mean the textbook OLS standard errors are biased (typically too small), so t-statistics are overstated. (Clustering SEs by region fixes the inference but not the efficiency.)

  2. Inefficiency. Even with corrected SEs, OLS is not BLUE because the error is not spherical. It treats correlated region-years as independent draws, effectively double-counting redundant information. GLS (random effects) reweights to account for the within-region correlation and achieves smaller variance.

So pooled OLS is right on average but needlessly noisy and wrongly certain. Random effects fixes both.

Exercise 3

Starting from the GLS operator \mathbf{I}_T - \theta\mathbf{P} derived in the Math Details, verify the two limiting cases and prove that the operator equals the within projection \mathbf{Q} exactly when \theta = 1.

Show answer

Recall \mathbf{P} = \tfrac{1}{T}\boldsymbol\iota_T\boldsymbol\iota_T' (time-averaging), \mathbf{Q} = \mathbf{I}_T - \mathbf{P} (within/demeaning), and the GLS operator \mathbf{A}(\theta) = \mathbf{I}_T - \theta\mathbf{P}.

Limit \theta = 0 (pure pooled OLS): \mathbf{A}(0) = \mathbf{I}_T - 0 \cdot \mathbf{P} = \mathbf{I}_T. The operator leaves the data untouched, so GLS reduces to OLS on the raw data. This is the \sigma_\alpha^2 = 0 case: \theta = 1 - \sqrt{\sigma_\varepsilon^2/\sigma_\varepsilon^2} = 0.

Limit \theta = 1 (pure fixed effects): \mathbf{A}(1) = \mathbf{I}_T - 1\cdot \mathbf{P} = \mathbf{I}_T - \mathbf{P} = \mathbf{Q}. The operator is the within projection, so GLS demeans completely — it is the within (fixed-effects) estimator. This is the \sigma_\alpha^2 \to \infty case, where the fraction under the root \to 0 and \theta \to 1.

Applying \mathbf{Q} to data gives \mathbf{Q}\mathbf{z}_i = \mathbf{z}_i - \bar{z}_i\boldsymbol\iota_T, i.e. the full demeaning z_{it} - \bar{z}_i. So at \theta = 1 the quasi-demeaning z_{it} - \theta\bar{z}_i becomes the exact demeaning of fixed effects. \checkmark

Exercise 4

A researcher studies whether a region’s mean patient age (a slowly changing, nearly time-invariant regressor) affects the share of poorly-controlled HbA1c patients. A colleague insists on fixed effects “to be safe.” Explain why fixed effects may be unable to estimate this coefficient at all, and why random effects can — and state the price random effects pays for that ability.

Show answer

Fixed effects cannot use a time-invariant regressor. The within transformation subtracts each region’s time mean: \tilde{x}_{it} = x_{it} - \bar{x}_i. If mean patient age is (nearly) constant within a region over the study window, then x_{it} \approx \bar{x}_i, so \tilde{x}_{it} \approx 0 for every observation. A regressor that is all zeros after demeaning carries no variation, and its coefficient is not identified (it is collinear with the demeaned constant / drops out). Fixed effects can only estimate effects of variables that move within a unit over time.

Random effects can. Because \theta < 1, quasi-demeaning leaves a fraction (1-\theta) of the between-region variation in x intact. The cross-region differences in patient age survive the transformation, so the coefficient on a time-invariant (or slowly moving) regressor is identified. This is a genuine advantage of random effects: it exploits between-unit variation that fixed effects discards.

The price. Random effects buys this only by assuming \operatorname{Cov}(\alpha_i, \mathbf{x}_{it}) = 0. If regions with older populations also differ systematically in unmeasured ways correlated with the outcome (high \alpha_i), then the age coefficient is biased — it absorbs that correlation. Fixed effects is “safe” precisely because it never makes this assumption, but the safety costs you the ability to estimate time-invariant effects at all. The choice between them is the subject of the next page.

Key takeaways

  • Random effects treats the unit effect \alpha_i as a random draw uncorrelated with the regressors, \operatorname{Cov}(\alpha_i, \mathbf{x}_{it}) = 0 — it is structured noise, not a confounder.
  • The composite error u_{it} = \alpha_i + \varepsilon_{it} is serially correlated within a unit (covariance \sigma_\alpha^2), so pooled OLS is unbiased but inefficient with wrong standard errors.
  • The RE estimator is feasible GLS, implemented as quasi-demeaning z_{it} - \theta\bar{z}_i with \theta = 1 - \sqrt{\sigma_\varepsilon^2/(\sigma_\varepsilon^2 + T\sigma_\alpha^2)}.
  • \theta is a single dial: \theta = 0 is pooled OLS, \theta = 1 is fixed effects, and RE interpolates — \theta rises with the variance ratio and with T.
  • When its uncorrelatedness assumption holds, RE is efficient and can estimate time-invariant regressors; when it fails, RE is biased and fixed effects is the safe choice.

We have built the random-effects estimator as feasible GLS, derived its quasi-demeaning parameter \theta, and seen it interpolate between pooled OLS and fixed effects as the variance ratio and panel length change. But everything rested on one fragile pillar: \operatorname{Cov}(\alpha_i, \mathbf{x}_{it}) = 0. If that fails, random effects is the efficient estimator of the wrong number, while fixed effects — which never assumes it — stays consistent.
