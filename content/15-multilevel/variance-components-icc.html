Variance Components and the ICC

How much variation lives at each level

In this section, you will learn to

  • Decompose the total variance of a clustered outcome into a between-group component \tau_0^2 and a within-group component \sigma^2
  • Define the intraclass correlation \text{ICC} = \tau_0^2 / (\tau_0^2 + \sigma^2) and read it two ways — as a variance share and as the correlation between two observations in the same group
  • Compute the design effect 1 + (m-1)\,\text{ICC} and translate it into an effective sample size and a standard-error inflation factor
  • Explain why ignoring clustering deflates standard errors, connecting the variance decomposition to Module 5’s cluster-robust inference
  • Decide when multilevel modelling actually earns its keep — and when the ICC is small enough to ignore

How Much of the Variation Is the Vårdcentral?

Sweden’s 21 regioner (regions) run roughly 1,100 vårdcentraler (primary care centers), and each center serves thousands of patients. Suppose Socialstyrelsen (the National Board of Health and Welfare) pulls the HbA1c reading of every Type 2 diabetes patient in the country and asks a deceptively simple question: how much of the variation in blood-sugar control is about the patient, and how much is about the clinic they happen to attend?

Two patients at the same vårdcentral share a läkare (physician), a distriktssköterska (district nurse), a local recall system, a waiting-time culture, and a catchment population. Two patients at different centers share none of that. So we should expect patients within a center to resemble each other more than patients drawn at random from across the country. The question is how much more — and that single number turns out to govern everything from study design to standard errors.

This is the question of variance components. Instead of asking “what is the average HbA1c?” we ask “where does the spread come from?” Some of it is the genuine person-to-person difference between patients at the same clinic — the within component. Some of it is the systematic difference between clinics — the between component. Splitting the total variance into these two pieces, and forming their ratio, is the foundation of every multilevel model in this module.

The headline number is the intraclass correlation (ICC): the share of total variance that lives between groups. An ICC near 0 means clinics are interchangeable — knowing a patient’s center tells you nothing about their HbA1c. An ICC near 1 means the center is almost everything — patients within a clinic are nearly identical and the real variation is across clinics. Real primary-care data usually sit modestly above zero (ICCs of 0.05 to 0.20), and even those small numbers have outsized consequences for how much your data actually tell you.

The Two-Level Variance Decomposition

Index patients by j and the vårdcentral they attend by i. Write y_{ij} for the HbA1c of patient j at center i. The simplest multilevel model — the random-intercept model from the previous page with no predictors, often called the empty or unconditional model — splits each observation into three pieces:

y_{ij} = \gamma_{00} + u_{0i} + \varepsilon_{ij}.

Read this aloud as: “Each patient’s HbA1c equals a grand mean \gamma_{00} that holds across all of Sweden, plus a clinic-level bump u_{0i} that shifts every patient at center i up or down together, plus an individual deviation \varepsilon_{ij} that is unique to that patient.”

The two random pieces carry the two variances we care about:

u_{0i} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\, \tau_0^2), \qquad \varepsilon_{ij} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\, \sigma^2),

and crucially u_{0i} and \varepsilon_{ij} are assumed independent of each other.

The model has exactly two variance components:

  • \tau_0^2 — the between-group variance (read “tau-zero-squared”). It measures how much clinic means scatter around the national grand mean. A large \tau_0^2 means some vårdcentraler run systematically higher or lower HbA1c than others.
  • \sigma^2 — the within-group variance (read “sigma-squared”). It measures how much patients scatter around their own clinic’s mean. A large \sigma^2 means patients within a clinic differ a lot from one another.

Because u_{0i} and \varepsilon_{ij} are independent, the variance of an observation adds up cleanly:

\operatorname{Var}(y_{ij}) = \tau_0^2 + \sigma^2.

This is the total variance, and the split \tau_0^2 + \sigma^2 is the variance decomposition.

The decomposition is the whole game. Total variation in HbA1c is partitioned into the part attributable to which clinic you are at (\tau_0^2) and the part attributable to which patient you are within that clinic (\sigma^2). Everything that follows is a ratio or a function of these two numbers.

The Intraclass Correlation

The ICC is the between-group share of total variance.

The intraclass correlation coefficient is

\rho = \text{ICC} = \frac{\tau_0^2}{\tau_0^2 + \sigma^2}.

Read this as: “the between-clinic variance divided by the total variance” — the fraction of all the variation in HbA1c that is between vårdcentraler rather than within them. It is bounded in [0, 1]: it is 0 when \tau_0^2 = 0 (clinics are identical on average) and approaches 1 as \tau_0^2 dominates \sigma^2 (patients within a clinic are nearly identical).

The name “correlation” is not a metaphor. The ICC is literally the correlation between two patients drawn from the same clinic. Take two patients j and k at the same center i. They share the clinic bump u_{0i} but have independent personal deviations \varepsilon. Their covariance is therefore exactly \tau_0^2 (the only thing they share), while each has total variance \tau_0^2 + \sigma^2. The correlation is the covariance over the product of standard deviations:

\operatorname{Corr}(y_{ij},\, y_{ik}) = \frac{\operatorname{Cov}(y_{ij}, y_{ik})}{\sqrt{\operatorname{Var}(y_{ij})}\sqrt{\operatorname{Var}(y_{ik})}} = \frac{\tau_0^2}{\tau_0^2 + \sigma^2} = \text{ICC}.

Two readings of one number. The ICC is both (i) the share of variance that lives between groups and (ii) the correlation between two observations in the same group. These are the same quantity. That is why it controls clustering: a high within-group correlation is exactly a large between-group variance share, and both say the same thing — observations in the same clinic are not independent draws.

See It: The ICC Slider

The lab below simulates HbA1c for patients spread across eight vårdcentraler. As you raise the ICC, hold your eye on two things at once: the dots tighten within each clinic (points in the same column pull toward their clinic mean), while the clinic means spread apart (the columns drift away from the national line). That is the variance decomposition happening in front of you — variance migrating from the within component into the between component while the total stays fixed.

The readouts on the right turn the picture into the numbers that matter for inference: the between/within split, the design effect, the effective sample size, and the standard-error inflation versus a naive analysis that pretends every patient is an independent draw.

How to Read the ICC Lab

Work through the controls in order; each isolates one idea.

Step 1: Start at the bottom. Set ICC to 0. Every clinic’s red diamond sits on the ochre national line — the clinics are interchangeable — and the slate patient dots fill the full vertical range. All the variation is within clinics. In the split bar, the slate “Within” segment fills the whole width; the red “Between” segment has vanished. The design effect reads 1.00 and effective n equals the full sample.

Step 2: Crank the ICC up. Drag ICC toward 0.5. Two things happen at once. The slate dots in each column tighten toward their clinic’s diamond (within variance is shrinking), and the red diamonds fan apart from the national line (between variance is growing). The split bar redistributes from slate to red. Total height of the dot cloud barely changes — the total variance is fixed; variance is simply migrating from within to between.

Step 3: Watch the design effect climb. As ICC rises, the Design effect card grows above 1, the Effective n card collapses well below the true count, and SE inflation rises above \times 1. With 25 patients per clinic and an ICC of just 0.10, the design effect is already 1 + 24 \times 0.10 = 3.4: your 200 observations carry the information of only about 59 independent ones.

Step 4: Change the cluster size. Hold ICC fixed and drag Cluster size m. Bigger clusters amplify the damage: the design effect is 1 + (m-1)\,\text{ICC}, so adding patients to existing clinics buys you very little once the ICC is nonzero. This is the design lesson — to gain real information, recruit more clinics, not more patients per clinic.

Step 5: Resample. Press Draw new sample a few times. The diamonds and dots jump around, but the design effect, effective n, and SE inflation do not — they are functions of the ICC and m, not of any one noisy draw.

The Design Effect and Effective Sample Size

The reason the ICC matters for inference — not just description — is that it determines how much independent information a clustered sample actually contains. Classical statistics assumes every observation is an independent draw. Clustered data violate that: patients in the same vårdcentral are correlated, so each new patient at an existing clinic adds less than one fresh observation’s worth of information.

For a balanced design with m observations per group, the design effect is

\text{DEFF} = 1 + (m - 1)\,\text{ICC}.

Read this as: “one, plus the number of extra observations per cluster times the within-cluster correlation.” It is the factor by which the variance of an estimated mean is inflated relative to a simple random sample of the same size. The effective sample size divides the nominal count by this factor:

n_{\text{eff}} = \frac{n}{\text{DEFF}} = \frac{n}{1 + (m-1)\,\text{ICC}},

and the standard error of the estimated mean is inflated by \sqrt{\text{DEFF}} relative to the naive iid formula.

Three limiting cases make the formula intuitive, and reading each aloud cements it:

  • ICC = 0: \text{DEFF} = 1. Observations are genuinely independent; the clustering is decorative; n_{\text{eff}} = n. Naive standard errors are correct.
  • ICC = 1: \text{DEFF} = m, so n_{\text{eff}} = n/m equals the number of clusters. Every patient in a clinic is identical, so a clinic of m patients carries exactly one clinic’s worth of information — you effectively have one observation per vårdcentral.
  • Large m, small ICC: even a tiny ICC bites once clusters are big. With m = 1000 and \text{ICC} = 0.01, \text{DEFF} = 1 + 999 \times 0.01 \approx 11. A thousand patients per clinic collapse to fewer than a hundred effective ones.

Ignoring clustering deflates standard errors. If you run ordinary OLS on clustered data as though every patient were independent, your reported standard error is too small by a factor of \sqrt{\text{DEFF}}. With \text{DEFF} = 3.4 that is a factor of 1.84 — your confidence intervals are nearly half as wide as they should be, your t-statistics nearly double, and you will declare effects “significant” that the data do not support. This is the same disease diagnosed in Module 5’s practical-issues page: there, serial correlation within a unit over time inflated the variance; here it is correlation within a cluster over members. The design-effect multiplier is identical in spirit, and the cluster-robust standard errors of Module 5 are one honest fix. A multilevel model that estimates \tau_0^2 and \sigma^2 directly is another.

When Does Multilevel Modelling Actually Matter?

The ICC is the deciding diagnostic. If it is genuinely negligible, the clustering carries no information and a single-level model with cluster-robust standard errors is perfectly adequate — you do not need the machinery of random intercepts. If it is not negligible, two things are true at once: (i) your naive standard errors are dishonest, and (ii) there is real, structured between-clinic variation worth modelling explicitly.

A rough working rule for primary-care data: an ICC below \sim 0.01 with small clusters means multilevel modelling adds little; an ICC in the 0.05 to 0.20 range — typical for clinic-level health outcomes — means clustering meaningfully reduces your effective sample and a random-intercept model is warranted. But the design effect depends on m as much as on the ICC: large clusters make even a small ICC consequential. Always evaluate 1 + (m-1)\,\text{ICC}, not the ICC alone.

The empty model serves two further purposes beyond inference. First, it gives you a baseline ICC against which to judge how much between-clinic variation your predictors later explain — a multilevel analogue of R^2. Second, it tells you where to spend effort: a high ICC says the action is at the clinic level (staffing, protocols, recall systems) and points you toward clinic-level predictors; a low ICC says the action is at the patient level (age, comorbidity, adherence). The variance decomposition is not just a nuisance correction — it is the first substantive finding.

Math Details: The ICC as a correlation, and the design-effect derivation

The ICC equals the within-cluster correlation

Start from the empty random-intercept model, y_{ij} = \gamma_{00} + u_{0i} + \varepsilon_{ij}, with u_{0i} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \tau_0^2) and \varepsilon_{ij} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2), mutually independent. The variance of a single observation is

\operatorname{Var}(y_{ij}) = \operatorname{Var}(u_{0i}) + \operatorname{Var}(\varepsilon_{ij}) = \tau_0^2 + \sigma^2,

since the two random terms are independent (no covariance term). Now take two distinct patients j \neq k in the same cluster i. They share the same u_{0i}, but their idiosyncratic terms \varepsilon_{ij}, \varepsilon_{ik} are independent of each other and of u_{0i}. Their covariance is

\operatorname{Cov}(y_{ij}, y_{ik}) = \operatorname{Cov}(u_{0i} + \varepsilon_{ij},\; u_{0i} + \varepsilon_{ik}) = \operatorname{Var}(u_{0i}) = \tau_0^2,

because every cross-term involving an \varepsilon vanishes. Dividing the covariance by the product of the (equal) standard deviations gives the correlation:

\rho = \operatorname{Corr}(y_{ij}, y_{ik}) = \frac{\tau_0^2}{\tau_0^2 + \sigma^2} = \text{ICC}. \qquad\checkmark

So the ICC is simultaneously a variance ratio and a correlation. (For two patients in different clusters the shared u_{0i} disappears, the covariance is zero, and they are uncorrelated — exactly as a clustering structure should imply.)

The design effect for a cluster mean

Consider estimating the grand mean from N clusters of m observations each, n = Nm. The variance of the overall sample mean \bar{y} under the clustered structure is, with all observations weighted equally,

\operatorname{Var}(\bar{y}) = \frac{\sigma_y^2}{n}\,\big[\,1 + (m-1)\,\rho\,\big],

where \sigma_y^2 = \tau_0^2 + \sigma^2 is the total variance and \rho is the ICC. The bracket is the design effect. To see where it comes from, write the cluster total T_i = \sum_{j=1}^m y_{ij}. Its variance has m “diagonal” variance terms and m(m-1) “off-diagonal” covariance terms:

\operatorname{Var}(T_i) = m\,\sigma_y^2 + m(m-1)\,\operatorname{Cov}(y_{ij}, y_{ik}) = m\,\sigma_y^2 + m(m-1)\,\rho\,\sigma_y^2 = m\,\sigma_y^2\,[1 + (m-1)\rho].

The cluster mean \bar{y}_i = T_i / m therefore has variance (\sigma_y^2/m)[1 + (m-1)\rho], and averaging over N independent clusters divides by N:

\operatorname{Var}(\bar{y}) = \frac{\sigma_y^2}{Nm}\,[1 + (m-1)\rho] = \frac{\sigma_y^2}{n}\,\underbrace{[1 + (m-1)\rho]}_{\text{DEFF}}.

Under naive independence the variance would be just \sigma_y^2 / n. The ratio of the true variance to the naive one is precisely \text{DEFF} = 1 + (m-1)\rho (Kish, 1965).

Effective sample size and SE inflation

Equating the clustered variance to the variance a simple random sample of size n_{\text{eff}} would have, \sigma_y^2 / n_{\text{eff}} = (\sigma_y^2 / n)\,\text{DEFF}, gives

n_{\text{eff}} = \frac{n}{\text{DEFF}} = \frac{n}{1 + (m-1)\rho}.

Because variance scales with \text{DEFF}, the standard error — its square root — scales with \sqrt{\text{DEFF}}. An analyst who ignores clustering reports an SE that is too small by exactly this factor:

\frac{\text{SE}_{\text{naive}}}{\text{SE}_{\text{clustered}}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\text{DEFF}}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 + (m-1)\rho}}.

This is the algebra behind the warning card: with m = 25 and \rho = 0.10, \text{DEFF} = 3.4 and the naive SE is a factor of \sqrt{3.4} \approx 1.84 too small.

Extracting Variance Components from a Fitted Model

In practice you do not assume \tau_0^2 and \sigma^2 — you estimate them by fitting the empty random-intercept model with REML (restricted maximum likelihood), then read the two variance components off the fit and form the ICC. Both implementations below simulate diabetes patients nested in vårdcentraler with a known ICC, fit the empty model, and recover it.

  • R
  • Python
library(lme4)      # mixed models via REML
library(performance)  # icc() convenience function

# --- Simulate: 40 vårdcentraler, ~25 diabetes patients each ---
# True components: tau0^2 = 0.30 (between), sigma^2 = 1.20 (within)
#   => true ICC = 0.30 / (0.30 + 1.20) = 0.20
set.seed(2024)
n_clinic <- 40
m        <- 25
tau0     <- sqrt(0.30)
sigma    <- sqrt(1.20)
grand    <- 7.5                       # national mean HbA1c (%)

u0 <- rnorm(n_clinic, 0, tau0)        # one clinic bump per vårdcentral
dat <- data.frame(
  clinic = factor(rep(1:n_clinic, each = m)),
  hba1c  = grand + rep(u0, each = m) + rnorm(n_clinic * m, 0, sigma)
)

# --- Fit the empty (unconditional) random-intercept model ---
m0 <- lmer(hba1c ~ 1 + (1 | clinic), data = dat, REML = TRUE)

# --- Extract the two variance components ---
vc      <- as.data.frame(VarCorr(m0))
tau0_sq <- vc$vcov[vc$grp == "clinic"]      # between-group variance
sigma_sq<- vc$vcov[vc$grp == "Residual"]    # within-group variance
icc     <- tau0_sq / (tau0_sq + sigma_sq)

cat(sprintf("Between (tau0^2): %.3f\n", tau0_sq))
cat(sprintf("Within  (sigma^2): %.3f\n", sigma_sq))
cat(sprintf("ICC: %.3f\n", icc))            # ~0.20

# Design effect and effective n for an average cluster of size m
deff <- 1 + (m - 1) * icc
cat(sprintf("Design effect (m=%d): %.2f\n", m, deff))
cat(sprintf("Effective n: %.0f of %d\n", n_clinic * m / deff, n_clinic * m))

# Convenience: performance::icc() returns the same number
performance::icc(m0)
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

# --- Simulate: 40 vårdcentraler, 25 diabetes patients each ---
# True components: tau0^2 = 0.30 (between), sigma^2 = 1.20 (within)
#   => true ICC = 0.30 / (0.30 + 1.20) = 0.20
rng      = np.random.default_rng(2024)
n_clinic = 40
m        = 25
tau0     = np.sqrt(0.30)
sigma    = np.sqrt(1.20)
grand    = 7.5                                   # national mean HbA1c (%)

u0  = rng.normal(0, tau0, n_clinic)              # one clinic bump each
dat = pd.DataFrame({
    "clinic": np.repeat(np.arange(n_clinic), m),
    "hba1c":  grand + np.repeat(u0, m) + rng.normal(0, sigma, n_clinic * m),
})

# --- Fit the empty random-intercept model (REML) ---
m0 = smf.mixedlm("hba1c ~ 1", data=dat, groups=dat["clinic"]).fit(reml=True)

# --- Extract the two variance components ---
# MixedLM reports the random-effect (cov_re) and residual (scale) variances.
tau0_sq  = float(m0.cov_re.iloc[0, 0])           # between-group variance
sigma_sq = float(m0.scale)                       # within-group variance
icc      = tau0_sq / (tau0_sq + sigma_sq)

print(f"Between (tau0^2): {tau0_sq:.3f}")
print(f"Within  (sigma^2): {sigma_sq:.3f}")
print(f"ICC: {icc:.3f}")                         # ~0.20

# Design effect and effective n for clusters of size m
deff = 1 + (m - 1) * icc
print(f"Design effect (m={m}): {deff:.2f}")
print(f"Effective n: {n_clinic * m / deff:.0f} of {n_clinic * m}")

Both fits recover the planted values — a between-group variance near 0.30, a within-group variance near 1.20, and an ICC near 0.20 — because REML estimates exactly the two variance components that the decomposition defines. The design effect for clusters of 25 is then 1 + 24 \times 0.20 = 5.8: the 1{,}000 simulated patients carry the information of roughly 170 independent ones.

Exercises

Exercise 1

A regional analysis of väntetider (waiting times, in days) for a diabetes-nurse appointment fits an empty random-intercept model with patients nested in vårdcentraler. The fit reports a between-clinic variance of \tau_0^2 = 9 days^2 and a within-clinic variance of \sigma^2 = 36 days^2.

  1. Compute the ICC and state its two interpretations. (b) With m = 16 patients per clinic, compute the design effect and the SE inflation factor. (c) The naive analysis reported a 95\% confidence interval of width \pm 1.0 day for the mean waiting time. What is the honest width?
Show answer

(a) \text{ICC} = \dfrac{\tau_0^2}{\tau_0^2 + \sigma^2} = \dfrac{9}{9 + 36} = \dfrac{9}{45} = 0.20. Interpreted two ways: (i) 20\% of the total variation in waiting times is between clinics rather than within them; (ii) the correlation between the waiting times of two patients at the same clinic is 0.20.

(b) \text{DEFF} = 1 + (m-1)\,\text{ICC} = 1 + 15 \times 0.20 = 1 + 3 = 4.0. The SE inflation factor is \sqrt{\text{DEFF}} = \sqrt{4} = 2.0.

(c) The naive CI is too narrow by the SE-inflation factor, so the honest half-width is 1.0 \times 2.0 = 2.0 days, i.e. \pm 2.0 days — twice as wide as the naive analysis claimed.

Exercise 2

Two study designs are proposed to estimate mean HbA1c, both with a fixed budget of 400 patient records and a known \text{ICC} = 0.05. Design A: 20 vårdcentraler with 20 patients each. Design B: 80 vårdcentraler with 5 patients each.

  1. Compute the design effect and effective sample size for each. (b) Which design yields more information, and what is the general principle?
Show answer

(a) Design A: \text{DEFF} = 1 + (20-1)\times 0.05 = 1 + 0.95 = 1.95, so n_{\text{eff}} = 400 / 1.95 \approx 205. Design B: \text{DEFF} = 1 + (5-1)\times 0.05 = 1 + 0.20 = 1.20, so n_{\text{eff}} = 400 / 1.20 \approx 333.

(b) Design B yields far more effective information (\approx 333 vs \approx 205 effective observations) from the same number of records. The general principle: because \text{DEFF} = 1 + (m-1)\,\text{ICC} grows with the cluster size m, spreading a fixed budget across more clusters with fewer members each shrinks the design effect. To buy precision, recruit more vårdcentraler, not more patients per vårdcentral.

Exercise 3

An analyst studying a fallpreventionsprogram (fall-prevention program) for elderly patients runs ordinary OLS on 30 clinics \times 40 patients = 1{,}200 records, ignoring clustering. The true ICC of the fall-risk outcome is 0.03. They report a coefficient with a t-statistic of 3.1 and declare it significant at the 5\% level.

  1. Compute the design effect. (b) By what factor is their reported standard error too small, and what is the honest t-statistic? (c) At the conventional |t| > 1.96 threshold, does the conclusion survive?
Show answer

(a) \text{DEFF} = 1 + (40-1)\times 0.03 = 1 + 39 \times 0.03 = 1 + 1.17 = 2.17.

(b) The naive SE is too small by \sqrt{\text{DEFF}} = \sqrt{2.17} \approx 1.47. The honest t-statistic is the naive one divided by that factor: 3.1 / 1.47 \approx 2.11.

(c) Barely. The honest t \approx 2.11 still exceeds 1.96, so the effect survives — but the comfortable-looking t = 3.1 was an illusion produced by treating correlated patients as independent. Even a small ICC of 0.03 nearly halved the apparent margin of significance, because the cluster size m = 40 amplified it. With a slightly larger ICC or cluster size, the same naive analysis would have crossed from “clearly significant” to “not significant.”

Exercise 4

Two empty random-intercept models are fit to different outcomes measured on the same patients nested in vårdcentraler. Outcome A (HbA1c) yields \text{ICC} = 0.18; outcome B (patient-reported satisfaction) yields \text{ICC} = 0.02. Both have m = 30.

  1. For each outcome, state where the substantive variation lives and what kind of predictor would explain the most variance. (b) For which outcome is a multilevel model most clearly warranted, and why? (c) Compute both design effects to support your answer.
Show answer

(a) Outcome A (\text{ICC} = 0.18): a meaningful 18\% of the variation is between clinics, so substantial action lives at the clinic level — clinic-level predictors (staffing ratios, recall-system quality, protocol adherence, läkare per patient) would explain the most variance. Outcome B (\text{ICC} = 0.02): almost all variation (98\%) is within clinics, so the action is at the patient level — patient-level predictors (age, comorbidity, expectations) matter most; clinics are nearly interchangeable for satisfaction.

(b) Outcome A. A nontrivial ICC means both that the naive SEs are seriously dishonest and that there is real structured between-clinic variation worth modelling with random intercepts. For outcome B the ICC is small enough that a single-level model (with cluster-robust SEs as a precaution) is largely adequate.

(c) Outcome A: \text{DEFF} = 1 + 29 \times 0.18 = 1 + 5.22 = 6.22 — a severe loss of effective information. Outcome B: \text{DEFF} = 1 + 29 \times 0.02 = 1 + 0.58 = 1.58 — modest. The order of magnitude difference in design effects confirms that the multilevel machinery earns its keep for HbA1c far more than for satisfaction.

Key takeaways

  • The empty random-intercept model splits total variance into a between-group component \tau_0^2 and a within-group component \sigma^2, with \operatorname{Var}(y_{ij}) = \tau_0^2 + \sigma^2.
  • The ICC, \tau_0^2 / (\tau_0^2 + \sigma^2), is both the between-group variance share and the correlation between two observations in the same group — one number with two readings.
  • The design effect 1 + (m-1)\,\text{ICC} converts the ICC into lost information: effective sample size is n / \text{DEFF} and the honest standard error is \sqrt{\text{DEFF}} times the naive one.
  • Ignoring clustering deflates standard errors by \sqrt{\text{DEFF}}, manufacturing false significance — the same disease as Module 5’s serial correlation, with the cluster taking the place of the unit-over-time.
  • Multilevel modelling earns its keep when the ICC is non-negligible or clusters are large; always judge 1 + (m-1)\,\text{ICC}, not the ICC alone.

What Comes Next

We have treated the clinic effect u_{0i} as a single random intercept — a fixed bump shared by every patient at a center, partitioning a single snapshot of variance into between and within. But health outcomes are rarely a single snapshot: we follow the same patient’s HbA1c across many visits, and what we really want is the trajectory — how fast control improves, and whether some patients improve faster than others. The next page, Growth Curve Models, extends the variance decomposition into time: each patient gets a random intercept and a random slope on time, the variance components now describe the spread of starting points and the spread of improvement rates, and the ICC generalizes into a covariance structure over repeated measurements. The two-level split you mastered here becomes the engine of longitudinal causal analysis.
