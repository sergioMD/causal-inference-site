Random Intercepts and Random Slopes

Letting relationships vary across groups

In this section, you will learn to

  • Write the varying-intercept model y_{ij} = (\beta_0 + b_{0j}) + \beta_1 x_{ij} + \varepsilon_{ij} and read its fixed part (the population-average line) against its random part (the group deviations)
  • Extend it to a varying-slope model in which both the intercept and the slope of the staffing–outcome relationship vary across regioner, governed by a 2\times2 random-effects covariance matrix
  • Explain why group-specific lines are partially pooled toward the population line, and how the amount of pooling depends on the variance components and the group’s sample size
  • Decide when a random slope is needed — when the relationship itself, not just the level, differs across groups
  • Fit the model in R with lmer(y ~ x + (x | group)) and in Python with statsmodels MixedLM, and interpret the variance–covariance estimates it returns

One Slope for Everyone, or One per Region?

Sweden’s 21 regioner (regions) each run a network of vårdcentraler (primary care centers), and the staffing of those centers varies enormously — a remote vårdcentral in Norrbotten may have one distriktssköterska (district nurse) per 1,400 listed patients, while an urban center in Stockholm has one per 700. A natural policy question is whether richer nurse staffing translates into better diabetes control, measured as the share of a center’s Type 2 diabetes patients whose HbA1c sits above the control threshold (lower is better).

The crude approach pools every vårdcentral in the country into one scatterplot and fits a single line: one intercept, one slope. This complete-pooling model assumes the staffing–outcome relationship is identical in Norrbotten and Stockholm. The opposite extreme fits a separate regression inside each region — no pooling — and treats the 21 slopes as 21 unrelated numbers, even though a region with only a handful of centers gives a wildly unstable estimate.

The previous page, Partial Pooling and Shrinkage, showed that the honest middle ground is partial pooling: borrow strength across regions, pulling each region’s estimate toward the national average by an amount that reflects how noisy that region’s own data are. This page builds the engine that does the partial pooling for both the level of the relationship (the intercept) and its steepness (the slope). That engine is the linear mixed model with random intercepts and random slopes.

A mixed model splits every group’s regression line into two pieces: a fixed part shared by all groups (the population-average line) and a random part that lets each group deviate from it. The deviations are not free parameters — they are draws from a distribution whose variance the model estimates. That single act of treating the deviations as draws is what produces partial pooling automatically.

The Varying-Intercept Model

Index vårdcentral i within region j. Let y_{ij} be the center’s poor-control share (in percentage points) and x_{ij} its nurse staffing (nurses per 1,000 listed patients, centered at the national mean so x=0 is an average-staffed center). The varying-intercept model is

y_{ij} = (\beta_0 + b_{0j}) + \beta_1 x_{ij} + \varepsilon_{ij}, \qquad b_{0j} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\,\tau_0^2), \quad \varepsilon_{ij} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\,\sigma^2).

Read the mean structure aloud: “Region j’s line has the same slope \beta_1 as every other region, but its intercept is the population intercept \beta_0 shifted up or down by a region-specific bump b_{0j}.” The bumps b_{0j} are not estimated one by one as free parameters; they are assumed to be draws from a single normal distribution centered at zero with variance \tau_0^2. A region with a strong baseline care system draws a negative b_{0j} (lower poor-control share at average staffing); a struggling region draws a positive one.

This produces a fan of parallel lines — all sharing slope \beta_1, vertically offset by their b_{0j}. The quantity \tau_0 controls how widely the fan spreads: \tau_0 = 0 collapses every region onto the single population line (complete pooling), while a large \tau_0 lets the regions sit far apart.

The model has two kinds of parameters, and keeping them straight is the whole game:

  • Fixed effects (\beta_0, \beta_1) — the population-average intercept and slope. These describe the average vårdcentral in the average region and are the quantities a national policymaker reports.
  • Variance components (\tau_0^2, \sigma^2) — the spread of the between-region intercept deviations and the spread of the within-region residuals. These describe how much regions differ and how noisy individual centers are.

The region-specific bumps b_{0j} are predicted, not estimated as fixed parameters — they are the best linear unbiased predictors (BLUPs), and they are what get partially pooled.

Letting the Slope Vary Too

Parallel lines assume the return to staffing is the same everywhere: one extra nurse per 1,000 patients buys the same improvement in Norrbotten as in Stockholm. That is often false. In a sparsely populated region, an extra nurse may cover enormous distances and move the needle little; in a dense urban region the same nurse may run many more diabetes follow-ups. When the relationship itself varies across groups — not merely its level — we need a random slope.

y_{ij} = (\beta_0 + b_{0j}) + (\beta_1 + b_{1j})\,x_{ij} + \varepsilon_{ij}.

Read this aloud: “Region j’s line has both its own intercept (\beta_0 + b_{0j}) and its own slope (\beta_1 + b_{1j}).” Now the region deviations come in pairs — a level shift b_{0j} and a slope shift b_{1j} — and these two are generally correlated. A region with an unusually high intercept (poor baseline control) might also have an unusually steep slope (staffing helps it more), or the reverse. We capture this with a bivariate normal distribution and a 2\times2 covariance matrix:

\begin{pmatrix} b_{0j} \\ b_{1j} \end{pmatrix} \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left( \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix},\; \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_b \right), \qquad \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_b = \begin{pmatrix} \tau_0^2 & \rho\,\tau_0\tau_1 \\[2pt] \rho\,\tau_0\tau_1 & \tau_1^2 \end{pmatrix}.

Read the matrix aloud: “The intercept deviations have variance \tau_0^2, the slope deviations have variance \tau_1^2, and the off-diagonal \rho\,\tau_0\tau_1 is their covariance — so \rho is the correlation between a region’s level and its steepness.” When \tau_1 = 0 we are back to parallel lines; as \tau_1 grows, the region lines fan out, crossing one another, and the sign of \rho decides whether high-intercept regions tilt up or down.

The interactive below lets you grow \tau_1 from zero and watch the parallel fan turn into a crossing fan, all while the fixed (population-average) line stays put.

How to Read the Fan Plot

Work through the controls in order; each isolates one idea.

Step 1: See the parallel fan. With the model on “Random intercepts only”, the eight slate region lines are all parallel — they share the population slope \beta_1 = -3.2 and differ only in height. The thick red line is the population-average fit. Each region line sits above or below it by its own draw b_{0j}.

Step 2: Spread the levels. Drag Intercept SD (\tau_0) up and down. At \tau_0 = 0 all eight lines collapse onto the red line (complete pooling). As \tau_0 grows, the parallel lines spread vertically — regions disagree about the level of poor control at average staffing, but still agree on the return to staffing.

Step 3: Turn on random slopes. Switch the model to “Random intercepts + slopes.” The Slope SD (\tau_1) slider activates. Push it up from zero and the lines stop being parallel: they fan out and begin to cross. Now regions disagree about how steeply staffing helps. Notice the readout: the Region slope range widens as \tau_1 grows, while the population slope \beta_1 stays fixed at -3.2.

Step 4: Tilt the fan with \rho. Drag the Intercept–slope correlation (\rho). With \rho < 0, regions with high intercepts (worse baseline control, sitting above the red line near x=0) tend to have more negative slopes (steeper improvement) — the lines pivot so the high-and-struggling regions catch up fastest. Flip \rho > 0 and the pattern reverses: the worst regions also gain least from staffing. The sign of \rho is a substantive claim about whether staffing is equalizing or compounding.

Step 5: Watch what never moves. Through every change, the red population-average line stays exactly where it is (toggle it off and on to confirm). The fixed part answers “what does the average region look like?”; the random part answers “how much do regions differ?” The two are separately estimated and separately interpreted.

Step 6: Resample. Click “Draw new regions” to draw a fresh set of eight (b_{0j}, b_{1j}) pairs from the same covariance matrix. The individual lines jump, but their spread is governed by \tau_0, \tau_1, \rho — the quantities the mixed model actually estimates.

Partial Pooling: Where the Group Lines Come From

In the plot above we drew the region deviations from a known covariance matrix. In a real analysis we do the reverse: we observe noisy data and must infer each region’s line. This is where partial pooling does its work. A region’s predicted line is not its own least-squares fit, and it is not the population line — it is a precision-weighted compromise between the two.

Partial pooling in one sentence. Each region’s predicted deviation is its own data’s estimate, shrunk toward zero (the population line) by a factor that grows with the noise in that region and shrinks with the spread \tau between regions.

For the intercept-only model, the predicted region effect is

\hat{b}_{0j} \;=\; \underbrace{\frac{\tau_0^2}{\tau_0^2 + \sigma^2 / n_j}}_{\text{shrinkage weight } \lambda_j} \;\times\; \big(\bar{y}_j - \hat{\beta}_0 - \hat{\beta}_1 \bar{x}_j\big).

Read this aloud: “Take region j’s raw average residual; keep only the fraction \lambda_j of it, and discard the rest by pulling that region toward the population line.” A region with many centers (n_j large) or large between-region spread (\tau_0^2 large) has \lambda_j \to 1 and is barely shrunk; a region with few, noisy centers has \lambda_j \to 0 and is pulled almost all the way to the national average.

This is exactly the shrinkage you met on the previous page, now applied not to a single mean per group but to a whole line per group. With random slopes, the same logic acts on the pair (b_{0j}, b_{1j}) jointly through the matrix \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_b: regions with little data have both their intercept and their slope dragged toward the population values, so a region with two centers cannot post an absurd slope just because its two points happen to line up steeply. The mixed model regularizes the per-group lines for free.

What the random-effects assumptions buy you. Partial pooling is valid only if the group deviations behave like genuine draws from the assumed distribution. The mixed model assumes (i) b_{0j}, b_{1j} are normal with the covariance \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_b, (ii) the residuals \varepsilon_{ij} are normal with constant variance \sigma^2, and crucially (iii) the random effects are uncorrelated with the regressor x_{ij}. Assumption (iii) is the same exogeneity condition that separates random effects from fixed effects: if better-staffed regions also draw systematically better intercepts, the fixed-effect estimate \hat\beta_1 is biased, and you should compare against a fixed-effects specification before trusting the mixed model.

When Do You Need a Random Slope?

Adding a random slope is not free: it spends parameters (\tau_1, \rho) and small samples often cannot estimate them, producing a singular fit in which the model collapses \tau_1 to zero. Include a random slope when:

  • Theory says the relationship varies. If you believe the return to staffing genuinely differs across regions — because geography, patient mix, or organization differs — a random slope encodes that belief and, just as importantly, propagates the extra uncertainty into the standard error of \hat\beta_1. Omitting a needed random slope makes the fixed-effect standard errors too small and inferences over-confident.
  • The grouping variable is also the cluster of treatment. If staffing is assigned at the region level, a random slope on staffing is the multilevel analogue of cluster-robust inference: it prevents you from treating the centers in one region as independent replicates of the staffing effect.
  • The data can support it. You need enough groups (a common rule of thumb is at least 5–8, ideally many more) and enough units per group for \tau_1 to be estimable.

Conversely, drop the random slope when the data are too thin, when the variance estimate is essentially zero, or when theory says the slope is structurally common across groups. The interactive’s \tau_1 = 0 position is exactly the parallel-lines model you fall back to.

Math Details: The random-effects covariance matrix and the partially-pooled BLUPs

Stacking the model in matrix form

Collect the n_j observations in region j into a vector \mathbf{y}_j. The varying-slope model for that region is

\mathbf{y}_j = \mathbf{X}_j \boldsymbol\beta + \mathbf{Z}_j \mathbf{b}_j + \boldsymbol\varepsilon_j, \qquad \mathbf{b}_j \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\,\boldsymbol\Sigma_b), \quad \boldsymbol\varepsilon_j \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\,\sigma^2 \mathbf{I}).

Here \mathbf{X}_j and \mathbf{Z}_j are the fixed- and random-effect design matrices. For a model with a random intercept and a random slope on x, each row of \mathbf{Z}_j is (1,\;x_{ij}), and \mathbf{b}_j = (b_{0j}, b_{1j})' with

\boldsymbol\Sigma_b = \begin{pmatrix} \tau_0^2 & \rho\,\tau_0\tau_1 \\ \rho\,\tau_0\tau_1 & \tau_1^2 \end{pmatrix}.

Why the marginal model has correlated errors

Integrating out \mathbf{b}_j gives the marginal distribution of the data in region j:

\mathbf{y}_j \sim \mathcal{N}\!\big(\mathbf{X}_j\boldsymbol\beta,\; \mathbf{V}_j\big), \qquad \mathbf{V}_j = \mathbf{Z}_j \boldsymbol\Sigma_b \mathbf{Z}_j' + \sigma^2 \mathbf{I}.

Read \mathbf{V}_j aloud: “Within a region, two centers are correlated because they share the same random intercept and slope; the matrix \mathbf{Z}_j\boldsymbol\Sigma_b\mathbf{Z}_j' is exactly that shared structure, and \sigma^2\mathbf{I} is the leftover idiosyncratic noise.” This is why a mixed model is not the same as OLS with extra dummies: the off-diagonal correlations are modeled, not ignored.

Generating correlated draws (what the interactive does)

To simulate \mathbf{b}_j we use the Cholesky factor \mathbf{L} with \mathbf{L}\mathbf{L}' = \boldsymbol\Sigma_b:

\mathbf{L} = \begin{pmatrix} \tau_0 & 0 \\ \rho\,\tau_1 & \tau_1\sqrt{1-\rho^2} \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \begin{pmatrix} b_{0j} \\ b_{1j} \end{pmatrix} = \mathbf{L} \begin{pmatrix} z_{0j} \\ z_{1j} \end{pmatrix}, \quad z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1).

You can verify \operatorname{Var}(b_{0j}) = \tau_0^2, \operatorname{Var}(b_{1j}) = \rho^2\tau_1^2 + (1-\rho^2)\tau_1^2 = \tau_1^2, and \operatorname{Cov}(b_{0j},b_{1j}) = \rho\,\tau_0\tau_1 — exactly \boldsymbol\Sigma_b. This is the construction used in the fan plot.

The BLUP is a shrinkage estimator

Given \boldsymbol\beta and the variance components, the best linear unbiased predictor of the random effects is

\hat{\mathbf{b}}_j = \boldsymbol\Sigma_b \mathbf{Z}_j' \mathbf{V}_j^{-1} (\mathbf{y}_j - \mathbf{X}_j\boldsymbol\beta).

For the intercept-only case (\mathbf{Z}_j = \mathbf{1}, \boldsymbol\Sigma_b = \tau_0^2), this collapses to the scalar shrinkage formula in the text:

\hat{b}_{0j} = \frac{\tau_0^2}{\tau_0^2 + \sigma^2/n_j}\,\bar{r}_j,

where \bar{r}_j is region j’s mean residual. The factor \lambda_j = \tau_0^2 / (\tau_0^2 + \sigma^2/n_j) is the reliability of region j’s data: it is the fraction of region j’s apparent deviation that the model believes is signal rather than noise. As n_j \to \infty, \lambda_j \to 1 (no pooling); as n_j \to 0 or \tau_0 \to 0, \lambda_j \to 0 (complete pooling toward the population line).

Estimation

In practice \boldsymbol\beta and the variance components are estimated jointly by maximum likelihood or, to remove the downward bias in the variance estimates, restricted maximum likelihood (REML) — the default in lmer. The BLUPs are then computed by plugging the estimated variance components into the formula above (technically EBLUPs, “empirical” BLUPs).

Fitting the Mixed Model

Both implementations simulate centers nested in regions, then fit the varying-intercept-and-slope model and read off the fixed effects and the random-effects covariance.

  • R
  • Python
library(lme4)
library(tidyverse)

# --- Simulate 21 regioner, ~12 vårdcentraler each ---
# x = nurse staffing (centered);  y = poor-control share (%, lower is better).
# True fixed part: beta0 = 22, beta1 = -3.2.
# Region random effects: tau0 = 3 (intercepts), tau1 = 1.5 (slopes), rho = -0.4.
set.seed(2025)
J  <- 21
nj <- 12

Sigma <- matrix(c(3^2,            -0.4 * 3 * 1.5,
                  -0.4 * 3 * 1.5,  1.5^2), nrow = 2)
b <- MASS::mvrnorm(J, mu = c(0, 0), Sigma = Sigma)   # one (b0, b1) per region

panel <- map_dfr(1:J, function(j) {
  x <- rnorm(nj, mean = 0, sd = 1.2)                 # centered staffing
  y <- (22 + b[j, 1]) + (-3.2 + b[j, 2]) * x + rnorm(nj, 0, 2.5)
  tibble(region = factor(j), x = x, y = y)
})

# --- Varying intercept AND slope: (x | region) ---
m_rs <- lmer(y ~ x + (x | region), data = panel)
summary(m_rs)

# Fixed effects (population-average intercept and slope):
fixef(m_rs)

# Random-effects covariance: tau0^2, tau1^2 and the intercept-slope correlation:
VarCorr(m_rs)

# Partially-pooled region lines (BLUPs added to the fixed part):
coef(m_rs)$region |> head()

# Compare with the random-INTERCEPT-only model (parallel lines):
m_ri <- lmer(y ~ x + (1 | region), data = panel)
anova(m_ri, m_rs)   # LRT: does letting the slope vary improve fit?
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

# --- Simulate 21 regioner, ~12 vårdcentraler each ---
rng = np.random.default_rng(2025)
J, nj = 21, 12
tau0, tau1, rho = 3.0, 1.5, -0.4
Sigma = np.array([[tau0**2,        rho * tau0 * tau1],
                  [rho * tau0 * tau1, tau1**2]])
b = rng.multivariate_normal([0, 0], Sigma, size=J)   # one (b0, b1) per region

rows = []
for j in range(J):
    x = rng.normal(0, 1.2, nj)                        # centered staffing
    y = (22 + b[j, 0]) + (-3.2 + b[j, 1]) * x + rng.normal(0, 2.5, nj)
    rows.append(pd.DataFrame({"region": j, "x": x, "y": y}))
panel = pd.concat(rows, ignore_index=True)

# --- Varying intercept AND slope ---
# re_formula="~x" adds a random slope on x; the model then estimates the full
# 2x2 random-effects covariance (intercept var, slope var, their covariance).
m_rs = smf.mixedlm("y ~ x", data=panel, groups="region",
                   re_formula="~x").fit(reml=True)
print(m_rs.summary())

# Fixed effects (population-average intercept and slope):
print(m_rs.fe_params)

# Random-effects covariance matrix (tau0^2, cov, tau1^2):
print(m_rs.cov_re)

# Random-intercept-only model for comparison (parallel lines):
m_ri = smf.mixedlm("y ~ x", data=panel, groups="region").fit(reml=True)
print(m_ri.cov_re)

In both languages the fitted fixed effects recover the population-average line (intercept near 22, slope near -3.2), while the random-effects covariance recovers the spread of region intercepts (\tau_0 \approx 3), the spread of region slopes (\tau_1 \approx 1.5), and their negative correlation (\rho \approx -0.4). The coef() / per-region predictions return the partially pooled region lines — never the raw within-region OLS fits.

Exercises

Exercise 1

A Folkhälsomyndigheten (Public Health Agency) analyst fits a varying-intercept model of vårdcentral poor-control share on centered nurse staffing and reports \hat\beta_0 = 22.0, \hat\beta_1 = -3.2, \hat\tau_0 = 3.0. Region 7 (a large region with many centers) has predicted intercept deviation \hat b_{0,7} = +4.5; Region 18 (a tiny region) has predicted deviation \hat b_{0,18} = -1.0.

  1. Write Region 7’s predicted regression line. (b) At average staffing (x=0), what poor-control share does the model predict for Region 7 versus Region 18? (c) The raw (no-pooling) intercept for Region 18 was 19.0 (i.e. a raw deviation of -3.0). Explain why the model reports only -1.0.
Show answer

(a) In a varying-intercept model every region shares the population slope, so Region 7’s line is \hat y = (\hat\beta_0 + \hat b_{0,7}) + \hat\beta_1 x = (22.0 + 4.5) - 3.2\,x = 26.5 - 3.2\,x.

(b) At x = 0 the slope term vanishes, so the prediction is just the intercept. Region 7: 26.5\%. Region 18: 22.0 + (-1.0) = 21.0\%. Region 7 has a substantially worse baseline.

(c) Region 18 is small, so n_{18} is small and the shrinkage weight \lambda_{18} = \tau_0^2/(\tau_0^2 + \sigma^2/n_{18}) is well below 1. The model trusts its few noisy centers only partially and pulls the raw deviation of -3.0 toward the population line (zero). Here the reported -1.0 implies a shrinkage weight of about \lambda_{18} \approx 1.0/3.0 \approx 0.33 — only a third of the raw signal survives. A large region like Region 7 has \lambda_7 \approx 1 and is barely shrunk, which is why its sizable +4.5 deviation is reported nearly intact.

Exercise 2

A colleague estimates a varying-slope model and reports \hat\tau_0 = 4.0 (intercept SD), \hat\tau_1 = 2.0 (slope SD), and \hat\rho = -0.6 (intercept–slope correlation).

  1. Write the estimated 2\times2 random-effects covariance matrix \hat{\boldsymbol\Sigma}_b. (b) Interpret the sign of \rho in plain language about regions. (c) A region with b_{0j} = +6 (high baseline poor control) — what slope deviation would you expect for it, and would that be equalizing or compounding?
Show answer

(a) The covariance is \rho\,\tau_0\tau_1 = (-0.6)(4.0)(2.0) = -4.8, so \hat{\boldsymbol\Sigma}_b = \begin{pmatrix} \tau_0^2 & \rho\tau_0\tau_1 \\ \rho\tau_0\tau_1 & \tau_1^2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 16.0 & -4.8 \\ -4.8 & 4.0 \end{pmatrix}.

(b) \rho = -0.6 < 0 means regions with higher intercepts (worse baseline poor-control share) tend to have more negative slopes (a steeper improvement in poor control per extra nurse). The struggling regions get more out of staffing.

(c) The conditional expectation of the slope deviation given the intercept deviation is E[b_{1j}\mid b_{0j}] = \frac{\operatorname{Cov}(b_0,b_1)}{\operatorname{Var}(b_0)}\,b_{0j} = \frac{-4.8}{16.0}\times 6 = -1.8. So a region 6 points above average at baseline is expected to have a slope about 1.8 more negative than the population slope — i.e. staffing helps it more. Because the worst-off regions improve fastest, this pattern is equalizing: it narrows the gap between regions as staffing rises.

Exercise 3

Using the shrinkage formula \lambda_j = \tau_0^2 / (\tau_0^2 + \sigma^2/n_j) with \tau_0 = 3 (so \tau_0^2 = 9) and residual variance \sigma^2 = 25:

  1. Compute \lambda_j for a region with n_j = 4 centers and for one with n_j = 40. (b) A region’s raw mean residual is \bar r_j = +6.0. Give the partially-pooled intercept deviation \hat b_{0j} in each case. (c) What happens to \lambda_j as \tau_0 \to 0, and why does that make sense?
Show answer

(a) For n_j = 4: \sigma^2/n_j = 25/4 = 6.25, so \lambda_j = 9/(9 + 6.25) = 9/15.25 \approx 0.590. For n_j = 40: \sigma^2/n_j = 25/40 = 0.625, so \lambda_j = 9/(9 + 0.625) = 9/9.625 \approx 0.935.

(b) \hat b_{0j} = \lambda_j \bar r_j. Small region: 0.590 \times 6.0 \approx 3.54. Large region: 0.935 \times 6.0 \approx 5.61. The small, noisy region is pulled much harder toward the population line; the large region keeps most of its raw signal.

(c) As \tau_0 \to 0, the numerator \tau_0^2 \to 0 while the denominator stays positive, so \lambda_j \to 0 and every \hat b_{0j} \to 0. This is complete pooling: if there is no genuine between-region spread, the model concludes that all apparent region differences are noise and collapses every region onto the single population line. That is the correct behavior — with \tau_0 = 0 there is nothing to distinguish the regions.

Exercise 4

An analyst fits lmer(y ~ x + (1 | region)) (random intercept only) and finds the staffing coefficient \hat\beta_1 = -3.2 with standard error 0.4. A reviewer insists the staffing effect plausibly varies across regions and asks for (x | region) instead.

  1. Mechanically, what extra parameters does the reviewer’s model estimate? (b) Why might the standard error on \hat\beta_1 increase under the random-slope model even though the point estimate barely moves? (c) Under what circumstance would lmer warn of a singular fit, and what would you conclude?
Show answer

(a) The random-slope model (x | region) adds two parameters to the variance structure: the slope variance \tau_1^2 and the intercept–slope covariance \rho\,\tau_0\tau_1 (equivalently \tau_1 and \rho). The fixed effects (\beta_0,\beta_1) and the existing \tau_0, \sigma^2 remain.

(b) The random-intercept-only model treats every center as an independent observation on the staffing slope, so it acts as if it has far more independent information about \beta_1 than it really does — understating the standard error. Allowing the slope to vary acknowledges that the staffing effect is itself uncertain across regions, and that extra between-region variability is propagated into the standard error of the average slope. The point estimate is roughly unbiased either way; the honest model just admits more uncertainty, so the SE rises (often substantially when \tau_1 is non-trivial). This is the multilevel analogue of why cluster-robust SEs are larger than naive ones.

(c) A singular fit occurs when the estimated random-effects covariance is on the boundary — typically \hat\tau_1 \approx 0 or |\hat\rho| \approx 1 — meaning the data cannot support a separate slope variance (too few regions or too few centers per region). The right conclusion is not that slopes are truly identical, but that this dataset cannot estimate their spread; you would simplify to the random-intercept model (or a model with uncorrelated random effects) and note the limitation, rather than report unstable boundary estimates.

Key takeaways

  • A mixed model splits each group’s line into a fixed part (the population-average intercept and slope \beta_0, \beta_1) and a random part (group deviations b_{0j}, b_{1j} drawn from a distribution) — describing the average relationship and how groups differ as two separate questions.
  • The varying-intercept model gives a fan of parallel lines (spread set by \tau_0); the varying-slope model lets the lines fan out and cross, governed by the 2\times2 covariance matrix \boldsymbol\Sigma_b with entries \tau_0^2, \tau_1^2, and \rho\,\tau_0\tau_1.
  • Group lines are partially pooled toward the population line by a shrinkage weight \lambda_j = \tau_0^2/(\tau_0^2 + \sigma^2/n_j): data-rich groups keep their own signal, data-poor groups are dragged toward the average — regularization for free.
  • Add a random slope when the relationship itself varies across groups or when the grouping is the cluster of treatment; omitting a needed one makes the fixed-effect standard error too small. Drop it when the data are too thin and the fit goes singular.
  • Fit with lmer(y ~ x + (x | group)) in R or MixedLM(..., re_formula="~x") in Python; read the fixed effects for the population line and the random-effects covariance for the between-group spread.

What Comes Next

We have built the mixed model end to end: the fixed part is the population-average line, the random part lets each region’s intercept and slope deviate, and the 2\times2 covariance matrix \boldsymbol\Sigma_b controls how widely the group lines fan out and how their levels and slopes co-move. We saw that the group lines are partially pooled — precision-weighted compromises between each region’s own fit and the national average.

But we glossed over a deeper question: how much of the total variation in poor-control share lives between regions versus within them? That decomposition — \sigma^2 against \tau_0^2 — is the subject of the next page, Variance Components and the ICC, which turns the variance components we just estimated into the intraclass correlation coefficient, a single number summarizing how much a vårdcentral’s outcome is determined by which region it sits in. The ICC is the bridge from “letting relationships vary” to “quantifying how much they vary.”
