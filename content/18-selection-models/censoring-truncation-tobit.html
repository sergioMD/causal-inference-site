Censoring, Truncation, and the Tobit Model

When the outcome is observed only partly

In this section, you will learn to

  • Distinguish censoring (the outcome is recorded but pinned at a limit) from truncation (observations past the limit are absent from the sample)
  • Explain why ordinary least squares on a censored or truncated outcome is biased toward zero (attenuated), and predict the direction of the bias
  • Write the latent-variable model y^* = \mathbf{x}'\boldsymbol\beta + \varepsilon with y = \max(0, y^*) and read the Tobit likelihood as a mixture of a density (uncensored) and a CDF mass (censored)
  • Separate the marginal effect on the latent y^* from the marginal effect on the observed y, and report the right one for a given question
  • Fit a Tobit by maximum likelihood and a truncated regression in both R (AER::tobit, censReg, truncreg) and Python (statsmodels, manual MLE)

The Question Behind a Pile-Up at Zero

A vårdcentral (primary care center) in Region Östergötland is studying patients’ annual out-of-pocket spending on prescriptions, measured in SEK. Sweden runs a högkostnadsskydd (high-cost ceiling), but well below that ceiling lies a more mundane fact: many patients in a given year spend exactly zero kronor — they collected no prescriptions at all. The spending variable does not trail off smoothly toward small positive numbers; it piles up at the boundary 0.

A health economist regresses out-of-pocket spending on a frailty index built from age, number of chronic diagnoses, and recent läkarbesök (physician visits). The expectation is clear: frailer patients should spend more. Yet the ordinary least squares (OLS) slope comes back suspiciously flat — much flatter than the clinicians’ lived experience suggests. Nothing is wrong with the arithmetic. The problem is the boundary: a large clump of patients sits at exactly 0, and OLS, which assumes the outcome can range freely above and below the line, is fooled by that clump.

This page is about outcomes we observe only partly. There are two distinct ways partial observation happens, and they call for two different fixes.

Two forms of partial observation of an outcome y at a limit L (here L = 0):

  • Censoring. Every unit stays in the sample, but any latent value below the limit is recorded as the limit itself. We see y = \max(L,\, y^*). The pile-up at L is real data: those patients exist, we just cannot see how far below the limit they would have gone. (Top-coded income — “≥ 1{,}000{,}000 SEK” — is the mirror image: censoring from above.)

  • Truncation. Units whose latent value falls beyond the limit are missing from the sample entirely. We never even know they existed. We observe y only when y^* > L, and the count of dropped units is unknown.

Censoring throws away the value; truncation throws away the whole observation.

Both distortions bias OLS, and — this is the key intuition — they bias it in the same direction: toward zero. The slope is attenuated. The rest of the page builds the model that repairs it: the Tobit model (Tobin, 1958), estimated by maximum likelihood, which you met in the Maximum Likelihood module.

The Latent-Variable Model

The cleanest way to think about censoring is to imagine a complete, well-behaved outcome that we only get to see through a clouded window. Call the complete outcome the latent variable y^*.

The Tobit model (Type I, left-censored at zero) posits a latent linear model and an observation rule:

y_i^* = \mathbf{x}_i'\boldsymbol\beta + \varepsilon_i, \qquad \varepsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2),

y_i = \max(0,\; y_i^*) = \begin{cases} y_i^* & \text{if } y_i^* > 0 \quad (\text{uncensored}),\\[2pt] 0 & \text{if } y_i^* \le 0 \quad (\text{censored}). \end{cases}

Read the first line as: “the latent out-of-pocket spending of patient i equals a linear index of their characteristics \mathbf{x}_i'\boldsymbol\beta plus a normal error.” Read the second as: “we observe the latent value when it is positive; otherwise we observe exactly zero.” A general censoring point L replaces 0 with L throughout; top-coding flips the \max to a \min.

The latent y_i^* can be negative — mathematically, it represents how strongly below the threshold a patient sits (a patient who would “spend -300 SEK” is one with no demand for prescriptions at all). We never see those negatives; the observation rule flattens them all to 0. That flattening is exactly what bends the OLS line.

Why OLS attenuates. OLS fits a line through the observed y. For the censored units it sees 0 instead of the (more negative) latent value, so the cloud of points on the low end is lifted up toward the boundary. A line forced through a cloud whose bottom has been pushed up must rotate flatter — its slope shrinks toward zero. The more censoring, the flatter the line. Tobit instead models the latent line and treats each zero as the statement “y^* \le 0,” recovering the true slope.

See It: Censoring Bends the OLS Line

The laboratory below makes the attenuation visible. A true latent relationship — frailty index on the x-axis, latent out-of-pocket SEK spending on the y-axis — is drawn once. You then slide a censoring threshold upward: every point below it is clamped to the threshold (the pile-up you would see in real data). Three lines compete: the true latent line (ochre, the data-generating truth), the naive OLS line fit to the censored data (slate, what a careless analyst reports), and the Tobit line (red, the MLE that undoes the damage). Watch the slate line flatten as the threshold climbs, while the red Tobit line stays locked onto the ochre truth.

Censoring attenuates OLS; Tobit recovers the latent slope

// True data-generating process for latent out-of-pocket spending (SEK):
//   y* = beta0 + beta1 * frailty + N(0, sigma^2)
// These are the TRUTH the Tobit must recover. Frailty index runs 0..10.
seltob_truth = ({ beta0: -900, beta1: 360 })
// Generate n patients. seltob_draw is read so the button forces a fresh sample;
// d3.randomNormal supplies the noise. Each patient has a latent y* and an
// OBSERVED y that is clamped up to the current threshold (left-censoring).
seltob_data = {
  seltob_draw;                                  // dependency: reshuffle on click
  const n = 120;
  const rnd = d3.randomNormal(0, seltob_noise);
  const rx = d3.randomUniform(0, 10);
  return Array.from({ length: n }, () => {
    const frailty = rx();
    const ystar = seltob_truth.beta0 + seltob_truth.beta1 * frailty + rnd();
    const yobs  = Math.max(seltob_threshold, ystar);
    return { frailty, ystar, yobs, censored: ystar <= seltob_threshold };
  })
}
// Ordinary least squares slope+intercept for an array of {x,y} pairs.
seltob_ols = function (rows, xkey, ykey) {
  const n = rows.length;
  const mx = d3.mean(rows, d => d[xkey]);
  const my = d3.mean(rows, d => d[ykey]);
  let sxx = 0, sxy = 0;
  for (const r of rows) {
    sxx += (r[xkey] - mx) ** 2;
    sxy += (r[xkey] - mx) * (r[ykey] - my);
  }
  const b1 = sxy / sxx;
  const b0 = my - b1 * mx;
  return { b0, b1 };
}
// Naive OLS fit to the CENSORED (observed) outcome — the biased line.
seltob_olsFit = seltob_ols(seltob_data, "frailty", "yobs")
// A practical Tobit estimator via the EM / iterated-expectation idea, kept light
// for the browser: we recover the latent slope by imputing each censored point's
// conditional expectation E[y* | y* <= L] under the current fit and re-running
// OLS to convergence. This is a faithful, didactic stand-in for full Tobit MLE
// and lands on the latent truth as censoring grows. (Production code uses
// AER::tobit / censReg / statsmodels — see the code tabs.)
seltob_tobitFit = {
  const L = seltob_threshold;
  const rows = seltob_data;
  // Standard normal pdf and cdf.
  const phi = z => Math.exp(-0.5 * z * z) / Math.sqrt(2 * Math.PI);
  const Phi = z => 0.5 * (1 + seltob_erf(z / Math.SQRT2));
  // Initialise from OLS on the observed data, plus a residual-based sigma.
  let { b0, b1 } = seltob_ols(rows, "frailty", "yobs");
  let sigma = Math.max(50, d3.deviation(rows, d => d.yobs) || seltob_noise);
  for (let iter = 0; iter < 60; iter++) {
    const imputed = rows.map(r => {
      const mu = b0 + b1 * r.frailty;            // current latent mean
      if (!r.censored) return { frailty: r.frailty, z: r.yobs };
      // E[y* | y* <= L]  =  mu - sigma * phi(a)/Phi(a),  a = (L - mu)/sigma.
      const a = (L - mu) / sigma;
      const lam = phi(a) / Math.max(Phi(a), 1e-9);   // inverse Mills (lower tail)
      const cond = mu - sigma * lam;
      return { frailty: r.frailty, z: cond };
    });
    const fit = seltob_ols(imputed, "frailty", "z");
    // Update sigma from imputed residuals (kept positive, gently regularised).
    const rss = d3.mean(imputed, d => (d.z - (fit.b0 + fit.b1 * d.frailty)) ** 2);
    const newSigma = Math.max(50, Math.sqrt(rss));
    const moved = Math.abs(fit.b1 - b1) + Math.abs(fit.b0 - b0);
    b0 = fit.b0; b1 = fit.b1; sigma = 0.5 * sigma + 0.5 * newSigma;
    if (moved < 1e-4) break;
  }
  return { b0, b1, sigma };
}
// Abramowitz-Stegun rational approximation to the error function (max abs error
// ~1.5e-7), so the Tobit imputation has a self-contained normal CDF.
seltob_erf = function (x) {
  const s = Math.sign(x);
  const ax = Math.abs(x);
  const t = 1 / (1 + 0.3275911 * ax);
  const y = 1 - (((((1.061405429 * t - 1.453152027) * t) + 1.421413741) * t
            - 0.284496736) * t + 0.254829592) * t * Math.exp(-ax * ax);
  return s * y;
}
// Endpoints for drawing each fitted line across the full frailty range.
seltob_linePts = function (fit) {
  return [0, 10].map(f => ({ frailty: f, y: fit.b0 + fit.b1 * f }));
}

How to Read the Censoring Lab

Work through these in order; each isolates one mechanism.

Step 1: Read the scene with no censoring. Leave the threshold at 0 SEK. (The true line dips below zero for low-frailty patients — that latent region is where censoring will bite.) With L=0, only the genuinely negative latent points get clamped. Note where the three lines lie: the ochre latent truth, the slate OLS, and the red Tobit. Read the four tiles — the slopes are already close.

Step 2: Turn up the censoring. Drag the threshold L upward. Hollow markers march up onto the dashed threshold line — those are patients whose true spending we can no longer see; we only know it was “\le L.” The censored share in the caption climbs.

Step 3: Watch OLS flatten. As L rises, the slate OLS line rotates flatter and its slope tile falls below the ochre truth. The “OLS attenuation” tile shows the percentage of the true slope that OLS has thrown away — it grows with censoring. This is the bias in the opening puzzle.

Step 4: Watch Tobit hold. The red Tobit line stays glued to the ochre truth even as half the sample piles onto the threshold. Tobit does not treat the zeros as real spending of 0; it reads each as “the latent value fell below L” and integrates over that region. Its slope tile stays near the true 360 SEK, and its attenuation reads near -0\% (green).

Step 5: Stress-test with noise and resampling. Raise the error spread \sigma: more noise pushes more points below the threshold, so censoring (and OLS bias) worsens, yet Tobit remains roughly centered on the truth. Click Resample patients a few times to confirm the pattern is structural, not a lucky draw.

The Tobit Likelihood: A Mixture of Density and Mass

Why does Tobit work where OLS fails? Because it writes the correct probability statement for each observation, and maximizes that likelihood (the machinery of the Maximum Likelihood module). The trick is that censored and uncensored observations contribute different kinds of terms.

For an uncensored patient (y_i = y_i^* > 0), we observed the exact value, so they contribute the usual normal density evaluated at the residual:

\frac{1}{\sigma}\,\phi\!\left(\frac{y_i - \mathbf{x}_i'\boldsymbol\beta}{\sigma}\right),

where \phi is the standard normal probability density. Read this as: “how probable is it that the latent value landed exactly here?” — the same term OLS implicitly uses.

For a censored patient (y_i = 0, meaning y_i^* \le 0), we did not observe a value; we observed only the event “y^* fell at or below the limit.” The probability of that whole event is a CDF mass:

\Pr(y_i^* \le 0) = \Phi\!\left(\frac{0 - \mathbf{x}_i'\boldsymbol\beta}{\sigma}\right) = \Phi\!\left(-\frac{\mathbf{x}_i'\boldsymbol\beta}{\sigma}\right),

where \Phi is the standard normal cumulative distribution function. Read this as: “the probability that this patient’s latent spending was at or below the threshold” — a single lump of probability, not a density at a point.

The Tobit log-likelihood sums a density term over the uncensored units and a CDF-mass term over the censored units. With d_i = \mathbf{1}\{y_i > 0\} marking the uncensored,

\ell(\boldsymbol\beta,\sigma) = \sum_{i:\,d_i=1} \log\!\left[\frac{1}{\sigma}\,\phi\!\left(\frac{y_i - \mathbf{x}_i'\boldsymbol\beta}{\sigma}\right)\right] \;+\; \sum_{i:\,d_i=0} \log\!\left[\Phi\!\left(-\frac{\mathbf{x}_i'\boldsymbol\beta}{\sigma}\right)\right].

Read this as: “add up the log normal-density for everyone we measured exactly, plus the log probability-of-being-censored for everyone pinned at the limit.” The maximum-likelihood estimates \hat{\boldsymbol\beta},\hat\sigma are the values that make this mixed surface as high as possible. The first sum is OLS-like; the second sum is the probit-like piece that corrects the attenuation.

This mixture is the heart of the model. OLS keeps only the first sum and pretends the censored zeros are exact measurements of 0 — which is precisely the lie that flattens its line. Tobit’s second sum says, honestly, “I do not know how far below the limit these patients sit; I only know they are below it,” and weighs that statement correctly.

Marginal Effects: Latent versus Observed

A subtlety trips up almost everyone the first time. In OLS, \beta_1 is the marginal effect — end of story. In Tobit there are two marginal effects, and reporting the wrong one is a common error.

For the Tobit model there are two distinct marginal effects of a regressor x_k:

  • Effect on the latent outcome y^*: simply \dfrac{\partial \mathrm{E}[y^* \mid \mathbf{x}]}{\partial x_k} = \beta_k. This is the slope of the underlying line — “how much would latent spending change.”

  • Effect on the observed outcome y: scaled by the probability of being uncensored, \frac{\partial \mathrm{E}[y \mid \mathbf{x}]}{\partial x_k} = \beta_k \cdot \Phi\!\left(\frac{\mathbf{x}'\boldsymbol\beta}{\sigma}\right), which is smaller in magnitude than \beta_k because \Phi(\cdot) \in (0,1). This is “how much would actual recorded spending change.”

Read the second formula aloud: “the marginal effect on observed spending equals the latent coefficient times the probability that a patient with these characteristics is uncensored.” The factor \Phi(\mathbf{x}'\boldsymbol\beta/\sigma) is the share of such patients who are above the limit; for those clamped at the boundary, a small nudge in x does nothing to their recorded 0. So the observed-y effect is always attenuated relative to \beta_k — but, crucially, it is attenuated for a known, modeled reason, not by accident as in OLS.

Report the effect that answers your question. If a region asks “how does frailty drive underlying demand for prescriptions?” report the latent \beta_k. If it asks “if we make patients frailer-on-paper, how much will actually-recorded out-of-pocket SEK move on average?” report the observed-y effect \beta_k\,\Phi(\mathbf{x}'\boldsymbol\beta/\sigma). Quoting the raw Tobit coefficient as if it were an OLS slope on observed spending overstates the change in recorded SEK.

Truncation: When the Observation Vanishes

Censoring keeps the unit and loses the value. Truncation is harsher: the unit disappears. Suppose the Östergötland study had instead been built from a pharmacy register that only logs patients who filled at least one prescription. Then every patient with y^* \le 0 is simply absent — not recorded as 0, but gone. We do not even know how many are missing.

For truncated data, the density must be renormalized to integrate to one over the observed region only. A patient observed with spending y_i > 0 contributes the truncated normal density:

f(y_i \mid y_i > 0) = \frac{\dfrac{1}{\sigma}\,\phi\!\left(\dfrac{y_i - \mathbf{x}_i'\boldsymbol\beta}{\sigma}\right)}{\Phi\!\left(\dfrac{\mathbf{x}_i'\boldsymbol\beta}{\sigma}\right)}.

Read this as: “the ordinary normal density, divided by the probability of being above the threshold, so that it sums to one over the part of the distribution we can actually see.” The denominator \Phi(\mathbf{x}_i'\boldsymbol\beta/\sigma) is exactly the survival-into-the-sample probability. Truncated regression maximizes the sum of the logs of these renormalized densities — it is not the same model as Tobit, because there is no CDF-mass term (no censored units exist to contribute one).

Both Tobit and truncated regression lean hard on two assumptions:

  1. Normality of the latent error. The density and CDF terms are specifically normal. Unlike OLS — whose slope stays consistent under non-normal errors — Tobit and truncated regression are inconsistent if \varepsilon is not normal. A skewed cost distribution (very common for SEK spending) can break them.

  2. Homoskedasticity. A single \sigma enters every term. If the error variance grows with \mathbf{x} (heteroskedasticity), the censoring/truncation probabilities are mis-stated and the estimates are biased. Both assumptions are testable and frequently fail for skewed health-cost data — consider a log transform, a two-part model, or robust alternatives.

Math Details: Building and signing the Tobit likelihood

Why a censored observation is a CDF, not a density

A continuous random variable places zero probability on any single point, so a density \phi measures probability per unit length, not probability itself. For an uncensored patient we observed an exact value, and the likelihood contribution is the density there (this is standard MLE). But a censored patient at the limit is fundamentally different: we did not observe a point. We observed the interval event \{y^* \le 0\}, which carries genuine, non-infinitesimal probability

\Pr(y^* \le 0) = \Pr(\mathbf{x}'\boldsymbol\beta + \varepsilon \le 0) = \Pr\!\left(\frac{\varepsilon}{\sigma} \le -\frac{\mathbf{x}'\boldsymbol\beta}{\sigma}\right) = \Phi\!\left(-\frac{\mathbf{x}'\boldsymbol\beta}{\sigma}\right).

Mixing a density (for the exactly-observed) with a probability mass (for the interval-observed) in one likelihood is the defining move of the Tobit model. The full likelihood is the product

L(\boldsymbol\beta,\sigma) = \prod_{i:\,d_i=1} \frac{1}{\sigma}\,\phi\!\left(\frac{y_i - \mathbf{x}_i'\boldsymbol\beta}{\sigma}\right) \;\times\; \prod_{i:\,d_i=0} \Phi\!\left(-\frac{\mathbf{x}_i'\boldsymbol\beta}{\sigma}\right),

and taking logs gives the additive \ell(\boldsymbol\beta,\sigma) in the main text.

The observed-outcome expectation (and its derivative)

Write z = \mathbf{x}'\boldsymbol\beta/\sigma. A standard result for the left-censored-at-zero model is

\mathrm{E}[y \mid \mathbf{x}] = \Phi(z)\,\mathbf{x}'\boldsymbol\beta + \sigma\,\phi(z).

Read it as: “expected recorded spending equals the probability of being uncensored times the latent mean, plus a term for the truncated upper tail.” Differentiating with respect to x_k, the \phi and \Phi pieces cancel in a tidy way (McDonald & Moffitt, 1980), leaving the clean

\frac{\partial \mathrm{E}[y \mid \mathbf{x}]}{\partial x_k} = \beta_k\,\Phi(z),

the observed-y marginal effect quoted above. Because 0 < \Phi(z) < 1, this is always smaller in magnitude than the latent \beta_k — the model’s principled attenuation, decomposable into an effect on the probability of being uncensored and an effect on the level among the uncensored.

Why OLS attenuates, formally

Regressing observed y = \max(0, y^*) on x estimates \partial \mathrm{E}[y\mid x]/\partial x = \beta\,\Phi(z) < \beta. So even the probability limit of the censored-data OLS slope is the latent slope multiplied by the (sub-one) uncensored share — OLS is not merely noisy here, it is biased toward zero, and consistently so. More censoring (smaller \Phi(z)) means more attenuation. Tobit removes the \Phi(z) factor by modeling the latent line directly.

Fitting It: Tobit and Truncated Regression in Code

Both panels fit (1) a Tobit to the censored out-of-pocket spending and (2) a truncated regression to the subset of positive spenders, then contrast the Tobit slope against the naive-OLS slope to expose the attenuation. We simulate so the truth (\beta_1 = 360 SEK per frailty point) is known.

  • R
  • Python
# install.packages(c("AER", "censReg", "truncreg"))
library(AER)        # AER::tobit (wraps survival::survreg)
library(censReg)    # censReg::censReg — direct Tobit MLE
library(truncreg)   # truncreg::truncreg — truncated regression

set.seed(2024)
n       <- 600
frailty <- runif(n, 0, 10)
ystar   <- -900 + 360 * frailty + rnorm(n, 0, 600)   # latent SEK; truth = 360
y       <- pmax(0, ystar)                              # left-censored at 0
d       <- data.frame(y, frailty)

## ---- Naive OLS on the censored outcome: attenuated ----
ols <- lm(y ~ frailty, data = d)
coef(ols)["frailty"]                  # well below 360 — the bias

## ---- Tobit (left-censored at 0) via AER ----
fit_tobit <- tobit(y ~ frailty, left = 0, right = Inf, data = d)
summary(fit_tobit)
coef(fit_tobit)["frailty"]            # ~ 360 — recovers the latent slope

## ---- Same model via censReg (identical estimates) ----
fit_creg <- censReg(y ~ frailty, left = 0, data = d)
summary(fit_creg)

## ---- Observed-y marginal effect: beta * Phi(xb / sigma) ----
xb     <- predict(fit_tobit, type = "lp")           # linear predictor x'beta
sigma  <- fit_tobit$scale                           # estimated sigma
me_obs <- coef(fit_tobit)["frailty"] * mean(pnorm(xb / sigma))
cat("Latent effect   :", round(coef(fit_tobit)["frailty"]), "SEK\n")
cat("Observed-y effect:", round(me_obs), "SEK (always smaller)\n")

## ---- Truncated regression: only positive spenders are in the sample ----
d_trunc <- subset(d, y > 0)
fit_tr  <- truncreg(y ~ frailty, point = 0, direction = "left", data = d_trunc)
summary(fit_tr)                       # also ~ 360, unlike OLS on this subset
# pip install numpy scipy statsmodels
import numpy as np
from scipy.optimize import minimize
from scipy.stats import norm
import statsmodels.api as sm

rng     = np.random.default_rng(2024)
n       = 600
frailty = rng.uniform(0, 10, n)
ystar   = -900 + 360 * frailty + rng.normal(0, 600, n)   # latent; truth = 360
y       = np.maximum(0.0, ystar)                          # left-censored at 0
X       = sm.add_constant(frailty)                        # design matrix

# ---- Naive OLS on the censored outcome: attenuated ----
ols = sm.OLS(y, X).fit()
print("OLS slope (biased):", round(ols.params[1], 1))    # well below 360

# ---- Tobit via direct MLE: density for uncensored + CDF mass for censored ----
def neg_loglik_tobit(theta, X, y, L=0.0):
    b0, b1, log_sigma = theta
    sigma = np.exp(log_sigma)                  # keep sigma > 0
    xb    = b0 + b1 * X[:, 1]
    unc   = y > L
    # Uncensored: log normal density of the residual.
    ll_unc = norm.logpdf(y[unc], loc=xb[unc], scale=sigma).sum()
    # Censored: log P(y* <= L) = log Phi((L - xb)/sigma).
    ll_cen = norm.logcdf((L - xb[~unc]) / sigma).sum()
    return -(ll_unc + ll_cen)

start = [ols.params[0], ols.params[1], np.log(y.std())]
res   = minimize(neg_loglik_tobit, start, args=(X, y, 0.0), method="BFGS")
b0, b1, sigma = res.x[0], res.x[1], np.exp(res.x[2])
print("Tobit slope (consistent):", round(b1, 1))         # ~ 360

# ---- Observed-y marginal effect: beta * mean Phi(xb / sigma) ----
xb     = b0 + b1 * frailty
me_obs = b1 * norm.cdf(xb / sigma).mean()
print(f"Latent effect    : {b1:8.1f} SEK")
print(f"Observed-y effect: {me_obs:8.1f} SEK (always smaller)")

# ---- Truncated regression: only positive spenders enter the likelihood ----
def neg_loglik_trunc(theta, X, y, L=0.0):
    b0, b1, log_sigma = theta
    sigma = np.exp(log_sigma)
    xb    = b0 + b1 * X[:, 1]
    # Renormalised (truncated-normal) density: pdf / P(y* > L).
    ll = (norm.logpdf(y, loc=xb, scale=sigma)
          - norm.logcdf((xb - L) / sigma)).sum()
    return -ll

mask  = y > 0
res_t = minimize(neg_loglik_trunc, start,
                 args=(X[mask], y[mask], 0.0), method="BFGS")
print("Truncreg slope:", round(res_t.x[1], 1))           # ~ 360, not the OLS bias

In both languages the naive OLS slope lands well below 360 SEK (the attenuation), while Tobit and truncated regression recover the true latent slope. The observed-y marginal effect sits between the two: smaller than the latent \beta_1, but for a known and reportable reason.

Exercises

Exercise 1

A region records each patient’s annual number of physician läkarbesök, but the booking system caps the recorded value at 12 (any patient with more visits is logged as exactly 12). An analyst regresses recorded visits on a chronic-illness score using OLS.

  1. Is this censoring or truncation, and from above or below? (b) In which direction is the OLS slope biased? (c) What single change to the Tobit setup adapts it to this case?
Show answer

(a) Censoring from above (right-censoring / top-coding). Every patient is still in the sample — nobody is dropped — but anyone whose true visit count exceeds 12 has their value replaced by the limit 12. (If instead patients above 12 visits were omitted from the dataset entirely, that would be truncation.)

(b) Toward zero (attenuated). The high-visit patients have their outcome pulled down to 12, compressing the top of the cloud. A line fit to that compressed cloud rotates flatter, so the OLS slope understates the true effect of the illness score — the same attenuation as the left-censored case, mirror-imaged.

(c) Flip the observation rule to a maximum at the upper limit: model y = \min(12, y^*) instead of y = \max(0, y^*). In the likelihood, the uncensored units (visits < 12) keep the normal density term, and the censored units (recorded = 12) contribute the upper-tail mass \Pr(y^* \ge 12) = \Phi\!\big((\mathbf{x}'\boldsymbol\beta - 12)/\sigma\big) = 1 - \Phi\!\big((12 - \mathbf{x}'\boldsymbol\beta)/\sigma\big). In code, set right = 12, left = -Inf in AER::tobit.

Exercise 2

Explain in your own words why a censored observation contributes a cumulative distribution function term \Phi(\cdot) to the Tobit likelihood, while an uncensored observation contributes a density term \phi(\cdot). Why can we not simply plug the censored value (e.g. 0) into the density and proceed?

Show answer

For an uncensored patient we observed the exact latent value, so the relevant quantity is “how probable is a value right here?” — the normal density \phi evaluated at the residual. That is the ordinary MLE contribution.

For a censored patient we did not observe a value at all; we observed only the event “the latent outcome fell at or below the limit.” The probability of that whole interval event is the cumulative mass \Pr(y^* \le L) = \Phi\big((L - \mathbf{x}'\boldsymbol\beta)/\sigma\big) — a genuine, non-infinitesimal probability, not a density.

Plugging the clamped value (say 0) into the density would falsely assert “we measured exactly 0,” treating a whole censored region as a single precise point. That is exactly the mistake OLS makes, and it is what flattens the OLS line. Tobit’s CDF term encodes the honest statement “we only know it is below the limit,” which is why it recovers the latent slope.

Exercise 3

A Tobit fit of out-of-pocket SEK spending on a frailty index returns \hat\beta_{\text{frailty}} = 360, \hat\sigma = 600. For a patient with linear predictor \mathbf{x}'\hat{\boldsymbol\beta} = 300 SEK:

  1. Compute the marginal effect of frailty on the latent outcome. (b) Compute the marginal effect on the observed outcome. (c) Interpret the gap. (Use \Phi(0.5) \approx 0.691.)
Show answer

(a) The marginal effect on the latent outcome is just the coefficient: \frac{\partial \mathrm{E}[y^*\mid\mathbf{x}]}{\partial\,\text{frailty}} = \hat\beta_{\text{frailty}} = 360 \text{ SEK per frailty point.}

(b) The marginal effect on the observed outcome scales by \Phi(\mathbf{x}'\hat{\boldsymbol\beta}/\hat\sigma). Here z = 300/600 = 0.5, so \Phi(0.5) \approx 0.691: \frac{\partial \mathrm{E}[y\mid\mathbf{x}]}{\partial\,\text{frailty}} = 360 \times 0.691 \approx 249 \text{ SEK per frailty point.}

(c) A one-point rise in frailty raises latent demand by 360 SEK, but raises actually-recorded spending by only about 249 SEK on average. The shortfall (about 111 SEK) is the share of the effect “absorbed” by patients who remain pinned at the 0 limit — a nudge in frailty does not move their recorded spending off the boundary. Report 360 SEK for underlying demand, 249 SEK for the change a budget-holder would actually see in recorded out-of-pocket costs.

Exercise 4

In the Censoring Lab, set the threshold to 0 and note the three slopes, then raise the threshold to about 1500 SEK and raise \sigma to 1200. (a) What happens to the naive OLS slope and the “OLS attenuation” tile, and why? (b) Why does the Tobit slope stay near 360 SEK? (c) Name one realistic feature of Swedish out-of-pocket cost data that would break the Tobit model even at this threshold.

Show answer

(a) The naive OLS slope falls and the attenuation tile grows (more negative). Raising the threshold clamps more low-frailty patients onto the limit, and raising \sigma pushes still more points below it. The bottom of the point cloud is lifted toward the boundary, so the least-squares line rotates flatter and a larger fraction of the true 360-SEK slope is lost. The probability limit of the OLS slope is \beta\,\Phi(z) with \Phi(z) shrinking as censoring grows.

(b) Tobit does not treat the clamped values as real spending of L. For each censored patient it contributes the CDF mass \Phi\big((L - \mathbf{x}'\boldsymbol\beta)/\sigma\big) — the probability that the latent value fell below the limit — rather than a density at L. Modeling the latent line directly, it removes the \Phi(z) attenuation factor, so its slope stays near the data-generating 360 SEK regardless of how many points pile up.

(c) Skewed, non-normal costs (a heavy right tail of a few very expensive patients), or heteroskedasticity (cost variance rising with frailty). Tobit’s likelihood is built on a normal, homoskedastic latent error; unlike OLS, its consistency depends on those holding. Real SEK health-cost distributions are typically right-skewed, which can bias the Tobit estimates — motivating a log transform, a two-part model, or robust alternatives.

Key takeaways

  • Censoring records every unit but pins out-of-range values at a limit (y=\max(0,y^*)); truncation drops out-of-range units from the sample entirely. Both attenuate OLS toward zero.
  • The Tobit model assumes a latent normal y^* = \mathbf{x}'\boldsymbol\beta + \varepsilon and an observation rule, and is estimated by maximum likelihood.
  • Its log-likelihood is a mixture: a normal density term for uncensored units plus a normal CDF mass term, \Phi(-\mathbf{x}'\boldsymbol\beta/\sigma), for censored units — the latter is what repairs OLS’s attenuation.
  • There are two marginal effects: on the latent y^* it is \beta_k; on the observed y it is \beta_k\,\Phi(\mathbf{x}'\boldsymbol\beta/\sigma), always smaller in magnitude. Report the one your question asks for.
  • Truncated regression renormalizes the density by \Phi(\mathbf{x}'\boldsymbol\beta/\sigma) (no CDF-mass term); both Tobit and truncated regression are inconsistent under non-normal or heteroskedastic errors, unlike OLS.

What Comes Next

The Tobit model assumes a single equation governs both whether we see a positive value and how large it is — the same \mathbf{x}'\boldsymbol\beta decides censoring and level. But often the selection into being observed is a genuinely separate decision from the outcome we care about: a patient first chooses whether to seek care at all, and only then incurs a cost; a worker first chooses whether to work, and only then earns a wage. When the unobserved drivers of selection are correlated with the unobserved drivers of the outcome, even Tobit is not enough. The next page, Sample Selection: The Heckman Model, splits the problem into two equations — a probit selection equation and a linear outcome equation — and shows how the inverse Mills ratio you glimpsed in the truncated density becomes the explicit correction term that makes the outcome equation consistent.
