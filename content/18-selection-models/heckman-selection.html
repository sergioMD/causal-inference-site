Sample Selection: The Heckman Model

When who you observe depends on the outcome

In this section, you will learn to

  • Distinguish sample selection (the outcome is observed only for a self-selected subsample) from the censoring and truncation of the Tobit model
  • Write the two-equation Heckman model — a probit selection equation and a linear outcome equation linked by correlated errors \rho
  • Derive the selection-bias term E[\varepsilon \mid \text{selected}] and recognise it as the inverse Mills ratio (IMR)
  • Implement the Heckman correction two ways: the two-step estimator (add the IMR as a regressor) and the full-information MLE
  • Explain why a credible exclusion restriction — a variable that shifts selection but not the outcome — is what rescues identification from collinearity

The Question Behind the Missing Rows

A vårdcentral (primary care center) in Region Skåne wants to know how a patient’s age relates to the cost of a course of physiotherapy for lower-back pain. The natural move is to regress treatment cost (in SEK) on age, using the patients who completed a course last year. But there is a catch that the regression cannot see: cost is only recorded for patients who actually sought and started treatment. Many people with back pain never came in — they self-managed, used a private clinic, or simply endured it. Their cost is not zero; it is missing, and they are missing from the dataset entirely.

This is not a nuisance about a few absent rows. The patients who came in are not a random slice of the population. Those in more pain, with more time, or with stronger expectations of benefit are more likely to seek care — and those same traits plausibly drive how expensive their care turns out to be. Who we observe depends on the outcome we are trying to model. A regression on the observed (selected) sample will recover a line that is bent away from the truth, and no amount of extra data of the same kind will fix it.

James Heckman’s 1979 insight — which earned a share of the 2000 Nobel Prize — was to treat selection as a problem we can model rather than ignore. If we can write down the process that decides who is observed, we can compute exactly how it distorts the outcome equation, and then subtract that distortion off. This page builds that correction from the ground up. Like the Tobit model before it and the count models before that, the engine underneath is maximum likelihood — but the two-step version cleverly turns the hard part into an ordinary OLS regression with one extra regressor.

Selection Is Not Censoring

The previous page studied the Tobit model, where a latent outcome is clipped at a threshold: spending below zero is recorded as zero, but the same equation governs the latent value and the observation rule. Sample selection is a different animal, and the distinction decides which model you should reach for.

Sample selection occurs when the outcome Y is observed only for units that clear a separate selection process, and the unobservable driving selection is correlated with the unobservable in the outcome. Formally there are two equations:

\underbrace{S_i^* = \mathbf{z}_i'\boldsymbol\gamma + u_i, \qquad S_i = \mathbf{1}\{S_i^* > 0\}}_{\text{selection (probit)}} \qquad \underbrace{Y_i = \mathbf{x}_i'\boldsymbol\beta + \varepsilon_i}_{\text{outcome}}

and Y_i is observed only when S_i = 1. The errors are jointly normal with correlation \rho = \mathrm{Corr}(u_i, \varepsilon_i).

Read this as: “a latent selection index S^* equals observed drivers \mathbf{z}'\boldsymbol\gamma plus noise u; we select the unit (observe its Y) when that index crosses zero. The outcome Y is its own equation with its own noise \varepsilon. The two noises are correlated through \rho.” When \rho = 0 the noises are unrelated and ordinary OLS on the selected sample is fine; the whole problem lives in \rho \ne 0.

The contrast with censoring is sharp, and worth pinning down.

One equation versus two. In the Tobit model, the same latent variable both generates the outcome and triggers the censoring — a patient with low latent demand spends little and is the one censored at zero. There is one equation and one error. In the Heckman model, a separate selection equation (with its own regressors \mathbf{z} and its own error u) decides who is observed; the outcome equation has its own error \varepsilon. Selection bites only because the two errors are correlated. Tobit is “the outcome censors itself”; Heckman is “a different process picks who you get to see.”

To make this concrete: a Tobit fits when we record care spending for everyone but it piles up at zero for non-users (the zero is a real, observed value). A Heckman fits when spending is simply absent for non-users — there is no row at all — and whether a row exists is governed by who chose to seek care.

How Selection Bends the Line

Why does dropping the unobserved rows bias the slope? Because conditioning on “was selected” (S_i = 1) is the same as conditioning on u_i > -\mathbf{z}_i'\boldsymbol\gamma — we keep only units whose selection noise was high enough. And if \varepsilon is correlated with u (\rho \ne 0), keeping the high-u units also keeps the high-\varepsilon (or low-\varepsilon) units. The conditional mean of the outcome error among the selected is therefore not zero:

E[\varepsilon_i \mid S_i = 1, \mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{z}_i] = \rho\,\sigma_\varepsilon \, \lambda(\mathbf{z}_i'\boldsymbol\gamma) \;\ne\; 0 \quad \text{when } \rho \ne 0.

That extra term \rho\,\sigma_\varepsilon\,\lambda(\cdot) is an omitted variable. Run OLS on the selected sample and it folds into the residual; because \lambda(\mathbf{z}_i'\boldsymbol\gamma) varies with the regressors, it contaminates \hat{\boldsymbol\beta}. The function \lambda is the inverse Mills ratio, which we now define precisely.

The inverse Mills ratio (IMR) of a standard normal at index a is the hazard ratio

\lambda(a) \;=\; \frac{\phi(a)}{\Phi(a)},

where \phi is the standard normal density and \Phi its cumulative distribution function.

Read this as: “lambda of a is the normal density at a divided by the probability of being above -a.” It is exactly the expected value of a standard normal given that it exceeds -a — the mean of the truncated tail. The smaller the selection probability \Phi(a), the larger \lambda(a): when few units clear the bar, those that do are an extreme, heavily-selected slice, so the selection distortion is large.

The Heckman correction follows immediately. If the omitted term is \rho\,\sigma_\varepsilon\,\lambda(\mathbf{z}_i'\boldsymbol\gamma), then add it back as a regressor. We do not know \boldsymbol\gamma, but we can estimate it with a probit on the full sample (everyone is either selected or not), form the fitted IMR \hat\lambda_i = \lambda(\mathbf{z}_i'\hat{\boldsymbol\gamma}), and include it in the outcome regression:

Y_i = \mathbf{x}_i'\boldsymbol\beta + \beta_\lambda\,\hat\lambda_i + \text{error}, \qquad \beta_\lambda = \rho\,\sigma_\varepsilon.

Once \hat\lambda_i absorbs the selection, the remaining error is mean-zero and OLS on the selected sample recovers \boldsymbol\beta consistently. The coefficient on the IMR, \hat\beta_\lambda, is itself a test: if it is statistically zero, \rho = 0 and there was no selection problem to begin with.

selheck_truth = ({ b0: 4.0, b1: 1.4 })   // true cost intercept & age slope (1000s SEK)

selheck_data = {
  selheck_draw;                              // re-draw on button press
  const n = 600;
  const rho = selheck_rho;
  const rn = d3.randomNormal.source(d3.randomLcg(0.42 + 0.001 * selheck_draw))(0, 1);

  // Choose the selection intercept γ0 so the realised selection rate ≈ slider.
  // Φ(γ0 + ...) averaged over the covariate cloud ≈ selectShare; with mean-zero
  // covariates the average index is γ0, so γ0 ≈ Φ⁻¹(share).
  const g0 = selheck_qnorm(selheck_selectShare);
  const g_age = 0.5;        // age also raises the chance of seeking care
  const g_pain = 1.1;       // EXCLUSION RESTRICTION: pain drives selection only

  const rows = [];
  for (let i = 0; i < n; i++) {
    const age = rn();                        // standardized age
    const pain = rn();                       // standardized baseline pain (exclusion)
    // Correlated errors via Cholesky of a 2×2 with unit variances and corr ρ.
    const u = rn();
    const e = rho * u + Math.sqrt(1 - rho * rho) * rn();
    const sIndex = g0 + g_age * age + g_pain * pain + u;
    const selected = sIndex > 0;
    const cost = selheck_truth.b0 + selheck_truth.b1 * age + e;  // cost in 1000s SEK
    rows.push({ age, pain, u, e, sIndex, selected, cost });
  }
  return rows;
}

selheck_selected = selheck_data.filter(d => d.selected)
selheck_phi = a => Math.exp(-0.5 * a * a) / Math.sqrt(2 * Math.PI)

// Φ via the Abramowitz–Stegun rational approximation (max error ≈ 7.5e-8).
selheck_Phi = function (a) {
  const sign = a < 0 ? -1 : 1;
  const x = Math.abs(a) / Math.SQRT2;
  const t = 1 / (1 + 0.3275911 * x);
  const y = 1 - (((((1.061405429 * t - 1.453152027) * t) + 1.421413741) * t
            - 0.284496736) * t + 0.254829592) * t * Math.exp(-x * x);
  return 0.5 * (1 + sign * y);
}

// Φ⁻¹ (quantile), central-region rational approximation (Acklam). The slider
// only ever passes p ∈ [0.2, 0.9], so the central branch is all we need.
selheck_qnorm = function (p) {
  const a = [-3.969683028665376e+01, 2.209460984245205e+02, -2.759285104469687e+02,
             1.383577518672690e+02, -3.066479806614716e+01, 2.506628277459239e+00];
  const b = [-5.447609879822406e+01, 1.615858368580409e+02, -1.556989798598866e+02,
             6.680131188771972e+01, -1.328068155288572e+01];
  const q = p - 0.5, r = q * q;
  return (((((a[0]*r+a[1])*r+a[2])*r+a[3])*r+a[4])*r+a[5])*q /
         (((((b[0]*r+b[1])*r+b[2])*r+b[3])*r+b[4])*r+1);
}

selheck_imr = a => selheck_phi(a) / selheck_Phi(a)
selheck_ols1 = function (rows, xKey, yKey) {
  const mx = d3.mean(rows, d => d[xKey]);
  const my = d3.mean(rows, d => d[yKey]);
  let sxx = 0, sxy = 0;
  for (const r of rows) {
    sxx += (r[xKey] - mx) ** 2;
    sxy += (r[xKey] - mx) * (r[yKey] - my);
  }
  const slope = sxy / sxx;
  return { slope, intercept: my - slope * mx };
}

// ── Two-regressor OLS (cost on age and the fitted IMR) via normal equations.
//    Returns {b0, bAge, bLambda}. Used for the Heckman two-step line.
selheck_ols2 = function (rows, yKey, x1Key, x2Key) {
  const n = rows.length;
  let Sx1=0,Sx2=0,Sy=0,Sx1x1=0,Sx2x2=0,Sx1x2=0,Sx1y=0,Sx2y=0;
  for (const r of rows) {
    const x1=r[x1Key], x2=r[x2Key], y=r[yKey];
    Sx1+=x1; Sx2+=x2; Sy+=y;
    Sx1x1+=x1*x1; Sx2x2+=x2*x2; Sx1x2+=x1*x2;
    Sx1y+=x1*y; Sx2y+=x2*y;
  }
  // Solve the 3×3 normal equations [n, Sx1, Sx2; ...] β = [Sy; Sx1y; Sx2y].
  const A = [[n, Sx1, Sx2], [Sx1, Sx1x1, Sx1x2], [Sx2, Sx1x2, Sx2x2]];
  const rhs = [Sy, Sx1y, Sx2y];
  const sol = selheck_solve3(A, rhs);
  return { b0: sol[0], bAge: sol[1], bLambda: sol[2] };
}

// 3×3 linear solve by Gaussian elimination with partial pivoting.
selheck_solve3 = function (A, b) {
  const M = A.map((row, i) => [...row, b[i]]);
  for (let c = 0; c < 3; c++) {
    let piv = c;
    for (let r = c + 1; r < 3; r++) if (Math.abs(M[r][c]) > Math.abs(M[piv][c])) piv = r;
    [M[c], M[piv]] = [M[piv], M[c]];
    for (let r = 0; r < 3; r++) {
      if (r === c) continue;
      const f = M[r][c] / M[c][c];
      for (let k = c; k < 4; k++) M[r][k] -= f * M[c][k];
    }
  }
  return [M[0][3] / M[0][0], M[1][3] / M[1][1], M[2][3] / M[2][2]];
}
selheck_probit = {
  const rows = selheck_data;
  let g = [0, 0, 0];                          // [intercept, age, pain]
  for (let iter = 0; iter < 40; iter++) {
    const grad = [0, 0, 0];
    const H = [[0,0,0],[0,0,0],[0,0,0]];      // gradient & Hessian
    for (const r of rows) {
      const xi = [1, r.age, r.pain];
      const idx = g[0] + g[1]*r.age + g[2]*r.pain;
      const Pi = Math.min(1 - 1e-9, Math.max(1e-9, selheck_Phi(idx)));
      const phi = selheck_phi(idx);
      const y = r.selected ? 1 : 0;
      const w = phi * (y - Pi) / (Pi * (1 - Pi));    // score weight
      const hw = phi * phi / (Pi * (1 - Pi));        // information weight
      for (let a = 0; a < 3; a++) {
        grad[a] += w * xi[a];
        for (let b = 0; b < 3; b++) H[a][b] -= hw * xi[a] * xi[b];
      }
    }
    // Newton step: g ← g − H⁻¹ grad (H negative definite ⇒ negate then solve).
    const step = selheck_solve3(H.map(row => row.map(v => -v)), grad);
    g = [g[0] + step[0], g[1] + step[1], g[2] + step[2]];
    const mag = Math.hypot(step[0], step[1], step[2]);
    if (mag < 1e-8) break;
  }
  return { g0: g[0], gAge: g[1], gPain: g[2] };
}

// Attach the fitted IMR to each selected unit, then run the two-step OLS.
selheck_selectedWithIMR = selheck_selected.map(r => {
  const idx = selheck_probit.g0 + selheck_probit.gAge * r.age + selheck_probit.gPain * r.pain;
  return { ...r, lambda: selheck_imr(idx) };
})

    biased: biased,                                  // OLS on selected sample
    heck:   { intercept: two.b0, slope: two.bAge, bLambda: two.bLambda }
  };
}

How to Read the Selection-Bias Lab

Work through these in order; each isolates one idea.

Step 1: See the full population. The faint grey cloud is every patient, including those who never sought care. The ochre line is the true relationship between age and cost, fit to the whole cloud — the answer we wish we could compute. Its slope is 1.40 (each standardized year of age adds 1,400 SEK).

Step 2: See what you actually get. The solid slate dots are the selected sample — patients who sought care and so have a recorded cost. Notice they are not a random slice of the cloud. Age helps push a patient over the selection bar (older patients seek care more readily), so low-age patients only appear when their cost-side luck was unusually high. With positive \rho this lift falls hardest on the left of the plot: low-age selected dots sit well above the true line, while high-age dots — who clear the bar easily — sit close to it.

Step 3: Watch the bias appear. Drag error correlation \rho up from 0 to +0.9. The red dashed line — ordinary OLS on the selected dots only — swings away from the ochre truth. Because the upward lift is concentrated at low ages, the left end of the red line is pulled up while the right end barely moves, so the red slope flattens and drops below the true 1.40 (watch the “Biased OLS slope” tile fall). The “Biased error” tile grows in magnitude. This is sample-selection bias: the missing rows are missing for a reason that correlates with cost, and OLS has no way to know.

Step 4: Watch the correction work. The green line is the Heckman two-step fit. It estimates a probit selection model, forms the inverse Mills ratio for each selected patient, adds it as a regressor, and re-runs OLS. Across the whole \rho range the green line stays glued to the ochre truth: the “Heckman error” tile stays near zero while the red one blows up.

Step 5: Read the IMR coefficient. The “IMR coef. \beta_\lambda” tile is \hat\rho\,\hat\sigma_\varepsilon. At \rho = 0 it sits near zero — a built-in test that there was no selection problem. As you raise \rho it grows in magnitude, quantifying exactly how much the selection process was contaminating the cost equation.

Step 6: Vary the selection rate. Lower share who seek care to 0.2. Now selection is severe (the IMR is large), the red line’s bias worsens, and you can feel how much the green line is leaning on the model. Resample with Draw a new patient sample to confirm the pattern is structural, not a fluke of one draw.

Identification: The Exclusion Restriction

The two-step estimator looks like magic: add one regressor and bias evaporates. There is a catch, and it is the single most important thing to understand about the Heckman model.

The IMR \lambda(\mathbf{z}_i'\hat{\boldsymbol\gamma}) is a smooth, nearly-linear function of the index \mathbf{z}_i'\hat{\boldsymbol\gamma} over much of its range. If the selection regressors \mathbf{z} are exactly the same as the outcome regressors \mathbf{x}, then \hat\lambda_i is nearly a linear function of \mathbf{x}_i — and it is fighting the other regressors for the same variation. Identification then rests entirely on the curvature of \lambda, which is weak. Standard errors explode and estimates become wildly unstable.

Credible Heckman identification requires an exclusion restriction. There must be at least one variable in the selection equation \mathbf{z} that is excluded from the outcome equation \mathbf{x}: a variable that plausibly affects whether a unit is observed but has no direct effect on the outcome of interest.

In our example, baseline pain severity is the exclusion restriction: more pain makes a patient far more likely to seek physiotherapy (it enters selection), but conditional on seeking care, baseline pain is assumed not to shift the cost of a standard course (it is excluded from the outcome). This gives \hat\lambda_i variation that is independent of the cost regressors, so the correction is identified by data rather than by functional form.

Without an exclusion restriction, Heckman is identified “off the normality assumption” alone — and that is fragile. When \mathbf{z} = \mathbf{x}, the model technically estimates because \lambda is not perfectly linear, but the correction leans entirely on the joint-normality of (u, \varepsilon) and the curvature of the Mills ratio. Results become hypersensitive to distributional misspecification and the IMR is often near-collinear with the regressors (a variance-inflation problem). Always report whether you have a genuine exclusion restriction and defend it substantively; a Heckman model without one should be read with deep skepticism.

A second subtlety: in the two-step estimator the regressor \hat\lambda_i is generated from a first-stage probit, so the naive OLS standard errors in the second stage are wrong (too small). Use the Heckman-corrected (Murphy–Topel / Greene) standard errors that the software reports, or estimate the full model by MLE, which gets the standard errors right automatically.

Two-Step versus Full MLE

There are two ways to fit the model, and they trade transparency against efficiency.

The Heckman two-step estimator (Heckit):

  1. Probit of S_i on \mathbf{z}_i over the full sample \Rightarrow \hat{\boldsymbol\gamma}.
  2. Form \hat\lambda_i = \phi(\mathbf{z}_i'\hat{\boldsymbol\gamma}) / \Phi(\mathbf{z}_i'\hat{\boldsymbol\gamma}) for the selected units.
  3. OLS of Y_i on \mathbf{x}_i and \hat\lambda_i over the selected sample \Rightarrow \hat{\boldsymbol\beta}, \hat\beta_\lambda.

The full-information MLE maximises the joint likelihood of the selection indicator and (for the selected) the outcome directly over (\boldsymbol\beta, \boldsymbol\gamma, \sigma_\varepsilon, \rho) in one step.

The two-step is robust, transparent, and never fails to converge — you literally watch the selection term enter as a regressor. The MLE is more efficient (smaller standard errors) when the joint-normality assumption holds, and it returns \rho and \sigma_\varepsilon as proper parameters with valid standard errors, but it can struggle to converge when selection is weak or the exclusion restriction is thin. A common workflow uses the two-step estimates as starting values for the MLE.

# install.packages("sampleSelection")
library(sampleSelection)

set.seed(2024)
n   <- 4000
age  <- rnorm(n)                      # standardized age
pain <- rnorm(n)                      # baseline pain = EXCLUSION RESTRICTION
rho  <- 0.6                           # correlation of selection & cost errors

# Correlated bivariate-normal errors (unit variances).
u   <- rnorm(n)
eps <- rho * u + sqrt(1 - rho^2) * rnorm(n)

# Selection: more pain & older patients more likely to seek physiotherapy.
s_star   <- -0.2 + 0.5 * age + 1.1 * pain + u
selected <- s_star > 0

# Outcome: physiotherapy cost (1000s SEK), true age slope = 1.4. Pain is
# EXCLUDED from the cost equation.
cost_full <- 4.0 + 1.4 * age + eps
cost      <- ifelse(selected, cost_full, NA)   # missing for non-seekers

dat <- data.frame(cost, age, pain, selected)

## ---- Naive OLS on the selected sample (BIASED) ----
naive <- lm(cost ~ age, data = subset(dat, selected))
cat("Naive age slope  =", round(coef(naive)["age"], 3), "\n")  # ~1.3, too flat

## ---- Heckman two-step (Heckit): exclusion restriction = pain ----
two_step <- heckit(
  selection = selected ~ age + pain,   # probit selection equation
  outcome   = cost ~ age,              # outcome equation (pain EXCLUDED)
  data      = dat,
  method    = "2step"
)
summary(two_step)
cat("2-step age slope =", round(coef(two_step)["age"], 3), "\n")  # ~1.4

## ---- Full-information MLE (more efficient under joint normality) ----
mle <- selection(
  selection = selected ~ age + pain,
  outcome   = cost ~ age,
  data      = dat,
  method    = "ml"
)
summary(mle)        # reports rho and sigma with valid SEs
cat("MLE rho estimate =", round(coef(mle)["rho"], 3), "\n")       # ~0.6

print(f"IMR coef (rho*sigma) = {two_step.params[2]:.3f}")

Math Details: Deriving the inverse Mills ratio and the selection-bias term

Goal

We want E[\varepsilon_i \mid S_i = 1] — the mean of the outcome error among the selected. If this is nonzero and varies with the regressors, OLS on the selected sample is biased.

Setup

Let the selection index be S_i^* = \mathbf{z}_i'\boldsymbol\gamma + u_i, with S_i = \mathbf{1}\{S_i^* > 0\}, and let the outcome error be \varepsilon_i. Normalise u_i \sim N(0,1) (the probit scale is not separately identified) and let (u_i, \varepsilon_i) be jointly normal with

\begin{pmatrix} u_i \\ \varepsilon_i \end{pmatrix} \sim N\!\left( \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \begin{pmatrix} 1 & \rho\,\sigma_\varepsilon \\ \rho\,\sigma_\varepsilon & \sigma_\varepsilon^2 \end{pmatrix}\right).

Step 1: A truncated-normal moment

Selection S_i = 1 means S_i^* > 0, i.e. u_i > -\mathbf{z}_i'\boldsymbol\gamma. The first result we need is the mean of a standard normal above a cutoff. For u \sim N(0,1) and any c,

E[u \mid u > c] = \frac{\phi(c)}{1 - \Phi(c)} = \frac{\phi(-c)}{\Phi(-c)},

using the symmetry \phi(c) = \phi(-c) and 1 - \Phi(c) = \Phi(-c). Setting c = -\mathbf{z}_i'\boldsymbol\gamma and writing a_i = \mathbf{z}_i'\boldsymbol\gamma,

E[u_i \mid S_i = 1] = E[u_i \mid u_i > -a_i] = \frac{\phi(a_i)}{\Phi(a_i)} \;\equiv\; \lambda(a_i).

This ratio is the inverse Mills ratio \lambda(a_i).

Step 2: Project \varepsilon onto u

Because (u_i, \varepsilon_i) are jointly normal, we can write the regression of \varepsilon on u exactly:

\varepsilon_i = \big(\rho\,\sigma_\varepsilon\big)\, u_i + \eta_i, \qquad \eta_i \perp u_i,\quad E[\eta_i] = 0,

where the coefficient is \mathrm{Cov}(u,\varepsilon)/\mathrm{Var}(u) = \rho\,\sigma_\varepsilon / 1 = \rho\,\sigma_\varepsilon, and \eta_i is independent of u_i (a property of the bivariate normal). Taking the conditional expectation given S_i = 1 (a statement purely about u_i, to which \eta_i is independent):

E[\varepsilon_i \mid S_i = 1] = \rho\,\sigma_\varepsilon \, E[u_i \mid S_i = 1] + \underbrace{E[\eta_i \mid S_i=1]}_{=\,0} = \rho\,\sigma_\varepsilon \, \lambda(a_i).

Step 3: The selection-corrected conditional mean

Substituting into the outcome equation Y_i = \mathbf{x}_i'\boldsymbol\beta + \varepsilon_i and conditioning on selection,

\boxed{\; E[Y_i \mid \mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{z}_i,\, S_i = 1] = \mathbf{x}_i'\boldsymbol\beta \;+\; \rho\,\sigma_\varepsilon \, \lambda(\mathbf{z}_i'\boldsymbol\gamma) \;}

This is the whole model in one line. The selected sample’s conditional mean is the structural line \mathbf{x}_i'\boldsymbol\beta plus an extra term proportional to the inverse Mills ratio. If we omit \lambda(\cdot) (plain OLS), that term is absorbed into the error; since \lambda(\mathbf{z}_i'\boldsymbol\gamma) correlates with \mathbf{x}_i (they share variables, or are linked through \mathbf{z}), the omission biases \hat{\boldsymbol\beta}. Including the fitted \hat\lambda_i = \lambda(\mathbf{z}_i'\hat{\boldsymbol\gamma}) as a regressor restores a mean-zero error, and its coefficient is exactly \beta_\lambda = \rho\,\sigma_\varepsilon.

Why \beta_\lambda = 0 \iff \rho = 0

Since \sigma_\varepsilon > 0, the IMR coefficient \beta_\lambda = \rho\,\sigma_\varepsilon vanishes if and only if \rho = 0. A t-test on \hat\beta_\lambda is therefore a direct test of the null “no selection bias.” Fail to reject and you may use plain OLS on the selected sample; reject and the correction is doing real work.

Exercise 1

A region studies the relationship between a diabetes patient’s education and their annual out-of-pocket spending on a structured self-management program. Spending is recorded only for patients who enrolled in the program; non-enrollees are absent from the data. Enrollment is driven partly by how far the patient lives from the nearest vårdcentral offering the program.

  1. Why is naive OLS of spending on education, using enrollees only, potentially biased? (b) Identify a candidate exclusion restriction and justify it. (c) What does a statistically insignificant coefficient on the inverse Mills ratio tell you?
Show answer

(a) Patients self-select into enrollment, and the unobservables driving enrollment (motivation, health literacy, time) plausibly also drive spending. So the enrolled sample is non-random with respect to the spending error: E[\varepsilon \mid \text{enrolled}] \ne 0. OLS on enrollees omits the selection term \rho\,\sigma_\varepsilon\,\lambda(\cdot), which correlates with education, biasing the education coefficient.

(b) Distance to the nearest program-offering vårdcentral is a good candidate: it strongly affects whether a patient enrolls (a selection driver) but, conditional on enrolling, has no plausible direct effect on how much an enrollee spends on the program itself. It belongs in \mathbf{z} (selection) but is excluded from \mathbf{x} (outcome).

(c) An insignificant IMR coefficient means \hat\beta_\lambda = \hat\rho\,\hat\sigma_\varepsilon \approx 0, i.e. the selection and spending errors are essentially uncorrelated (\rho \approx 0). There is no detectable selection bias, and plain OLS on the enrolled sample is consistent. The correction was not needed — but you only learned that by running it.

Exercise 2

Using the standard normal, compute the inverse Mills ratio \lambda(a) = \phi(a)/\Phi(a) at (a) a = 0 and (b) a large positive a, and (c) explain what a large \lambda implies about the severity of selection. Useful values: \phi(0) = 0.3989, \Phi(0) = 0.5.

Show answer

(a) At a = 0: \lambda(0) = \phi(0)/\Phi(0) = 0.3989 / 0.5 = 0.7979 \approx 0.80. (This is the mean of a standard normal truncated below at 0 — the classic \sqrt{2/\pi} \approx 0.7979.)

(b) For large positive a, \Phi(a) \to 1 while \phi(a) \to 0, so \lambda(a) \to 0. Almost everyone is selected, so “being selected” carries essentially no information and the truncated-tail mean shrinks toward zero. (Conversely, for large negative a, \Phi(a) \to 0 faster than \phi(a), and \lambda(a) \to \infty.)

(c) A large \lambda(\mathbf{z}_i'\boldsymbol\gamma) arises when the selection probability \Phi(\mathbf{z}_i'\boldsymbol\gamma) is small — few units clear the bar. Those that do are an extreme, heavily-truncated slice of the error distribution, so the selection distortion \rho\,\sigma_\varepsilon\,\lambda(\cdot) is large. Severe selection (low selection rates) means a big correction and a model leaning hard on its assumptions.

Exercise 3

In the interactive, set \rho = 0 and note the three lines coincide; then raise \rho to +0.8. (a) Which direction does the biased OLS slope move, and why? (b) What happens to the IMR coefficient tile? (c) Now set \rho to -0.8. Predict the direction of bias before dragging, then check.

Show answer

(a) With \rho > 0, selection noise u and cost noise \varepsilon move together. Age itself raises the selection index (older patients seek care more readily), so low-age patients enter the sample only when their cost-side luck (\varepsilon) is unusually high — their selected dots are lifted above the true line. High-age patients clear the bar easily, so they are barely lifted. Because the upward push is largest at the left, the left end of the fitted line rises more than the right and the slope flattens — the biased OLS slope falls below the true 1.40 (typically to around 1.3). The bias here is downward, not upward.

(b) The IMR coefficient \hat\beta_\lambda = \hat\rho\,\hat\sigma_\varepsilon grows positive and moves away from zero. It is the model detecting and measuring the selection.

(c) With \rho < 0, the correlation flips: selected low-age patients now tend to have low cost-side luck, so the differential push at the left is downward — the left end of the line is pulled down more than the right, steepening the slope above 1.40. The IMR coefficient turns negative. The Heckman line, in both cases, snaps back to 1.40.

Exercise 4

A colleague fits a Heckman model where the selection equation and the outcome equation use the exact same regressors (no exclusion restriction). The point estimates look reasonable but the standard errors are enormous. Explain what is going wrong, and what they should do.

Show answer

Without an exclusion restriction, the only thing distinguishing the IMR \hat\lambda_i = \lambda(\mathbf{x}_i'\hat{\boldsymbol\gamma}) from the outcome regressors \mathbf{x}_i is the curvature of the Mills ratio function. But over much of its range \lambda is close to linear, so \hat\lambda_i is nearly a linear combination of \mathbf{x}_i — it is severely collinear with the other regressors. Collinearity inflates the variance of every coefficient (high variance-inflation factor), which is exactly the enormous standard errors observed. The model is then identified only “off functional form” — the assumed joint normality and the slight nonlinearity of \lambda — which is fragile and not credible.

The fix is substantive, not statistical: find a genuine exclusion restriction — a variable that affects selection but is defensibly absent from the outcome equation (e.g., distance to clinic, a policy-induced eligibility quirk, local supply shocks) — and put it in \mathbf{z} only. That gives \hat\lambda_i independent variation, breaks the collinearity, and makes the correction rest on data rather than on the normality assumption. If no credible exclusion restriction exists, the honest move is to report the OLS result with a frank discussion of potential selection bias rather than a fragile Heckman fit.

Key takeaways

  • Sample selection means the outcome is observed only for a self-selected subsample chosen by a separate process whose error is correlated (\rho) with the outcome error — distinct from Tobit censoring, where one equation does both jobs.
  • Conditioning on “selected” makes the outcome error have nonzero mean: E[\varepsilon \mid \text{selected}] = \rho\,\sigma_\varepsilon\,\lambda(\mathbf{z}'\boldsymbol\gamma), where \lambda = \phi/\Phi is the inverse Mills ratio.
  • The Heckman correction adds the fitted IMR as a regressor in the outcome equation; its coefficient is \rho\,\sigma_\varepsilon, so testing it against zero is a direct test for selection bias.
  • Fit it via the transparent two-step (probit → IMR → OLS) or the more efficient full-information MLE; remember the two-step’s second-stage SEs need the generated-regressor correction.
  • Credible identification requires an exclusion restriction — a variable that shifts selection but not the outcome; without one, the IMR is near-collinear with the regressors and the model leans entirely on the normality assumption.
