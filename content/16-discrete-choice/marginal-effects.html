Interpreting Nonlinear Models: Marginal Effects and Odds Ratios

From coefficients to effects you can report

In this section, you will learn to

  • Explain why a logit or probit coefficient is not, by itself, an interpretable effect on the probability of the outcome
  • Define the marginal effect of a continuous regressor and read it off the slope (tangent) of the fitted S-curve
  • Distinguish the Average Marginal Effect (AME) from the Marginal Effect at the Mean (MEM), and say when each is appropriate
  • Convert a logit coefficient into an odds ratio \exp(\beta) and state exactly what it does and does not mean
  • Compute the partial effect of a dummy regressor as a difference in predicted probabilities, and report effects in R and Python

The Question Behind Every Logit Table

A vårdcentral (primary care center) in Region Uppsala runs a logistic regression to predict whether a Type 2 diabetes patient reaches the HbA1c target (at or below 52 mmol/mol) within a year. One regressor is the number of distriktssköterska (district nurse) follow-up visits. The software reports a coefficient of \hat\beta = 0.42. The verksamhetschef (clinic manager) asks the obvious question: “so how much does one extra visit raise a patient’s chance of hitting target?”

The honest first answer is uncomfortable: it depends on the patient. In ordinary least squares, the coefficient \hat\beta is the answer — every patient’s predicted outcome rises by exactly \hat\beta per extra visit, full stop. But logit and probit run the linear index x'\beta through an S-shaped curve. On that curve, the same one-unit nudge in x moves the probability a lot in the steep middle and almost nothing in the flat tails. A patient already at 90% probability of success has little room to gain; a patient near 50% gains the most. The coefficient 0.42 is measured on the latent index / log-odds scale, not on the probability scale anyone actually reports.

This page is about closing that gap: turning the coefficients from Binary Outcomes: LPM, Logit, and Probit into numbers a clinic manager, a regional board, or a referee can read — marginal effects on the probability scale, and odds ratios for logit. These models are fit by maximum likelihood (see the Maximum Likelihood module); here we take the fitted \hat\beta as given and ask what it means.

Why a Coefficient Is Not an Effect

Write the logit model for the probability that patient i reaches target:

P(y_i = 1 \mid x_i) = \Lambda(x_i'\beta) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-x_i'\beta}},

where \Lambda is the logistic (sigmoid) CDF. Read this as: “the probability of success equals the logistic function evaluated at the linear index x_i'\beta.” The index x_i'\beta can be any real number; \Lambda squashes it into (0,1). For probit, replace \Lambda with \Phi, the standard Normal CDF.

The effect of a small change in a continuous regressor x_k is the derivative of this probability with respect to x_k — the slope of the curve at that patient’s position. By the chain rule,

\frac{\partial P(y=1 \mid x)}{\partial x_k} \;=\; \underbrace{\lambda(x'\beta)}_{\text{density at the index}} \cdot \beta_k ,

where \lambda = \Lambda(1-\Lambda) is the logistic density (for probit it is \phi, the Normal density). Read this as: “the marginal effect equals the coefficient \beta_k scaled by the height of the density at the patient’s index value.” That scaling factor \lambda(x'\beta) is the punchline: it is largest where the index is near zero (probability near 0.5) and shrinks toward zero in the tails. The coefficient sets the direction and a common ceiling; the curve decides how much of that ceiling each patient actually gets.

The marginal effect of a continuous regressor x_k is the partial derivative of the predicted probability with respect to x_k:

\text{ME}_k(x) \;=\; \frac{\partial P(y=1\mid x)}{\partial x_k} \;=\; f(x'\beta)\,\beta_k,

where f = \lambda for logit and f = \phi for probit. It is the instantaneous slope of the S-curve at the point x, in units of “change in probability per one-unit change in x_k.” Because f(x'\beta) depends on where the patient sits, every individual has their own marginal effect.

So \hat\beta = 0.42 does not mean “+0.42 in probability.” It means “+0.42 on the log-odds scale,” and the probability effect ranges from near zero (for patients already almost certain to succeed or fail) up to a maximum of 0.25 \times 0.42 \approx 0.105 at the steepest point of the curve, since the logistic density peaks at \lambda = 0.25 when \Lambda = 0.5.

How to Read the S-Curve and Its Tangent

Work through these in order; each isolates one idea.

Step 1: Read the curve. The red S-curve is the fitted probability \Lambda(\hat\beta_0 + \hat\beta_1 x) that a patient reaches the HbA1c target, as a function of follow-up visits x. It is flat near 0 visits (almost no one hits target), rises through the middle, and flattens again near 1 (almost everyone hits target). This is the shape that makes the coefficient non-interpretable on its own.

Step 2: Watch the tangent pivot. Drag the patient’s visit count. The slate dashed line is the tangent to the curve at that patient. Its slope is the marginal effect — the “ME at your patient” tile. Park the patient near the middle (around 4 to 5 visits, where p \approx 0.5) and the tangent is steep: an extra visit buys the most probability. Drag to the far right (10+ visits) and the tangent goes nearly flat: that patient was already near-certain, so an extra visit barely moves them.

Step 3: Confirm the maximum is at p = 0.5. The thin horizontal reference line marks p = 0.5. The tangent is steepest exactly where the red curve crosses it. The largest possible marginal effect for this regressor is 0.25 \times \hat\beta_1 = 0.25 \times 0.42 \approx 0.105 — you cannot get a bigger probability bump than that, no matter the patient.

Step 4: Compare AME and MEM. The green “AME” tile averages each of 300 patients’ own tangent slopes; the slate “MEM” tile is the single slope at the average patient (the hollow slate dot at “mean x”). They are close here but not identical — AME averages the curve’s slope over the whole spread of patients, while MEM reads it at one representative point. When patients are bunched in the steep middle the two nearly coincide; when they straddle the flat tails they diverge.

AME vs. MEM: Two Ways to Summarize a Curve

Because every patient has their own marginal effect, we need one number to report. There are two standard ways to collapse the distribution of effects, and they answer subtly different questions.

The Average Marginal Effect (AME) computes each observation’s marginal effect at its own covariate values, then averages:

\text{AME}_k \;=\; \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} f(x_i'\hat\beta)\,\hat\beta_k.

Read this as: “average, over all n patients, of each patient’s own slope.” It is the mean of the individual effects — “if every patient in the sample got one more visit, the average increase in the probability of hitting target.”

The Marginal Effect at the Mean (MEM) instead builds one synthetic average patient and evaluates the slope there:

\text{MEM}_k \;=\; f(\bar{x}'\hat\beta)\,\hat\beta_k.

Read this as: “the slope at the single hypothetical patient whose covariates equal the sample means.” It describes a patient who may not exist (e.g., 0.6 of a comorbidity).

Prefer the AME. The AME averages a genuine quantity — each real patient’s own effect — so it answers the policy question “what happens on average if I nudge x for everyone?” The MEM evaluates the effect at an average patient who is often a fiction (half a diagnosis, 0.3 of a region). With dummy regressors the MEM is especially odd: the “mean patient” is 73\% female. The two coincide only when f is roughly linear over the range of the data; they diverge when patients sit in the curve’s flat tails. Modern packages (marginaleffects, margins, get_margeff) default to the AME for exactly this reason.

Partial Effects for Dummy Regressors

The derivative formula assumes x_k can change by an infinitesimal amount. A dummy regressor — say \text{kvinna}_i = 1 for female patients — cannot. It only takes the values 0 and 1, so its effect is not a slope but a discrete difference in predicted probability between the two states, holding everything else fixed.

The partial effect of a dummy d is the difference in predicted probability when d is switched from 0 to 1, with all other covariates x held at fixed values:

\Delta_d \;=\; F\!\big(\beta_0 + \beta_d \cdot 1 + x'\beta\big) \;-\; F\!\big(\beta_0 + \beta_d \cdot 0 + x'\beta\big),

where F = \Lambda (logit) or \Phi (probit). Read this as: “the predicted probability with the dummy on, minus the predicted probability with it off.” Averaging \Delta_d over the sample gives the average discrete change — the dummy analogue of the AME.

For example, if switching \text{kvinna} from 0 to 1 (other covariates at their observed values, averaged) raises the predicted probability of hitting target from 0.61 to 0.68, the reportable partial effect is +0.07 — “women in this program are 7 percentage points more likely to reach target, holding visits and age fixed.” That is a sentence a regional board understands; the raw coefficient \beta_d on the log-odds scale is not.

Odds Ratios: The Logit-Specific Shortcut

Logit has one extra interpretive trick that probit lacks. Exponentiating a logit coefficient gives an odds ratio, which is constant across patients — it does not depend on where you sit on the curve. The price is that it speaks the language of odds, not probabilities.

Recall the odds of success are \text{odds} = p/(1-p). The logit model is linear in the log-odds:

\log\frac{P(y=1\mid x)}{P(y=0\mid x)} \;=\; x'\beta.

Read this as: “the log of the odds of success equals the linear index x'\beta.” Increase x_k by one unit and the log-odds rise by exactly \beta_k — for every patient, because this scale is linear. Exponentiating, the odds get multiplied by e^{\beta_k}.

The odds ratio for a one-unit increase in x_k is

\text{OR}_k \;=\; e^{\beta_k}.

Read this as: “e raised to the coefficient.” It is the multiplicative factor by which the odds of success change per one-unit increase in x_k. \text{OR} > 1 means the odds rise, \text{OR} < 1 means they fall, \text{OR} = 1 means no effect. For our visits coefficient, \text{OR} = e^{0.42} \approx 1.52: each extra district-nurse visit multiplies the odds of hitting target by about 1.5 — a 52\% increase in the odds (not the probability).

An odds ratio is not a risk ratio, and not a probability change. “52\% higher odds” is routinely misread as “52\% more likely.” When the baseline probability is small the two are close, but for a patient already at p = 0.8 (odds 4{:}1), multiplying the odds by 1.52 gives odds 6.08{:}1, i.e. p = 0.859 — a rise of only 5.9 percentage points, nowhere near 52\%. The odds ratio’s virtue (constant across patients) is also its limitation: a constant odds ratio corresponds to a probability effect that still depends on the baseline. Report odds ratios for compactness, but report a marginal effect (AME) when you want the answer on the probability scale your audience thinks in.

Math Details: Deriving the marginal effects and the AME/MEM distinction

Marginal effect by the chain rule

For an index model P(y=1\mid x) = F(x'\beta) with a continuous regressor x_k, differentiate with respect to x_k. Only the index x'\beta depends on x_k, and \partial(x'\beta)/\partial x_k = \beta_k, so by the chain rule

\frac{\partial F(x'\beta)}{\partial x_k} = F'(x'\beta)\cdot \frac{\partial (x'\beta)}{\partial x_k} = f(x'\beta)\,\beta_k,

where f = F' is the density. Two specializations:

Probit. F = \Phi, so f = \phi, the standard Normal density. The marginal effect is \text{ME}_k^{\text{probit}} = \phi(x'\beta)\,\beta_k.

Logit. F = \Lambda, the logistic CDF. Its derivative is \Lambda'(z) = \frac{e^{-z}}{(1+e^{-z})^2} = \Lambda(z)\big(1 - \Lambda(z)\big), so writing \Lambda = \Lambda(x'\beta), \text{ME}_k^{\text{logit}} = \Lambda\,(1-\Lambda)\,\beta_k.

The factor \Lambda(1-\Lambda) is the variance of a Bernoulli with success probability \Lambda. It is maximized at \Lambda = 1/2, where it equals 1/4; hence the largest possible logit marginal effect of regressor k is \tfrac14\beta_k. It vanishes as \Lambda \to 0 or \Lambda \to 1 — the flat tails of the S-curve.

Why the odds ratio is constant

From \Lambda(z) = 1/(1+e^{-z}) we get 1-\Lambda(z) = e^{-z}/(1+e^{-z}), so the odds are \frac{\Lambda(z)}{1-\Lambda(z)} = \frac{1}{e^{-z}} = e^{z} = e^{x'\beta}. Increase x_k by 1: the odds become e^{x'\beta + \beta_k} = e^{x'\beta}\cdot e^{\beta_k}. The multiplicative factor e^{\beta_k} is free of x — this is special to the logistic link and is why odds ratios are a logit-only convenience. The corresponding probability change, \Lambda(x'\beta+\beta_k) - \Lambda(x'\beta), still depends on the baseline x'\beta.

AME vs MEM as two functionals of the same fit

Both summaries push the per-observation marginal effect m_i = f(x_i'\hat\beta)\,\hat\beta_k through a different operator:

\text{AME}_k = \frac1n\sum_i f(x_i'\hat\beta)\,\hat\beta_k = \frac1n\sum_i m_i, \qquad \text{MEM}_k = f\!\Big(\Big(\tfrac1n\sum_i x_i\Big)'\hat\beta\Big)\,\hat\beta_k.

The AME applies f first and averages second; the MEM averages the covariates first and applies f once. Because f (the Normal or logistic density) is nonlinear — concave near its peak, convex in the tails — Jensen’s inequality says \frac1n\sum_i f(x_i'\hat\beta) \neq f(\bar x'\hat\beta) in general, so \text{AME} \neq \text{MEM}. They agree exactly only if f is linear over the support of the data, or if all x_i are identical. Standard errors for both come from the delta method applied to the (smooth) functions above, using the MLE variance of \hat\beta.

# install.packages(c("marginaleffects"))  # if needed
library(marginaleffects)

set.seed(2024)
n <- 600
visits <- pmax(0, round(rnorm(n, 4.6, 2.3)))     # district-nurse follow-ups
age    <- round(rnorm(n, 64, 11))                 # patient age, years
kvinna <- rbinom(n, 1, 0.55)                      # 1 = female

# True data-generating index, then a Bernoulli draw for the target outcome.
idx <- -1.9 + 0.42 * visits - 0.015 * (age - 64) + 0.30 * kvinna
y   <- rbinom(n, 1, plogis(idx))                  # 1 = reached HbA1c target

df  <- data.frame(y, visits, age, kvinna)
fit <- glm(y ~ visits + age + kvinna, family = binomial(link = "logit"), data = df)

# Raw coefficients are on the LOG-ODDS scale — NOT effects on probability.
coef(fit)

# Odds ratios = exp(beta), with CIs. Logit-specific, constant across patients.
exp(cbind(OR = coef(fit), confint(fit)))

# Average Marginal Effect (recommended default): each patient's own slope,
# averaged. "dydx" for continuous regressors; for the dummy it reports the
# average discrete 0 -> 1 change automatically.
avg_slopes(fit)

# Marginal Effect at the Mean: evaluate the slope at the average covariate vector.
slopes(fit, newdata = "mean")

# Average partial effect of the female dummy as an explicit contrast.
avg_comparisons(fit, variables = "kvinna")
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

rng = np.random.default_rng(2024)
n = 600
visits = np.clip(np.round(rng.normal(4.6, 2.3, n)), 0, None)  # follow-ups
age    = np.round(rng.normal(64, 11, n))                       # age, years
kvinna = rng.binomial(1, 0.55, n)                              # 1 = female

# True index, then a Bernoulli draw for "reached HbA1c target".
idx = -1.9 + 0.42 * visits - 0.015 * (age - 64) + 0.30 * kvinna
p   = 1 / (1 + np.exp(-idx))
y   = rng.binomial(1, p)

df  = pd.DataFrame({"y": y, "visits": visits, "age": age, "kvinna": kvinna})
fit = smf.logit("y ~ visits + age + kvinna", data=df).fit()

# Raw coefficients are LOG-ODDS, not probability effects.
print(fit.params)

# Odds ratios = exp(beta). Logit-specific and constant across patients.
print(np.exp(fit.params))

# Average Marginal Effect (default): each obs's own slope, then averaged.
# 'dydx' for continuous; the dummy gets an average discrete change.
print(fit.get_margeff(at="overall", method="dydx").summary())

# Marginal Effect at the Mean: evaluate at the average covariate vector.
print(fit.get_margeff(at="mean", method="dydx").summary())

In both languages the AME for visits lands near +0.08 — “one extra district-nurse visit raises the probability of hitting target by about 8 percentage points on average” — while the odds ratio is e^{0.42}\approx 1.52. The numbers agree across R and Python up to the random draw; the AME and odds ratio describe the same coefficient on two different scales.

Exercises

Exercise 1

A logit for “patient readmitted to hospital within 30 days” includes a continuous regressor daily medications with coefficient \hat\beta = 0.25.

  1. What is the largest possible marginal effect of one extra daily medication on the probability of readmission? (b) For a patient currently at predicted probability p = 0.10, what is the marginal effect there? (c) Explain why (a) and (b) differ.
Show answer

(a) The logit marginal effect is \Lambda(1-\Lambda)\,\hat\beta, maximized at \Lambda = 0.5 where \Lambda(1-\Lambda) = 0.25. So the largest possible effect is 0.25 \times 0.25 = 0.0625, i.e. about +6.25 percentage points per extra medication — attained only for a patient at p = 0.5.

(b) At p = 0.10, \Lambda(1-\Lambda) = 0.10 \times 0.90 = 0.09. The marginal effect is 0.09 \times 0.25 = 0.0225, about +2.25 percentage points.

(c) They differ because the marginal effect is the slope of the S-curve, which depends on where the patient sits. At p = 0.5 the curve is steepest; at p = 0.10 it is in the lower tail and much flatter, so the same coefficient buys far less probability. The coefficient is constant; the probability effect is not.

Exercise 2

A logit for “reached HbA1c target” reports an odds ratio of 1.80 for being enrolled in a structured Diabetessamordning (diabetes coordination program).

  1. State the effect in words on the odds scale. (b) A patient under standard care has probability 0.40 of hitting target. What is their probability if enrolled, according to this odds ratio? (c) What is the percentage-point change, and why is it not 80\%?
Show answer

(a) Enrolling in the program multiplies a patient’s odds of reaching target by 1.80 — an 80\% increase in the odds, holding other covariates fixed.

(b) Baseline odds at p = 0.40: 0.40/0.60 = 0.6\overline{6}. Multiply by 1.80: new odds = 1.20. Convert back: p = \text{odds}/(1+\text{odds}) = 1.20/2.20 = 0.5\overline{45} \approx 0.545.

(c) The probability rises from 0.40 to about 0.545 — a change of roughly +14.5 percentage points, not 80\%. The 80\% refers to odds, a ratio of probabilities, not the probability itself. The percentage-point change depends on the baseline; at a different starting probability the same odds ratio gives a different probability change. This is exactly why an AME is more transparent to a non-technical audience.

Exercise 3

A colleague reports the MEM for follow-up visits and you report the AME from the same fitted logit. Yours is +0.081; theirs is +0.103.

  1. Define each quantity in one sentence. (b) Give a concrete reason they differ. (c) Which would you put in a policy brief, and why?
Show answer

(a) The AME averages each patient’s own marginal effect f(x_i'\hat\beta)\hat\beta_k over the sample; the MEM evaluates a single marginal effect f(\bar x'\hat\beta)\hat\beta_k at the average covariate vector.

(b) The density factor f is nonlinear, so by Jensen’s inequality the average of the slopes (\text{AME}) generally differs from the slope at the average (\text{MEM}). Here the MEM is larger, which happens when the average patient sits closer to the steep middle of the curve (p \approx 0.5) than the bulk of the actual patients, many of whom lie in the flatter tails where their individual slopes are smaller — dragging the average (AME) down.

(c) The AME, for a policy brief. It is the average of effects on real patients and answers “if every patient got one more visit, how much does the average probability rise?” The MEM describes a hypothetical average patient (possibly 0.55 female, 4.6 visits) who may not exist, and is harder to defend to a non-technical audience.

Exercise 4

Using the interactive at the top of the page, set the patient to x = 4.5 visits (near the 50% crossover) and note the slope; then drag to x = 11 visits.

  1. What happens to the marginal effect (the tangent slope), and why? (b) The fitted \hat\beta_1 = 0.42 never changes as you drag — so what does change to make the probability effect shrink? (c) Convert \hat\beta_1 to an odds ratio and confirm it is the same regardless of where the patient sits.
Show answer

(a) At x = 4.5 the patient is near p = 0.5, where the curve is steepest, so the tangent slope (marginal effect) is near its maximum, 0.25 \times 0.42 \approx 0.105. At x = 11 the patient is far up the curve near p \approx 0.95, in the flat upper tail, so the slope is small — an extra visit barely raises an already-near-certain probability.

(b) The coefficient \hat\beta_1 is fixed; what changes is the density factor \Lambda(x'\hat\beta)\big(1-\Lambda(x'\hat\beta)\big). It is \approx 0.25 at p = 0.5 but collapses toward 0 in the tails (at p = 0.95 it is 0.95 \times 0.05 \approx 0.0475). The marginal effect is this factor times \hat\beta_1, so it shrinks even though \hat\beta_1 is constant.

(c) The odds ratio is e^{0.42} \approx 1.52. Because the logit is linear in the log-odds, a one-unit increase in x multiplies the odds by e^{\hat\beta_1} regardless of the starting point — the odds ratio is constant across patients even though the probability effect (part b) is not.

Key takeaways

  • A logit or probit coefficient lives on the latent index / log-odds scale; it sets direction and a ceiling, but is not the effect on the probability of the outcome.
  • The marginal effect of a continuous regressor is f(x'\hat\beta)\,\hat\beta_k — the slope of the S-curve, largest near p = 0.5 and vanishing in the tails, so every patient has their own.
  • Report the AME (average each patient’s own slope) over the MEM (slope at the average patient): the AME averages effects on real units, the MEM describes a possibly-fictional average patient.
  • For a dummy regressor the partial effect is a discrete difference in predicted probability (0 vs 1), averaged over the sample.
  • The odds ratio e^{\hat\beta_k} is a logit-only convenience: constant across patients on the odds scale, but “higher odds” is not “higher probability” — use the AME when your audience thinks in probabilities.

What Comes Next

We can now turn any logit or probit fit into a reportable effect: a marginal effect on the probability scale, an AME for the headline number, a partial effect for dummies, and an odds ratio for compact summaries. So far the outcome has been binary — one threshold, target reached or not. But many primary-care outcomes have more than two categories: which of several vårdcentraler a patient chooses, or an ordered severity scale from “well controlled” to “poorly controlled.” The same maximum-likelihood machinery and the same “coefficients are not effects” warning carry over, but the marginal effects now come as a vector across categories.
