Binary Outcomes: LPM, Logit, and Probit

Modeling a yes/no outcome

In this section, you will learn to

  • Set up a regression for a 0/1 outcome and explain why the conditional mean E[y \mid x] is the probability P(y=1\mid x)
  • Fit and read the linear probability model (LPM), reading each slope directly as a change in probability — and diagnose its two structural flaws: out-of-range fitted probabilities and built-in heteroskedasticity
  • Derive logit and probit from a latent-variable / random-utility model, y^\* = x'\beta + \varepsilon with y = \mathbf{1}\{y^\* > 0\}
  • Map a linear index through the logistic or standard-normal CDF onto an S-curve that is guaranteed to stay in [0,1]
  • Write the logit log-likelihood and connect its estimation to the maximum likelihood machinery of Module 11

The Question Behind a Yes/No Outcome

A distriktssköterska (district nurse) at a vårdcentral (primary care center) in Region Uppsala manages a panel of Type 2 diabetes patients. For the regional quality register, each patient is scored on one binary indicator: did they reach the HbA1c target of 52 mmol/mol at their annual review? The answer is yes (y_i = 1) or no (y_i = 0) — there is no “partly.”

The verksamhetschef (clinic manager) wants to know how that yes/no outcome depends on nurse staffing — the number of diabetes-nurse full-time equivalents (FTE) per 1,000 enrolled patients — after adjusting for patient age. Better-staffed centers presumably reach target more often, but by how much, and on what scale? “Each extra nurse raises the chance of hitting target by 8 percentage points” is a very different statement from “each extra nurse multiplies the odds of hitting target by 1.6.”

Ordinary regression was built for a continuous outcome that can wander anywhere on the real line; a probability cannot — it is trapped between 0 and 1. This page develops three ways to regress a binary outcome — the linear probability model, logit, and probit — and shows exactly where the simplest one breaks and how the curved models repair it.

The Outcome Is a Probability

Start from a fact that makes the whole enterprise possible. When y_i \in \{0,1\}, its conditional expectation is a conditional probability:

E[\,y_i \mid x_i\,] \;=\; 1\cdot P(y_i = 1\mid x_i) + 0\cdot P(y_i = 0\mid x_i) \;=\; P(y_i = 1 \mid x_i).

Read this as: “the expected value of y given x equals one times the probability that y=1, plus zero times the probability that y=0, which collapses to just the probability that y=1.” So any model for the mean of a 0/1 variable is automatically a model for the probability that it equals one. The entire question becomes: what functional form should P(y_i=1\mid x_i) take?

For a binary outcome y_i \in \{0,1\}, define the conditional response probability

p(x_i) \;=\; P(y_i = 1 \mid x_i) \;=\; E[\,y_i \mid x_i\,].

A binary-outcome model specifies a functional form p(x_i) = G(x_i'\beta) for some function G mapping the linear index \eta_i = x_i'\beta into [0,1]. The three models on this page differ only in the choice of G:

  • LPM: G(\eta) = \eta (the identity — a straight line).
  • Logit: G(\eta) = \Lambda(\eta) = \dfrac{e^{\eta}}{1+e^{\eta}} (the logistic CDF).
  • Probit: G(\eta) = \Phi(\eta) (the standard-normal CDF).

The Linear Probability Model

The most direct idea is to ignore the [0,1] constraint and run OLS straight onto the 0/1 outcome:

y_i \;=\; \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_{i1} + \dots + \beta_k x_{ik} + u_i, \qquad P(y_i = 1\mid x_i) \;=\; \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_{i1} + \dots + \beta_k x_{ik}.

This is the linear probability model (LPM). Its great virtue is interpretability. Because the right-hand side is the probability, each slope is a change in probability per unit of the regressor, read off directly with no transformation:

\frac{\partial P(y_i = 1\mid x_i)}{\partial x_{ij}} \;=\; \beta_j .

Read this as: “the partial derivative of the probability of success with respect to regressor j equals \beta_j” — a constant marginal effect, the same for every patient regardless of their other characteristics. If the staffing slope is \hat\beta_1 = 0.11, then one more diabetes-nurse FTE per 1,000 patients is associated with an estimated 11-percentage-point rise in the probability of reaching target, period.

Why practitioners still reach for the LPM. Its coefficients are probabilities, so they need no exponentiating, no CDF, no “marginal effect at the mean.” For describing average associations, for interaction terms, and inside fixed-effects or instrumental-variables designs — where the curved models are awkward — a robust-standard-error LPM is often the workhorse. It is a genuinely useful model, provided you know its two failure modes.

Flaw 1: Predictions escape [0,1]

A straight line, extended far enough, leaves the unit interval. With \hat p(x) = 0.18 + 0.11\,x where x is nurse FTE per 1,000 patients, the fitted probability hits 1 at x = (1-0.18)/0.11 \approx 7.45 and keeps climbing: a center with x=8 gets a predicted probability of 0.18 + 0.11(8) = 1.06. A probability of 1.06 is meaningless. In the other direction the line dips below 0 for small or negative indices. The LPM cannot promise a legitimate probability for every patient.

Flaw 2: The errors are inherently heteroskedastic

Even where it stays in range, the LPM violates the homoskedasticity assumption by construction. For a Bernoulli outcome with success probability p(x_i), the conditional variance of the error is

\operatorname{Var}(u_i \mid x_i) \;=\; p(x_i)\,\big(1 - p(x_i)\big),

which depends on x_i through p(x_i). Read this as: “the error variance equals p times (1-p)” — largest at p = 0.5 (variance 0.25) and shrinking toward 0 as the probability approaches either 0 or 1. Because the variance changes with the regressors, the textbook OLS standard errors are wrong. The fix is routine — heteroskedasticity-robust (White) standard errors — but it is not optional, and it does nothing about Flaw 1.

Two LPM habits to keep. (1) Always report robust standard errors; the default OLS errors are biased because \operatorname{Var}(u_i\mid x_i)=p(x_i)(1-p(x_i)) is non-constant. (2) Always inspect the fitted values: if a noticeable share fall outside [0,1], the linear approximation is failing in the region you care about, and a logit or probit is the honest choice.

A Curve That Cannot Escape: The Latent-Variable Story

To keep the probability inside [0,1] we need an S-shaped curve: nearly flat near 0 for very negative indices, nearly flat near 1 for very positive indices, and steepest in the middle. Both logit and probit deliver such a curve, and both fall out of the same compelling derivation — a latent variable (equivalently, a random-utility) model.

Imagine that behind the observed yes/no lies an unobserved continuous “propensity to reach target,” y_i^\* — think of it as the patient’s underlying metabolic control net of everything the clinic does. It is linear in the regressors plus noise:

y_i^\* \;=\; x_i'\beta + \varepsilon_i .

We never see y_i^\*. We see only whether it crosses a threshold (normalized to 0):

y_i \;=\; \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } y_i^\* > 0,\\[2pt] 0 & \text{if } y_i^\* \le 0. \end{cases}

Read this as: “we observe y_i = 1 exactly when the latent propensity y_i^\* rises above zero, and y_i = 0 otherwise.” Now translate the observation rule into a probability:

P(y_i = 1 \mid x_i) = P(y_i^\* > 0 \mid x_i) = P(\varepsilon_i > -x_i'\beta) = P(\varepsilon_i \le x_i'\beta) = F(x_i'\beta),

where the last step uses the symmetry of \varepsilon around zero and writes F for the CDF of the error. The shape of the S-curve is just the CDF of the latent error. Two natural choices for that error distribution give the two classic models.

With latent error \varepsilon_i symmetric about 0 and CDF F, the response probability is p(x_i) = F(x_i'\beta).

  • Logit assumes \varepsilon_i follows the standard logistic distribution, so F = \Lambda: p(x_i) \;=\; \Lambda(x_i'\beta) \;=\; \frac{e^{\,x_i'\beta}}{1 + e^{\,x_i'\beta}} \;=\; \frac{1}{1 + e^{-x_i'\beta}}.

  • Probit assumes \varepsilon_i follows the standard normal distribution, so F = \Phi: p(x_i) \;=\; \Phi(x_i'\beta) \;=\; \int_{-\infty}^{x_i'\beta} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\,e^{-t^2/2}\,dt .

Both \Lambda and \Phi are CDFs, so both return a value in [0,1] for any index x_i'\beta — the out-of-range problem is gone by design.

The logit form has an extra gift: its log-odds are linear. Rearranging p = \Lambda(\eta) gives

\log\!\frac{p(x_i)}{1 - p(x_i)} \;=\; x_i'\beta .

Read this as: “the log of the odds of success is linear in the regressors.” A unit increase in x_{ij} adds \beta_j to the log-odds, which means it multiplies the odds by e^{\beta_j} — the odds ratio. This is why logit coefficients are so often reported as odds ratios; probit has no comparably clean closed form, which (along with heavier tails for the logistic) is the main reason logit dominates in practice.

Logit vs. probit: a distinction without much difference. The logistic distribution has fatter tails and a larger variance (\pi^2/3) than the standard normal (variance 1), so logit coefficients run about 1.6–1.8 times the corresponding probit coefficients. But the two fitted probability curves are visually almost identical across the bulk of the data, and they yield nearly the same marginal effects and predictions. Choose logit for the odds-ratio interpretation; choose probit when a normal latent error is natural (e.g. selection models). The substantive conclusions rarely hinge on which you pick.

See It: One Outcome, Three Curves

The interactive below puts all three models on the same picture. The dots are 400 patients: each sits at its nurse-staffing level on the x-axis and at its outcome (1 = reached target, on top; 0 = missed, on the bottom). The slate straight line is the LPM; the Uppsala-red S-curve is the logistic fit. Drag the LPM’s intercept and slope and watch the straight line march out of the legal [0,1] band (its illegal stretches turn bright red) while the logistic curve, anchored to the same data, stays politely inside.

// Logistic CDF helper used by the data-generating process and both curves.
bclpm_Lam = z => 1 / (1 + Math.exp(-z))
// ── Synthetic patient panel. x = diabetes-nurse FTE per 1000 patients, U(0.5,6).
// The true DGP is itself a logit, so the fitted logit recovers a sensible curve
// and the LPM is a visibly imperfect straight-line proxy. Reproducible via LCG.
bclpm_data = {
  const rng = d3.randomLcg(42);            // reproducible uniform stream
  const n = 400;
  const b0_true = -1.6, b1_true = 0.62;    // latent logit truth
  const out = [];
  for (let i = 0; i < n; i++) {
    const x = 0.5 + 5.5 * rng();           // staffing 0.5 .. 6.0
    const p = bclpm_Lam(b0_true + b1_true * x);
    const y = rng() < p ? 1 : 0;
    out.push({ x, y });
  }
  return out;
}
// ── OLS fit of the LPM (closed form: simple regression of y on x). ──
bclpm_olsFit = {
  const d = bclpm_data;
  const n = d.length;
  const mx = d3.mean(d, p => p.x);
  const my = d3.mean(d, p => p.y);
  let sxx = 0, sxy = 0;
  for (const p of d) { sxx += (p.x - mx) ** 2; sxy += (p.x - mx) * (p.y - my); }
  const b1 = sxy / sxx;
  const b0 = my - b1 * mx;
  return ({ b0, b1 });
}
// ── MLE fit of the logit by Newton–Raphson; converges in a few steps. ──
bclpm_logitFit = {
  const d = bclpm_data;
  let b0 = 0, b1 = 0;
  for (let iter = 0; iter < 60; iter++) {
    let g0 = 0, g1 = 0, h00 = 0, h01 = 0, h11 = 0;
    for (const pt of d) {
      const eta = b0 + b1 * pt.x;
      const p = bclpm_Lam(eta);
      const w = p * (1 - p);
      const r = pt.y - p;
      g0 += r;          g1 += r * pt.x;
      h00 += w;         h01 += w * pt.x;   h11 += w * pt.x * pt.x;
    }
    const det = h00 * h11 - h01 * h01;
    if (Math.abs(det) < 1e-10) break;
    const d0 = (h11 * g0 - h01 * g1) / det;
    const d1 = (-h01 * g0 + h00 * g1) / det;
    b0 += d0; b1 += d1;
    if (Math.abs(d0) + Math.abs(d1) < 1e-9) break;
  }
  return ({ b0, b1 });
}
bclpm_grid = d3.range(0, 6.51, 0.05)

// The reader's LPM line, split into "legal" (inside [0,1]) and "illegal"

// Bin the raw 0/1 data into staffing deciles and plot the empirical share that
// reached target in each bin — the "truth" the curves are trying to trace.
bclpm_bins = {
  const d = bclpm_data;
  const nbins = 8;
  const xs = d.map(p => p.x);
  const lo = d3.min(xs), hi = d3.max(xs);
  const w = (hi - lo) / nbins;
  const out = [];
  for (let b = 0; b < nbins; b++) {
    const a = lo + b * w, z = (b === nbins - 1) ? hi + 1e-9 : lo + (b + 1) * w;
    const grp = d.filter(p => p.x >= a && p.x < z);
    if (grp.length > 0) out.push({ x: (a + z) / 2, p: d3.mean(grp, p => p.y), n: grp.length });
  }
  return out;
}

How to Read the Three-Curve Chart

Work through these in order; each isolates one idea.

Step 1: Read the cloud of dots. Faint grey dots are the 400 patients — the top band is “reached target” (y=1), the bottom band is “missed” (y=0). More dots sit in the top band as staffing rises to the right. The ochre dots are the empirical share on target within each staffing bin: they trace the truth the models are chasing, and they already curve.

Step 2: Watch the LPM leave the legal band. The green dashed lines at 0 and 1 mark the only legal values a probability can take; the faint green shading is the legal zone. Drag the slope up: the slate LPM line tilts, and the moment any stretch pokes above 1 or below 0 it switches to alarm red. The “Your LPM out of [0,1]” tile counts how much of the plotted range is illegal. There is no setting of a straight line that fits a steep cloud and stays legal everywhere.

Step 3: Watch the S-curve stay home. The bold red logistic curve uses your intercept and slope, but pushed through the logistic CDF. No matter how hard you drag the sliders, it flattens out smoothly toward 0 on the left and 1 on the right — it physically cannot leave the band. That is the latent-variable derivation paying off.

Step 4: Compare the two fits to the truth. Toggle Show OLS-fitted LPM line and Show MLE-fitted logit S-curve. The thin dashed slate line is the best straight line OLS can draw; the thin dashed red curve is the maximum-likelihood logit. Notice the logit hugs the ochre empirical dots through the curved middle and tails, while the straight line can only split the difference.

Step 5: Read the coefficients. The tiles report the OLS-fitted (\beta_0,\beta_1) in probability units, the logit-fitted (\beta_0,\beta_1) in log-odds units, and the implied odds ratio e^{\beta_1}. These scales are not comparable directly — converting a logit back to a probability change is the subject of the next page.

Estimation: Maximum Likelihood

The LPM is fit by OLS. Logit and probit have no closed-form estimator; they are fit by maximum likelihood, the machinery built in Module 11. Because each y_i is a Bernoulli draw with success probability p_i = G(x_i'\beta), the contribution of patient i to the likelihood is p_i^{\,y_i}(1-p_i)^{1-y_i}, and the log-likelihood over the sample is

\ell(\beta) \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{n}\Big[\, y_i \log G(x_i'\beta) + (1 - y_i)\log\big(1 - G(x_i'\beta)\big)\,\Big].

Read this as: “sum over patients of: if they reached target, the log of the model’s predicted probability; if they did not, the log of one minus that probability.” Each success rewards parameters that push G(x_i'\beta) up; each failure rewards parameters that push it down. The MLE \hat\beta is the value that balances all n of these pulls — the argmax of \ell, found by setting the score to zero exactly as in Module 11. For logit, G=\Lambda makes the score especially clean; the Math Details carry the derivation through.

What logit/probit by MLE assume:

  1. Correct link and index. The probability really is G(x_i'\beta) with the chosen CDF G and a linear index. A badly wrong link or omitted nonlinearity biases \hat\beta.
  2. Correct latent-error distribution. Logit assumes logistic errors, probit assumes normal — and (unlike linear regression) the error variance is not separately estimated; it is normalized to fix the scale of \beta. Get the family badly wrong and the coefficients are distorted.
  3. Independent observations. The likelihood factorizes across patients. If outcomes cluster by läkare (physician) or vårdcentral, use cluster-robust standard errors.

logit  <- glm(y ~ x, data = dat, family = binomial(link = "logit"))
probit <- glm(y ~ x, data = dat, family = binomial(link = "probit"))

summary(logit)$coefficients
summary(probit)$coefficients

Math Details: the latent-variable derivation and the logit log-likelihood

From latent variable to S-curve

Posit the latent regression y_i^\* = x_i'\beta + \varepsilon_i with \varepsilon_i independent of x_i, symmetric about 0, and CDF F. We observe y_i = \mathbf{1}\{y_i^\* > 0\}. Then

\begin{aligned} P(y_i = 1 \mid x_i) &= P(x_i'\beta + \varepsilon_i > 0 \mid x_i)\\ &= P(\varepsilon_i > -x_i'\beta \mid x_i)\\ &= 1 - F(-x_i'\beta)\\ &= F(x_i'\beta), \end{aligned}

where the last line uses symmetry, 1 - F(-a) = F(a). Choosing F = \Lambda (logistic) gives logit; F = \Phi (standard normal) gives probit.

Why the scale of \beta is only identified up to the error variance. Multiply the latent equation by any constant c>0: c\,y_i^\* = x_i'(c\beta) + c\varepsilon_i. The sign of y_i^\* — and therefore y_i — is unchanged, so the data cannot distinguish (\beta, \operatorname{sd}(\varepsilon)) from (c\beta, c\operatorname{sd}(\varepsilon)). We must fix the scale by normalizing the error variance: probit sets \operatorname{Var}(\varepsilon)=1, logit sets it to \pi^2/3. This normalization is exactly why logit coefficients are about \pi/\sqrt{3}\approx 1.81 times probit coefficients in large samples, and why neither set of coefficients is a probability.

The logit log-likelihood and score

For logit, p_i = \Lambda(\eta_i) with \eta_i = x_i'\beta. A convenient identity is \log(1-\Lambda(\eta)) = -\eta + \log\Lambda(\eta) = -\log(1+e^{\eta}). The Bernoulli log-likelihood is

\ell(\beta) = \sum_{i=1}^n \Big[\, y_i \log \Lambda(\eta_i) + (1-y_i)\log\big(1-\Lambda(\eta_i)\big)\,\Big] = \sum_{i=1}^n \Big[\, y_i \eta_i - \log\!\big(1 + e^{\eta_i}\big)\,\Big].

Differentiate using the defining property of the logistic CDF, \Lambda'(\eta) = \Lambda(\eta)\big(1-\Lambda(\eta)\big). The score is remarkably clean:

\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \beta} = \sum_{i=1}^n \Big( y_i - \Lambda(\eta_i) \Big)\, x_i = \sum_{i=1}^n \big( y_i - p_i \big)\, x_i \;=\; 0 .

Read this as: “the MLE sets the sum of x_i weighted by the residual (y_i - \hat p_i) to zero.” The fitted probabilities make the prediction errors orthogonal to the regressors — the binary-outcome echo of the OLS normal equations. There is no closed-form solution, so we solve it numerically (Newton–Raphson / IRLS); the negative second derivative

-\frac{\partial^2 \ell}{\partial\beta\,\partial\beta'} = \sum_{i=1}^n p_i(1-p_i)\, x_i x_i'

is positive definite whenever the data are not perfectly separated, so \ell is globally concave and the MLE is unique. (Perfect separation — a regressor that predicts the outcome flawlessly — sends a coefficient to \pm\infty, the logit analogue of the boundary problem from Module 11.)

Where the LPM heteroskedasticity comes from

For the LPM, u_i = y_i - p(x_i) with y_i Bernoulli(p(x_i)), so \operatorname{Var}(u_i\mid x_i)=\operatorname{Var}(y_i\mid x_i)=p(x_i)\big(1-p(x_i)\big). This is a maximum at p=0.5 (value 0.25) and vanishes as p\to 0 or p\to 1 — it varies with x_i by construction, which is why robust standard errors are mandatory.

Exercises

Exercise 1

A vårdcentral fits an LPM for reaching the HbA1c target as a function of nurse staffing x (FTE per 1000 patients): \hat p(x) = 0.18 + 0.11\,x.

  1. Interpret the slope 0.11 in plain language. (b) What is the predicted probability at x = 2 and at x = 8? (c) What is structurally wrong with the prediction at x=8, and at what staffing level does the line first leave the legal range?
Show answer

(a) Each additional diabetes-nurse FTE per 1,000 patients is associated with a 0.11 = 11-percentage-point increase in the probability of reaching the HbA1c target — a constant effect, identical at every staffing level (that constancy is itself an LPM limitation).

(b) At x=2: \hat p = 0.18 + 0.11(2) = 0.40. At x=8: \hat p = 0.18 + 0.11(8) = 1.06.

(c) A predicted probability of 1.06 is impossible — probabilities cannot exceed 1. The line crosses 1 at x = (1 - 0.18)/0.11 \approx 7.45, so any vårdcentral staffed above about 7.5 nurse-FTE per 1,000 patients gets an illegal prediction. (It also dips below 0 for x < (0-0.18)/0.11 \approx -1.64, outside the data here.) A logit or probit would bend toward 1 instead and stay legal.

Exercise 2

Explain why the LPM error term is heteroskedastic by construction, derive the conditional error variance, and state at what success probability the variance is largest. What is the practical consequence for inference?

Show answer

The outcome y_i is Bernoulli with success probability p(x_i), so its variance is \operatorname{Var}(y_i\mid x_i)=p(x_i)\big(1-p(x_i)\big). The LPM error is u_i = y_i - p(x_i), which has the same conditional variance:

\operatorname{Var}(u_i \mid x_i) = p(x_i)\big(1 - p(x_i)\big).

This depends on x_i through p(x_i), so it cannot be constant unless p(x_i) is constant — i.e. heteroskedasticity is unavoidable. The variance is a downward parabola in p, maximized at p=0.5 (value 0.25) and falling to 0 at p=0 and p=1. For example p=0.1\Rightarrow 0.09, p=0.5\Rightarrow 0.25, p=0.9\Rightarrow 0.09.

Practical consequence: the default OLS standard errors are biased (usually too small), so t-statistics and p-values are unreliable. The remedy is heteroskedasticity-robust (White / HC1) standard errors, which the LPM should always report.

Exercise 3

A logit for reaching target gives the model \log\!\dfrac{p}{1-p} = -0.4 + 0.6931\,x, where x is nurse staffing.

  1. What is the odds ratio for a one-unit increase in staffing? (b) Compute the predicted probability at x=0 and at x=2. (c) The probability rose from x=0 to x=2; did the odds rise by the same multiplicative factor each step? Verify.
Show answer

(a) The odds ratio is e^{\beta_1} = e^{0.6931} = 2.00. Each extra nurse-FTE per 1,000 patients doubles the odds of reaching target.

(b) Index at x=0: \eta = -0.4, so p = \Lambda(-0.4) = 1/(1+e^{0.4}) = 0.4013. Index at x=2: \eta = -0.4 + 0.6931(2) = 0.9863, so p = \Lambda(0.9863) = 0.7284.

(c) Yes for the odds, no for the probability. Odds at x=0: 0.4013/(1-0.4013) = 0.670. Odds at x=1: e^{-0.4+0.6931} = e^{0.2931} = 1.341 = 0.670 \times 2. Odds at x=2: e^{0.9863} = 2.681 = 1.341 \times 2. The odds multiply by exactly 2 at each step — that is what “linear in log-odds” means. The probabilities (0.401 \to 0.573 \to 0.728) do not rise by a constant amount or factor: the probability change per step depends on where you are on the S-curve, which is precisely why a single logit coefficient is not a marginal effect.

Exercise 4

Four patients have staffing levels x = (1, 2, 3, 4) and outcomes y = (0, 0, 1, 1). For the candidate logit coefficients \beta_0 = -1, \beta_1 = 0.5, compute each patient’s predicted probability and the total log-likelihood \ell(\beta). Recall \Lambda(\eta) = 1/(1+e^{-\eta}).

Show answer

Compute the index \eta_i = -1 + 0.5\,x_i, the probability p_i = \Lambda(\eta_i), and each contribution \ell_i = y_i\log p_i + (1-y_i)\log(1-p_i):

x_i y_i \eta_i p_i contribution \ell_i
1 0 -0.5 0.3775 \log(1-0.3775) = -0.4741
2 0 0.0 0.5000 \log(0.5) = -0.6931
3 1 0.5 0.6225 \log(0.6225) = -0.4741
4 1 1.0 0.7311 \log(0.7311) = -0.3133

Summing the contributions:

\ell(\beta) = -0.4741 - 0.6931 - 0.4741 - 0.3133 = -1.9546 .

The total log-likelihood at these candidate coefficients is \approx -1.95. Maximum likelihood would search over (\beta_0,\beta_1) for the values that push this number as high (close to 0) as possible — exactly the score-equals-zero optimization from Module 11, here solved numerically because no closed form exists.

Key takeaways

  • For a 0/1 outcome the conditional mean is the response probability, E[y\mid x] = P(y=1\mid x), so every binary-outcome model is a model of a probability.
  • The LPM (OLS on y) gives coefficients that read directly as probability changes, but it produces fitted values outside [0,1] and has built-in heteroskedasticity \operatorname{Var}(u\mid x)=p(x)(1-p(x)) — always pair it with robust standard errors.
  • Logit and probit come from one latent-variable / random-utility model, y^\*=x'\beta+\varepsilon with y=\mathbf{1}\{y^\*>0\}; the response probability is the CDF of the latent error, P(y=1\mid x)=F(x'\beta), which keeps the S-curve inside [0,1] by design.
  • Logit uses the logistic CDF (log-odds linear in x, so e^{\beta_j} is an odds ratio); probit uses the normal CDF; logit coefficients run about 1.6–1.8 times probit, but fitted probabilities and conclusions barely differ.
  • Logit and probit are estimated by maximum likelihood (Module 11); the logit score \sum_i (y_i - \hat p_i)x_i = 0 mirrors the OLS normal equations, and the log-likelihood is globally concave absent perfect separation.
