Fixed Effects

Within-unit estimation and the demean operator

In this section, you will learn to

  • write the unobserved-effects model y_{it} = \alpha_i + \beta x_{it} + e_{it} and explain why fixed effects, unlike random effects, permits \operatorname{Cov}(\alpha_i, x_{it}) \neq 0
  • derive the pooled-OLS bias as omitted-variable bias in \alpha_i and explain why more data cannot fix it
  • apply the demean (within) operator to obtain the fixed-effects estimator and show that \alpha_i differences out
  • explain why the within estimator and the least-squares dummy variable (LSDV) estimator give identical \hat\beta via the Frisch–Waugh–Lovell theorem
  • distinguish what fixed effects can remove (time-invariant confounders) from what it cannot (time-varying confounding, reverse causality, time-invariant regressors), and extend to two-way fixed effects

The Confounder You Cannot Measure

Sweden’s 21 regioner (regions) each run their own network of vårdcentraler (primary care centers), and they differ from one another in ways that are stubbornly hard to quantify. Region Stockholm has dense urban clinics, a young commuting population, and deep tax revenues. Region Norrbotten covers a vast, sparsely populated north with long ambulance distances and an older population. Region Gotland is an island. These differences are real, they are persistent, and they shape health outcomes — but most of them never appear as a clean column in any dataset.

Now suppose a researcher wants to know whether hiring more distriktssköterskor (district nurses) per capita reduces the average number of days patients wait for a primary care appointment. We observe each of the 21 regions for several years: nurse staffing density on the right, waiting times on the left. The naive instinct is to pool all the region-year observations and run one big regression. But there is a trap. Regions that staff generously may also be the wealthy, well-organized regions that would have had short waiting times anyway, for reasons — administrative culture, capital stock, population health — we cannot measure. The staffing coefficient would then absorb all of that hidden regional advantage and overstate the true effect.

This page is about a remarkably clean solution to that problem. When the same units are observed repeatedly, we can let each unit be its own control. We never have to measure “administrative culture” or “capital stock” at all. We only have to assume that, whatever those things are, they stay roughly constant within a region over our study window. That single structural assumption — time-invariant unobserved heterogeneity — is what the fixed effects estimator exploits.

This is the omitted-variable-bias logic from Module 4, turned into an opportunity. There, the omitted variable was a measured-but-excluded covariate. Here, the omitted variable is an entire bundle of unmeasurable regional traits — and panel data let us sweep all of it away at once.

The Unobserved-Effects Model

Let y_{it} be the outcome (mean waiting time, in days) for region i in year t, and let x_{it} be the regressor of interest (district nurses per 10,000 residents). The unobserved-effects model is:

y_{it} = \alpha_i + \beta\, x_{it} + e_{it}

In the model y_{it} = \alpha_i + \beta x_{it} + e_{it}:

  • \alpha_i is the unit fixed effect — a region-specific intercept that is constant over time. It captures everything about region i that does not change across our years: geography, baseline population health, administrative tradition, capital stock. It is the unobserved heterogeneity.
  • \beta is the structural parameter we want: the effect of one more nurse per 10,000 residents on waiting time, holding the region fixed.
  • e_{it} is the idiosyncratic error — the part of region i’s waiting time in year t that is not explained by its fixed traits or by staffing. We assume E[e_{it} \mid x_{i1}, \dots, x_{iT}, \alpha_i] = 0 (strict exogeneity of the idiosyncratic shock — the shock in any year is unrelated to staffing in every year, past and future, not just the current one). This is stronger than ordinary exogeneity: it rules out feedback in which this year’s shock changes next year’s staffing, which is exactly why reverse causality (limitation 3 below) breaks fixed effects.

Read this as: “Region i’s waiting time in year t equals a region-specific baseline \alpha_i, plus \beta times its nurse density, plus an idiosyncratic shock.” The whole game is that \alpha_i carries no t subscript — it is the same in 2015 as in 2022.

The crucial subtlety is the relationship between \alpha_i and x_{it}. The fixed-effects model makes no assumption that they are uncorrelated. We explicitly allow

\operatorname{Cov}(\alpha_i,\, x_{it}) \neq 0,

which is exactly the realistic case: rich, well-run regions (high \alpha_i in the sense of better baseline systems) also tend to hire more nurses (high x_{it}). This is what distinguishes fixed effects from its more restrictive cousin, the random effects estimator, which assumes \operatorname{Cov}(\alpha_i, x_{it}) = 0. Because fixed effects permits arbitrary correlation between the unobserved trait and the regressor, it is the workhorse of credible panel analysis.

Why Pooled OLS Is Biased

Suppose we ignore the panel structure entirely and absorb \alpha_i into a single composite error. Define the composite error v_{it} = \alpha_i + e_{it} and run pooled OLS of y_{it} on x_{it}:

y_{it} = \beta\, x_{it} + \underbrace{(\alpha_i + e_{it})}_{v_{it}}.

For pooled OLS to be consistent, the regressor must be uncorrelated with the whole composite error. But x_{it} is correlated with \alpha_i by assumption, so \operatorname{Cov}(x_{it}, v_{it}) \neq 0. This is textbook omitted-variable bias, with the omitted variable being the unit effect \alpha_i itself:

\operatorname{plim}\ \hat\beta_{\text{pooled}} = \beta + \frac{\operatorname{Cov}(x_{it},\, \alpha_i)}{\operatorname{Var}(x_{it})}.

Here \operatorname{plim} is the probability limit — the single value the estimate settles on as the sample grows without bound (the large-sample analogue of an average). Read this as: “Pooled OLS converges not to \beta but to \beta plus a bias term equal to the covariance of the regressor with the unit effect, divided by the variance of the regressor.” If generous-staffing regions tend to be the high-baseline-quality regions, the bias term has a definite sign, and pooled OLS reports a contaminated number. The bias does not shrink as we add more region-years — it is a bias of inconsistency, not of small samples.

Adding more data does not rescue pooled OLS here. Throwing in another decade of region-years drives the estimate ever more precisely toward the wrong number \beta + \operatorname{Cov}(x,\alpha)/\operatorname{Var}(x). Precision is not accuracy. The problem is structural — the omitted \alpha_i — and it requires a structural fix, not a bigger sample.

The Within Transformation: Let Each Region Be Its Own Control

Here is the central idea, and it is beautiful. The thing that contaminates pooled OLS — the time-invariant \alpha_i — can be eliminated by a simple algebraic trick, precisely because it does not vary over time.

For each region i, average the model over its T time periods. Let an overbar denote a within-unit time average, \bar{z}_i = \frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T} z_{it}. Averaging y_{it} = \alpha_i + \beta x_{it} + e_{it} over t gives:

\bar{y}_i = \alpha_i + \beta\, \bar{x}_i + \bar{e}_i.

Note that the time-average of the constant \alpha_i is just \alpha_i again — a constant averaged over time does not move. Now subtract this unit-mean equation from the original equation, term by term:

\underbrace{(y_{it} - \bar{y}_i)}_{\tilde{y}_{it}} = \underbrace{(\alpha_i - \alpha_i)}_{=\;0} + \beta\,\underbrace{(x_{it} - \bar{x}_i)}_{\tilde{x}_{it}} + \underbrace{(e_{it} - \bar{e}_i)}_{\tilde{e}_{it}}.

The unit effect \alpha_i differences away completely. This is the magic. We are left with a regression in deviations from unit means:

\tilde{y}_{it} = \beta\, \tilde{x}_{it} + \tilde{e}_{it}.

The demean (within) operator M subtracts each unit’s own time-average from every observation:

\tilde{z}_{it} = z_{it} - \bar{z}_i, \qquad \bar{z}_i = \frac{1}{T}\sum_{s=1}^{T} z_{is}.

The within (fixed-effects) estimator is OLS applied to the demeaned data:

\hat\beta_{\text{FE}} = \frac{\sum_{i}\sum_{t} \tilde{x}_{it}\, \tilde{y}_{it}}{\sum_{i}\sum_{t} \tilde{x}_{it}^{2}} = \frac{\sum_{i}\sum_{t} (x_{it}-\bar{x}_i)(y_{it}-\bar{y}_i)}{\sum_{i}\sum_{t} (x_{it}-\bar{x}_i)^{2}}.

Read this as: “The fixed-effects slope is the ordinary least-squares slope you get after wiping out each region’s average level — it uses only the within-region, over-time co-movement of staffing and waiting times.” Because \alpha_i is gone, its correlation with x_{it} can no longer bias anything.

The interpretation is the heart of the method. \hat\beta_{\text{FE}} asks: when a single region staffs up relative to its own usual level, does its waiting time fall relative to its own usual level? It compares Stockholm-2022 to Stockholm-2016, not Stockholm to Norrbotten. Every fixed, between-region difference — the very stuff we could not measure — is held constant by construction, because we only ever look at movements around each region’s own mean.

Fixed effects converts an across-units comparison (which is confounded by everything that makes units differ) into a within-unit-over-time comparison (which automatically holds constant everything about a unit that does not change). You did not have to name the confounders, measure them, or even know they exist. You only had to assume they are time-invariant.

Before the multi-region simulation, it helps to watch demeaning act on a single region. The algebra “subtract the unit mean” is just a recentering: take one region’s cloud of points, find its average (\bar{x}_i, \bar{y}_i), and slide the whole cloud so that average sits on the origin. The shape of the cloud — its within-region slope — is untouched; only its position (which is what \alpha_i controls) changes.

viewof fe_reseed = Inputs.button("New random draw", { value: 0, reduce: v => v + 1 })
fe_trueBeta = -2.4          // one more nurse / 10k lowers waiting time by 2.4 days
fe_T = 6                    // years per region
fe_alphaSpread = 16         // spread of regional baselines (days) — large enough that strong confounding can flip the pooled slope positive
fe_xSpread = 2.2            // within-region spread of staffing
fe_noiseSD = 1.6            // idiosyncratic noise (days)
fe_xBaseMean = 7.0          // average nurse density (per 10k)
fe_yBaseMean = 22
// Simulate the panel. Reactive on the slider, region count, and reseed button.
fe_panel = {
  fe_reseed; // take a dependency so the button forces a fresh draw
  const rng = d3.randomNormal.source(Math.random)(0, 1);
  const rho = fe_corr;
  const nR = fe_nRegions;
  const rows = [];

  // One latent normal per region drives BOTH its baseline alpha and its
  // average staffing level, producing Corr(alpha, xbar) ≈ rho.
  for (let i = 0; i < nR; i++) {
    const z = rng();                       // shared latent for region i
    const indepA = rng();                  // independent component for alpha
    // alpha_i correlated with the latent z with correlation |rho|
    const alphaStd = rho * z + Math.sqrt(1 - rho * rho) * indepA;
    const alpha_i = fe_yBaseMean + fe_alphaSpread * alphaStd * 0.5;
    // region's average staffing also driven by the latent z
    const xbar_i = fe_xBaseMean + 2.0 * z;

    for (let t = 0; t < fe_T; t++) {
      // within-region year-to-year staffing variation around xbar_i
      const x = xbar_i + fe_xSpread * rng() * 0.6;
      const e = fe_noiseSD * rng();
      // structural model: same beta for every region
      const y = alpha_i + fe_trueBeta * (x - fe_xBaseMean) + e;
      rows.push({ region: i, t, x, y, alpha: alpha_i });
    }
  }
  return rows;
}
// Within-region means
fe_means = {
  const g = d3.group(fe_panel, d => d.region);
  const m = new Map();
  for (const [r, rows] of g) {
    m.set(r, { xbar: d3.mean(rows, d => d.x), ybar: d3.mean(rows, d => d.y) });
  }
  return m;
}
// Demeaned (within-transformed) data
fe_demeaned = fe_panel.map(d => {
  const mr = fe_means.get(d.region);
  return { ...d, xt: d.x - mr.xbar, yt: d.y - mr.ybar };
})
// OLS slope helper
fe_olsSlope = function(pts, xkey, ykey) {
  const xb = d3.mean(pts, d => d[xkey]);
  const yb = d3.mean(pts, d => d[ykey]);
  let num = 0, den = 0;
  for (const p of pts) {
    num += (p[xkey] - xb) * (p[ykey] - yb);
    den += (p[xkey] - xb) ** 2;
  }
  return den === 0 ? NaN : num / den;
}
fe_betaPooled = fe_olsSlope(fe_panel, "x", "y")
fe_betaWithin = fe_olsSlope(fe_demeaned, "xt", "yt")

How to Read This Visualization

Step 1: Start with no confounding. Drag the Confounding slider to 0. In the Raw data view, each colored cloud is one region’s six years of observations (hover any point for its region number). The thin colored lines are each region’s own within-region fit; the thick red line is pooled OLS run across all points at once. With zero confounding, the regional clouds are scattered at random heights, and the red pooled line lies close to the true downward slope. Compare the three cards: pooled OLS β̂ and fixed-effects β̂ are both near the true -2.4.

Step 2: Turn the confounding up. Now drag the slider toward +0.9. Watch the clouds rearrange: regions with high baseline waiting times (high clouds) are pulled toward high staffing (right side), and low-baseline regions slide left. The thick red pooled line rotates — it flattens, and at strong confounding can even tilt the wrong way (upward), because it is now tracing the between-region pattern (high-baseline regions hire more) instead of the causal within-region effect. The red card drifts away from -2.4 and may turn positive — the same sign flip you will compute by hand in Exercise 1.

Step 3: Confirm the within slopes never moved. Look at the thin colored per-region lines throughout Step 2. Each region’s own slope stays downward and parallel — the true effect of -2.4 is baked into every region. Only the arrangement of the clouds changed, not the slope inside any cloud. The pooled line is fooled by the arrangement; the within slopes are not.

Step 4: Switch to “After demeaning.” Flip the View toggle. Every region’s cloud is now recentered on the origin — we subtracted each region’s own (\bar{x}_i, \bar{y}_i). The vertical separation that encoded \alpha_i is gone; all the clouds collapse into one common scatter. The slate line is OLS on this pooled-and-demeaned cloud — the fixed-effects estimate. Notice it sits on the true -2.4 regardless of where you left the confounding slider. That is the whole point: demeaning erased \alpha_i, so its correlation with staffing can no longer bias the slope.

Step 5: Stress-test it. Click New random draw a few times and slide the confounding back and forth. The red (pooled) card jumps around with the confounding; the slate (fixed-effects) card stays anchored near -2.4 every time. Pooled OLS is inconsistent under confounding; fixed effects is consistent.

Equivalence to Least-Squares Dummy Variables (LSDV)

Demeaning looks like a clever shortcut. But there is a second, completely mechanical way to remove \alpha_i: just estimate it. Include a separate dummy variable for each region and run one big OLS:

y_{it} = \sum_{j=1}^{N} \alpha_j\, D_{ij} + \beta\, x_{it} + e_{it},

where D_{ij} = 1 if observation belongs to region j and 0 otherwise. This is the Least-Squares Dummy Variables (LSDV) estimator. The dummies soak up each region’s baseline level directly.

LSDV and the within estimator give numerically identical \hat\beta. Including a full set of unit dummies and demeaning the data are two routes to exactly the same slope coefficient. This is not a coincidence — it is the Frisch–Waugh–Lovell (FWL) theorem.

Recall FWL from Module 4: the coefficient on x in a multiple regression equals the coefficient from a simple regression of residualized y on residualized x, where “residualized” means we have partialled out the other regressors. In LSDV, the “other regressors” are the unit dummies. Regressing any variable on a full set of unit dummies and taking residuals is exactly subtracting each unit’s mean — because the OLS fit of a variable on group indicators is the group means. So:

\text{partial out unit dummies} \;\;\Longleftrightarrow\;\; \text{subtract unit means} \;\;\Longleftrightarrow\;\; \text{demean}.

The single least-obvious link in that chain is the first arrow: why is “regress on unit dummies and keep the residuals” the same as “subtract the unit mean”? The micro-example below makes it concrete. Regress a variable y on nothing but a region indicator. OLS has no choice: with only a group dummy to work with, the best-fitting prediction for every observation in a group is simply that group’s average. So the fitted value equals the group mean, and the residual equals y minus the group mean — which is exactly the demeaned variable \tilde{y}_{it}.

Regressing y on a region dummy returns each region’s mean as the fitted value (\bar{y}_A=28, \bar{y}_B=16); the residual column is therefore identical to the demeaned outcome. Do the same to x, regress residual-y on residual-x, and you have reproduced the within estimator — this is FWL in one table.
Region Year y_{it} Fitted \hat{y}=\bar{y}_i Residual y_{it}-\bar{y}_i=\tilde{y}_{it}
A 1 30 28 +2
A 2 26 28 -2
B 1 18 16 +2
B 2 14 16 -2

FWL guarantees the \hat\beta is identical either way. The practical lesson: with N large (thousands of vårdcentraler, say), you would never literally invert a matrix with thousands of dummy columns; software like fixest uses the demeaning route, which is computationally trivial. But conceptually, fixed effects = controlling for a dummy per unit.

There is one caveat about the standard errors: LSDV correctly counts the N estimated intercepts as parameters, costing N degrees of freedom. A naive demeaning routine that forgets this will report degrees of freedom that are too large and standard errors that are too small. Good panel software corrects for this automatically.

What Fixed Effects Can and Cannot Do

Fixed effects is powerful but not magic. Be precise about its reach.

Fixed effects removes ALL time-invariant confounders — observed or unobserved. Geography, founding-era capital stock, regional administrative culture, fixed population composition: anything constant within a unit over the study window is differenced away, even things you never measured and cannot name. This is its signature strength.

But this strength has three sharp limits.

1. It cannot identify the effect of time-invariant regressors. If you want to estimate the effect of a variable that never changes within a unit — say, whether a region is coastal, or its land area — fixed effects is useless for it. Such a variable is collinear with \alpha_i; demeaning sends it to a column of zeros (z_{it} - \bar{z}_i = 0 when z_{it} is constant). The very operation that saves you from time-invariant confounders also blinds you to time-invariant causes. You cannot have it both ways: \alpha_i absorbs all of them.

The mechanics are worth seeing literally. Take “coastal,” a region trait that is the same in every year. Its within-region mean equals its constant value, so every demeaned entry is zero — a column of zeros carries no information, and a coefficient on it cannot be estimated:

A constant-within-unit regressor demeans to all zeros, so fixed effects cannot estimate its effect. Contrast staffing, which varies within a region year to year and so survives demeaning with nonzero entries — that surviving variation is what identifies \hat\beta.
Region Year Coastal z_{it} Region mean \bar{z}_i Demeaned \tilde{z}_{it}=z_{it}-\bar{z}_i
Gotland 1 1 1 0
Gotland 2 1 1 0
Värmland 1 0 0 0
Värmland 2 0 0 0

2. It does NOT solve time-varying confounding. Fixed effects holds constant everything that is fixed within a unit. It does nothing about a confounder that changes over time and is correlated with x_{it}. If, in the same years a region hired nurses, it also rolled out a digital triage system that independently cut waiting times, the staffing coefficient still absorbs that. Within-transformation cannot difference out a moving target. This is why later pages turn to difference-in-differences and event studies, which add a parallel-trends logic on top.

3. It does NOT solve reverse causality. If long waiting times cause regions to hire more nurses (a feedback loop), then x_{it} responds to past y, strict exogeneity fails, and fixed effects is biased like any other regression. Demeaning addresses confounding from fixed traits, not simultaneity.

A common misconception is that “controlling for fixed effects” makes an analysis causal. It does not. Fixed effects is a tool for one specific threat — time-invariant unobserved heterogeneity. Time-varying confounding, reverse causality, and measurement error all survive the within transformation. Always state which threat your fixed effects is and is not addressing.

Two-Way Fixed Effects

So far \alpha_i absorbs unit-specific, time-invariant factors. But there are also time-specific, unit-invariant shocks: a nationwide pay agreement for nurses, a pandemic year, a change in national reimbursement rules. These hit all 21 regions in the same year and would confound a within-region comparison if staffing trends happen to line up with them.

The fix is symmetric: add a time fixed effect \lambda_t, common to all units in year t:

y_{it} = \alpha_i + \lambda_t + \beta\, x_{it} + e_{it}.

Read this as: “Region i’s waiting time in year t equals a region baseline, plus a national year effect, plus the staffing effect, plus noise.” This is the two-way fixed effects (TWFE) model. Now \hat\beta is identified from variation that is both within-region (deviations from each region’s mean) and within-year (deviations from each year’s national mean). It nets out any shock that is common across regions in a given year and any trait fixed within a region.

The picture to keep in mind is a grid: regions are rows, years are columns. The unit effect \alpha_i is one number per row; the time effect \lambda_t is one number per column. Two-way demeaning sweeps both out — subtract each row’s mean and each column’s mean — and \hat\beta is read off whatever variation is left in the interior cells once row and column levels are gone.

The interior cells are the observed region-year outcomes y_{it}. The slate band on the left is the region effect \alpha_i (one per row); the ochre band on top is the year effect \lambda_t (one per column). TWFE subtracts the row levels and the column levels, leaving \hat\beta to be identified from the residual variation inside the grid — the staffing movements that survive after both a region’s baseline and a year’s national shock are removed.

Estimation generalizes the demean operator to two-way demeaning (the algorithm subtracts unit means and time means iteratively until convergence; this is what fixest does under the hood with | unit + year). Two-way fixed effects is the standard specification for region-year panels, and it is the engine behind the canonical difference-in-differences design.

The incidental-parameters problem (conceptual). When the number of fixed effects grows with the sample — for instance, with very short panels where T is small but N is large — the estimated \alpha_i are each based on only a handful of observations and are individually noisy. For linear fixed effects, \hat\beta remains consistent as N \to \infty with T fixed, because the slope is identified by pooling within-unit variation across all units. But in nonlinear models (fixed-effects logit, probit, Poisson), the noisy \hat\alpha_i contaminate \hat\beta itself, and it can be inconsistent for fixed T — this is the incidental-parameters problem (Neyman & Scott, 1948). The lesson: linear FE is forgiving of short panels; nonlinear FE is not, and needs special estimators.

Math Details: Deriving the Within Estimator and Showing αᵢ Differences Out

Step 1: The model and the within average

Start from the unobserved-effects model for unit i in period t:

y_{it} = \alpha_i + \beta\, x_{it} + e_{it}, \qquad t = 1, \dots, T.

Average over the T periods for unit i. Define \bar{y}_i = \frac{1}{T}\sum_t y_{it}, and similarly \bar{x}_i, \bar{e}_i:

\bar{y}_i = \frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T}\big(\alpha_i + \beta x_{it} + e_{it}\big) = \alpha_i + \beta\, \bar{x}_i + \bar{e}_i.

The key fact: \frac{1}{T}\sum_t \alpha_i = \alpha_i, because \alpha_i does not depend on t. The unit effect survives averaging unchanged.

Step 2: Subtract — the within transformation

Subtract the unit-mean equation from the original:

y_{it} - \bar{y}_i = (\alpha_i - \alpha_i) + \beta(x_{it} - \bar{x}_i) + (e_{it} - \bar{e}_i).

The \alpha_i terms cancel exactly. Writing \tilde{z}_{it} = z_{it} - \bar{z}_i:

\tilde{y}_{it} = \beta\, \tilde{x}_{it} + \tilde{e}_{it}.

The transformed model contains no unit effect and therefore no term that can be correlated with \tilde{x}_{it} through \alpha_i. This is why \operatorname{Cov}(\alpha_i, x_{it}) \neq 0 no longer matters.

Step 3: OLS on the demeaned data

Apply OLS to \tilde{y}_{it} = \beta \tilde{x}_{it} + \tilde{e}_{it} (no intercept needed; the demeaned variables already have mean zero):

\hat\beta_{\text{FE}} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N}\sum_{t=1}^{T} \tilde{x}_{it}\,\tilde{y}_{it}} {\sum_{i=1}^{N}\sum_{t=1}^{T} \tilde{x}_{it}^{2}}.

Step 4: Consistency

Substitute the true transformed model \tilde{y}_{it} = \beta\tilde{x}_{it} + \tilde{e}_{it} into the estimator:

\hat\beta_{\text{FE}} = \beta + \frac{\sum_i\sum_t \tilde{x}_{it}\,\tilde{e}_{it}}{\sum_i\sum_t \tilde{x}_{it}^{2}}.

Under strict exogeneity, E[e_{it} \mid x_{i1},\dots,x_{iT}, \alpha_i] = 0, which implies E[\tilde{x}_{it}\,\tilde{e}_{it}] = 0 (the demeaned regressor is a function of the full x history, all of which the error is mean-independent of). By the law of large numbers the second term converges to zero, so \hat\beta_{\text{FE}} \xrightarrow{p} \beta. Fixed effects is consistent even when \alpha_i is correlated with x_{it}, because \alpha_i never enters the demeaned estimating equation.

Contrast with pooled OLS, where \alpha_i does enter the error: there the analogous term is \operatorname{Cov}(x_{it}, \alpha_i)/\operatorname{Var}(x_{it}), which is nonzero under confounding. That single difference is the whole value of panel data.

Step 5: Why time-invariant regressors drop out

Suppose w_i is constant over time within unit i (e.g., land area). Then \bar{w}_i = w_i, so \tilde{w}_{it} = w_i - w_i = 0 for every t. A column of zeros carries no information and cannot be estimated — its coefficient is not identified under fixed effects. Geometrically, w_i lies in the span of the unit dummies, so partialling out the dummies annihilates it (FWL again).

Code Implementations

The code below builds a small region-year panel, then shows three things that must agree: fixest::feols with unit fixed effects, plain lm with factor(region) dummies (LSDV), and manual demeaning. All three return the same \hat\beta.

library(fixest)
library(dplyr)

set.seed(2024)

# --- Simulate a region-year panel (21 regions x 6 years) ---
N <- 21; T <- 6
true_beta <- -2.4

regions <- tibble(
  region    = 1:N,
  alpha     = rnorm(N, mean = 22, sd = 4.5),  # baseline waiting time (days)
  staff_bar = 7 + 0.9 * scale(rnorm(N))[, 1]  # region avg staffing
)
# Make staffing correlate with the baseline -> confounding for pooled OLS
regions$staff_bar <- regions$staff_bar + 0.45 * (regions$alpha - mean(regions$alpha))

panel <- expand.grid(region = 1:N, year = 1:T) |>
  left_join(regions, by = "region") |>
  mutate(
    staff = staff_bar + rnorm(n(), 0, 1.0),                 # within variation
    wait  = alpha + true_beta * (staff - 7) + rnorm(n(), 0, 1.6)
  )

# --- 1. Pooled OLS (biased: ignores alpha_i) ---
pooled <- feols(wait ~ staff, data = panel)

# --- 2. Fixed effects via feols (within / demeaning under the hood) ---
fe <- feols(wait ~ staff | region, data = panel)

# --- 3. LSDV: a dummy per region (numerically identical beta) ---
lsdv <- lm(wait ~ staff + factor(region), data = panel)

# --- 4. Manual demeaning: subtract each region's mean, then OLS ---
panel_dm <- panel |>
  group_by(region) |>
  mutate(staff_dm = staff - mean(staff),
         wait_dm  = wait  - mean(wait)) |>
  ungroup()
manual <- lm(wait_dm ~ staff_dm - 1, data = panel_dm)

cat("True beta            :", true_beta, "\n")
cat("Pooled OLS beta      :", round(coef(pooled)["staff"], 3), "\n")
cat("feols FE beta        :", round(coef(fe)["staff"], 3), "\n")
cat("LSDV beta            :", round(coef(lsdv)["staff"], 3), "\n")
cat("Manual demeaning beta:", round(coef(manual)["staff_dm"], 3), "\n")
# feols, LSDV, and manual demeaning agree to numerical precision.

# --- Two-way FE (region + year) with clustered SEs ---
twfe <- feols(wait ~ staff | region + year, data = panel,
              cluster = ~ region)
summary(twfe)

Exercise 1

A tiny panel of two regions observed over two years records district-nurse density x (per 10,000) and mean waiting time y (days):

Region Year x y
A 1 6 30
A 2 8 26
B 1 4 18
B 2 6 14
  1. Compute the fixed-effects (within) estimate of \beta by demeaning each region.
  2. Compute the pooled-OLS slope ignoring region. Why does it differ?
Show answer

(a) Within estimate. Demean within each region.

Region A: \bar{x}_A = 7, \bar{y}_A = 28.

  • Year 1: \tilde{x} = 6 - 7 = -1, \tilde{y} = 30 - 28 = 2.
  • Year 2: \tilde{x} = 8 - 7 = 1, \tilde{y} = 26 - 28 = -2.

Region B: \bar{x}_B = 5, \bar{y}_B = 16.

  • Year 1: \tilde{x} = 4 - 5 = -1, \tilde{y} = 18 - 16 = 2.
  • Year 2: \tilde{x} = 6 - 5 = 1, \tilde{y} = 14 - 16 = -2.

Now run OLS through the origin on the four demeaned points:

\hat\beta_{\text{FE}} = \frac{\sum \tilde{x}\tilde{y}}{\sum \tilde{x}^2} = \frac{(-1)(2) + (1)(-2) + (-1)(2) + (1)(-2)}{(-1)^2 + 1^2 + (-1)^2 + 1^2} = \frac{-2 - 2 - 2 - 2}{4} = \frac{-8}{4} = -2.

So \hat\beta_{\text{FE}} = -2: one more nurse per 10,000 lowers waiting time by 2 days, within region.

(b) Pooled OLS. Across all four points, \bar{x} = 6, \bar{y} = 22. Deviations from the grand means:

x - \bar{x} y - \bar{y}
0 8
2 4
-2 -4
0 -8

\hat\beta_{\text{pooled}} = \frac{\sum (x-\bar{x})(y-\bar{y})}{\sum(x-\bar{x})^2} = \frac{(0)(8) + (2)(4) + (-2)(-4) + (0)(-8)}{0 + 4 + 4 + 0} = \frac{16}{8} = +2.

Pooled OLS reports +2 — the opposite sign from the truth. Why? Region A has both higher staffing on average and higher waiting times (it is the larger, busier region: high \alpha_A = 28 vs. \alpha_B = 16). The between-region pattern is “more nurses go with longer waits,” which is pure confounding by \alpha_i. Pooled OLS is dominated by that between variation and gets the sign wrong. Demeaning removes the \alpha_i gap and recovers the true within effect of -2.

Exercise 2

A colleague says: “I added a fixed effect for every vårdcentral in my regression, so my estimate of the effect of being a rural clinic on patient satisfaction is now free of confounding.” What is wrong with this claim?

Show answer

Whether a clinic is rural is (over the study window) a time-invariant characteristic of that clinic. The clinic fixed effect \alpha_i already absorbs everything fixed about clinic i — including its rural/urban status. After demeaning, the “rural” indicator becomes a column of zeros within every clinic (z_{it} - \bar{z}_i = 0 for a constant z), and its coefficient is not identified. In practice, software either drops the variable or returns NA/collinearity warnings.

The colleague has confused two things:

  1. Fixed effects removes the confounding influence of all time-invariant clinic traits on other (time-varying) regressors. That part is true and valuable.
  2. But fixed effects cannot estimate the effect of a time-invariant regressor, because that effect is perfectly collinear with the unit dummy.

To estimate the rural-vs-urban effect, you would need between-clinic variation, which fixed effects deliberately throws away. Options include random effects (if you are willing to assume \operatorname{Cov}(\alpha_i, x_{it}) = 0), a hierarchical model, or interacting the time-invariant variable with a time-varying one (e.g., does the effect of staffing differ between rural and urban clinics — that interaction is identified, because staffing varies within clinic).

Exercise 3

Using the structural model y_{it} = \alpha_i + \beta x_{it} + e_{it} with \operatorname{Cov}(\alpha_i, x_{it}) > 0 and \beta < 0 (more nurses, shorter waits), state the sign of the pooled-OLS bias and explain in one sentence why fixed effects is immune to it.

Show answer

The pooled-OLS probability limit is

\operatorname{plim}\ \hat\beta_{\text{pooled}} = \beta + \frac{\operatorname{Cov}(x_{it}, \alpha_i)}{\operatorname{Var}(x_{it})}.

Since \operatorname{Cov}(x_{it}, \alpha_i) > 0 and \operatorname{Var}(x_{it}) > 0, the bias term is positive. Added to a negative \beta, it pushes the pooled estimate upward (toward zero, or even positive). So pooled OLS understates the magnitude of the beneficial effect — it makes nurses look less helpful than they are, and could even flip the sign (as in Exercise 1).

Fixed effects is immune because the within transformation subtracts each unit’s mean, which removes \alpha_i entirely from the estimating equation; with no \alpha_i left, there is no \operatorname{Cov}(x, \alpha) term to create bias. The only requirement is strict exogeneity of the idiosyncratic shock e_{it}.

Exercise 4

In the interactive, set the confounding slider to a strong positive value and switch between “Raw data” and “After demeaning.” Explain (a) what visual feature of the raw plot encodes \alpha_i, and (b) why the demeaned fixed-effects line lands on the true \beta no matter where the slider is.

Show answer

(a) In the raw plot, \alpha_i is encoded as the vertical height of each region’s cloud — its average waiting-time level. A high-\alpha region sits high on the y-axis; a low-\alpha region sits low. With strong positive confounding, the high clouds are also pushed to the right (high staffing), because \operatorname{Cov}(\alpha_i, \bar{x}_i) > 0. This diagonal arrangement of cloud centers is what rotates the pooled (red) line away from the true slope: the red line traces the between-cloud pattern, not the within-cloud slope.

(b) Demeaning subtracts each region’s own (\bar{x}_i, \bar{y}_i), which recenters every cloud on the origin. The vertical separation that encoded \alpha_i disappears, and with it the diagonal arrangement of cloud centers — the confounding is literally subtracted out of the picture. What remains is only the within-cloud co-movement of staffing and waiting time, and that co-movement carries the same true slope \beta = -2.4 in every region by construction. OLS on the collapsed cloud therefore recovers \beta regardless of how the slider arranged the clouds before demeaning. The slider changes the between structure (which demeaning erases) but never the within structure (which demeaning preserves).

Key takeaways

  • The within (fixed-effects) estimator is OLS on data demeaned by unit; because \alpha_i is constant over time it differences out, so \operatorname{Cov}(\alpha_i, x_{it}) \neq 0 no longer biases \hat\beta.
  • Pooled OLS suffers omitted-variable bias of \operatorname{Cov}(x_{it}, \alpha_i)/\operatorname{Var}(x_{it}) — a bias of inconsistency that more region-years only make more precise, not less wrong.
  • Demeaning, LSDV (a dummy per unit), and partialling out unit indicators are three routes to the identical \hat\beta, guaranteed by Frisch–Waugh–Lovell.
  • Fixed effects removes all time-invariant confounders (named or not) but cannot estimate time-invariant regressors and does nothing about time-varying confounding or reverse causality.
  • Two-way fixed effects adds a time effect \lambda_t to absorb common year shocks; linear FE stays consistent in short panels, but nonlinear FE faces the incidental-parameters problem.

What Comes Next

The within estimator gives us a consistent \hat\beta even when unmeasured, time-invariant traits confound a naive comparison — a genuinely powerful result. But a point estimate is only half the story. Demeaning induces correlation across a unit’s residuals over time, observations within a region are not independent, and short panels raise degrees-of-freedom subtleties we glossed over. Getting the standard errors right — clustering, serial correlation, and the small-sample corrections that honest inference demands — is the subject of the next page, Practical Issues.
