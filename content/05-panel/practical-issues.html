Practical Issues

Serial correlation, clustering, and unbalanced panels

In this section, you will learn to

  • explain why serial correlation leaves the fixed-effects point estimate consistent but deflates the iid standard error, using the design-effect multiplier
  • diagnose the over-rejection problem documented by Bertrand, Duflo & Mullainathan and choose the right clustering level for panel inference
  • estimate cluster-robust standard errors and interpret confidence-interval coverage when errors persist within a unit
  • handle the few-clusters problem with t_{G-1} critical values and the wild cluster bootstrap
  • distinguish benign from biasing attrition in unbalanced panels, and decide between fixed and random effects using the Hausman logic

When the Point Estimate Is Right but the Standard Error Lies

The previous page showed how the Fixed Effects estimator sweeps away every time-invariant confounder by demeaning each unit’s data. That is a triumph for identification: under the within transformation, a region’s permanent characteristics — its age structure, its terrain, its political culture — can no longer bias the slope. But identification is only half of an honest causal claim. The other half is inference: how precise is the estimate, and would a different draw of the data have moved it much?

Here is the uncomfortable fact this page is built around. You can have a perfectly consistent fixed-effects point estimate and a standard error that is wildly too small — so small that it manufactures statistical significance out of nothing. The culprit is serial correlation: the errors of a single unit are correlated over time. A vårdcentral that has an unusually bad year for fall-related hospitalizations among its elderly patients tends to have a bad next year too. When you ignore that persistence, you fool yourself into thinking you have far more independent information than you actually do.

To make this concrete, picture all 21 regioner (Sweden’s regions) observed annually from 2014 to 2023. Starting in different years, some regions roll out a structured fallpreventionsprogram — a falls-prevention program with balance training, medication reviews, and home-hazard assessments for patients over 75. The outcome Y_{it} is the rate of fall-related hospitalizations per 1,000 elderly residents in region i during year t. We want the effect of the program on that rate. The estimate is easy. Getting the standard error right is where careers are made and lost.

Serial correlation does not bias the fixed-effects point estimate — \hat{\beta} is still consistent. What it corrupts is the standard error. Naive (iid) standard errors treat each region-year as an independent observation, vastly overstating how much you know. The fix is not a better estimator; it is an honest variance estimator that respects the within-unit dependence.

How Serial Correlation Deflates the Standard Error

Begin with the within-transformed fixed-effects model for a single covariate x_{it} (the program indicator), where \alpha_i is the region fixed effect and \varepsilon_{it} is the idiosyncratic error:

Y_{it} = \alpha_i + \beta\, x_{it} + \varepsilon_{it}, \qquad i = 1,\dots,N,\quad t = 1,\dots,T.

Read this as: “Region i’s fall-hospitalization rate in year t equals a region-specific intercept \alpha_i, plus \beta times whether the program is active, plus an error \varepsilon_{it}.” The classical inference machinery assumes the errors \varepsilon_{it} are independent across both i and t. The first part is plausible — Skåne’s bad year says little about Norrbotten’s. The second part is almost never true: a region’s shocks persist.

Model that persistence as a first-order autoregressive process, \varepsilon_{it} = \rho\,\varepsilon_{i,t-1} + \nu_{it}, where |\rho| < 1 and \nu_{it} is white noise. The parameter \rho is the year-to-year correlation in a region’s luck.

Suppose, for intuition, that the regressor x_{it} is itself persistent (a program, once adopted, stays on). Then the variance of \hat\beta is inflated relative to the naive formula by a design-effect multiplier that grows with the number of periods, the error persistence, and the regressor’s own persistence. A simple upper bound captures the qualitative behaviour:

\text{DEFF} \;=\; \frac{\operatorname{Var}_{\text{true}}(\hat\beta)}{\operatorname{Var}_{\text{iid}}(\hat\beta)} \;\le\; 1 + (T-1)\,\rho\,\rho_x ,

where \rho is the serial correlation of the errors and \rho_x is the serial correlation of the regressor. Read this as: “The true sampling variance can exceed the iid formula by a multiplier that grows with the number of periods T, the error persistence \rho, and the regressor’s own persistence \rho_x.” (The exact closed form, derived in the Math Details below, is \text{DEFF} = 1 + \tfrac{2}{T}\sum_{k=1}^{T-1}(T-k)\rho^{k} when the regressor is perfectly persistent.) With T = 10 years and \rho = 0.5, that exact formula gives \text{DEFF}\approx 2.6: the naive standard error is too small by a factor of \sqrt{2.6}\approx 1.6, so a true t-statistic of 2 is reported as roughly 3.2, and a marginal result becomes a “discovery.”

This is exactly the phenomenon documented by Bertrand, Duflo & Mullainathan (2004) in their celebrated critique of difference-in-differences practice. They took real outcome series, inserted placebo policies that affected nothing, and ran the standard panel regression. With conventional standard errors, they rejected the (true) null of no effect roughly 45% of the time instead of the nominal 5%. The estimator was unbiased; the inference was catastrophically over-confident. The lesson generalizes far beyond DiD to every panel regression with serially correlated errors — which is to say, essentially all of them.

The picture below makes the villain visible. On the left are two simulated ten-year error paths for a single region: with no persistence (\rho = 0) the line is jagged and forgetful — a bad year says nothing about the next — while with strong persistence (\rho = 0.8) the line drifts in long swells, so a bad year drags the next one down with it. On the right is the resulting 10 \times 10 error covariance matrix at \rho = 0.8. The iid standard error keeps only the bright diagonal (t = s) and silently throws away the entire shaded off-diagonal band of within-region covariances — and it is precisely that discarded mass that the cluster-robust estimator recovers.

// Both paths share the SAME innovations, so the only difference is ρ.

  const T = 10;
  const rng0 = d3.randomNormal.source(d3.randomLcg(424242))(0, 1);
  const innov = d3.range(T).map(() => rng0());
  const build = (rho) => {
    const sdInnov = 3 * Math.sqrt(1 - rho * rho);
    let eps = innov[0] * 3;            // stationary start, sd ≈ 3
    const path = [{ year: 2014, eps, label: `ρ = ${rho}` }];
    for (let t = 1; t < T; t++) {
      eps = rho * eps + sdInnov * innov[t];
      path.push({ year: 2014 + t, eps, label: `ρ = ${rho}` });
    }
    return path;
  };
  return [...build(0), ...build(0.8)];
}
// AR(1) covariance matrix Cov(ε_t, ε_s) = σ² ρ^{|t−s|} for the right-hand heatmap.
pi_covCells = {
  const T = 10, rho = 0.8, sigma2 = 9; // σ ≈ 3
  const cells = [];
  for (let t = 0; t < T; t++)
    for (let s = 0; s < T; s++)
      cells.push({ t, s, cov: sigma2 * Math.pow(rho, Math.abs(t - s)), diag: t === s });
  return cells;
}

The Fix: Cluster at the Level of the Correlation

The remedy is to stop pretending the region-years are independent. We computed the conceptual groundwork on Module 4’s Inference page; here we apply it to panels. A cluster-robust variance estimator groups the data into clusters — here, one cluster per region — and allows the errors within a cluster to be correlated in any way, while assuming independence across clusters.

The cluster-robust variance of \hat\beta stacks each cluster’s contribution:

\widehat{\operatorname{Var}}_{\text{cl}}(\hat\beta) = (X'X)^{-1}\left(\sum_{g=1}^{G} X_g'\,\hat u_g\,\hat u_g'\,X_g\right)(X'X)^{-1},

where g indexes the G clusters (regions), X_g stacks that cluster’s rows, and \hat u_g is its vector of residuals. Read this as: “Instead of summing squared residuals observation-by-observation, we form the outer product \hat u_g \hat u_g' within each region — which captures all the within-region correlations — and then sum across regions.” Because \hat u_g \hat u_g' is a full T_g \times T_g matrix, every pair of years inside a region is allowed to covary.

For the single regressor used in the simulation below, the matrices collapse to plain numbers, and this is exactly what the in-browser code computes:

\widehat{\operatorname{Var}}_{\text{cl}}(\hat\beta) = \frac{\sum_{g=1}^{G} s_g^{\,2}}{\left(\sum_i\sum_t \tilde x_{it}^2\right)^2}, \qquad s_g = \sum_{t} \tilde x_{gt}\,\hat u_{gt},

where \tilde x_{gt} is the demeaned program indicator and s_g is region g’s “score” — the within-region sum of demeaned-regressor times residual. The iid formula instead uses s^2 \big/ \sum_i\sum_t \tilde x_{it}^2 with a single pooled s^2; clustering replaces that with the sum of squared region scores, which grows whenever a region’s residuals share a sign year after year — the fingerprint of serial correlation.

Two points make clustering intuitive. First, cluster at the level where the errors are correlated and the treatment is assigned. The falls program is adopted region-wide, and a region’s shocks are persistent within region — so the cluster is the region, not the region-year and not the individual patient. Second, clustering changes only the standard error, never the coefficient. You get the same \hat\beta; you simply stop lying about its precision.

A useful slogan: cluster on the dimension along which you cannot honestly claim independent draws. If a policy is assigned to whole regions and you observe each region for ten years, you do not have 21 \times 10 = 210 independent observations — you have something much closer to 21. Cluster-robust standard errors encode exactly that deflation of effective sample size.

See It: Coverage of the Fixed-Effects Confidence Interval

The simulation below makes the whole argument visible. It generates many synthetic panels of 21 regions observed over 10 years. In every panel the true program effect is fixed at \beta = -2.0 (the program lowers fall hospitalizations by 2 per 1,000 elderly). For each simulated panel it fits the within (region fixed-effects) regression and builds two 95% confidence intervals for \hat\beta: one using iid standard errors that treat each region-year as an independent draw, the other using standard errors clustered by region. A confidence interval is doing its job if it covers the truth 95% of the time. Drag the serial-correlation slider and watch which method keeps its promise.

pi_N = 21        // regioner
pi_T = 10        // years 2014–2023
pi_betaTrue = -2.0   // true program effect (per 1,000 elderly)
pi_sigmaNu = 3.0
// Build a sticky, staggered treatment design once: each region adopts the
// program in some year (or never). Persistent regressor => worst case for
// naive SEs, exactly the Bertrand–Duflo–Mullainathan setting.
pi_design = {
  const rng = d3.randomLcg(20240619);   // fixed seed: same design every redraw
  const adoptYear = [];
  for (let i = 0; i < pi_N; i++) {
    // ~1/3 of regions never adopt; the rest adopt in a random year 3..8
    const u = rng();
    adoptYear.push(u < 0.33 ? Infinity : 2 + Math.floor(rng() * 6));
  }
  const x = [];               // x[i][t] = 1 if program active
  for (let i = 0; i < pi_N; i++) {
    x.push(d3.range(pi_T).map(t => (t >= adoptYear[i] ? 1 : 0)));
  }
  return { adoptYear, x };
}
// Within (fixed-effects) OLS for a single regressor + its two variances.
// Demean x and y by region, regress, then compute iid and clustered SEs.
pi_fitOne = function(x, y) {
  const N = x.length, T = x[0].length;
  // region means
  const xbar = x.map(row => d3.mean(row));
  const ybar = y.map(row => d3.mean(row));
  let sxx = 0, sxy = 0;
  for (let i = 0; i < N; i++)
    for (let t = 0; t < T; t++) {
      const xd = x[i][t] - xbar[i];
      sxx += xd * xd;
      sxy += xd * (y[i][t] - ybar[i]);
    }
  const beta = sxy / sxx;
  // residuals from the within regression
  const u = [];
  for (let i = 0; i < N; i++) {
    u.push([]);
    for (let t = 0; t < T; t++) {
      const xd = x[i][t] - xbar[i];
      u[i].push((y[i][t] - ybar[i]) - beta * xd);
    }
  }
  // iid variance: s^2 / Sxx with dof = NT - N - 1 (regressor + N fixed effects)
  let sse = 0;
  for (let i = 0; i < N; i++) for (let t = 0; t < T; t++) sse += u[i][t] * u[i][t];
  const dof = N * T - N - 1;
  const s2 = sse / dof;
  const varIID = s2 / sxx;
  // cluster-robust (region) variance: sandwich with finite-sample scaling
  let meat = 0;
  for (let i = 0; i < N; i++) {
    let sg = 0;
    for (let t = 0; t < T; t++) sg += (x[i][t] - xbar[i]) * u[i][t];
    meat += sg * sg;
  }
  const G = N;
  const scale = (G / (G - 1)) * ((N * T - 1) / (N * T - N - 1));
  const varCL = scale * meat / (sxx * sxx);
  return { beta, seIID: Math.sqrt(varIID), seCL: Math.sqrt(varCL) };
}
// Run the Monte Carlo: many panels with AR(1) errors of strength pi_rho.
pi_sims = {
  pi_resim;  // re-run when the button is pressed
  const { x } = pi_design;
  const N = pi_N, T = pi_T, rho = pi_rho;
  const sdInnov = pi_sigmaNu * Math.sqrt(1 - rho * rho); // keep marginal var ~const
  const out = [];
  const norm = d3.randomNormal.source(d3.randomLcg(Math.random()))(0, 1);
  for (let s = 0; s < pi_nSim; s++) {
    const y = [];
    for (let i = 0; i < N; i++) {
      const row = [];
      let eps = norm() * pi_sigmaNu;          // stationary start
      for (let t = 0; t < T; t++) {
        if (t > 0) eps = rho * eps + sdInnov * norm();
        // y = alpha_i + beta*x + eps ; alpha_i drops out under demeaning
        const alpha = 8 + 2 * Math.sin(i);     // arbitrary region intercept
        row.push(alpha + pi_betaTrue * x[i][t] + eps);
      }
      y.push(row);
    }
    const fit = pi_fitOne(x, y);
    out.push({
      id: s,
      beta: fit.beta,
      loIID: fit.beta - 1.96 * fit.seIID, hiIID: fit.beta + 1.96 * fit.seIID,
      loCL:  fit.beta - 1.96 * fit.seCL,  hiCL:  fit.beta + 1.96 * fit.seCL,
      covIID: (fit.beta - 1.96 * fit.seIID <= pi_betaTrue) && (pi_betaTrue <= fit.beta + 1.96 * fit.seIID),
      covCL:  (fit.beta - 1.96 * fit.seCL  <= pi_betaTrue) && (pi_betaTrue <= fit.beta + 1.96 * fit.seCL)
    });
  }
  return out;
}

How to Read the Coverage Plot

Work through these steps to internalize what the simulation is telling you.

Step 1: Find the truth line. The dashed black vertical line sits at the true program effect, \beta = -2.0. Every horizontal bar is one simulated panel’s 95% confidence interval for \hat\beta. A bar that crosses the dashed line covers the truth (green); a bar entirely to one side misses it (red).

Step 2: Start with no serial correlation. Drag \rho to 0. Both methods give nearly identical intervals, and the scoreboard shows both covering the truth about 95% of the time. When errors really are independent, the iid formula is correct and clustering buys you nothing. This is the benign baseline.

Step 3: Turn on persistence. Slide \rho up to 0.6, then 0.9. Toggle Highlight method to “iid (independent).” The intervals barely widen — the iid formula does not “see” the persistence — yet many more bars now turn red. The iid coverage on the scoreboard collapses, often to 70–80% or worse. These are confidence intervals that claim 95% confidence but deliver far less: the over-confidence that Bertrand, Duflo & Mullainathan warned about, reproduced on your screen.

Step 4: Switch to clustering. With \rho still high, toggle Highlight method to “Clustered by region.” The bars visibly lengthen — the clustered standard errors are larger because they account for the within-region dependence — and the share of green bars climbs back toward 95%. Compare the two “mean SE” figures on the scoreboard: the clustered SE is substantially larger, and that extra width is precisely what restores honest coverage.

Step 5: Stress-test it. Increase the number of simulated panels to 1000 for a more stable coverage estimate, and press Re-simulate a few times. The qualitative verdict never changes: under serial correlation, iid intervals under-cover, and clustering by the unit repairs them. The point estimate \hat\beta is centered on -2.0 either way — only the width of the interval, and therefore the honesty of your inference, depends on the variance estimator.

The Few-Clusters Problem

Clustering is the right idea, but it leans on an asymptotic argument: the cluster-robust variance is consistent as the number of clusters G \to \infty, holding cluster size fixed. With Swedish regions, G = 21. That is uncomfortably few. With a small number of clusters, the sandwich estimator is biased downward (too small), the residuals \hat u_g are themselves shrunk toward zero by the fixed effects, and the reference distribution is no longer a standard normal.

With only G \approx 21 clusters, cluster-robust t-statistics should not be compared to \pm 1.96. Two standard adjustments help:

  1. A t-distribution with G - 1 degrees of freedom instead of the normal. With G = 21 that is t_{20}, whose 97.5th percentile is 2.086, not 1.96 — a wider, more honest interval. Software such as fixest does this automatically.
  2. The wild cluster bootstrap (Cameron, Gelbach & Miller, 2008). Rather than trusting a formula, you resample at the cluster level: re-impose the null, multiply each cluster’s residuals by a random \pm 1 (a Rademacher weight), refit, and build the reference distribution of the t-statistic empirically. This delivers correct test sizes with as few as a dozen clusters and is the recommended default when G is small.

The figure below shows why the cutoff moves. The standard normal (slate) and the t_{20} distribution (red) look almost identical near the center, but the t_{20} has fatter tails — more probability mass far from zero. To keep only 2.5% in the upper tail you therefore have to push the cutoff out from 1.96 to 2.086. The shaded sliver between the two cutoffs is the extra benefit of the doubt that the few-clusters correction grants you; with even fewer clusters, the t tails fatten further and the gap widens.

pi_lgamma = function (z) {
  // Lanczos approximation to ln Γ(z), accurate to ~1e-10 for z > 0.
  const g = 7;
  const c = [
    0.99999999999980993, 676.5203681218851, -1259.1392167224028,
    771.32342877765313, -176.61502916214059, 12.507343278686905,
    -0.13857109526572012, 9.9843695780195716e-6, 1.5056327351493116e-7
  ];
  z -= 1;
  let a = c[0];
  const tt = z + g + 0.5;
  for (let i = 1; i < g + 2; i++) a += c[i] / (z + i);
  return 0.5 * Math.log(2 * Math.PI) + (z + 0.5) * Math.log(tt) - tt + Math.log(a);
}
pi_tDensity = function (x, nu) {
  const lnK = pi_lgamma((nu + 1) / 2) - pi_lgamma(nu / 2)
    - 0.5 * Math.log(nu * Math.PI);
  return Math.exp(lnK - ((nu + 1) / 2) * Math.log(1 + (x * x) / nu));
}
pi_normDensity = function (x) {
  return Math.exp(-0.5 * x * x) / Math.sqrt(2 * Math.PI);
}

Unbalanced Panels and Attrition

So far we have assumed every region is observed in every year — a balanced panel. Real data are messier. A new region forms from a merger; a vårdcentral opens midway through the study or closes; a reporting system changes and a year goes missing. The result is an unbalanced panel, where T_i differs across units.

Unbalancedness per se is not a problem. The within transformation simply demeans each unit using whatever periods it has, and fixed-effects software handles ragged panels automatically. The danger is not the missingness but its mechanism.

When is attrition benign? Missing observations are harmless for the fixed-effects estimator when the reason a region-year is missing is unrelated to the idiosyncratic error \varepsilon_{it} — formally, when the data are missing at random conditional on the fixed effect and covariates. A vårdcentral that closes for administrative reasons unconnected to its fall-hospitalization shocks creates a benign gap.

When does attrition bias? Attrition is dangerous when units drop out because of their outcome path — selection on the realized \varepsilon_{it}. If clinics with the worst fall-prevention results are exactly the ones that lose funding and exit the panel, then the surviving sample is no longer representative: the demeaning is performed over a self-selected subset, and \hat\beta becomes biased even though the model looks identical.

The two panel grids below make the distinction concrete. Each row is a region, each column a year; a filled cell means “observed,” a hollow cell means “missing.” On the left, the gaps are scattered at random — a reporting glitch here, an administrative closure there — unrelated to how the region was doing, so demeaning over the surviving cells is still honest. On the right, the missing cells all sit in the post-treatment years and concentrate in the regions with the worst outcomes (darkest rows): the high-rate units vanish exactly when the program turns on, so the surviving post-period average drops for reasons that have nothing to do with the program. That second pattern is what biases \hat\beta.

Fixed Effects vs. Random Effects

A recurring temptation is to replace fixed effects with random effects, which treats \alpha_i not as a parameter to be estimated for each unit but as a random draw from a distribution. The random-effects estimator is a precision-weighted average of the within (over-time) and between (cross-region) variation, and it is more efficient than fixed effects — smaller standard errors — when its key assumption holds.

The random-effects assumption is that the unit effect is uncorrelated with the regressors:

\operatorname{Cov}(\alpha_i, x_{it}) = 0 \quad \text{for all } t.

Read this as: “A region’s permanent, unobserved propensity for falls (\alpha_i) is unrelated to whether and when it adopts the program (x_{it}).” If this holds, random effects is consistent and efficient. If it fails — if, say, regions with structurally older, frailer populations (high \alpha_i) are precisely the ones that rush to adopt the program (high x_{it}) — then random effects is biased, while fixed effects remains consistent because it never uses the cross-region comparison that the correlation contaminates.

This is the classic efficiency-versus-consistency trade-off. Fixed effects throws away the between-unit variation to buy robustness against \alpha_i-confounding; random effects keeps that variation for efficiency but stakes everything on \operatorname{Cov}(\alpha_i, x_{it}) = 0. The table summarizes the choice at a glance.

The Hausman test adjudicates. Its logic is elegant: if the random-effects assumption holds, the FE and RE estimates both target the same \beta and should differ only by sampling noise; if the assumption fails, only FE is consistent, so the two estimates diverge systematically.

H = (\hat\beta_{\text{FE}} - \hat\beta_{\text{RE}})' \left[\widehat{\operatorname{Var}}(\hat\beta_{\text{FE}}) - \widehat{\operatorname{Var}}(\hat\beta_{\text{RE}})\right]^{-1} (\hat\beta_{\text{FE}} - \hat\beta_{\text{RE}}) \;\sim\; \chi^2_k.

Read this as: “The Hausman statistic is the squared distance between the FE and RE estimates, scaled by the variance of their difference; a large value means the gap is too big to be chance, so we reject random effects in favor of fixed effects.” A small H (large p-value) does not prove the RE assumption — it merely fails to detect a violation, which may reflect low power.

On the left, the RE assumption holds: both estimators target the truth and agree (intervals overlap heavily), so the Hausman test fails to reject and RE’s extra efficiency — its tighter interval — is yours to keep. A Caution: Fixed Effects Can Starve the Estimate of Variation

Robustness is not free. The within transformation discards all between-unit variation and identifies \beta purely from movements within a unit over time. If x_{it} barely moves within units — if almost all of its variation is cross-sectional — then fixed effects leaves very little signal behind, and \hat\beta becomes imprecise, with a large standard error, even though it remains consistent.

Concretely: if the falls program is adopted by every region in the same year, there is no staggered within-region variation to exploit beyond a common time shift, and a two-way fixed-effects model with a full set of year dummies would be nearly collinear with the treatment. The more the program’s timing varies across regions, the more within-variation survives demeaning, and the tighter your estimate. A symptom to watch for is a fixed-effects standard error far larger than the pooled-OLS one: that is the price of robustness when within-variation is scarce. The remedy is not to abandon fixed effects but to recognize that your design may simply lack the variation to answer the question precisely — a substantive limitation, not a statistical bug.

The two strips below show what “x barely moves within units” looks like. Each row is a region across ten years; a filled cell means the program is active that year. On the left the timing is staggered — regions switch on in different years, so within each region the indicator genuinely moves from 0 to 1, and demeaning leaves plenty of signal (a tight \hat\beta). On the right the treatment is almost purely cross-sectional — each region is either always-on or always-off, so within every region the indicator never changes, demeaning erases nearly all of it, and the standard error balloons. // Two treatment designs over 12 regions × 10 years: staggered (within-rich)
// vs cross-sectional (within-scarce). Returns cells + the within-variance share.
pi_withinDesigns = {
  const nRow = 12, nCol = 10;
  const rng = d3.randomLcg(0xC0FFEE);
  // Staggered: each region adopts in a random year 1..8 (or never for a few).
  const stag = d3.range(nRow).map(() => {
    const a = rng() < 0.18 ? Infinity : 1 + Math.floor(rng() * 8);
    return d3.range(nCol).map(t => (t >= a ? 1 : 0));
  });
  // Cross-sectional: each region is constant — always-on or always-off.
  const cross = d3.range(nRow).map(() => {
    const on = rng() < 0.5 ? 1 : 0;
    return d3.range(nCol).map(() => on);
  });
  // Share of total variance that is WITHIN (= survives demeaning).
  const withinShare = (grid) => {
    let totSS = 0, withinSS = 0, gAll = 0, n = 0;
    for (const row of grid) for (const v of row) { gAll += v; n++; }
    gAll /= n;
    for (const row of grid) {
      const rm = d3.mean(row);
      for (const v of row) { totSS += (v - gAll) ** 2; withinSS += (v - rm) ** 2; }
    }
    return totSS === 0 ? 0 : withinSS / totSS;
  };
  const toCells = (grid, design) =>
    grid.flatMap((row, i) => row.map((v, t) => ({ design, region: i, year: t, on: v })));
  return {
    cells: [...toCells(stag, "Staggered (within-rich)"), ...toCells(cross, "Cross-sectional (within-scarce)")],
    shareStag: withinShare(stag),
    shareCross: withinShare(cross)
  };
}

Math Details: Why iid Standard Errors Are Too Small Under Serial Correlation

The variance of a sum of correlated terms

The within estimator for a single regressor can be written as

\hat\beta = \beta + \frac{\sum_{i}\sum_{t}\tilde x_{it}\,\varepsilon_{it}}{\sum_i\sum_t \tilde x_{it}^2},

where \tilde x_{it} = x_{it} - \bar x_i is the demeaned regressor. Conditioning on X, the sampling variance is driven by the numerator S = \sum_i\sum_t \tilde x_{it}\,\varepsilon_{it}. Because errors are independent across regions, \operatorname{Var}(S) = \sum_i \operatorname{Var}\!\left(\sum_t \tilde x_{it}\,\varepsilon_{it}\right), and within a region,

\operatorname{Var}\!\left(\sum_t \tilde x_{it}\,\varepsilon_{it}\right) = \underbrace{\sum_t \tilde x_{it}^2\,\sigma_\varepsilon^2}_{\text{iid term (diagonal)}} + \underbrace{2\sum_{t<s} \tilde x_{it}\,\tilde x_{is}\,\operatorname{Cov}(\varepsilon_{it},\varepsilon_{is})}_{\text{cross-time term (off-diagonal)}}.

The iid standard error keeps only the first term and silently sets every covariance to zero. When the errors are positively serially correlated, \operatorname{Cov}(\varepsilon_{it},\varepsilon_{is}) > 0; and when the regressor is positively persistent, \tilde x_{it}\,\tilde x_{is} > 0 on average. The product of two positives makes the second term positive, so the true variance exceeds the iid approximation. The iid formula is too small by exactly the off-diagonal mass it discards.

Quantifying the gap for AR(1) errors

Suppose \operatorname{Cov}(\varepsilon_{it},\varepsilon_{is}) = \sigma_\varepsilon^2\,\rho^{|t-s|} (the AR(1) structure) and, for a clean closed form, that \tilde x_{it} = \tilde x_i is constant within a region across the T periods. Then the within sum for region i has variance

\operatorname{Var}\!\left(\tilde x_i \sum_t \varepsilon_{it}\right) = \tilde x_i^2\,\sigma_\varepsilon^2\left[T + 2\sum_{t<s}\rho^{|t-s|}\right] = \tilde x_i^2\,\sigma_\varepsilon^2\left[T + 2\!\sum_{k=1}^{T-1}(T-k)\rho^{k}\right].

The iid formula would report only \tilde x_i^2\,\sigma_\varepsilon^2\,T. The ratio of true to iid variance is therefore the design effect

\text{DEFF} = 1 + \frac{2}{T}\sum_{k=1}^{T-1}(T-k)\rho^{k}.

For \rho = 0, \text{DEFF} = 1 (no inflation). For \rho \to 1 it approaches T, meaning the effective sample size collapses to one observation per region. With T = 10 and \rho = 0.5, \text{DEFF} \approx 2.6, so the correct standard error is about \sqrt{2.6} \approx 1.6 times the iid one — a t-statistic reported as 3.2 is really about 2.0. This is the arithmetic behind the over-rejection in the simulation, and behind the Bertrand–Duflo–Mullainathan 45% false-positive rate. The cluster-robust estimator recovers the full bracketed quantity directly from the data, without assuming any particular \rho.

library(fixest)

# --- Simulate a staggered falls-program panel: 21 regions x 10 years ---
set.seed(2024)
N <- 21; T <- 10; beta_true <- -2.0; rho <- 0.6
adopt <- sample(c(rep(Inf, 7), sample(3:8, N - 7, replace = TRUE)))  # ~1/3 never adopt

panel <- do.call(rbind, lapply(1:N, function(i) {
  eps <- numeric(T); eps[1] <- rnorm(1, 0, 3)
  for (t in 2:T) eps[t] <- rho * eps[t - 1] + rnorm(1, 0, 3 * sqrt(1 - rho^2))
  x     <- as.integer(seq_len(T) >= adopt[i])
  alpha <- 8 + 2 * sin(i)                       # region fixed effect
  data.frame(region = i, year = 2013 + 1:T,
             treat = x, y = alpha + beta_true * x + eps)
}))

N, T, beta_true, rho = 21, 10, -2.0, 0.6

rng.shuffle(adopt)

rows = []
for i in range(N):
    eps = np.empty(T); eps[0] = rng.normal(0, 3)
    for t in range(1, T):
        eps[t] = rho * eps[t - 1] + rng.normal(0, 3 * np.sqrt(1 - rho**2))
    x = (np.arange(1, T + 1) >= adopt[i]).astype(int)
    alpha = 8 + 2 * np.sin(i)                       # region fixed effect
    for t in range(T):
        rows.append((i, 2014 + t, x[t], alpha + beta_true * x[t] + eps[t]))

panel = pd.DataFrame(rows, columns=["region", "year", "treat", "y"])

Exercises

Exercise 1

A researcher fits a fixed-effects regression of fall-hospitalization rates on a falls-program indicator across 21 regions over 10 years. Errors follow an AR(1) process with \rho = 0.5, and the program indicator is highly persistent within regions. The iid standard error is reported as 0.40, yielding \hat\beta = -2.0 and a t-statistic of -5.0.

  1. Using the closed-form design effect from the Math Details with T = 10 and \rho = 0.5, estimate how much larger the correct (clustered) standard error should be.
  2. What is the corrected t-statistic?
  3. With only 21 clusters, what reference distribution should you use, and does the conclusion change?
Show answer

(a) The design effect is \text{DEFF} = 1 + \frac{2}{T}\sum_{k=1}^{T-1}(T-k)\rho^{k}. With T = 10, \rho = 0.5:

\sum_{k=1}^{9}(10-k)(0.5)^k = 9(0.5) + 8(0.25) + 7(0.125) + 6(0.0625) + 5(0.03125) + 4(0.015625) + 3(0.0078125) + 2(0.00390625) + 1(0.001953125).

Term by term: 4.5 + 2.0 + 0.875 + 0.375 + 0.15625 + 0.0625 + 0.0234375 + 0.0078125 + 0.001953125 \approx 8.0020.

So \text{DEFF} = 1 + \frac{2}{10}(8.0020) = 1 + 1.6004 \approx 2.60. The variance is inflated by \approx 2.60, so the standard error is larger by \sqrt{2.60} \approx 1.61. The corrected SE is approximately 0.40 \times 1.61 = 0.645.

(b) The corrected t-statistic is -2.0 / 0.645 \approx -3.10 — substantially smaller in magnitude than the naive -5.0, but still well beyond conventional thresholds.

(c) With G = 21 clusters, compare to a t-distribution with G - 1 = 20 degrees of freedom rather than the normal. The 97.5th percentile of t_{20} is 2.086. Since |{-3.10}| = 3.10 > 2.086, we still reject the null at the 5% level. The conclusion (a real effect) survives, but the naive analysis overstated the strength of the evidence by reporting |t| = 5.0 when the honest figure is closer to 3.1.

Exercise 2

Explain why clustering at the region level (rather than the region-year level, or the patient level) is the appropriate choice for evaluating the regionwide falls program. Then describe a scenario in which clustering at the region level would be insufficient and you would need to cluster more coarsely.

Show answer

Why region: The cluster should be the level at which (i) treatment is assigned and (ii) errors are correlated. The falls program is adopted region-wide, so all of a region’s observations share the same treatment timing, and a region’s idiosyncratic shocks (a heat wave, a hospital reorganization, a reporting change) persist across its years. Clustering by region allows arbitrary correlation among all of a region’s region-year errors. Clustering by region-year would treat each region-year as its own cluster — which is just the heteroskedasticity-robust (iid-across-time) estimator and ignores the serial correlation entirely. Clustering by patient would be finer than the treatment-assignment level and would again fail to capture the region-level dependence.

When region is insufficient: Suppose several regions share a common shock — for instance, a national guideline from Socialstyrelsen changes fall-risk coding in the same year for all regions in a macro-area, or neighboring regions coordinate their programs and their outcomes co-move. Then errors are correlated across regions within that grouping, violating the across-cluster independence that region-level clustering assumes. You would need to cluster more coarsely (e.g., by macro-region or by a national-shock grouping), or use two-way clustering (region and year) so that common year shocks are absorbed. The guiding principle is unchanged: cluster at the coarsest level along which you cannot defend independent draws.

Exercise 3

A region adopts the falls program at all of its vårdcentraler simultaneously, but the clinics with the worst pre-program fall rates are subsequently closed and merged into better-performing ones, so they disappear from the panel after the program starts. The analyst runs a fixed-effects regression on the resulting unbalanced panel and finds the program “reduced” fall rates substantially. Is this estimate trustworthy? Explain the mechanism and propose a diagnostic.

Show answer

Not trustworthy — this is selection-on-the-outcome attrition. The clinics that exit are exactly those with the worst realized outcomes (\varepsilon_{it} large and adverse). Their disappearance after the program starts mechanically lowers the post-period average fall rate among the surviving clinics, even if the program itself did nothing. The within transformation demeans each surviving clinic over the periods it is present, but the composition of survivors changed in a way correlated with the outcome path — so the estimated drop conflates a true program effect with the removal of high-rate units. This violates the benign-attrition condition: the probability of being observed depends on the realized error.

Diagnostic: Compare the estimate on the full unbalanced panel against the estimate on the balanced subpanel (clinics present in all periods, before and after). If the balanced-subpanel estimate is much smaller (or vanishes), selective attrition is driving the result. A complementary check is to model the probability of remaining in the panel as a function of pre-program outcomes; if exit is strongly predicted by bad prior outcomes, reweight by the inverse probability of being observed, or restrict to the balanced panel and report both. One should also test whether attrition is differential by treatment timing, which is the specific threat to a panel/DiD design.

Exercise 4

Using the interactive simulation, set \rho = 0 and note the coverage of both methods. Then set \rho = 0.8. (a) Predict, before reading off the scoreboard, what happens to iid coverage and to clustered coverage. (b) The point estimate \hat\beta stays centered near -2.0 in both cases — why does serial correlation hurt coverage but not consistency? (c) The clustered SE is larger than the iid SE under high \rho. In a real study with G = 21, why might even the clustered interval still be slightly too narrow?

Show answer

(a) At \rho = 0, both methods cover at about 95% — with independent errors the iid formula is correct and clustering is unnecessary (it should give nearly the same width). At \rho = 0.8, iid coverage collapses (typically to 70% or lower) because the iid SE ignores the large positive off-diagonal covariances and is far too small, so its intervals are too narrow and frequently miss the truth. Clustered coverage stays near 95% because the sandwich estimator absorbs the within-region covariances directly, widening the intervals appropriately.

(b) Consistency concerns whether \hat\beta converges to the true \beta; coverage concerns whether the interval around \hat\beta is the right width. Serial correlation does not bias the within estimator — the demeaned regressor is still uncorrelated with the error in expectation, so \hat\beta \to \beta. What serial correlation changes is the sampling variance of \hat\beta: persistent errors mean fewer effective independent observations, so \hat\beta bounces around more from sample to sample. The iid formula mis-measures that bounce. Thus the center (consistency) is fine while the spread (coverage) is wrong.

(c) With only G = 21 clusters, the cluster-robust estimator is itself biased downward in finite samples: the residuals \hat u_g are shrunk by estimation of the many fixed effects, and the normal reference distribution is too thin-tailed. Even after the sandwich widens the interval, \pm 1.96 understates the true critical value. Using t_{G-1} (here t_{20}, with critical value 2.086) or, better, a wild cluster bootstrap restores the remaining shortfall in coverage.

Key takeaways

  • Serial correlation does not bias \hat\beta — it corrupts the standard error; iid SEs treat each region-year as independent and can understate uncertainty by a factor of \sqrt{\text{DEFF}} (about 1.6 when T=10, \rho=0.5).
  • Cluster at the level where treatment is assigned and errors are correlated — here the region — which changes only the standard error, never the coefficient, and encodes the true effective sample size.
  • With few clusters (G \approx 21), do not compare t-statistics to \pm 1.96; use t_{G-1} critical values and prefer the wild cluster bootstrap.
  • Unbalanced panels are handled automatically, but attrition driven by the realized outcome biases \hat\beta — compare the full panel to the balanced subpanel as a diagnostic.
  • Fixed effects trades efficiency for robustness against \alpha_i-confounding; default to it in observational health-policy data and reserve random effects for when \operatorname{Cov}(\alpha_i, x_{it}) = 0 is defensible.

We now have the full panel toolkit: the within transformation that purges time-invariant confounders, and the cluster-robust inference that keeps our standard errors honest when a unit’s errors persist over time. The natural next step is the design that fuses both ideas into a single, transparent identification strategy. When some units adopt a policy and others do not, and we observe both groups before and after, the comparison of changes — treated-group change minus control-group change — isolates the causal effect under a parallel-trends assumption.
