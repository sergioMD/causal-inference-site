Interpretation and External Validity

The effect at the cutoff and what it does (and does not) tell you

In this section, you will learn to

  • State precisely what a regression discontinuity (RD) design estimates: a local average treatment effect at the cutoff, \tau_{\text{RD}} = E[\tau_i \mid X_i = c], and in fuzzy RD, the effect for compliers at the cutoff.
  • Distinguish the RD estimand from the population ATE, and explain why they coincide only under restrictive homogeneity conditions.
  • Read a heterogeneous treatment-effect curve along the running variable and see that RD recovers exactly one point on it — the value at c — and nothing about the rest.
  • Reason about when extrapolating the cutoff effect to other parts of the population is defensible, and name recent methods that attempt principled extrapolation.
  • Reframe the “local” limitation as a policy feature: the cutoff is precisely the margin a policymaker can move.

The Right Answer to a Narrow Question

The previous page on validity and falsification taught you how to defend an RD design: check that the running variable is not manipulated, that covariates are continuous at the cutoff, that the jump survives bandwidth and polynomial choices. Suppose all of those tests pass. You have a credible, well-identified estimate. The natural reaction is relief — and then a dangerous temptation: to treat that number as the effect of the program.

It is not. It is the effect of the program at one point on the running variable — the cutoff — and the central interpretive discipline of RD is to never forget that qualifier.

Return to a concrete Swedish setting, but a fresh one. A region (one of Sweden’s 21 regional health authorities) runs a structured diabetes coordination program (Diabetessamordning) out of its vårdcentraler (primary care centers): a dedicated distriktssköterska (district nurse) coordinates appointments, titrates medication, and tracks blood sugar for the most at-risk patients. Capacity is finite, so eligibility is rationed by a clinical risk score X_i — a composite built from HbA1c, blood pressure, BMI, and comorbidity flags, normalized so that the enrollment threshold sits at c = 0. Patients with a score at or above the cutoff (X_i \ge 0) are enrolled; those below are not. The outcome Y_i is each patient’s HbA1c twelve months later (lower is better).

RD will hand us a clean estimate of the program’s effect on the HbA1c of patients whose risk score is right at the threshold. That is genuinely valuable. But the regiondirektör (regional health director) who reads the report will almost certainly ask a different question — “should we expand the program to lower-risk patients?” or “what would it do for the whole diabetic population?” — and the honest answer is that the RD estimate, by itself, does not say.

RD buys exceptional internal validity (a credible answer for the units at the cutoff) at the cost of external validity (silence about units far from the cutoff). It is the mirror image of a representative survey, which has broad coverage but weak identification. Knowing which trade you have made is the whole of interpretation.

What RD Estimates: The Effect at the Cutoff

Recall the potential-outcomes machinery from Module 1: each patient has an individual treatment effect \tau_i = Y_i(1) - Y_i(0), and these effects vary across patients (heterogeneity). The sharp RD estimand is the jump in the conditional mean of the outcome at the cutoff. Under the continuity assumption, that jump equals a very specific average of the \tau_i.

Under continuity of E[Y_i(0)\mid X_i = x] and E[Y_i(1)\mid X_i = x] at c, the sharp RD estimand is the average treatment effect at the cutoff:

\tau_{\text{RD}} \;=\; \lim_{x \downarrow c} E[Y_i \mid X_i = x] - \lim_{x \uparrow c} E[Y_i \mid X_i = x] \;=\; E\big[\,Y_i(1) - Y_i(0) \;\big|\; X_i = c\,\big].

Read this as: “The RD estimand equals the average, among patients whose running variable sits exactly at the cutoff, of the difference between their treated and untreated potential outcomes.” It is a conditional average treatment effect — conditional on X_i = c — not a population average.

(An estimand is simply the quantity you are trying to learn — the target of estimation, here a specific average effect — as opposed to the estimator or the eventual number. And \lim_{x \downarrow c}, a one-sided limit, is “the height the curve approaches as you slide in toward the cutoff from the right (the treated side)”; \lim_{x \uparrow c} is the same from the left.)

This is a CATE (conditional average treatment effect) evaluated at a single value of the conditioning variable. Two things follow immediately.

First, RD never reveals \tau_i for any individual — it is still an average, subject to the fundamental problem of causal inference. Second, and more importantly for this page, RD averages the \tau_i over a vanishingly thin slice of the population: patients with X_i \approx c. If the treatment effect is the same for everyone, that slice tells you everything. If the treatment effect varies along X — which is the realistic case — the slice tells you about the slice, and only the slice.

The cutoff effect versus the ATE

Define the population average treatment effect in the usual way, \text{ATE} = E[\tau_i], where the expectation is over the whole distribution of patients. Write the treatment-effect curve as the function

\tau(x) \;=\; E\big[\,Y_i(1) - Y_i(0) \;\big|\; X_i = x\,\big],

which gives the average effect at each level of the risk score. Then RD recovers exactly one value of this curve,

\tau_{\text{RD}} = \tau(c),

while the ATE is the average of the whole curve, weighted by the density f_X of the running variable:

\text{ATE} \;=\; \int \tau(x)\, f_X(x)\, dx.

Read the last line as: “The population ATE is the treatment-effect curve \tau(x) averaged over the entire distribution of risk scores.” Concretely, the integral adds up the effect at every risk score, weighting each by how many patients actually have that score — the density f_X is just “how common each score is.” RD gives you the single height \tau(c); the ATE wants the (density-weighted) area under \tau(x). These coincide only in special cases — when \tau(x) is flat (constant treatment effects, so \tau(x) = \tau everywhere), or by coincidence if the cutoff happens to fall where \tau(x) crosses its own population mean. In general,

\tau_{\text{RD}} = \tau(c) \;\neq\; \int \tau(x)\, f_X(x)\, dx = \text{ATE}.

The extrapolation trap. The most common abuse of RD is to quote \tau_{\text{RD}} as if it were the ATE — “the diabetes program lowers HbA1c by 0.4 points” full stop, with no mention of for whom. If the program helps the highest-risk patients a great deal but barely touches moderate-risk patients, then \tau(c) (a moderate-risk margin) understates the effect for the sickest and overstates it for the healthiest. The number is correct; the sentence that drops the qualifier is wrong.

The picture below makes the “one height versus the weighted area” contrast literal. The slate curve is the effect curve \tau(x); the shaded hill beneath it is the density f_X (how common each risk score is). rdinterp_tau = function (x) {
  const h = rdinterp_hetero;
  const base = rdinterp_meanEffect;
  if (rdinterp_curveShape === "Flat (constant effect)") {
    return base;                                  // τ(x) = constant
  }
  if (rdinterp_curveShape === "Hump (mid-risk gain most)") {
    // Sickest benefit most at moderate risk, less at the extremes.
    return base - h * 0.6 * Math.exp(-0.5 * (x * x));
  }
  // Monotone: sicker patients (higher x) gain more (more-negative τ).
  return base - h * 0.45 * x;
}

rdinterp_tauCurve = d3.range(rdinterp_xMin, rdinterp_xMax + 1e-9, 0.05)

// curve by the standard normal density and renormalize on the grid.
rdinterp_ate = {
  const grid = rdinterp_tauCurve;
  const phi = x => Math.exp(-0.5 * x * x);        // unnormalized N(0,1) density
  let wsum = 0, num = 0;
  for (const g of grid) {
    const w = phi(g.x);
    wsum += w;
    num += w * g.tau;
  }
  return num / wsum;
}

// OLS helper: fit y = a + b*(x − c) on a set of rows, centered at the cutoff
// so the intercept `a` is the predicted value AT the cutoff.
rdinterp_ols = function (rows, c) {
  const n = rows.length;
  const mx = d3.mean(rows, d => d.x - c);
  const my = d3.mean(rows, d => d.y);
  let sxx = 0, sxy = 0;
  for (const r of rows) {
    const xc = (r.x - c) - mx;
    sxx += xc * xc;
    sxy += xc * (r.y - my);
  }
  const b = sxy / sxx;
  const a = my - b * mx;
  return { a, b, predict: x => a + b * (x - c) };
}
rdinterp_bw = 1.2

rdinterp_fits = {
  const c = rdinterp_cutoff;
  const inWin = d => Math.abs(d.x - c) <= rdinterp_bw;
  const left  = rdinterp_data.filter(d => d.treated === 0 && inWin(d));
  const right = rdinterp_data.filter(d => d.treated === 1 && inWin(d));
  // Guard: if a side is empty (cutoff at an extreme), fall back to all of it.
  const L = left.length  >= 2 ? left  : rdinterp_data.filter(d => d.treated === 0);
  const R = right.length >= 2 ? right : rdinterp_data.filter(d => d.treated === 1);
  const fitL = rdinterp_ols(L, c);
  const fitR = rdinterp_ols(R, c);
  return {
    left: fitL, right: fitR,
    limitLeft: fitL.predict(c),
    limitRight: fitR.predict(c),
    jump: fitR.predict(c) - fitL.predict(c),
    bw: rdinterp_bw
  };
}

How to Read the Effect-Curve Explorer

Work through the panels in order; each step isolates one idea about locality.

Step 1: Read the two panels as a pair. The top panel is the truth you would love to know — the full effect curve \tau(x), slate, with the population ATE as a black dashed line. The bottom panel is the data you actually have — noisy HbA1c against risk score, with two local lines fitted on each side of the cutoff. The red dot in the top panel and the shaded jump in the bottom panel are the same quantity viewed two ways: the effect at the cutoff. (The cutoff c is the ochre dashed line in both panels.)

Step 2: Confirm RD hits its target. With the default monotone curve, read the three tiles below the plot. The RD estimate (the jump) and the τ(c) it targets agree up to noise — RD is unbiased for the local effect. Press Draw new sample a few times: the jump jitters around τ(c), never around the ATE.

Step 3: Slide the cutoff and watch “local” become literal. Drag the Cutoff c slider slowly from left to right. The red dot in the top panel rides along the curve \tau(x), and the jump in the bottom panel grows and shrinks to match. This is the entire lesson in one motion: RD reports \tau evaluated wherever the administrative line happens to fall. Move the line, move the answer — even though the program and the patients are unchanged.

Step 4: Compare local to global. Keep an eye on the gap between the red dot (τ(c)) and the black dashed ATE line. Park the cutoff at the far right (sickest patients): with the monotone curve, the local effect is far more beneficial than the ATE, so reporting it as “the” effect would overstate the program’s average value. Park it at the far left and the opposite happens. The one-line verdict box spells out the direction of the error.

Step 5: Turn off heterogeneity. Drag Effect heterogeneity to 0 (or pick the Flat shape). Now \tau(x) is a horizontal line: the local effect equals the ATE for every cutoff. The verdict turns green. This is the only situation in which RD generalizes for free — and it is exactly the assumption (constant effects) you are usually unwilling to make.

Step 6: Try the hump. Switch to Hump (mid-risk gain most). Now the program helps moderate-risk patients most and the extremes least. Slide the cutoff across the hump: the local effect peaks in the middle and fades at both tails. A policymaker who set the threshold in the tails would conclude the program barely works — a conclusion about where they drew the line, not about the program.

Fuzzy RD: Local and for Compliers

Everything above assumed sharp RD, where crossing the cutoff changes treatment with certainty. In the more common fuzzy RD, crossing the cutoff changes only the probability of treatment: some eligible patients decline the diabetes program, some ineligible ones get in through a clinician’s discretion. As shown on the fuzzy-RD page, the estimand becomes a ratio (a local Wald / instrumental-variables estimand), and its interpretation narrows further.

The fuzzy RD estimand identifies a local average treatment effect for compliers at the cutoff:

\tau_{\text{fuzzy}} \;=\; \frac{\displaystyle \lim_{x\downarrow c} E[Y\mid X{=}x] - \lim_{x\uparrow c} E[Y\mid X{=}x]}{\displaystyle \lim_{x\downarrow c} E[D\mid X{=}x] - \lim_{x\uparrow c} E[D\mid X{=}x]} \;=\; E\big[\tau_i \;\big|\; X_i = c,\ i \text{ is a complier}\big].

Read this as: “The fuzzy RD estimand is the jump in the outcome divided by the jump in the treatment probability, and it equals the average effect, at the cutoff, restricted to the patients who actually take up treatment because they crossed the threshold — the compliers.”

So fuzzy RD layers a second restriction on top of locality. It is local in X (only patients near c) and local in compliance type (only compliers — not always-takers who would enroll regardless, nor never-takers who would refuse regardless). A complier here is a patient who enrolls because they crossed the cutoff and would not have enrolled had they fallen just below — the only type whose behavior actually responds to the threshold. The director who wants to know the effect for “all diabetic patients” is now two generalizations removed from the estimate: from compliers-at-c to everyone-at-c to the whole population.

When Is Extrapolation Defensible?

The “local” critique is not a counsel of despair. There are disciplined ways to reach beyond the cutoff — but each one buys generalization by adding an assumption, and you must be able to defend that assumption on substantive grounds.

Routes to (cautious) external validity. Extrapolating \tau_{\text{RD}} away from the cutoff is credible only when you can argue one of the following:

  1. Effect homogeneity near c. If \tau(x) is approximately flat in a neighborhood of the cutoff, \tau_{\text{RD}} generalizes to that neighborhood. Defend it by showing the treated and untreated slopes of E[Y\mid X] are similar around c (parallel local trends), or that pre-determined risk factors do not predict \tau.
  2. A modeled effect curve. If you observe the same cutoff effect at several different cutoffs (e.g., a program reorganized over years with different thresholds, or different regions using different scores), you can trace out \tau(x) at multiple points and interpolate. This is the multi-cutoff / “RD with many thresholds” idea (Cattaneo, Keele, Titiunik, Vazquez-Bare 2016, 2021).
  3. Bounds, not points. Rather than assume a shape, derive bounds on the ATE using monotonicity or smoothness restrictions on the potential-outcome functions (Dong & Lewbel 2015 use the derivative of the RD curve — the “treatment effect derivative” — to extrapolate locally; Angrist & Rokkanen 2015 extrapolate by conditioning on covariates that render the running variable ignorable away from c).

Two further remarks keep this honest. The derivative of the RD curve at the cutoff (Dong & Lewbel’s \tau'(c), the treatment effect derivative) tells you the direction and local rate at which the effect changes as you move away from c — a first-order extrapolation that is far more defensible than assuming the effect is constant. And the bandwidth debate from the estimation and bandwidth page is a within-cutoff concern (how wide a window estimates \tau(c) well); it is not a route to external validity. A wider bandwidth does not estimate the ATE — it estimates \tau(c) with different bias-variance properties. Confusing “wide bandwidth” with “population effect” is a common and serious error.

Why “Local” Is Often Exactly What Policy Needs

It is tempting to treat the locality of RD as a pure weakness. For many policy questions it is the opposite — a near-perfect match between what RD estimates and what a policymaker can actually do.

The marginal-policy argument. A region almost never asks “should we treat everyone or no one?” It asks “should we move the threshold?” — enroll patients with risk scores down to -0.2 instead of 0, say. The patients affected by that decision are exactly the ones at the current cutoff. So \tau_{\text{RD}} = \tau(c) is precisely the effect for the marginal patients a threshold change would pull in or push out. RD answers the marginal-expansion question with no extrapolation at all.

Put differently: the cutoff effect is the policy-relevant effect whenever the policy lever is the cutoff itself. If the regiondirektör is deciding whether to lower the enrollment threshold by a notch, the average effect over the whole diabetic population (the ATE) is the wrong number; the effect at the margin (the RD estimate) is the right one. The book’s running theme — match the estimand to the decision (from the potential-outcomes page) — resolves the “local” worry cleanly: locality is a defect only when the decision is global.

But state the decision out loud. The marginal-policy defense works only for marginal decisions. If the report will be used to justify a wholesale rollout to low-risk patients, or to compare the program against an unrelated intervention for the average patient, then \tau_{\text{RD}} is being asked to do a job it cannot do, and you must say so. The same number is policy-perfect for one question and misleading for another.

Math Details: Why τ_RD = τ(c), and the cutoff-vs-ATE gap

The jump is the conditional average effect at c

Write the observed outcome through the switching equation, Y_i = D_i Y_i(1) + (1 - D_i) Y_i(0), and recall that in sharp RD D_i = \mathbf{1}\{X_i \ge c\}. Just to the right of the cutoff everyone is treated, so

\lim_{x \downarrow c} E[Y_i \mid X_i = x] = \lim_{x \downarrow c} E[Y_i(1) \mid X_i = x] = E[Y_i(1)\mid X_i = c],

where the last equality uses right-continuity of x \mapsto E[Y_i(1)\mid X_i = x] at c. Just to the left everyone is untreated, so by left-continuity of E[Y_i(0)\mid X_i = x],

\lim_{x \uparrow c} E[Y_i \mid X_i = x] = E[Y_i(0)\mid X_i = c].

Subtracting,

\tau_{\text{RD}} = E[Y_i(1)\mid X_i = c] - E[Y_i(0)\mid X_i = c] = E[\,Y_i(1) - Y_i(0)\mid X_i = c\,] = \tau(c).

The continuity assumption does the work: it lets the untreated limit (which we observe only below c) stand in for the treated group’s missing untreated potential outcome at c. That is why RD identifies the effect at the cutoff and nowhere else — continuity is a statement about behavior at a single point.

Decomposing the cutoff-vs-ATE gap

Let \mu = \text{ATE} = \int \tau(x) f_X(x)\,dx. The interpretive error of quoting \tau_{\text{RD}} as the ATE is exactly

\tau_{\text{RD}} - \text{ATE} = \tau(c) - \int \tau(x)\, f_X(x)\, dx .

A first-order Taylor expansion of \tau(x) around the population mean score \bar{X} = E[X] makes the drivers explicit. Writing \tau(x) \approx \tau(\bar X) + \tau'(\bar X)(x - \bar X), the ATE is approximately \tau(\bar X) (the linear term integrates against the mean-zero deviation x - \bar X to zero), so

\tau_{\text{RD}} - \text{ATE} \;\approx\; \tau'(\bar X)\,(c - \bar X).

Read this as: “The gap between the cutoff effect and the population ATE is approximately the slope of the effect curve times the distance from the cutoff to the average patient.” Two conditions make the gap vanish: a flat effect curve (\tau' = 0, i.e., no heterogeneity), or a cutoff that sits at the average patient (c = \bar X). Move the cutoff into the tail of the score distribution while effects are heterogeneous, and the gap grows linearly — precisely the behavior you see when you drag the cutoff slider in the interactive.

The treatment-effect derivative

Dong & Lewbel (2015) note that with a richer local fit you can identify not just \tau(c) but its derivative \tau'(c) at the cutoff, by contrasting the slopes of E[Y\mid X] on the two sides:

\tau'(c) = \lim_{x\downarrow c}\frac{d}{dx}E[Y\mid X{=}x] - \lim_{x\uparrow c}\frac{d}{dx}E[Y\mid X{=}x].

This gives a local linear extrapolation of the effect, \tau(c + \delta) \approx \tau(c) + \tau'(c)\,\delta, valid for small \delta — a principled half-step beyond the cutoff that does not require assuming the effect is constant.

Estimating and Interpreting the Local Effect

The code below simulates the diabetes-coordination RD with a heterogeneous effect curve, estimates the local effect at the cutoff with rdrobust, and then — crucially — contrasts it with the population ATE that a researcher would wrongly report if they ignored heterogeneity. The gap between the two is the entire lesson of this page, made numerical.

  • Python
library(rdrobust)
library(tidyverse)

set.seed(70)
n <- 4000

# Running variable: normalized clinical risk score, ~ N(0, 1), cutoff c = 0.
x <- rnorm(n)
c <- 0
treated <- as.integer(x >= c)

# Untreated potential outcome: HbA1c rises with risk.
y0 <- 7.0 + 0.55 * (x + 3) + rnorm(n, 0, 0.2)

# HETEROGENEOUS effect: sicker patients (higher x) gain more (more-negative tau).
tau_x <- -0.45 - 0.45 * x            # tau(x): the true effect curve
y1 <- y0 + tau_x
y  <- ifelse(treated == 1, y1, y0)   # observed outcome

# --- RD estimate: the local effect at the cutoff ---
rd <- rdrobust(y = y, x = x, c = c)
tau_rd <- rd$coef["Conventional", 1]

# What RD TARGETS: tau(c) = tau evaluated at x = 0.
tau_at_cutoff <- -0.45 - 0.45 * 0    # = -0.45

# The population ATE: average of tau(x) over the WHOLE score distribution.
ate <- mean(tau_x)

cat(sprintf("RD estimate (local, at cutoff): %.3f\n", tau_rd))
cat(sprintf("Targets tau(c) = tau(0):        %.3f\n", tau_at_cutoff))
cat(sprintf("Population ATE (whole curve):    %.3f\n", ate))
cat(sprintf("Interpretive gap (RD - ATE):     %.3f\n", tau_rd - ate))

# Lesson: tau_rd ~ tau(c) = -0.45, but ATE ~ -0.45 here only because the mean
# score is ~0 = c. Shift the cutoff (e.g., c <- 1) and RD tracks tau(1) = -0.90
# while the ATE is unchanged -- "local" and "global" diverge.
import numpy as np
from rdrobust import rdrobust

rng = np.random.default_rng(70)
n = 4000

# Running variable: normalized clinical risk score, ~ N(0, 1), cutoff c = 0.
x = rng.normal(size=n)
c = 0.0
treated = (x >= c).astype(int)

# Untreated potential outcome: HbA1c rises with risk.
y0 = 7.0 + 0.55 * (x + 3) + rng.normal(0, 0.2, n)

# HETEROGENEOUS effect: sicker patients (higher x) gain more (more-negative tau).
tau_x = -0.45 - 0.45 * x              # tau(x): the true effect curve
y1 = y0 + tau_x
y  = np.where(treated == 1, y1, y0)   # observed outcome

# --- RD estimate: the local effect at the cutoff ---
rd = rdrobust(y=y, x=x, c=c)
tau_rd = rd.coef.loc["Conventional"].iloc[0]

# What RD TARGETS: tau(c) = tau evaluated at x = 0.
tau_at_cutoff = -0.45 - 0.45 * 0.0    # = -0.45

# The population ATE: average of tau(x) over the WHOLE score distribution.
ate = tau_x.mean()

print(f"RD estimate (local, at cutoff): {tau_rd:.3f}")
print(f"Targets tau(c) = tau(0):        {tau_at_cutoff:.3f}")
print(f"Population ATE (whole curve):    {ate:.3f}")
print(f"Interpretive gap (RD - ATE):     {tau_rd - ate:.3f}")

# Lesson: tau_rd ~ tau(c) = -0.45, but ATE ~ -0.45 here only because the mean
# score is ~0 = c. Shift the cutoff (e.g., c = 1) and RD tracks tau(1) = -0.90

Both implementations return an RD estimate close to \tau(c) = -0.45 — the local effect for marginal patients — not the effect for the sickest patients (\tau(2) = -1.35) nor the healthiest (\tau(-2) = +0.45, i.e., a slight harm). The single number is correct and useful; the sentence “the program lowers HbA1c by 0.45 points” is true only at the cutoff.

Exercises

Exercise 1

A region evaluates its diabetes coordination program with a sharp RD at risk-score cutoff c = 0 and reports a clean estimate of \tau_{\text{RD}} = -0.50 HbA1c points. The true effect curve is \tau(x) = -0.50 - 0.40\,x (more-negative = more beneficial; higher x = sicker), and the risk score is distributed standard normal across the diabetic population. (a) What is the program’s average effect for patients at risk score x = 1.5? At x = -1.5? (b) What is the population ATE? (c) The regiondirektör concludes “the program cuts HbA1c by half a point for our diabetic patients.” For which patients is this exactly right, and for which is it wrong?

Show answer

(a) Plug into the curve. At x = 1.5: \tau(1.5) = -0.50 - 0.40(1.5) = -0.50 - 0.60 = -1.10 — the sickest patients benefit by 1.10 HbA1c points, more than twice the cutoff effect. At x = -1.5: \tau(-1.5) = -0.50 - 0.40(-1.5) = -0.50 + 0.60 = +0.10 — for the healthiest patients the program is slightly harmful (HbA1c rises by 0.10).

(b) The ATE is E[\tau(X)] = E[-0.50 - 0.40 X] = -0.50 - 0.40\,E[X]. With a standard normal score E[X] = 0, so \text{ATE} = -0.50. (Here the ATE coincides with \tau_{\text{RD}} only because the cutoff sits at the mean score.)

(c) The statement “-0.50” is exactly right for patients at the cutoff (x = 0), and — in this particular case — it happens to equal the population average too, because c = \bar X = 0. But it is wrong for almost every individual group: it understates the benefit for high-risk patients (-1.10 at x = 1.5) and overstates it for low-risk patients (where the program may even harm). The director’s sentence is defensible as a statement about the average and about the marginal patient, but not as a description of what any particular non-marginal patient should expect.

Exercise 2

Using the same curve \tau(x) = -0.50 - 0.40\,x with X \sim N(0,1), suppose the region raises the enrollment threshold to c = 1.0 (rationing the program to only the sickest). (a) What does a valid RD design now estimate? (b) Has the population ATE changed? (c) Use the Taylor approximation \tau_{\text{RD}} - \text{ATE} \approx \tau'(\bar X)(c - \bar X) to predict the gap, and compare it to the exact gap.

Show answer

(a) RD now estimates \tau(c) = \tau(1.0) = -0.50 - 0.40(1.0) = -0.90. Moving the administrative line moved the answer, even though neither the program nor the patients changed — RD always reports \tau at whatever the cutoff is.

(b) No. The ATE is a property of the whole population and the curve, \text{ATE} = -0.50 - 0.40\,E[X] = -0.50, independent of where the cutoff sits. Changing c changes who is at the margin, not the population average.

(c) The curve is linear, so \tau'(x) = -0.40 everywhere and \bar X = 0. The approximation gives \tau_{\text{RD}} - \text{ATE} \approx -0.40 \times (1.0 - 0) = -0.40. The exact gap is \tau(1.0) - \text{ATE} = -0.90 - (-0.50) = -0.40. They match exactly, because for a linear effect curve the first-order Taylor expansion is the function itself — the approximation is only approximate when \tau(x) is curved.

Exercise 3

A fuzzy RD at c = 0 estimates the effect of the diabetes program where crossing the cutoff raises the enrollment probability from 0.15 (below) to 0.80 (above). The estimated jump in mean HbA1c at the cutoff is -0.39. (a) Compute the fuzzy RD estimate. (b) State, precisely, the subpopulation for which this is the average effect. (c) Why is this estimate two generalizations removed from “the effect for all diabetic patients in the region”?

Show answer

(a) The fuzzy RD estimate is the ratio of the outcome jump to the treatment-probability jump:

\tau_{\text{fuzzy}} = \frac{-0.39}{0.80 - 0.15} = \frac{-0.39}{0.65} = -0.60.

(b) It is the average effect for compliers at the cutoff — patients with risk score X_i \approx 0 who enroll in the program because they crossed the threshold (and would not have enrolled had they fallen just below). It is not the effect for always-takers (who would enroll regardless, e.g., through clinician discretion) nor never-takers (who decline regardless).

(c) First generalization: from compliers at the cutoff to all patients at the cutoff — requires that compliers’ effects equal always-/never-takers’ effects at c (an effect-homogeneity-across-compliance-type assumption). Second generalization: from all patients at the cutoff to the whole population — requires that \tau(x) is flat (or that you model/extrapolate the curve). Each step needs its own defense; the raw estimate -0.60 supports neither without further argument.

Exercise 4

In the interactive, set the shape to Hump (mid-risk gain most) with high heterogeneity. A colleague argues: “If we just widen the bandwidth enough, RD will average over more of the curve and recover the ATE.” (a) Explain why this is wrong. (b) What does widening the bandwidth change? (c) Propose a design that would let you trace out \tau(x) at several points, and name the assumption it relies on.

Show answer

(a) It is wrong because RD always targets \tau(c) — the effect at the cutoff — regardless of bandwidth. A wider window includes patients farther from c, but their job is to help estimate the limits of the regression function at c (by fitting the local slope), not to shift the estimand toward an average over the curve. As the bandwidth grows, the local-linear fit can become biased for \tau(c) (it starts mistaking curvature for a jump), but it never starts estimating the ATE. “Wide bandwidth” trades variance for bias in estimating \tau(c); it is not a route to external validity.

(b) Widening the bandwidth changes the bias-variance trade-off of the estimator of \tau(c): more data (lower variance) at the cost of including points where the linear approximation to E[Y\mid X] is poorer (higher bias). The optimal bandwidth (e.g., the MSE-optimal choice rdrobust uses) balances these — but the target is fixed at \tau(c) throughout.

(c) A multi-cutoff design: if the program (or comparable programs in different regions) uses several distinct thresholds c_1, c_2, \dots, each one identifies \tau(c_k), and stacking them traces the effect curve at multiple points — which you can then interpolate. The key assumption is that the same effect curve \tau(x) governs all the cutoffs (no other differences across the settings drive the effects), so that the points \{(c_k, \tau(c_k))\} lie on one common curve. (Cattaneo, Keele, Titiunik & Vazquez-Bare 2016/2021.) Alternatively, Angrist & Rokkanen (2015) extrapolate by conditioning on covariates rich enough to make the running variable ignorable away from c.

Key takeaways

  • RD estimates a local effect at the cutoff, \tau_{\text{RD}} = \tau(c) = E[\tau_i \mid X_i = c] — one point on the treatment-effect curve, not the population ATE.
  • In fuzzy RD the estimand narrows further, to the effect for compliers at the cutoff — local in the running variable and in compliance type.
  • \tau_{\text{RD}} = \text{ATE} only when effects are constant (\tau(x) flat) or the cutoff coincidentally sits at the mean score; the gap grows with heterogeneity \tau' and with the distance c - \bar X.
  • Widening the bandwidth changes the bias-variance trade-off in estimating \tau(c); it does not turn the local effect into the ATE.
  • Locality is a defect for global decisions but a feature for marginal ones: the cutoff effect is exactly the effect for the patients a threshold change would move — so match the estimand to the decision.

RD earns its credibility by exploiting a known, sharp assignment rule — and pays for it with a narrowly local estimand. But most observational settings hand us no such rule.
