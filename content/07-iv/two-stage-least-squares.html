Two-Stage Least Squares

The IV estimator, the first stage, and the reduced form

When You Cannot Hold the Confounder Fixed

The previous page, The Endogeneity Problem, left us in an uncomfortable place. We wanted to know whether assigning each Type 2 diabetes patient a dedicated distriktssköterska (district nurse) for structured follow-up — measured as nurse contact hours per patient per year — actually lowers their HbA1c. But the number of contact hours a patient receives is endogenous: the sickest, most engaged, most anxious patients demand and receive the most nursing time, and those same patients differ from everyone else in ways that also move their blood sugar. The regression of HbA1c on nursing hours conflates the effect of the nursing with the fact that high-need patients selected into high nursing intensity. OLS is biased, and no amount of data fixes it, because the bias is baked into the data-generating process.

The instrumental-variables (IV) strategy is a way out that does not require us to observe and adjust for the confounder. Instead, it requires us to find a third variable — an instrument — that nudges nursing intensity around for reasons that have nothing to do with the patient’s underlying health. If we can find such a lever, we can isolate the part of the variation in nursing hours that is “as good as random” and use only that part to estimate the causal effect.

Our instrument on this page is distance to the nearest staffed vårdcentral, in kilometres. A patient who lives 2 km from a well-staffed primary-care centre finds it cheap to drop in for an extra nurse visit; a patient 25 km away, on a rural route with infrequent transit, racks up fewer contact hours for the same level of need. Distance pushes nursing intensity around. And — this is the bet we are making — distance does not affect a patient’s HbA1c through any channel other than the nursing it enables. This page is about the estimator you build once you have such an instrument: two-stage least squares (2SLS). The page after this one, Relevance and Exclusion, interrogates whether those two bets are credible. Here, we take them as given and learn the machinery.

In this section, you will learn to

  • Construct the IV estimator from a first stage (regress the endogenous regressor on the instrument) and a reduced form (regress the outcome on the instrument), and read off \hat\beta_{IV} as their ratio.
  • State and apply the Wald formula \hat\beta_{IV} = \mathrm{Cov}(z,y)/\mathrm{Cov}(z,x) and explain why it equals the reduced-form slope divided by the first-stage slope.
  • Explain the “two-stage” interpretation — fit \hat x, then regress y on \hat x — and why running it by hand gives the wrong standard errors.
  • Distinguish the just-identified case (one instrument, one endogenous regressor) from the over-identified case, and name the estimand that IV recovers under heterogeneous effects (a forward reference to LATE).

The Three Regressions Behind One Estimate

Fix notation for the rest of the page. For patient i:

  • y_i is the outcome — HbA1c at 12-month follow-up, in percentage points (lower is better).
  • x_i is the endogenous regressor — nurse contact hours per year. We want the causal effect of x on y.
  • z_i is the instrument — distance to the nearest staffed vårdcentral, in km.
  • u_i is everything else that moves HbA1c and is correlated with x: baseline severity, health literacy, diet, comorbidities. This is the unobserved confounder.

The picture below shows why IV works before any algebra. The confounder u (the unobserved fork) opens a back-door path — a non-causal route linking nursing and HbA1c — so the raw x–y association is contaminated. The instrument z sidesteps it: distance pushes nursing (z \to x, green) but reaches HbA1c only through nursing. The crossed-out z \to y arrow is the exclusion restriction — the assumption that distance has no direct path to blood sugar — and the missing z \to u link is instrument exogeneity. Those two missing arrows are exactly what let us read the effect of x on y off the instrument.

Color key: green = open causal path (the channel we want); red = the open back-door the confounder u creates; grey dashed line struck through with a red ✕ = the forbidden direct z \to y path ruled out by the exclusion restriction. The instrument z is also assumed unconnected to u (no arrow between them). IV exploits the green z \to x \to y pipe while the red fork stays untouched.

The structural equation we care about — the one with the causal parameter \beta — is

y_i = \alpha + \beta\, x_i + u_i .

Read this as: “A patient’s follow-up HbA1c equals a baseline level \alpha, plus \beta for every additional hour of nurse contact, plus an idiosyncratic term u_i that bundles all the unobserved drivers of blood sugar.” The trouble, again, is that x_i and u_i are correlated — \mathrm{Cov}(x,u)\neq 0 — so OLS of y on x does not return \beta.

IV builds \hat\beta out of two auxiliary regressions on the instrument, plus the structural equation that ties them together.

The first stage

The first stage regresses the endogenous regressor on the instrument:

x_i = \pi_0 + \pi_1\, z_i + v_i .

Read this as: “Nurse contact hours equal a constant \pi_0, plus \pi_1 hours for each additional kilometre of distance, plus a residual v_i.” Because farther patients get less nursing, we expect \pi_1 < 0. The slope \pi_1 is the instrument strength (or relevance): it measures how hard the instrument pushes on x. A big |\pi_1| means distance is a powerful lever; \pi_1 = 0 means distance does nothing and IV is hopeless. The fitted values \hat x_i = \hat\pi_0 + \hat\pi_1 z_i are the “predicted nursing intensity from distance alone” — the slice of nursing variation that is driven by geography rather than by patient health.

The reduced form

The reduced form regresses the outcome directly on the instrument, skipping x entirely:

y_i = \gamma_0 + \gamma_1\, z_i + w_i .

Read this as: “Follow-up HbA1c equals a constant plus \gamma_1 for each kilometre of distance, plus a residual.” The slope \gamma_1 is the total effect of distance on HbA1c. If distance only matters because it changes nursing, then \gamma_1 is “the effect of distance on nursing, times the effect of nursing on HbA1c” — a composition of the first stage and the structural effect. We expect \gamma_1 > 0: living farther means less nursing means worse (higher) HbA1c.

The IV estimate is their ratio

Substitute the first stage into the structural equation. Since the only part of x that is uncorrelated with u is the part driven by z, the effect of z on y must run entirely through x:

\gamma_1 \;=\; \beta \cdot \pi_1 .

Read this as: “The reduced-form slope equals the structural effect times the first-stage slope — distance moves HbA1c only by moving nursing.” Solve for the one unknown we want:

The instrumental-variables (Wald) estimator of \beta is the ratio of the reduced form to the first stage:

\hat\beta_{IV} \;=\; \frac{\hat\gamma_1}{\hat\pi_1} \;=\; \frac{\widehat{\mathrm{Cov}}(z,y)}{\widehat{\mathrm{Cov}}(z,x)} .

Read this as: “The IV estimate of the effect of nursing on HbA1c is how much distance moves HbA1c, divided by how much distance moves nursing.” Equivalently, it is the sample covariance of the instrument with the outcome divided by the sample covariance of the instrument with the regressor. The two forms are identical because a simple-regression slope of a on b is exactly \mathrm{Cov}(b,a)/\mathrm{Var}(b), and the shared \mathrm{Var}(z) in numerator and denominator cancels.

This is the heart of IV. You do not need to see the confounder u. You need only two slopes against an instrument, and you divide one by the other. The unit check is reassuring: \gamma_1 is (percentage points of HbA1c) per km; \pi_1 is (nurse hours) per km; the km cancel and \hat\beta_{IV} comes out in (percentage points of HbA1c) per (nurse hour) — exactly the units of the structural effect \beta.

Distance does not appear in the answer. Look again at \hat\beta_{IV} = \hat\gamma_1 / \hat\pi_1: the units of the instrument cancel. Whether you measure distance in kilometres or miles, in travel-minutes or in a binary “rural vs urban” flag, the IV estimate of the effect of nursing on HbA1c is the same. The instrument is scaffolding — it generates the as-good-as-random variation we exploit — but it scales out of the final estimate. This is why a binary instrument and a continuous one can both identify the very same \beta.

See It: The Ratio of Two Slopes

The instrument’s whole job is to manufacture variation in x that is unrelated to the confounder, and the IV estimate is the slope of y on z divided by the slope of x on z. The interactive below lets you see those two slopes and watch their ratio. The left panel is the first stage (nursing hours vs distance); the right panel is the reduced form (HbA1c vs distance); the scoreboard underneath reports the resulting \hat\beta_{IV} next to the OLS estimate and the truth.

The keystone control is instrument strength: it sets the true first-stage slope \pi_1. Drag it toward zero and watch the IV estimate stay centred on the truth while its sampling spread explodes — a weak instrument is unbiased in expectation but wildly imprecise. Drag it strong and the cloud of repeated estimates collapses to a tight band around \beta.

// ── Fixed truths of the simulation ───────────────────────────────────────
// β_true is the causal effect of one extra nurse-hour on HbA1c (pp). Negative
// = nursing helps. Distance z (km) drives nursing x with slope −strength
// (farther → fewer hours). The confounder u (latent severity/literacy) RAISES
// both nursing demand (x) and HbA1c (y), which is what biases OLS upward.
// One top-level definition per OJS cell, so each constant gets its own cell.
iv2sls_betaTrue = -0.045          // pp of HbA1c per nurse-hour (truth)
iv2sls_alpha = 8.4               // baseline HbA1c
iv2sls_xMean = 18                // mean nurse-hours/yr at z = 0 distance
// Generate one patient sample deterministically from the controls + the
// "Draw new sample" counter. Referencing iv2sls_draw makes a fresh draw on
// each click without any mutable state.
iv2sls_data = {
  iv2sls_draw;                                   // reactive dependency: re-draw on click
  const n    = iv2sls_n;
  const pi1  = -iv2sls_strength;                 // first-stage slope (hours per km), negative
  const cf   = iv2sls_confound;                  // confounding strength
  const rnd  = d3.randomNormal.source(Math.random);

  const zN = rnd(0, 1);   // distance shock
  const uN = rnd(0, 1);   // latent confounder (severity/health literacy)
  const vN = rnd(0, 1);   // first-stage noise
  const eN = rnd(0, 1);   // structural noise

  const out = [];
  for (let i = 0; i < n; i++) {
    // Distance to nearest staffed vårdcentral, km: mean 12, sd ~7, clipped ≥ 0.5
    const z = Math.max(0.5, 12 + 7 * zN());
    // Latent confounder, standardized
    const u = uN();
    // First stage: nursing falls with distance; confounder ADDS demand (cf*u).
    const x = iv2sls_xMean + pi1 * (z - 12) + cf * 4 * u + 3 * vN();
    // Structural eq: HbA1c = α + β x + (confounder enters error) + noise.
    // The confounder u enters the structural error → Cov(x,error) ≠ 0 → OLS biased.
    const y = iv2sls_alpha + iv2sls_betaTrue * (x - iv2sls_xMean)
              + cf * 0.9 * u + 0.5 * eN();
    out.push({ i, z, x, y, u });
  }
  return out;
}
// Ordinary least squares slope & intercept of `yk` on `xk` over an array.
iv2sls_ols = function (rows, xk, yk) {
  const xb = d3.mean(rows, d => d[xk]);
  const yb = d3.mean(rows, d => d[yk]);

Step 3: Compare IV to OLS. The scoreboard shows three numbers: the truth, the IV estimate, and the naive OLS slope of y on x. OLS is biased upward (toward zero or positive) because the confounder — latent severity and engagement — pushes both nursing demand and HbA1c up together, masking the benefit. Step 4: Weaken the instrument. Now drag instrument strength toward 0.02. The first-stage line flattens. One caveat the picture understates: in real finite samples a weak instrument also nudges \hat\beta_{IV} slightly toward the biased OLS value — a median bias the symmetric green cloud here does not show. Step 5: Resample and crank confounding. Click Draw new sample a few times to feel the sampling variability of a single study. Then push confounding strength up: the pink OLS histogram marches further from the truth while the green IV histogram holds its position. More confounding hurts OLS and leaves IV (in expectation) alone — which is the entire reason to pay the precision cost of instrumenting.

The “Two-Stage” in Two-Stage Least Squares

The name two-stage least squares describes a literal recipe that produces the same number as the Wald ratio:

  1. Stage 1. Regress x on z by OLS and save the fitted values \hat x_i = \hat\pi_0 + \hat\pi_1 z_i. These fitted values are the projection of x onto the instrument — the part of nursing intensity explained by geography.
  2. Stage 2. Regress y on \hat x (the fitted values, not the original x) by OLS. The slope coefficient is \hat\beta_{2SLS}.

Why this works. The fitted values \hat x_i are a function of z_i alone, so they inherit the instrument’s key property: they are uncorrelated with the structural error u. By regressing y on \hat x instead of x, stage 2 uses only the clean, geography-driven variation in nursing and discards the confounded part. In the just-identified case (one instrument, one endogenous regressor) this two-stage procedure returns exactly the Wald ratio \hat\gamma_1/\hat\pi_1 — they are algebraically the same estimator. The Math Details prove it.

The two-stage picture generalizes in a way the Wald ratio does not: with several instruments you cannot take a single ratio, but you can still project x onto all the instruments in stage 1 and regress y on the projection in stage 2. That is why “2SLS” is the name that stuck, and why it is the default IV estimator in software.

Never run the two stages by hand for your final answer. It is tempting to literally fit stage 1, save \hat x, and run stage 2 in a second lm() call. The point estimate will be correct, but the standard errors will be wrong — too small. Here is why: stage 2’s software computes residuals as y_i - \hat\beta_{2SLS}\,\hat x_i, using the fitted regressor \hat x. But the correct residual for inference is y_i - \hat\beta_{2SLS}\, x_i, using the actual nursing hours, because \beta is the effect of real nursing on real HbA1c. The hand-rolled second stage never sees the original x, so it understates the residual variance and reports overconfident standard errors and too-narrow confidence intervals. A purpose-built IV command (R’s ivreg/feols, Python’s IV2SLS) computes the point estimate the same way but plugs the original x back in for the variance, delivering valid inference. Use the command, not the two-lm shortcut.

The warning rests on one fact: the fitted regressor \hat x (nursing predicted from distance alone) varies less than the actual nursing x, because \hat x keeps only the geography-driven slice and throws away every other source of variation. Just-Identified vs Over-Identified

The relationship between the number of instruments and the number of endogenous regressors has a name.

  • Just-identified. One instrument for one endogenous regressor. There is exactly one moment condition for one parameter, the system has a unique solution, and 2SLS collapses to the single Wald ratio \hat\gamma_1/\hat\pi_1. This is the case all over this page: distance instruments nursing.
  • Over-identified. More instruments than endogenous regressors — say, both distance and whether the patient’s nearest centre had a recent staffing surge, each instrumenting nursing. Now there are several ways to form a ratio, and they need not agree. 2SLS combines them optimally: it projects x on all the instruments in stage 1 and uses that single best-predicted \hat x in stage 2, which is equivalent to a precision-weighted average of the individual Wald ratios.
  • Under-identified. Fewer instruments than endogenous regressors (e.g. zero instruments). The parameter is not identified at all — there is no IV estimate.

What IV Actually Estimates: A Forward Glance

There is a subtlety we will only flag here and develop fully two pages on. Everything above implicitly assumed the structural effect \beta is the same for every patient — a constant treatment effect. When effects are heterogeneous — when an extra nurse-hour helps some patients a lot and others barely at all — IV does not recover the population average treatment effect. Instead, it recovers a weighted average that up-weights exactly the patients whose nursing intensity responds to the instrument. (The estimand is just the quantity an estimator is aiming at — the target effect, distinct from the \hat\beta number you compute in any one sample.)

Under heterogeneous effects, \hat\beta_{IV} estimates the Local Average Treatment Effect (LATE) — the average effect of nursing among the compliers, the patients whose nursing intensity is actually moved by distance. Patients whose nursing is fixed regardless of where they live (the “always-takers” and “never-takers”) contribute nothing to the IV estimate, because the instrument generates no variation in their treatment. The instrument can only teach us about the patients it moves. We make this precise on the LATE page; for now, just retain the slogan: IV identifies the effect for those whose behaviour the instrument changes.

Math Details: Deriving the IV estimator from the moment condition E[z·u] = 0

The identifying assumption as a moment condition

The instrument’s validity is captured by a single population statement: the instrument is uncorrelated with the structural error,

\mathbb{E}[z_i\, u_i] = 0 .

This is the exclusion restriction in moment form: distance affects HbA1c only through nursing, so once nursing’s effect is accounted for, distance carries no leftover association with the unobserved drivers u. (We also assume relevance, \mathrm{Cov}(z,x)\neq 0, and, for simplicity, that z is mean-centred so \mathbb{E}[z]=0 and the covariance is just \mathbb{E}[zu].)

Solve for β

Start from the structural equation and isolate the error:

u_i = y_i - \alpha - \beta\, x_i .

Substitute into the moment condition \mathbb{E}[z_i u_i] = 0:

\mathbb{E}\big[z_i\,(y_i - \alpha - \beta\, x_i)\big] = 0 .

With z mean-centred, \mathbb{E}[z_i] = 0 kills the \alpha term (\mathbb{E}[z_i\alpha] = \alpha\,\mathbb{E}[z_i] = 0), leaving

\mathbb{E}[z_i y_i] - \beta\,\mathbb{E}[z_i x_i] = 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \beta = \frac{\mathbb{E}[z_i y_i]}{\mathbb{E}[z_i x_i]} = \frac{\mathrm{Cov}(z,y)}{\mathrm{Cov}(z,x)} .

Read this as: “Because the instrument is clean (\mathbb{E}[zu]=0), the only way it can be associated with y is through x; so the ratio of those two associations is exactly the structural slope.” The sample analogue replaces population covariances with sample covariances, giving the Wald estimator \hat\beta_{IV} = \widehat{\mathrm{Cov}}(z,y)/\widehat{\mathrm{Cov}}(z,x).

Why this is the reduced form over the first stage

Divide numerator and denominator by \mathrm{Var}(z):

\hat\beta_{IV} = \frac{\widehat{\mathrm{Cov}}(z,y)/\widehat{\mathrm{Var}}(z)}{\widehat{\mathrm{Cov}}(z,x)/\widehat{\mathrm{Var}}(z)} = \frac{\hat\gamma_1}{\hat\pi_1} ,

because \mathrm{Cov}(z,y)/\mathrm{Var}(z) is the OLS slope of y on z (the reduced form \hat\gamma_1) and \mathrm{Cov}(z,x)/\mathrm{Var}(z) is the OLS slope of x on z (the first stage \hat\pi_1). The shared \mathrm{Var}(z) cancels — which is why the scale of the instrument never enters the answer.

Equivalence to the two-stage procedure

Stage 1 produces \hat x_i = \hat\pi_0 + \hat\pi_1 z_i, an exact linear function of z. The stage-2 slope is

\hat\beta_{2SLS} = \frac{\widehat{\mathrm{Cov}}(\hat x, y)}{\widehat{\mathrm{Var}}(\hat x)} .

Since \hat x is linear in z, \widehat{\mathrm{Cov}}(\hat x, y) = \hat\pi_1\,\widehat{\mathrm{Cov}}(z,y) and \widehat{\mathrm{Var}}(\hat x) = \hat\pi_1^2\,\widehat{\mathrm{Var}}(z). Therefore

\hat\beta_{2SLS} = \frac{\hat\pi_1\,\widehat{\mathrm{Cov}}(z,y)}{\hat\pi_1^2\,\widehat{\mathrm{Var}}(z)} = \frac{\widehat{\mathrm{Cov}}(z,y)}{\hat\pi_1\,\widehat{\mathrm{Var}}(z)} = \frac{\widehat{\mathrm{Cov}}(z,y)}{\widehat{\mathrm{Cov}}(z,x)} = \hat\beta_{IV} . \quad\checkmark

The two-stage recipe and the Wald ratio are the same number in the just-identified case.

Why hand-rolled stage-2 standard errors are wrong

The asymptotic variance of 2SLS is

\widehat{\mathrm{Var}}(\hat\beta_{2SLS}) = \hat\sigma^2_u \big(\hat X' \hat X\big)^{-1}, \qquad \hat\sigma^2_u = \frac{1}{n}\sum_i \big(y_i - \hat\beta_{2SLS}\, x_i\big)^2 ,

where the residual variance \hat\sigma^2_u uses the original regressor x_i, because \beta is the effect of actual nursing on actual HbA1c. A naive second-stage lm(y ~ x_hat) instead forms residuals y_i - \hat\beta_{2SLS}\,\hat x_i using the fitted \hat x_i. Since \hat x has smaller variance than x, those residuals are systematically too small, \hat\sigma^2_u is understated, and the reported standard errors are too narrow. A dedicated IV command substitutes x back in for the variance step, which is why you must use it for inference.

Exercise 1

A region studies whether nurse contact hours (x) lower HbA1c (y), using distance to the nearest staffed vårdcentral (z, km) as an instrument. From its data it estimates a first stage of \hat\pi_1 = -0.40 nurse-hours per km and a reduced form of \hat\gamma_1 = +0.018 HbA1c points per km.

  1. Compute \hat\beta_{IV} and state its units. (b) Interpret the sign. (c) The naive OLS regression of y on x gave -0.010. Is OLS biased toward or away from zero here, and what does that imply about the confounder?
Show answer

(a) \hat\beta_{IV} = \hat\gamma_1 / \hat\pi_1 = 0.018 / (-0.40) = -0.045. The units are (HbA1c percentage points) per (nurse-hour): the km in numerator and denominator cancel.

(b) Negative: each additional nurse-hour per year is estimated to lower HbA1c by 0.045 percentage points. Since lower HbA1c is better, nursing helps.

(c) OLS (-0.010) is closer to zero than IV (-0.045), so OLS is biased toward zero — it understates the benefit. That is the signature of a confounder u that pushes nursing and HbA1c in the same direction: sicker, higher-need patients demand more nursing and have higher HbA1c, which partly cancels the genuine downward effect of nursing in the raw x–y association. IV strips out that confounded variation and reveals the larger true benefit.

Exercise 2

Show, using the Wald formula, that the IV estimate is invariant to the scale of the instrument. Specifically, suppose a colleague re-expresses distance in metres instead of kilometres, i.e. z' = 1000\, z. What happens to the first stage \hat\pi_1, the reduced form \hat\gamma_1, and the ratio \hat\beta_{IV}?

Show answer

Rescaling z' = 1000\, z rescales every covariance with z by the same factor:

\hat\pi_1' = \frac{\mathrm{Cov}(z', x)}{\mathrm{Var}(z')} = \frac{1000\,\mathrm{Cov}(z,x)}{1000^2\,\mathrm{Var}(z)} = \frac{\hat\pi_1}{1000}, \qquad \hat\gamma_1' = \frac{\hat\gamma_1}{1000}.

Both the first-stage and reduced-form slopes shrink by a factor of 1000 (now “per metre” instead of “per km”). But the ratio is unchanged:

\hat\beta_{IV}' = \frac{\hat\gamma_1'}{\hat\pi_1'} = \frac{\hat\gamma_1 / 1000}{\hat\pi_1 / 1000} = \frac{\hat\gamma_1}{\hat\pi_1} = \hat\beta_{IV}. \quad\checkmark

The instrument’s scale cancels. This is the algebra behind the Key Insight box: the instrument is scaffolding for generating clean variation, and its units never appear in the estimate of the effect of nursing on HbA1c.

Exercise 3

A graduate student runs IV by hand: she regresses x on z, saves \hat x, then runs lm(y ~ x_hat) and reports the coefficient and its standard error from that second regression. Her supervisor says the point estimate is fine but the standard error is wrong. (a) Why is the point estimate correct? (b) Is the reported standard error too large or too small, and why? (c) What single change fixes the inference?

Show answer

(a) The point estimate is correct because, in the just-identified case, regressing y on the fitted values \hat x is algebraically identical to the Wald ratio \hat\gamma_1/\hat\pi_1 (proved in the Math Details). The fitted values are a linear function of z, and the covariance and variance factors of \hat\pi_1 cancel, leaving exactly \mathrm{Cov}(z,y)/\mathrm{Cov}(z,x).

(b) Too small. The second lm computes residuals as y_i - \hat\beta\,\hat x_i using the fitted regressor \hat x, whose variance is smaller than that of the actual x. The correct residual for the structural equation is y_i - \hat\beta\, x_i, using actual nursing hours. Underusing the variance of the regressor understates the residual variance \hat\sigma_u^2, so the reported standard errors — and the confidence intervals — are too narrow, making the result look more precise (and more “significant”) than it is.

(c) Use a dedicated IV command (ivreg(y ~ x | z) in R, feols(y ~ 1 | x ~ z), or IV2SLS in Python). These compute the same point estimate but plug the original x back in when forming the residual variance, delivering valid standard errors. The fix is to stop hand-rolling stage 2 for inference.

Exercise 4

In the interactive, set instrument strength near its minimum (|π₁| ≈ 0.02) and watch the green IV histogram. The estimate stays centred on the truth but becomes very wide. (a) Explain, using the Wald formula, why a weak first stage inflates the variance of \hat\beta_{IV} without (in expectation) creating bias. (b) Why is “unbiased but high-variance” still a serious practical problem for a single study?

Show answer

(a) Write \hat\beta_{IV} = \hat\gamma_1 / \hat\pi_1. As the true first stage \pi_1 \to 0, the denominator \hat\pi_1 is a small number estimated with its own sampling noise. Dividing the reduced form by a near-zero, noisy denominator magnifies that noise: a small wiggle in \hat\pi_1 produces a large swing in the ratio. Because the expected numerator and denominator still satisfy \gamma_1 = \beta\,\pi_1, the ratio remains (approximately) centred on \beta — so there is no first-order bias in the just-identified large-sample sense — but its variance blows up. Geometrically, you are reading the slope of y on z off an almost-flat first stage, where tiny vertical movements imply huge slope changes.

(b) A single study gives you one draw from that fat sampling distribution, not the average over 400 draws. If the distribution is wide, your one estimate can land far from the truth, your confidence interval will be enormous (or, if you mis-estimate it, misleadingly tight), and you cannot distinguish a real effect from zero. “Unbiased on average” is cold comfort when you only run the study once. This is exactly why the next page is devoted to measuring instrument strength (the first-stage F-statistic) before trusting any IV estimate.

Key takeaways

  • The IV estimator is the ratio of two simple regressions on the instrument: \hat\beta_{IV} = \hat\gamma_1/\hat\pi_1 = \mathrm{Cov}(z,y)/\mathrm{Cov}(z,x) — the reduced form divided by the first stage. The instrument’s scale cancels out of the answer.
  • It is derived from a single moment condition, \mathbb{E}[z\,u]=0 (the instrument is uncorrelated with the structural error): because the instrument is clean, its only association with y runs through x, so dividing one association by the other recovers the structural slope.
  • “Two-stage least squares” — fit \hat x from z, then regress y on \hat x — gives the identical point estimate in the just-identified case, but running it as two lm calls produces wrong (too small) standard errors. Always use a dedicated IV command (ivreg/feols/IV2SLS).
  • A weak first stage (\pi_1 near zero) keeps IV centred on the truth but inflates its variance: dividing by a tiny, noisy denominator magnifies noise. Just-identified means one instrument per endogenous regressor; over-identification lets you test the instruments against each other.
  • Under heterogeneous effects, IV does not recover the population average effect — it recovers the LATE, the effect for the compliers whose treatment the instrument actually moves.
