LATE and Compliers

What instrumental variables actually identify

When effects are heterogeneous, an instrument does not recover the average treatment effect for everyone. It recovers the effect for a specific, often invisible, subpopulation: the compliers — the units whose treatment status the instrument actually moves. This page is about that subpopulation, why it is the only one IV can speak for, and what that means for policy.

In this section, you will learn to

  • Partition a population into the four latent response types — compliers, always-takers, never-takers, defiers — defined by how a unit reacts to a binary instrument.
  • State the monotonicity (no-defiers) assumption and explain why it is what turns the Wald ratio into a clean local average treatment effect.
  • Derive that the IV/Wald estimand equals E[Y(1) - Y(0) \mid \text{complier}] — the LATE — and not the ATE or the ATT.
  • Explain why LATE may differ from the ATE and the ATT, and judge when that gap matters for an external-validity argument.
  • Read the Wald estimator as a ratio of two intention-to-treat effects (reduced form over first stage).

A Nudge, Not a Mandate

Sweden’s 21 regioner (regions) run their chronic-disease care through vårdcentraler (primary care centers). One region wants to know whether enrolling Type 2 diabetes patients in a structured diabetes coordination program (Diabetessamordning) — a dedicated distriktssköterska (district nurse), scheduled HbA1c follow-ups, medication review — actually lowers blood sugar twelve months later.

The region cannot randomize enrollment directly: the program is voluntary, and patients who sign up are exactly the patients who would have improved anyway (the selection-bias problem). So instead the region randomizes a nudge. Half of eligible patients (chosen by lottery) receive a personal letter and a phone call from their vårdcentral inviting them to join the program; the other half receive the standard generic leaflet. The letter is an encouragement, not an order. Patients are still free to enroll or not.

This is an encouragement design, and the random letter is an instrument Z. It is randomly assigned, so it is unconfounded. It plausibly affects HbA1c only through whether the patient enrolls (the letter itself does not treat diabetes — this is the exclusion restriction from the previous pages). And it has a real first stage: people who get the letter enroll at higher rates.

The picture below draws the causal structure that the three IV assumptions assert. The random letter Z acts only through enrollment D (green arrows — the first stage and the treatment effect). An unobserved confounder U (motivation, baseline severity) opens a back-door between D and the outcome Y (red) — exactly the selection problem that dooms the naive enrolled-vs-not comparison. The instrument’s power comes from the missing arrow: the grey dashed Z \dashrightarrow Y is the exclusion restriction made visible — the direct path that must not exist for IV to work. Because Z is randomized, nothing points into it.

    .attr("font-family", "'Inter', sans-serif")
    .style("max-width", "100%")
    .style("height", "auto");

  const defs = svg.append("defs");
  for (const [id, col] of [

      .attr("orient", "auto-start-reverse")
      .append("path").attr("d", "M0,-5L10,0L0,5").attr("fill", col);
  }

  const eg = svg.append("g");
  for (const e of edges) {
    const s = byId.get(e.source), t = byId.get(e.target);
    if (!s || !t) continue;
    const x1 = sx(s.x), y1 = sy(s.y), x2 = sx(t.x), y2 = sy(t.y);
    const dx = x2 - x1, dy = y2 - y1;

    const ux = dx / len, uy = dy / len;

      .attr("fill", "none")

    }

    const cx = sx(n.x), cy = sy(n.y);
    const g = ng.append("g");

      g.append("rect")

    }
  }

Color key: green = causal path the instrument exploits (Z \to D \to Y); red = open back-door through the unobserved confounder U (why naive enrolled-vs-not is biased); grey dashed = the excluded direct effect Z \to Y, which the exclusion restriction assumes is absent. But here is the subtlety that this whole page exists to unpack. The letter does not move everyone. Some patients would enroll no matter what. Some would never enroll, letter or not. The letter only changes the behavior of the patients who are on the fence — and it is only those patients whose causal effect the instrument can ever reveal.

With a binary instrument and heterogeneous treatment effects, IV does not estimate the average treatment effect (ATE) or the effect on the treated (ATT). It estimates the Local Average Treatment Effect (LATE): the average effect of the program among the patients whose enrollment decision the letter actually flipped. Change who is on the fence, and you change what IV measures — even though the program’s true ATE has not moved at all.

The framework that makes this precise is due to Imbens and Angrist (1994) and Angrist, Imbens, and Rubin (1996). It starts by sorting every patient into one of four latent types.

The Four Latent Types

Think of each patient as having two potential treatment decisions, one for each value of the instrument. Let D_i(z) be patient i’s enrollment status if their letter status were z:

  • D_i(1) — would patient i enroll if they received the letter?
  • D_i(0) — would patient i enroll if they received only the leaflet?

Each of these is 0 or 1, so there are exactly four combinations. These four combinations are the four principal strata — groups defined by a pair of potential outcomes rather than by anything we can observe, hence “latent.”

Every patient belongs to exactly one of four types, defined by the pair \big(D_i(0),\, D_i(1)\big):

Type D_i(0) D_i(1) Behavior
Complier 0 1 Enrolls only when nudged. The letter moves them.
Always-taker 1 1 Enrolls regardless. Letter is irrelevant.
Never-taker 0 0 Never enrolls. Letter is irrelevant.
Defier 1 0 Enrolls only when not nudged. Perverse.

Read the complier row as: “D_i(0) = 0 and D_i(1) = 1” — without the letter they stay out, with the letter they enroll. The instrument changes their decision in the intended direction.

The pair \big(D_i(0), D_i(1)\big) is naturally a 2×2 grid — one axis for what the patient does without the letter, one for what they do with it. The four types are just the four cells. The picture below lays them out so the structure is visible at a glance, and previews the next assumption: the defier cell (top-left: enrolls without the letter, refuses with it) is the one monotonicity will forbid.

The four latent types are the four cells of the \big(D(0), D(1)\big) grid. Compliers (red, bottom-right) switch into enrollment in the intended direction. Always-takers and never-takers sit on the diagonal where the letter changes nothing. The defier cell (hatched, top-left) is the one the next assumption deletes.

The tragedy of the four types is the same fundamental problem we met in potential outcomes: for any single patient we observe only one of D_i(0) and D_i(1) — whichever their realized letter status delivers. A patient who got the letter and enrolled (Z=1, D=1) could be a complier or an always-taker; we cannot tell which from their own data. The types are latent. We can never label an individual, but — remarkably — we can still estimate the average effect within one of these strata.

Notice immediately why these types matter for identification:

  • Always-takers are enrolled whether or not the letter arrives. Their treatment status does not respond to Z, so the instrument carries no information about their treatment effect.
  • Never-takers are out whether or not the letter arrives. Same problem: Z never moves them, so it can say nothing about their effect.
  • Compliers are the only type whose treatment status changes with Z. Any effect of Z on the outcome Y must, by the exclusion restriction, run through their changed enrollment. So the instrument’s leverage is entirely on the compliers.
  • Defiers are the saboteurs. They respond to Z in the wrong direction, and their effect enters the IV formula with the opposite sign — which can corrupt the whole estimate. The next assumption exists precisely to rule them out.

Monotonicity: No Defiers

Monotonicity (Imbens & Angrist, 1994). The instrument moves no one in the wrong direction. For every patient i,

D_i(1) \;\ge\; D_i(0).

Read this as: “Receiving the letter never makes a patient less likely to enroll than they would have been without it.” Equivalently: there are no defiers. The nudge can turn a non-enroller into an enroller (a complier) or leave a decision unchanged (always-taker, never-taker), but it cannot flip anyone from “would enroll” to “would not enroll.”

Monotonicity is a behavioral assumption, and in an encouragement design it is highly plausible: it is hard to imagine a patient who would enroll only if no one invited them and would stubbornly refuse the moment a friendly letter arrives. But it is a genuine assumption, not a free lunch. It can fail when an instrument pushes different people in different directions — for example, a price subsidy that nudges most people toward a product but triggers a contrarian backlash in others.

Why does ruling out defiers matter so much? Because the instrument’s effect on the population is a net of the directions it pushes. With defiers present, the letter pulls compliers into treatment and pushes defiers out of it, and these two movements partially cancel. The observed first stage understates the true churn, and the outcome effect mixes complier effects with sign-flipped defier effects. Monotonicity guarantees that everyone the instrument moves, it moves the same way — so the population splits cleanly into compliers, always-takers, and never-takers, with no defiers to muddy the algebra.

The schematic below makes that cancellation concrete. The letter pushes compliers into enrollment (green, rightward) but defiers out of it (red, leftward); the net first stage is what is left after the two arrows fight. Monotonicity deletes the red arrow, so the green movement stands alone and the first stage counts compliers cleanly.

With defiers present, the observed (net) first stage is the green complier movement minus the red defier movement — so it understates the true churn and contaminates the numerator with sign-flipped defier effects. Monotonicity removes the red arrow entirely.

Under monotonicity, the share of compliers in the population is identified and equals the first stage:

P(\text{complier}) = E[D \mid Z = 1] - E[D \mid Z = 0].

Read this as: “The fraction of compliers equals the enrollment rate among the letter group minus the enrollment rate among the leaflet group.” With no defiers, the only people who switch from “not enrolled” to “enrolled” when the letter arrives are the compliers, so the jump in the enrollment rate counts exactly them. A weak first stage is therefore not just a statistical nuisance — it literally means there are very few compliers, so IV is reporting an effect for a tiny sliver of the population.

The Wald Estimator: A Ratio of Two ITT Effects

With a single binary instrument and a single binary treatment, the IV estimate has a beautifully simple closed form: the Wald estimator. It is the ratio of two intention-to-treat (ITT) quantities.

The Wald estimator is

\widehat{\beta}_{\text{IV}} = \frac{\overbrace{E[Y \mid Z = 1] - E[Y \mid Z = 0]}^{\text{reduced form (ITT on outcome)}}} {\underbrace{E[D \mid Z = 1] - E[D \mid Z = 0]}_{\text{first stage (ITT on treatment)}}}.

Read this as: “Take the effect of the letter on HbA1c, and divide it by the effect of the letter on enrollment.” The numerator is the reduced form: the average outcome difference between the letter group and the leaflet group — the effect of being offered the program. The denominator is the first stage: how much the letter actually raised enrollment.

The intuition behind the division is the engine of the whole method. The letter raised HbA1c improvement by some amount (the reduced form), but it did so only by pushing compliers into the program. If the letter moved, say, 30% of patients into enrollment, then the reduced-form benefit was generated by that 30%. Dividing the reduced form by the first stage rescales the diluted, population-wide ITT effect back up to a per-complier effect:

\underbrace{\text{effect of the offer}}_{\text{reduced form}} = \underbrace{P(\text{complier})}_{\text{first stage}} \times \underbrace{\text{effect per complier}}_{\text{LATE}} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \text{LATE} = \frac{\text{reduced form}}{\text{first stage}}.

This rearrangement is the Wald estimator, and it makes the punchline unavoidable: the thing in the numerator was produced entirely by compliers, so the quotient is a complier-specific effect.

The IV estimand is the LATE. Under (1) random assignment of Z, (2) the exclusion restriction, (3) a nonzero first stage, and (4) monotonicity,

\beta_{\text{IV}} = E\big[\,Y(1) - Y(0) \;\big|\; \text{complier}\,\big] = \text{LATE}.

Read this as: “The instrumental-variables estimand equals the average treatment effect, restricted to compliers.” (An estimand is the population quantity you are trying to learn — the target, defined before any data — as distinct from the estimate your sample produces.) It is local in two senses: local to the compliers (not the whole population) and local to this instrument (a different nudge defines a different complier group, and so a different LATE).

Step 2: Confirm IV lands on the complier effect. Look at the number-line panel. The dark IV / Wald dot sits essentially on top of the red True LATE dot, and the “= Wald / IV” tile turns green to flag the match. IV recovers the average effect among compliers. Meanwhile the ochre True ATE dot — the effect averaged over everyone — sits somewhere else entirely. IV is not aiming at it and does not hit it.

Step 3: Make compliers special. Drag Effect on compliers to a strong -1.6 while leaving Effect on always/never-takers near -0.3. Now compliers benefit far more than the rest of the population. Watch the red LATE dot and the dark IV dot move together to the left, while the ochre ATE dot barely budges (compliers are only part of the population). This is the central lesson: IV chases the compliers, not the average.

Step 4: Shrink the complier pool. Drag Complier share down toward 0.05. Two things happen. The first-stage readout collapses toward zero — there are barely any patients for the letter to move — and the Wald estimate gets visibly noisier from draw to draw (press Draw new sample a few times to feel the jitter). A weak first stage and a tiny complier group are the same phenomenon. This is why the weak-instruments page matters: few compliers means a fragile, high-variance LATE.

Step 5: See the naive comparison drift. The slate Naive dot is the simple difference between enrolled and non-enrolled patients — ignoring the instrument. Because always-takers (who enroll voluntarily) are a healthier, self-selected group and never-takers are a different one again, this comparison mixes the treatment effect with a baseline severity gap. It does not recover the LATE that IV targets, and there is no assumption under which it cleanly equals the ATE, the ATT, or the complier effect — exactly how far it drifts depends on how the type-specific baselines differ. IV’s entire job is to avoid this selection trap by leaning only on the randomized letter.

Step 6: Find the homogeneous case. Set both effect sliders to the same value. Now LATE, ATE, and IV all coincide (the verdict turns green). This is the special case where heterogeneity vanishes: when everyone has the same effect, every sub-average equals the ATE, and the LATE-vs-ATE distinction dissolves. The gap between LATE and ATE is, precisely, a gap created by heterogeneity.

Why LATE Is Not the ATE (or the ATT)

The interactive made it visible; here is the principle. LATE answers a narrow question — “what is the effect for the patients this particular nudge moves?” — and three forces can drive a wedge between that and the broader estimands.

Compliers are a self-selected subpopulation. They are, by definition, the patients who sit on the margin of enrolling: undecided enough that a letter tips them, but not so motivated that they would have enrolled anyway. There is no reason this marginal group resembles the population average. Marginal patients might be more responsive (they were curious and engaged) or less responsive (they were lukewarm and half-hearted) than the always-takers who sought the program out on their own.

The external-validity caveat. LATE is silent about three of the four types:

  • It says nothing about always-takers — the eager patients who enroll without any nudge. These are often the people a program manager cares about most, yet IV cannot speak for them.
  • It says nothing about never-takers — the patients no letter will reach. If the policy question is “should we mandate the program for everyone,” the never-takers are exactly the group you are forcing in, and LATE has no information about them.
  • It is tied to this instrument. A gentler nudge (a text message) and a stronger nudge (a nurse home visit) define different complier groups and therefore estimate different LATEs. There is no single “the LATE” — only “the LATE for compliers with respect to instrument Z.”

Reporting a LATE as if it were the ATE — “the diabetes program lowers HbA1c by 1.2 points” with no qualifier — is one of the most common errors in applied IV work.

When does LATE coincide with the ATE or ATT? Two clean cases:

  1. Constant treatment effects. If Y_i(1) - Y_i(0) = \tau for every patient, then the effect is the same in every stratum, so LATE = ATT = ATU = ATE = \tau. Heterogeneity is the only reason these differ; remove it and they collapse (you saw this in Step 6).
  2. Everyone is a complier. If the instrument moves the entire population — no always-takers and no never-takers — then the complier group is the population, and LATE = ATE. This happens when the “instrument” is, in effect, a perfectly enforced randomized assignment (full compliance), in which case IV reduces to a simple experiment.

In every other case, be precise: IV gives you the complier effect, and “compliers” is a population you cannot point to in your data.

Math Details: Deriving LATE = E[Y(1) − Y(0) | complier] from monotonicity

We prove that the Wald estimand equals the average treatment effect among compliers, under four assumptions: (A1) independence — Z is independent of the potential outcomes and potential treatments, \big(Y(0), Y(1), D(0), D(1)\big) \perp Z (the symbol \perp reads “is statistically independent of”: the letter carries no information about a patient’s latent type or potential outcomes), guaranteed here by randomizing the letter; (A2) exclusion — Z affects Y only through D, so we may write potential outcomes as Y(d) rather than Y(z, d); (A3) relevance — the first stage is nonzero; (A4) monotonicity — D_i(1) \ge D_i(0) for all i (no defiers).

Step 1 — The first stage is the complier share

Under monotonicity, D_i(1) - D_i(0) \in \{0, 1\}: it is 1 for compliers and 0 for everyone else (always-takers, never-takers; defiers are excluded). By independence, E[D \mid Z = z] = E[D(z)], so

\begin{aligned} E[D \mid Z{=}1] - E[D \mid Z{=}0] &= E[D(1)] - E[D(0)] \\ &= E[D(1) - D(0)] \\ &= P\big(D(1) - D(0) = 1\big) = P(\text{complier}). \end{aligned}

Read this as: the first stage counts exactly the compliers, because they are the only people for whom D(1) - D(0) is nonzero, and that difference is +1 (never -1, thanks to monotonicity).

Step 2 — The reduced form is the complier share times the complier effect

By independence and exclusion, E[Y \mid Z = z] = E[Y(D(z))]. Take the difference:

E[Y \mid Z{=}1] - E[Y \mid Z{=}0] = E\big[\,Y(D(1)) - Y(D(0))\,\big].

Now decompose by type, using that Y(D(1)) - Y(D(0)) vanishes whenever D(1) = D(0):

  • Always-takers (D(1) = D(0) = 1): the term is Y(1) - Y(1) = 0.
  • Never-takers (D(1) = D(0) = 0): the term is Y(0) - Y(0) = 0.
  • Compliers (D(1) = 1,\ D(0) = 0): the term is Y(1) - Y(0).
  • Defiers: excluded by monotonicity.

Only compliers contribute. By the law of total expectation,

E\big[\,Y(D(1)) - Y(D(0))\,\big] = P(\text{complier}) \cdot E\big[\,Y(1) - Y(0) \mid \text{complier}\,\big].

Read this as: the letter changes the outcome for nobody except compliers, so the population-wide reduced form is just the complier share multiplied by the average effect within that group.

Step 3 — Take the ratio

Divide Step 2 by Step 1. The P(\text{complier}) factors cancel (nonzero by relevance, A3):

\beta_{\text{IV}} = \frac{E[Y \mid Z{=}1] - E[Y \mid Z{=}0]}{E[D \mid Z{=}1] - E[D \mid Z{=}0]} = \frac{P(\text{complier}) \cdot E[Y(1) - Y(0) \mid \text{complier}]}{P(\text{complier})} = E\big[\,Y(1) - Y(0) \mid \text{complier}\,\big].

\boxed{\;\beta_{\text{IV}} = \text{LATE} = E\big[Y(1) - Y(0) \mid \text{complier}\big].\;}

What breaks without monotonicity

Drop A4 and re-run Step 2. Defiers (D(1) = 0,\ D(0) = 1) now contribute a term Y(0) - Y(1) = -\big(Y(1) - Y(0)\big) — their effect enters with a flipped sign. The reduced form becomes

P(\text{complier})\,E[Y(1){-}Y(0)\mid\text{complier}] \;-\; P(\text{defier})\,E[Y(1){-}Y(0)\mid\text{defier}],

and the denominator becomes P(\text{complier}) - P(\text{defier}). The ratio is now a difference of two effects over a difference of two shares — no longer a clean weighted average of treatment effects, and capable of lying outside the range of every unit’s true effect. Monotonicity is exactly the assumption that deletes the defier terms and restores the clean LATE.

Both implementations simulate the encouragement design — a randomized letter, four latent types, monotonicity by construction — then recover the LATE three equivalent ways: the Wald ratio by hand, 2SLS, and a check against the known complier effect baked into the simulation.

# Per-type treatment effects on HbA1c (negative = improvement).
# Compliers benefit a lot; always/never-takers much less. Hence LATE != ATE.
tau <- case_when(
  type == "complier" ~ rnorm(N, -1.2, 0.4),
  TRUE               ~ rnorm(N, -0.3, 0.4)
)

y0 <- rnorm(N, 8.0, 0.5)                 # baseline HbA1c
z  <- rbinom(N, 1, 0.5)                   # randomized letter (the instrument)

# Realized enrollment under MONOTONICITY (no defiers):
d <- case_when(
  type == "complier" ~ z,                 # enroll only if nudged
  type == "always"   ~ 1L,                # always enroll
  type == "never"    ~ 0L                  # never enroll
)

y <- y0 + d * tau                          # switching equation
dat <- tibble(y, d, z, type, tau)

# ── 1. Wald estimator by hand: reduced form / first stage ─────────────
rf <- mean(dat$y[dat$z == 1]) - mean(dat$y[dat$z == 0])   # reduced form
fs <- mean(dat$d[dat$z == 1]) - mean(dat$d[dat$z == 0])   # first stage
wald <- rf / fs

# ── 2. 2SLS (numerically identical to Wald for one binary instrument) ─
iv  <- ivreg(y ~ d | z, data = dat)        # AER
iv2 <- feols(y ~ 1 | d ~ z, data = dat)    # fixest

# ── 3. Ground truth (only knowable in a simulation) ───────────────────
true_LATE <- mean(dat$tau[dat$type == "complier"])   # compliers only
true_ATE  <- mean(dat$tau)                            # everyone

cat(sprintf("First stage (= complier share):  %.3f\n", fs))
cat(sprintf("Reduced form:                    %.3f\n", rf))
cat(sprintf("Wald = RF / FS:                  %.3f\n", wald))

cat(sprintf("TRUE LATE (compliers):           %.3f\n", true_LATE))
cat(sprintf("TRUE ATE  (everyone):            %.3f\n", true_ATE))

import pandas as pd
from linearmodels.iv import IV2SLS

rng = np.random.default_rng(1994)   # Imbens & Angrist

# ── Simulate an encouragement design with 4 latent types ──────────────
N = 6000
type_ = rng.choice(["complier", "always", "never"],
                   size=N, p=[0.40, 0.20, 0.40])

# Per-type treatment effects (negative = HbA1c improvement).
tau = np.where(type_ == "complier",
               rng.normal(-1.2, 0.4, N),
               rng.normal(-0.3, 0.4, N))

y0 = rng.normal(8.0, 0.5, N)            # baseline HbA1c
z  = rng.binomial(1, 0.5, N)            # randomized letter (instrument)

# Realized enrollment under MONOTONICITY (no defiers):
d = np.select(
    [type_ == "complier", type_ == "always", type_ == "never"],
    [z,                    np.ones(N),        np.zeros(N)]
).astype(int)

y = y0 + d * tau                        # switching equation
dat = pd.DataFrame({"y": y, "d": d, "z": z, "type": type_, "tau": tau})

# ── 1. Wald estimator by hand: reduced form / first stage ─────────────
rf = dat.loc[dat.z == 1, "y"].mean() - dat.loc[dat.z == 0, "y"].mean()
fs = dat.loc[dat.z == 1, "d"].mean() - dat.loc[dat.z == 0, "d"].mean()
wald = rf / fs

# ── 2. 2SLS (identical to Wald for one binary instrument) ─────────────
dat["const"] = 1.0
iv = IV2SLS(dependent=dat["y"], exog=dat["const"],
            endog=dat["d"], instruments=dat["z"]).fit()

# ── 3. Ground truth (only knowable in a simulation) ───────────────────
true_LATE = dat.loc[dat.type == "complier", "tau"].mean()   # compliers
true_ATE  = dat["tau"].mean()                                # everyone

print(f"First stage (= complier share):  {fs:.3f}")
print(f"Reduced form:                    {rf:.3f}")
print(f"Wald = RF / FS:                  {wald:.3f}")
print(f"2SLS coefficient on d:           {iv.params['d']:.3f}")
print(f"TRUE LATE (compliers):           {true_LATE:.3f}")
print(f"TRUE ATE  (everyone):            {true_ATE:.3f}")
# Wald and 2SLS recover the LATE (~ -1.2), NOT the ATE (~ -0.66).

Exercise 2

Using the same numbers as Exercise 1, a colleague computes the naive comparison: among the invited group, enrolled patients (D=1) had 11.0 visits and non-enrolled (D=0) had 16.5 visits, so “the program cuts visits by 5.5.” Explain why -5.5 is not a valid causal estimate of anything, and why the IV estimate of -8.0 is preferable.

Show answer

The naive comparison contrasts patients who chose to enroll against those who did not, within a single group. But enrollment is self-selected: the enrolled group mixes compliers (who took the invitation) with always-takers (who enroll regardless), while the non-enrolled group is never-takers. These subpopulations differ systematically — always-takers are typically the most health-engaged patients, never-takers the least — so the -5.5 difference confounds the program’s effect with the baseline frailty gap between self-selecting groups. It estimates neither the LATE, the ATT, nor the ATE.

The IV estimate avoids this because it leans only on the randomized invitation Z. Comparing the invited group to the leaflet group (both randomly formed) is unconfounded, and dividing the resulting reduced form by the first stage rescales it to the per-complier effect. IV trades the naive estimate’s bias for a precisely defined — if local — causal quantity.

Exercise 3

Suppose monotonicity fails: 10% of patients are defiers (they enroll only when not invited), and the true effect among defiers is the same as among compliers, -1.0 HbA1c points. The population is 30% compliers, 10% defiers, 30% always-takers, 30% never-takers, and the complier effect is also -1.0. (a) Write the reduced form and the first stage in terms of the type shares and effects. (b) Show that the Wald ratio still happens to equal -1.0 here, and explain why this is a coincidence rather than a guarantee. (c) What single change to the defier effect would make the Wald ratio mislead?

Show answer

(a) With defiers, both the numerator and denominator pick up signed defier terms (see the Math Details). Writing \tau_c and \tau_d for the complier and defier effects:

\text{Reduced form} = P_c\,\tau_c - P_d\,\tau_d, \qquad \text{First stage} = P_c - P_d.

Plugging in P_c = 0.30, P_d = 0.10, \tau_c = \tau_d = -1.0:

  • Reduced form = 0.30(-1.0) - 0.10(-1.0) = -0.30 + 0.10 = -0.20.
  • First stage = 0.30 - 0.10 = 0.20.
  • Wald = -0.20 / 0.20 = -1.0.

(b) It equals -1.0 only because the defier effect exactly equals the complier effect (\tau_c = \tau_d). When the two effects are identical, the formula \frac{P_c \tau - P_d \tau}{P_c - P_d} = \frac{(P_c - P_d)\tau}{P_c - P_d} = \tau collapses cleanly. This is a knife-edge coincidence, not a property of IV — nothing in the design forces defiers to respond like compliers.

(c) Make the defier effect positive (the program harms defiers), say \tau_d = +2.0. Then reduced form = 0.30(-1.0) - 0.10(+2.0) = -0.30 - 0.20 = -0.50, first stage = 0.20, and Wald = -0.50/0.20 = -2.5 — a number more extreme than any individual’s true effect (-1.0 for compliers, +2.0 for defiers). The estimate now lies outside the range of every unit’s effect, which is exactly the pathology monotonicity rules out.

Exercise 4

In the Type-Mix Lab, set both effect sliders to the same value and confirm that LATE, ATE, and the IV estimate coincide. (a) Explain in one paragraph why they must coincide in this case. (b) Now suppose a health economist reports a single IV estimate from a text-message nudge and writes: “Enrolling in the diabetes program lowers HbA1c by 0.9 points.” State two distinct ways this sentence over-claims, referencing the locality of LATE.

Show answer

(a) When every patient has the same treatment effect \tau, treatment-effect heterogeneity is zero. The complier effect E[Y(1)-Y(0)\mid\text{complier}] is then just \tau, but so is the average over any subpopulation — always-takers, never-takers, the whole population. With no heterogeneity, every conditional average of the effect equals \tau, so LATE = ATT = ATU = ATE = \tau, and IV (which targets the LATE) numerically coincides with all of them. The LATE-vs-ATE distinction is created by heterogeneity; remove it and the distinction vanishes.

(b) Two over-claims: (i) Population locality. The estimate is the effect for compliers — the patients the text message moved — not for “enrolling” patients in general. It says nothing about always-takers (who enroll without any nudge) or never-takers (whom no nudge reaches), so it cannot justify a claim about everyone who enrolls or a mandate forcing everyone in. (ii) Instrument locality. The complier group is defined relative to this specific text-message nudge. A stronger instrument (a nurse home visit) or weaker one (a generic email) would move a different set of marginal patients and so estimate a different LATE. There is no single “the effect of enrolling” — only the effect for the compliers with respect to the instrument used, which the sentence should state explicitly.

Key takeaways

  • A binary instrument sorts the population into four latent types via \big(D(0), D(1)\big): compliers, always-takers, never-takers, defiers. Only compliers have treatment status the instrument actually moves.
  • Monotonicity (no defiers, D_i(1) \ge D_i(0)) is what makes the algebra clean: it deletes the sign-flipped defier terms and makes the first stage equal the complier share.
  • The Wald estimator is the reduced form divided by the first stage — the effect of the offer rescaled by how many people the offer moved — and it identifies the LATE = E[Y(1) - Y(0) \mid \text{complier}], not the ATE or ATT.
  • LATE differs from the ATE whenever effects are heterogeneous and compliers are a special subgroup. It is silent about always-takers, never-takers, and any other instrument’s compliers — the external-validity caveat.
  • A weak first stage is a small complier pool: IV then speaks for a tiny, high-variance sliver of the population. Always report whose effect your IV estimate is.

LATE taught us that with heterogeneous effects, an instrument identifies a local effect — the effect for the marginal units it actually moves. That theme of “the effect at the margin” reappears, in a different guise, in the next module. Regression Discontinuity Design studies settings where treatment is assigned by a sharp cutoff on a running variable — a patient just above an HbA1c threshold is referred to a specialist, one just below is not. There, the credibly identified quantity is again local: the effect at the cutoff, for the patients right at the threshold. We will see RDD as a limiting form of the LATE logic, where “compliers” become “units arbitrarily close to the boundary,” and the same external-validity discipline applies — the estimate is sharp, but local.
