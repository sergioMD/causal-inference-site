Staggered Adoption

When treatment rolls out at different times

In this section, you will learn to

  • Define staggered adoption with cohorts and an absorbing treatment indicator D_{it} = \mathbf{1}\{t \ge G_i\}
  • Interpret the Goodman-Bacon decomposition: TWFE as a weighted average of clean and forbidden 2x2 comparisons
  • Explain why “later-treated vs. already-treated” comparisons attach negative weights to real, beneficial effects and can flip the sign of \hat\beta
  • Distinguish the conditions under which TWFE is safe (homogeneous static effects or common timing) from when it is biased (staggered timing plus dynamic or heterogeneous effects)
  • Recognize why dropping never-treated units makes the bias worse rather than better

A Reform That Arrives in Waves

On the previous page we studied the clean two-group, two-period difference-in-differences design: one group adopts a reform at a single date, another never does, and we compare their before-and-after changes. Real Swedish health policy almost never looks like that.

Consider a concrete case. In 2016, Socialstyrelsen publishes new national guidelines for a structured fall-prevention program (fallpreventionsprogram) in elderly care. The 21 regioner do not all adopt it at once. Some early movers — Region Stockholm, Region Skåne — roll it out across their vårdcentraler in 2017. A middle wave adopts in 2019. The late adopters wait until 2021, constrained by budgets and competing priorities. A few small regions never adopt it during the study window at all.

You observe every region every year from 2014 to 2023. The outcome is the annual rate of fall-related hospitalizations per 1,000 residents aged 75+. The treatment — being enrolled in the program — switches on for each region at its own adoption year and stays on thereafter.

This is staggered adoption: an absorbing treatment that different units turn on at different times. It is the dominant structure in panel-data policy evaluation, and for two decades the default tool was a single regression. That regression, it turns out, can be badly — even catastrophically — wrong. This page explains why, and the next page tells you what to do instead.

A panel of units i = 1, \dots, N is observed over periods t = 1, \dots, T. Each unit has an adoption time G_i \in \{2, \dots, T\} \cup \{\infty\} — the first period in which its treatment switches on. Units that never adopt have G_i = \infty.

Units that share an adoption time form a cohort. The treatment indicator is

D_{it} = \mathbf{1}\{t \ge G_i\},

read as “D_{it} equals one when the current period t is at or past unit i’s adoption time G_i, and zero before.” Because D_{it} never switches back off, treatment is absorbing (staggered, not switching).

The picture below is the whole rollout at a glance. Each row is a cohort, each column a year; a cell turns filled the moment that cohort adopts and stays filled forever after — that “switches on and never off” pattern is exactly what absorbing means. The never-treated row stays empty across the entire window and supplies the clean comparison group.

Filled = treated (program switched on); empty = untreated. Treatment is absorbing: once a cohort’s cell fills, every later cell in that row is filled too — no region ever leaves the program. The never-treated row is empty throughout.

The whole difficulty of this page lives in one fact: when units adopt at different times, a unit that adopted early is already treated when a later cohort is still untreated. That makes the early adopter look, superficially, like a usable “control” for the late adopter — and that temptation is where everything goes wrong.

The Default Tool: Two-Way Fixed Effects

For decades, applied researchers estimated staggered designs with a single two-way fixed-effects (TWFE) regression:

Y_{it} = \alpha_i + \gamma_t + \beta\, D_{it} + \varepsilon_{it}.

How to read the TWFE regression.

  • \alpha_i is a unit fixed effect — one intercept per region. It absorbs every time-invariant difference between regions (Skåne is simply a higher-falls region than Jämtland, for reasons that never change over the window).
  • \gamma_t is a time fixed effect — one intercept per year. It absorbs every shock that hits all regions in a given year equally (a nationwide cold snap in 2018, a change in coding practice).
  • D_{it} is the treatment indicator from above; \beta is the coefficient we care about.
  • \varepsilon_{it} is the idiosyncratic error, clustered by region in practice.

The hope is that \hat\beta recovers “the” treatment effect of the fall-prevention program, purged of fixed regional differences and common time trends.

In the clean 2×2 case, this hope is justified: with one treated group, one control group, and one switch date, \hat\beta is exactly the difference-in-differences estimate. The trouble is that researchers assumed the same single coefficient would keep meaning “the treatment effect” once adoption was staggered. It does not.

The reason is subtle and was not widely understood until around 2018–2021, in a wave of papers — Goodman-Bacon (2021), de Chaisemartin & D’Haultfœuille (2020), Callaway & Sant’Anna (2021), Sun & Abraham (2021) — now collectively called the “DiD revolution.” We focus on the cleanest diagnostic: the Goodman-Bacon decomposition.

What \hat\beta Actually Averages

Goodman-Bacon’s (2021) result is a theorem about arithmetic, not about assumptions. It says: the TWFE coefficient \hat\beta from a staggered design is identically equal to a weighted average of every possible 2×2 difference-in-differences you could build by picking a pair of cohorts and a pair of periods.

There are three kinds of these 2×2 building blocks. Two are perfectly sensible. One is poison.

The three flavors of 2×2 comparison.

  1. Treated vs. never-treated. An adopting cohort is compared, before and after its switch, to units that never adopt. Clean. The control group’s treatment status never changes, so it provides an honest counterfactual trend.

  2. Earlier-treated vs. later-treated, in the window before the later group adopts. An early cohort (already on) is the treated group; a not-yet-treated late cohort is the control. Clean. The control is genuinely untreated during this window.

  3. Later-treated vs. earlier-treated, in the window after BOTH have adopted. Here the late cohort is the treated group — and the already-treated early cohort serves as its control. This is the forbidden comparison.

The schematic below draws the three flavors side by side. In each mini-panel, the bar’s green segment is a unit while it is untreated; the red segment is a unit while it is treated. A clean 2×2 needs a control bar that is green (untreated) throughout the comparison window. Flavor 3 fails exactly that test — its “control” is already red.

Green = unit is untreated; red = unit is treated. The dashed box marks the comparison window. Flavors 1 and 2 have a control bar that is green (untreated) across the window — a valid counterfactual. In flavor 3 the “control” bar is already red inside the window: an already-treated cohort masquerading as a control. That is the forbidden comparison.

The third comparison is the villain. Look closely at what it does. To estimate the late cohort’s effect, it differences out the change in the early cohort over the same window. But the early cohort is already treated and still responding — if the program’s benefit keeps growing year after year, the early cohort’s outcome is still moving because of its own treatment. Subtracting that movement does not net out a common trend; it subtracts a treatment effect.

In a forbidden 2×2, the control group’s outcome is contaminated by its own ongoing treatment dynamics. The DiD subtracts that dynamic path as if it were a counterfactual trend. When a beneficial effect grows over time, the early “control” cohort’s outcome is still falling because of its own treatment; subtracting that falling path adds a positive amount, so the estimated effect of the later cohort is pushed up — toward zero, toward apparent harm. Equivalently, the early cohort’s true (beneficial, negative) dynamics enter \hat\beta with a negative weight, turning real benefit into spurious harm.

The causal picture below makes the contamination explicit. The late cohort’s outcome moves for two reasons: its own treatment (the green causal arrow, the thing we want to measure) and the shared secular trend. The forbidden 2×2 tries to net out that trend by subtracting the early cohort’s change — but the early cohort’s outcome is being driven by its own treatment (a second green arrow). When that contaminated path is used as the “control trend,” it injects the early cohort’s effect into \hat\beta along the red route: a back-door that opens because an already-treated unit was pressed into service as a control.

// ── Small static DAG component for the forbidden-comparison structure.
// Page-tag-prefixed (p2_), pure: depends only on global d3, returns svg.node().
// nodes {id,x,y,label} in normalized [0,1]; conditioned ids => rounded squares;
// edges {source,target,kind}: 'causal'=#2e6f40, 'backdoor-open'=#b3000b,
// 'blocked'=#79766e dashed. One arrowhead marker per colour. ──
p2_drawDAG = function ({ nodes, edges, width = 460, height = 240, conditioned = [] }) {
  const pad = 52, r = 24;
  const sx = v => pad + v * (width - 2 * pad);
  const sy = v => pad + v * (height - 2 * pad);
  const byId = new Map(nodes.map(d => [d.id, d]));
  const condSet = new Set(conditioned);

  const colorFor = k =>
    k === "causal" ? "#2e6f40" :
    (k === "backdoor-open" || k === "biasing") ? "#b3000b" : "#79766e";
  const markerId = k =>
    k === "causal" ? "p2dag-green" :
    (k === "backdoor-open" || k === "biasing") ? "p2dag-red" : "p2dag-gray";

  const svg = d3.create("svg")
    .attr("width", width).attr("height", height)
    .attr("viewBox", `0 0 ${width} ${height}`)
    .attr("font-family", "'Inter', sans-serif")
    .style("max-width", "100%").style("height", "auto");

  const defs = svg.append("defs");
  for (const [id, col] of [["p2dag-green", "#2e6f40"], ["p2dag-red", "#b3000b"], ["p2dag-gray", "#79766e"]]) {
    defs.append("marker")
      .attr("id", id).attr("viewBox", "0 -5 10 10")
      .attr("refX", 9).attr("refY", 0)
      .attr("markerWidth", 7).attr("markerHeight", 7)
      .attr("orient", "auto-start-reverse")
      .append("path").attr("d", "M0,-5L10,0L0,5").attr("fill", col);
  }

  const eg = svg.append("g");
  for (const e of edges) {
    const s = byId.get(e.source), t = byId.get(e.target);
    if (!s || !t) continue;
    const x1 = sx(s.x), y1 = sy(s.y), x2 = sx(t.x), y2 = sy(t.y);
    const dx = x2 - x1, dy = y2 - y1;
    const len = Math.hypot(dx, dy) || 1;
    const ux = dx / len, uy = dy / len;
    eg.append("line")
      .attr("x1", x1 + ux * r).attr("y1", y1 + uy * r)
      .attr("x2", x2 - ux * (r + 4)).attr("y2", y2 - uy * (r + 4))
      .attr("stroke", colorFor(e.kind))
      .attr("stroke-width", e.kind === "blocked" ? 2 : 3)
      .attr("stroke-dasharray", e.kind === "blocked" ? "5,4" : null)
      .attr("marker-end", `url(#${markerId(e.kind)})`);
  }

  const ng = svg.append("g");
  for (const n of nodes) {
    const cx = sx(n.x), cy = sy(n.y);
    const g = ng.append("g");
    if (condSet.has(n.id)) {
      g.append("rect")
        .attr("x", cx - r).attr("y", cy - r)
        .attr("width", 2 * r).attr("height", 2 * r)
        .attr("rx", 7).attr("ry", 7)
        .attr("fill", "#eef0ee").attr("stroke", "#1c1c1a").attr("stroke-width", 4);
    } else {
      g.append("circle")
        .attr("cx", cx).attr("cy", cy).attr("r", r)
        .attr("fill", "#eef0ee").attr("stroke", "#1c1c1a").attr("stroke-width", 2);
    }
    g.append("text")
      .attr("x", cx).attr("y", cy)
      .attr("text-anchor", "middle").attr("dominant-baseline", "central")
      .attr("font-size", 14).attr("font-weight", 700).attr("fill", "#1c1c1a")
      .text(n.label);
    if (n.caption) {
      g.append("text")
        .attr("x", cx).attr("y", cy + r + 14)
        .attr("text-anchor", "middle").attr("dominant-baseline", "central")
        .attr("font-size", 10.5).attr("font-weight", 600).attr("fill", "#79766e")
        .text(n.caption);
    }
  }
  return svg.node();
}

p2_drawDAG({
  width: Math.min(540, width),
  height: 232,
  nodes: [
    { id: "De", x: 0.06, y: 0.16, label: "Dₑ", caption: "early treated" },
    { id: "Ye", x: 0.06, y: 0.84, label: "Yₑ", caption: "early outcome" },
    { id: "Dl", x: 0.94, y: 0.16, label: "Dₗ", caption: "late treated" },
    { id: "Yl", x: 0.94, y: 0.84, label: "Yₗ", caption: "late outcome" },
    { id: "C",  x: 0.50, y: 0.84, label: "ctrl", caption: "used as control trend" }
  ],
  conditioned: ["C"],
  edges: [
    { source: "De", target: "Ye", kind: "causal" },        // early's own effect (real)
    { source: "Dl", target: "Yl", kind: "causal" },         // late's own effect (target)
    { source: "Ye", target: "C",  kind: "blocked" },        // early outcome enters as "control"
    { source: "C",  target: "Yl", kind: "backdoor-open" }   // contaminating subtraction into late estimate
  ]
})

Green = real causal effect; red = the opened back-door; grey dashed = the early outcome being routed in as a “control.” The early cohort’s outcome (Y_e) is still falling because of D_e; subtracting it as the late cohort’s counterfactual trend (boxed node) opens a back-door into \hat\beta_\ell that injects the early cohort’s effect with the wrong sign.

Read the punchline carefully: \hat\beta can place negative weights on perfectly real, beneficial treatment effects. With enough dynamics, \hat\beta can be smaller in magnitude than the smallest unit-level effect, larger than the largest, or even carry the opposite sign of every single true effect in the data. A program that helps every region in every year can produce a TWFE estimate saying it does harm.

One subtlety worth settling now, because it sounds contradictory: the weights Goodman-Bacon attaches to the 2×2 comparisons are all positive (they are sample shares times a variance, both non-negative). The negative weights appear only when you re-express the same \hat\beta at the finer level of individual unit-time treatment effects \tau_{it}. A positively-weighted but poisoned forbidden comparison — one that subtracts a treatment effect as if it were a trend — is algebraically identical to attaching a negative weight to that subtracted effect. Same number, two bookkeeping levels: positive weights on contaminated 2×2 targets, negative weights on the underlying effects.

How to Read the Cohort Plot

Step 1: Start with no dynamics. Leave the dynamics slider at 0. Each adopting cohort’s line follows the shared secular decline until its adoption year — marked by the colored dot and the dashed vertical gridline — then drops by the on-impact amount and runs parallel again. The early cohort drops first (2017), the middle next (2019), the late last (2021). The gray dotted line is the never-treated group. In the scoreboard, the True ATT and the TWFE estimate sit right on top of each other and the bias is essentially zero. This is the world where TWFE is fine: a static, homogeneous effect.

Step 2: Turn dynamics on. Drag the dynamics slider toward 1. Now each treated cohort keeps falling further every year it stays in the program — the benefit compounds. The early cohort, having adopted in 2017, accumulates the most benefit by 2023; the late cohort has barely begun. The True ATT (green) becomes far more negative, correctly reflecting the larger average benefit. But watch the TWFE estimate (pink): it barely budges. By the time dynamics are near 1 with a modest on-impact effect (-0.5), the true ATT can be below -5 while TWFE still hovers near -2 — a massive attenuation toward zero. TWFE is already badly wrong; it just has not yet crossed zero, because the never-treated group still supplies enough clean comparisons to hold the sign.

Step 3: Catch the sign flip. Now toggle Drop the never-treated regions — the very “fix” a nervous analyst reaches for (Exercise 4). With the clean never-treated anchor gone, TWFE must lean almost entirely on already-treated cohorts as controls: the forbidden comparisons now dominate. Keep the on-impact effect modest (-0.5) and dynamics high, and the bias box turns red and reads WRONG SIGN. TWFE now reports that the program raises fall hospitalizations, even though every cohort in every year is lowering them. The decomposition tells you exactly why: the forbidden comparisons subtract the early cohorts’ accumulating benefit as if it were a counterfactual trend.

Step 4: Visualize the forbidden window. Toggle Show the forbidden window. The shaded pink band marks the period from 2021 onward, where the late cohort has just adopted and is “controlled” by the early and middle cohorts — which are deep into their own growing treatment effects. In that band, the early cohort’s line is steeply sloping downward because of its own treatment. Using it as the control trend for the late cohort manufactures a spurious upward (harmful) effect. That is the negative weight, made visible. The small panel directly beneath the scoreboard now draws that exact subtraction live — the late cohort’s change minus the already-treated early cohort’s change, with the running DiD — so you can watch it tip into spurious harm as dynamics rise; to do the same arithmetic by hand on a three-region version, work Exercises 2 and 3 below.

The lesson is not “fixed effects are bad.” It is that a single coefficient cannot summarize a heterogeneous, dynamic set of effects without an implicit weighting scheme — and that weighting scheme, chosen by OLS rather than by you, can include negative weights on real effects. When effects are static and homogeneous, the weighting is benign and TWFE is unbiased. The danger lives entirely in the interaction of staggered timing with non-constant effects.

When Is TWFE Safe?

The decomposition lets us state the conditions precisely.

The TWFE coefficient \hat\beta is an unbiased, sensibly weighted estimate of the ATT when either of the following holds:

  1. Homogeneous, static effects. Every unit has the same constant treatment effect \tau that does not vary over event-time. Then all 2×2 comparisons — forbidden ones included — estimate the same \tau, the contaminating terms vanish, and \hat\beta = \tau.

  2. No variation in treatment timing. If all adopters switch in the same period (a single 2×2), there are no forbidden comparisons, and TWFE collapses to ordinary DiD.

Conversely, TWFE is biased for the ATT whenever treatment timing is staggered and treatment effects vary — either across cohorts (heterogeneity) or across event-time within a cohort (dynamics). Real reforms almost always have dynamics, so this is the typical case.

In our fall-prevention setting, the program’s benefit plausibly grows for several years as clinics build routines, train distriktssköterskor, and accumulate at-risk patients into the program. That is exactly the dynamic-effects case where TWFE misleads.

Math Details: The Goodman-Bacon Weighting

The decomposition theorem

Goodman-Bacon (2021) proves that the TWFE estimator can be written exactly as

\hat\beta_{\text{TWFE}} = \sum_{(k,\ell)} s_{k\ell}\, \hat\beta^{2\times2}_{k\ell},

read as “the TWFE coefficient is a weighted sum, over all pairs of timing groups (k,\ell), of the simple 2×2 DiD estimate \hat\beta^{2\times2}_{k\ell} formed from that pair, with weights s_{k\ell}.” The weights are non-negative, sum to one, and are proportional to each pair’s share of the sample times the variance of treatment within that 2×2:

s_{k\ell} \;\propto\; (n_k + n_\ell)^2 \cdot \bar{D}_{k\ell}\,(1 - \bar{D}_{k\ell}),

where \bar{D}_{k\ell} is the fraction of treated observations in the pairwise subsample. How to read this: a comparison gets more weight when the two groups are large and when treatment is “balanced” (roughly half the cells treated) within the pair, because that is where OLS sees the most identifying variation.

Why the weights are benign but the targets are poisoned

The weights s_{k\ell} are always positive. The trouble is what each 2×2 estimates when effects are dynamic. Take a forbidden pair: the later group \ell (adopting at G_\ell) is the treated group, and the earlier group k (adopting at G_k < G_\ell) is the control during the post-G_\ell window. Its 2×2 estimand — the quantity this comparison actually targets in the population, as opposed to the noisy estimate a finite sample returns — decomposes as

\hat\beta^{2\times2}_{\text{forbidden}} \;=\; \underbrace{\text{ATT}_\ell(\text{post})}_{\text{what we want}} \;-\; \underbrace{\big[\text{ATT}_k(\text{post}) - \text{ATT}_k(\text{mid})\big]}_{\text{change in the EARLY group's own effect}}.

How to read this: the first term is the late cohort’s genuine effect, which we want. The second bracket is the change in the early cohort’s treatment effect between the two sub-windows. If the early cohort’s effect is constant (static), the bracket is zero and the forbidden 2×2 is clean. But if the early effect is growing more beneficial, the bracket is negative, so subtracting it adds a positive (harmful-looking) bias.

The bottom line

Aggregating across all forbidden pairs, de Chaisemartin & D’Haultfœuille (2020) show that \hat\beta equals a weighted average of unit-time treatment effects in which some weights can be negative:

\hat\beta = E\!\left[\sum_{i,t} w_{it}\,\tau_{it}\right], \qquad \sum_{i,t} w_{it} = 1, \quad \text{but possibly } w_{it} < 0.

If enough weight sits on the forbidden comparisons — which happens when early cohorts are large and treatment is dynamic — the negative weights can dominate, and \hat\beta can fall outside the convex hull of all the true \tau_{it} (the convex hull is just the range spanned by the true effects — everything from the smallest to the largest; an honest weighted average can never escape it, but one with negative weights can). That is the formal statement of “TWFE can have the wrong sign.” The fix, developed on the next page, is to estimate cohort-by-event-time effects directly and aggregate them with weights you choose, never using an already-treated unit as a control.

Code: Reproducing the Bias and Diagnosing It

The code below simulates a staggered fall-prevention rollout with growing effects (every cohort benefits, more so the longer it has been treated), then shows (a) that naive TWFE is severely attenuated toward zero — roughly half the true magnitude — and (b) the Goodman-Bacon decomposition that explains why. (With a never-treated comparison group present, TWFE keeps the right sign here; strip that clean anchor away — as in the interactive’s “drop never-treated” toggle — and the same forces flip the sign outright.)

library(tidyverse)
library(fixest)
library(bacondecomp)

set.seed(2016)

# --- Simulate a staggered panel: 21 regioner, years 2014–2023 ---
years   <- 2014:2023
cohorts <- tibble(
  region = 1:21,
  g = sample(c(2017, 2019, 2021, Inf), 21, replace = TRUE,
             prob = c(.3, .3, .25, .15))         # adoption year (Inf = never)
)

panel <- expand_grid(region = 1:21, year = years) |>
  left_join(cohorts, by = "region") |>
  mutate(
    event   = if_else(is.finite(g), year - g, -99L),
    treated = as.integer(is.finite(g) & year >= g),
    # GROWING, beneficial effect: -1.5 on impact, extra -1.1 per event-year
    tau     = if_else(treated == 1, -1.5 - 1.1 * pmax(event, 0), 0),
    reg_fe  = rnorm(21, 41, 3)[region],
    y = reg_fe - 0.25 * (year - 2014) + tau + rnorm(n(), 0, 0.8)
  )

# True ATT (size-weighted average over treated cells)
true_att <- panel |> filter(treated == 1) |> summarise(att = mean(tau)) |> pull()

# --- Naive TWFE (the wrong tool) ---
twfe <- feols(y ~ treated | region + year, data = panel, cluster = ~region)

cat("True overall ATT :", round(true_att, 3), "\n")
cat("TWFE estimate    :", round(coef(twfe)["treated"], 3), "\n")
# TWFE is biased heavily TOWARD zero (here ~half the true magnitude): the
# forbidden comparisons subtract the already-treated cohorts' growing effects
# as if they were trends. The never-treated group keeps the sign correct here;
# without a clean control group the same bias can flip the sign entirely.

# --- Goodman-Bacon decomposition: see the forbidden weights ---
bacon_out <- bacon(y ~ treated, data = panel,
                   id_var = "region", time_var = "year")
bacon_out |>
  group_by(type) |>
  summarise(weight = sum(weight),
            avg_est = weighted.mean(estimate, weight)) |>
  print()

# comparisons. With growing effects their estimates are badly biased toward
# zero (and can turn positive under stronger dynamics); they drag the overall

import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import pyfixest as pf

rng = np.random.default_rng(2016)

# --- Simulate a staggered panel: 21 regioner, years 2014–2023 ---
years   = np.arange(2014, 2024)
regions = np.arange(1, 22)
g = rng.choice([2017, 2019, 2021, np.inf], size=21,
               p=[.30, .30, .25, .15])           # adoption year (inf = never)
region_fe = rng.normal(41, 3, size=21)

rows = []
for r in regions:
    gi = g[r - 1]
    for t in years:
        treated = int(np.isfinite(gi) and t >= gi)
        event   = (t - gi) if np.isfinite(gi) else -99
        # GROWING beneficial effect: -1.5 on impact, extra -1.1 per event-year
        tau = (-1.5 - 1.1 * max(event, 0)) if treated else 0.0
        y = (region_fe[r - 1] - 0.25 * (t - 2014) + tau
             + rng.normal(0, 0.8))
        rows.append((r, t, gi, treated, tau, y))

panel = pd.DataFrame(rows, columns=["region", "year", "g",
                                    "treated", "tau", "y"])

true_att = panel.loc[panel.treated == 1, "tau"].mean()

# --- Naive TWFE (the wrong tool) ---
twfe = pf.feols("y ~ treated | region + year", data=panel,
                vcov={"CRV1": "region"})
beta = twfe.coef()["treated"]

print(f"True overall ATT : {true_att:6.3f}")
print(f"TWFE estimate    : {beta:6.3f}")

Exercises

Exercise 1

A study evaluates the fall-prevention program with TWFE and finds \hat\beta = +0.4 (i.e., the program appears to increase fall hospitalizations by 0.4 per 1,000), statistically significant. A colleague who knows the clinical literature insists the program is beneficial in every region that adopted it. Without re-running anything, explain how both claims can be simultaneously true, and name the specific features of the rollout that make this possible.

Show answer

Both can be true because \hat\beta is not “the” treatment effect — it is a weighted average of 2×2 DiDs in which some weights are effectively negative. If the program’s benefit grows over event-time (dynamic effects) and adoption is staggered, the forbidden comparisons (later-adopting cohorts “controlled” by already-treated earlier cohorts) subtract the early cohorts’ accumulating benefit as though it were a common trend. That subtraction manufactures a spurious positive (harmful-looking) contribution. With enough early adopters and strong enough dynamics, these contributions can dominate and push \hat\beta above zero even though every unit-time treatment effect \tau_{it} is negative (beneficial).

The two ingredients that make this possible, named precisely: (1) staggered adoption timing (so forbidden comparisons exist at all) and (2) treatment-effect dynamics or cohort heterogeneity (so the early cohorts’ outcomes keep moving because of their own treatment). Remove either one — a single adoption date, or static/homogeneous effects — and the paradox disappears.

Exercise 2

Consider a minimal staggered panel: three regions, three years (t = 1, 2, 3). Region A adopts at t = 2; Region B adopts at t = 3; Region C never adopts. Suppose the true treatment effect is constant and homogeneous: every treated cell has effect \tau = -2 (lowers falls by 2), with no trend and no noise, and every region has untreated outcome 40.

  1. Write out the outcome Y_{it} for all 9 cells.
  2. Identify the forbidden 2×2 comparison.
  3. Show that even the forbidden comparison gives the correct -2 here. Why does the negative-weight problem vanish?
Show answer

(a) With baseline 40 and \tau = -2 on every treated cell (D_{it} = 1 when t \ge G_i):

Region t=1 t=2 t=3
A (G=2) 40 38 38
B (G=3) 40 40 38
C (never) 40 40 40

(b) The forbidden comparison: in period t=3, the later cohort B is treated and the already-treated cohort A is used as its control (comparing t=2 \to t=3).

(c) Compute that forbidden 2×2 (B vs. A, t=2 \to t=3):

  • B’s change: 38 - 40 = -2.
  • A’s change: 38 - 38 = 0.
  • DiD = (-2) - (0) = -2. ✓ Correct.

It works because A’s effect is static: A had already fully absorbed its -2 by t=2, so A’s outcome does not move between t=2 and t=3. The “control trend” contributed by A is zero, exactly as a valid control should be. In the Math Details notation, the contaminating bracket is [\text{ATT}_A(t=3) - \text{ATT}_A(t=2)] = (-2) - (-2) = 0, so there is no bias. This is precisely condition (1) of the “When is TWFE safe?” card.

Exercise 3

Now keep the design of Exercise 2 but make the effect dynamic: a treated region’s effect is -2 in its first treated year and -4 in its second treated year (the benefit grows). Outcomes are otherwise as before (baseline 40, no trend, no noise).

  1. Rebuild the 3×3 outcome table.
  2. Compute the forbidden 2×2 (B treated at t=3, controlled by already-treated A, comparing t=2 \to t=3).
  3. Compare it to B’s true first-year effect of -2. What is the bias, and what sign does it have?
Show answer

(a) Region A adopts at t=2: effect -2 at t=2 (its 1st treated year), -4 at t=3 (its 2nd treated year). Region B adopts at t=3: effect -2 at t=3 (its 1st treated year). Region C: never treated.

Region t=1 t=2 t=3
A (G=2) 40 38 36
B (G=3) 40 40 38
C (never) 40 40 40

(b) Forbidden 2×2 (B vs. A, t=2 \to t=3):

  • B’s change: 38 - 40 = -2.
  • A’s change: 36 - 38 = -2.
  • DiD = (-2) - (-2) = 0.

(c) B’s true effect in its first treated year is -2. The forbidden comparison reports 0. The bias is 0 - (-2) = +2 — a positive (harmful-looking) bias that exactly wipes out B’s real benefit.

The mechanism is transparent: A’s outcome fell by 2 between t=2 and t=3 because A’s own treatment effect grew from -2 to -4. Using A as B’s “control” subtracts that -2 change, crediting it to a counterfactual trend. The contaminating bracket is [\text{ATT}_A(t=3) - \text{ATT}_A(t=2)] = (-4) - (-2) = -2, and subtracting it adds +2 of bias. A program that helps both regions can, through enough such comparisons, be reported as doing nothing — or, with stronger dynamics, as doing harm. This is the negative-weight problem in three regions.

Exercise 4

In the interactive, set the on-impact effect to -0.5 and push dynamics high. With the never-treated regions kept, TWFE is badly attenuated but still negative (still the right sign). A policy analyst now proposes “fixing” the attenuation by simply dropping the never-treated regions from the regression, reasoning that the never-treated group might be unusual. Toggle Drop the never-treated regions and watch the bias box. Did dropping them help? Explain why the result moves the way it does, and state what actually fixes the problem.

Show answer

No — dropping the never-treated group makes things worse, not better. In the interactive you can watch it happen: with the never-treated regions kept, TWFE is attenuated toward zero but still negative; the moment you toggle them off, the bias box flips to red and reads WRONG SIGN. The never-treated units are the source of the clean comparisons (flavor 1: treated vs. never-treated). Removing them forces \hat\beta to lean almost entirely on the earlier-vs-later and later-vs-earlier 2×2s built from the adopting cohorts — which is exactly where the forbidden comparisons live. With a small on-impact effect and strong dynamics, the contaminated forbidden comparisons then carry the lion’s share of the weight, so the wrong-sign problem does not just persist — it appears.

What actually fixes it is a different estimator, not a different sample. The fix is to:

  1. Estimate cohort-by-event-time average treatment effects \text{ATT}(g, t) directly, each using only a clean control group (never-treated or not-yet-treated units), so an already-treated unit is never used as a control; then
  2. Aggregate those clean building blocks with transparent, non-negative weights you choose (e.g., a simple overall ATT, or an event-study profile).

This is the Callaway–Sant’Anna (2021) approach (and the closely related Sun–Abraham and de Chaisemartin–D’Haultfœuille estimators), the subject of the next page. The honest target was never in doubt; the problem was forcing a single OLS coefficient to find it.

Key takeaways

  • Under staggered adoption, the single TWFE coefficient is not “the DiD” — the Goodman-Bacon decomposition shows it is a weighted average of every 2x2 comparison in the panel.
  • The villain is the forbidden comparison: using an already-treated cohort as the control for a later cohort subtracts that cohort’s own ongoing treatment dynamics as if it were a counterfactual trend.
  • With staggered timing and effects that grow or vary, \hat\beta can place negative weights on real effects — attenuating toward zero, or even reporting harm when every unit was helped.
  • TWFE is safe only with homogeneous, static effects or with no variation in treatment timing; real reforms with dynamic effects are the dangerous typical case.
  • Dropping the never-treated group — the source of clean comparisons — makes things worse; the fix is a different estimator, not a different sample.
