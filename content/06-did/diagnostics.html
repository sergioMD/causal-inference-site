Diagnostics

Event studies, pre-trend tests, and sensitivity analysis

In this section, you will learn to

  • Specify an event study and read its leads as a pre-trend diagnostic and lags as dynamic effects, attending to the reference-period choice
  • Explain why pre-trend tests have low power and why conditioning on passing them (pre-testing) distorts the estimate
  • Apply the Honest-DiD (Rambachan-Roth) relative-magnitudes restriction to form a robust confidence set and compute the breakdown value \bar{M}^\ast
  • Design placebo and falsification tests (fake dates, untreated outcomes, never-treated permutations) and interpret what passing or failing each implies
  • Assess specification sensitivity across levels vs. logs, control-group choice, covariate sets, and clustering

When the Estimate Is Not Enough

The previous page handed you a toolbox of modern difference-in-differences estimators — Callaway–Sant’Anna group-time effects, Sun–Abraham interaction-weighted regressions, the stacked design — each engineered to recover a clean average treatment effect on the treated when treatment timing is staggered and effects are heterogeneous. Run any of them and you get a number, a standard error, and a verdict.

But a DiD estimate is only as good as its identifying assumption, and that assumption — parallel trends — is fundamentally untestable. It is a claim about a counterfactual: what would the treated regions’ outcomes have done, after treatment, had they not been treated? Nobody observes that. The entire credibility of the design rests on a premise we can never directly check.

This is the capstone page of the book, and its subject is the discipline that separates a defensible causal claim from a wishful one: diagnostics. We will build the central diagnostic tool — the event-study plot — and then ask the hard question that every honest applied economist must confront. The pre-treatment coefficients look flat; the confidence bands cover zero; the referee is satisfied. How much should that reassure us? The modern answer, due to Rambachan and Roth (2023), is humbling and constructive at once: the flat pre-trends do not prove parallel trends, but they do let us bound how badly parallel trends could plausibly fail — and report an estimate that survives that bound.

We stay in the Swedish primary-care world. Our running example: in 2017, several of Sweden’s 21 regioner rolled out a fall-prevention program (fallpreventionsprogram) for elderly patients at their vårdcentraler — balance training, medication review by distriktssköterskor, home-hazard assessment. The outcome is the regional rate of fall-related hospital admissions per 1,000 residents aged 75+. Some regions adopted in 2017; others never did. Did the program work — and can we trust the comparison?

The Event-Study Specification

The single most informative thing you can do after a DiD analysis is to stop reporting one treatment effect and instead estimate a separate effect for each period relative to treatment. This is the event-study (or dynamic) specification.

Index calendar years by t and let E_i be the year region i adopted the program (its event time origin). Define relative time k = t - E_i: the number of years since (or until) adoption. Then estimate

Y_{it} = \alpha_i + \lambda_t + \sum_{k \neq -1} \beta_k \, \mathbf{1}\{t - E_i = k\} + \varepsilon_{it}

How to read this formula. “The fall-admission rate for region i in year t equals a region fixed effect \alpha_i (each region’s permanent level), plus a year fixed effect \lambda_t (a shock common to all regions that year), plus a sum of relative-time effects \beta_k — one coefficient for each number of years k away from the adoption year — plus noise.” The dummy \mathbf{1}\{t - E_i = k\} switches on for region i exactly in the year that is k periods from its own adoption.

The coefficients split into two families:

Leads and lags in an event study.

  • Lead coefficients (k < -1): effects estimated for periods before treatment. Under parallel trends these should all be zero — the treated and control groups were moving together before the program. Leads are the diagnostic.

  • Lag coefficients (k \geq 0): effects estimated for periods at and after treatment. These are the dynamic treatment effects — the object of interest. \beta_0 is the impact in the adoption year, \beta_1 one year later, and so on.

  • The reference period (k = -1): one period is omitted (here, the year just before adoption) to break collinearity with the fixed effects. Every \beta_k is read relative to this normalized baseline. By construction \beta_{-1} = 0; it is not estimated.

The picture below makes the relative-time axis concrete before you reach the live tool. The leads to the left of the dashed adoption line are the diagnostic (they should be flat); the hollow square at k=-1 is the omitted reference period (an estimand is the population quantity you are trying to recover — here each \beta_k is one); the lags to the right are the dynamic effects you actually care about.

Red = lead periods (k<-1), the pre-treatment diagnostic. The hollow square at k=-1 is the omitted reference period, pinned to zero (\beta_{-1}\equiv 0). Slate = lag periods (k\ge 0), the dynamic treatment effects. The dashed line is the adoption moment, falling between k=-1 and k=0.

The reference period matters more than it looks. Because all coefficients are measured against k=-1, a \beta_k is the change in the treated-vs-control gap between period -1 and period k. If you suspect anticipation — patients or staff changing behavior in the year before the official launch — then k=-1 is contaminated, and you should normalize against an earlier period (say k=-2) instead.

The reference period is a modeling choice, not a fact of nature. Two papers analyzing the same data can show event-study plots that look entirely different purely because they normalized to different baseline periods. Always state which period is omitted, plot the reference point explicitly (a zero at k=-1), and check that your conclusions are not an artifact of that choice. A “significant pre-trend” can sometimes be made to appear or vanish by re-baselining.

See It: The Event-Study Diagnostic and Honest-DiD Band

The interactive below is the heart of this page. It simulates the Swedish fall-prevention event study and lets you do two things that mirror the real diagnostic workflow.

First, with the pre-trend slope slider, you inject a genuine differential trend between treated and control regions — the kind of slow divergence that parallel trends rules out. Watch the lead coefficients tilt and, eventually, light up as “significant.” Second, the Honest DiD controls let you stop assuming parallel trends and instead bound the violation: you choose how large a post-treatment trend deviation you are willing to allow, expressed as a multiple \bar{M} of the worst pre-trend deviation you actually observed, and the chart returns the robust confidence set for the post-treatment effect. As \bar{M} grows, that interval widens; at the breakdown value \bar{M}^\ast it first touches zero, and you can no longer claim significance.

// the reference period, as in real data — fewer effective comparisons).

  [-5, 0.55], [-4, 0.48], [-3, 0.42], [-2, 0.40],
  [-1, 0.0],                                   // reference, not estimated
  [0, 0.40], [1, 0.44], [2, 0.50], [3, 0.58]

// ── Build the event-study coefficients ──
// Leads carry only the injected differential trend (relative to k = -1).
// Lags carry the true treatment effect PLUS the continuation of that trend
// (a violation of parallel trends contaminates the post-period too).
diag_coefs = diag_kGrid.map(k => {
  const trendComponent = diag_preSlope * (k - (-1));   // linear in (k + 1)
  let point;
  if (k === -1) {
    point = 0;                                  // reference period
  } else if (k < -1) {
    point = trendComponent;                     // pre: pure trend
  } else {
    point = diag_trueEffect + trendComponent;   // post: effect + trend
  }
  const se = diag_seByK.get(k);
  return {
    k,
    estimate: point,
    se,
    lo: point - 1.96 * se,
    hi: point + 1.96 * se,
    sig: k !== -1 && Math.abs(point) > 1.96 * se,
    period: k < 0 ? "Pre" : "Post"
  };
})
diag_maxPre = d3.max(
  diag_coefs.filter(d => d.k < -1),
  d => Math.abs(d.estimate)
)

// 2. Pooled post-treatment effect (simple average of the lag coefficients)
//    and its standard error (treat lags as independent for this teaching demo).
diag_postCoefs = diag_coefs.filter(d => d.k >= 0)

diag_postEffect = d3.mean(diag_postCoefs, d => d.estimate)

diag_postSE = Math.sqrt(
  d3.sum(diag_postCoefs, d => d.se * d.se)
) / diag_postCoefs.length

// 3. Original (parallel-trends-assumed) 95% CI for the post effect.
diag_origCI = [
  diag_postEffect - 1.96 * diag_postSE,
  diag_postEffect + 1.96 * diag_postSE

// 4. Robust CI under the relative-magnitudes restriction: allow the
//    post-treatment trend to deviate by up to M̄ × (max observed pre-trend).
//    The identified set for the effect inflates by ± that allowance, then we
//    add sampling uncertainty on top.
diag_allowance = diag_Mbar * diag_maxPre

diag_robustCI = [
  diag_postEffect - diag_allowance - 1.96 * diag_postSE,

How to Read the Event-Study Plot

Work through these steps in order; each isolates one idea.

Step 1: Read a clean event study. Start at the defaults — pre-trend slope = 0, true effect = -1.5, Honest-DiD off. The dark-red dots to the left of the dashed adoption line are the lead coefficients; they sit on the zero line (this is a noiseless teaching simulation, so a zero pre-trend slope places every lead exactly at zero), and their 95% confidence rules all cross the gray zero line. That is what a passing pre-trend looks like: no detectable divergence before the program. The slate dots to the right are the lag coefficients — the dynamic effects — sitting near -1.5, indicating fewer fall admissions after adoption. The hollow dot at k=-1 is the reference period, pinned to zero by construction. (Now drag the True post-treatment effect slider toward 0 and back to -3: the slate lag dots and the pooled-effect card slide with it, while the red leads stay put — this slider sets the genuine effect the diagnostics are trying to recover.)

Step 2: Inject a pre-trend and watch the diagnostic fail. Drag the pre-trend slope up to about +0.4. Now the lead coefficients tilt downward as you move left (they are measured relative to k=-1), their magnitudes grow, and the two leftmost ones (k=-5 and k=-4) turn significant — their confidence rules no longer cross zero. The “Pre-trend test” card flips from passes to FAILS. Crucially, notice the lag coefficients moved too: a real differential trend does not politely stop at adoption, it keeps contaminating the post-period. The estimate (the pooled post-effect card) drifts upward toward zero — it is now biased.

Step 3: Confront the power problem. Set the slope to a small value like +0.06. The leads are visibly tilted — there genuinely is a violation — yet the confidence rules still cross zero, so the test passes. This is the central lesson: a pre-trend test that passes is weak evidence. Small but real violations hide inside wide confidence bands, and a small violation in the pre-period can still imply a large bias in the post-period.

Step 4: Switch to Honest DiD. Set a small pre-trend slope — say +0.10 — then toggle Apply Honest-DiD robust band on. A shaded slate band appears over the post-treatment region: the robust confidence set for the pooled effect. With \bar{M}=1 it is wider than the naïve interval, because we are no longer assuming the pre-trend is exactly zero — we allow the post-treatment trend to deviate by as much as the largest deviation we saw before treatment. (Note the band’s extra width is \pm\,\bar{M} times that observed pre-trend deviation. With the slope at exactly 0 every lead is pinned to zero, so the observed deviation is zero, the allowance is \bar{M}\times 0 = 0, and the robust band coincides with the naïve one no matter how large \bar{M} is — that is why the breakdown card reads \infty. Honest DiD only “bites” once there is a pre-trend to measure against.)

Step 5: Find the breakdown value. Keeping the small slope from Step 4, slowly raise \bar{M}. The robust band widens symmetrically. At some point its upper edge crosses zero — the “Robust 95% set” card turns red and the result is no longer significant. The value of \bar{M} where this happens is the breakdown value \bar{M}^\ast, reported in the ochre card. Read it aloud as: “the conclusion that the program reduced fall admissions survives any trend violation up to \bar{M}^\ast times the worst pre-trend we observed.” A large \bar{M}^\ast (say above 2) is reassuring; a breakdown below 1 means the result cannot even withstand a violation the size of what you already see in the pre-period.

Step 6: Combine the two. Inject a modest slope and turn on Honest DiD. You will see the post-effect shift (bias) and the breakdown value collapse. The honest framework makes visible exactly what the pre-trend test hides: when there is a trend, the result is fragile.

Pre-Trend Tests and Their Fundamental Limitation

The natural temptation is to formalize Step 1 into a hypothesis test: jointly test H_0: \beta_k = 0 for all leads k < -1 with an F-test, and if you fail to reject, declare parallel trends “satisfied.” Almost every applied DiD paper does some version of this.

The logic is seductive but backwards. Recall from the Hypothesis Testing page that failing to reject a null is not evidence for the null — absence of evidence is not evidence of absence. A pre-trend test inherits this asymmetry in its most damaging form.

Pre-trend tests have low power, and the way they fail is dangerous.

  1. Low power. Pre-treatment coefficients are estimated on the same noisy data as everything else. A real but modest differential trend will frequently produce confidence bands that still cover zero. You “pass” the test even though parallel trends is violated.

  2. Pre-testing distorts the distribution. Conditioning your analysis on “the pre-trend test passed” selects for samples where, by chance, the leads happened to look flat. Roth (2022) shows this exacerbates bias in the surviving estimates and distorts their confidence-interval coverage — the studies that clear the pre-test are systematically the ones where the noise happened to mask the trend.

  3. A flat pre-trend does not imply a flat counterfactual. Even if the trends truly were parallel before treatment, nothing guarantees they would have remained parallel afterward. The pre-period is informative, not decisive.

This is why the field has moved from testing parallel trends to bounding its failure. We do not pretend the assumption holds exactly; we ask how robust the conclusion is to the assumption failing.

Honest DiD: Bounding the Violation

Rambachan and Roth (2023) propose a reframing that is now the gold standard for credible DiD. Instead of the knife-edge assumption “the post-treatment trend deviation is exactly zero,” posit that it lies in a set \Delta of plausible violations, and report a confidence interval that is valid for every violation in that set.

Let \delta_k denote the unobserved difference, at relative time k, between the treated group’s actual counterfactual trend and the parallel-trends benchmark. Standard DiD assumes \delta_k = 0 for all post-periods. The lead coefficients are noisy estimates of \delta_k for the pre-periods — and the key idea is to use the pre-period \delta_k’s to discipline the post-period ones.

The relative-magnitudes restriction \Delta^{RM}(\bar{M}).

The magnitude of any post-treatment violation is bounded by \bar{M} times the largest violation observed in the pre-treatment periods:

|\delta_k| \;\le\; \bar{M} \cdot \max_{s < -1} |\delta_s| \qquad \text{for all post-periods } k \ge 0

How to read this. “However the parallel-trends assumption fails after treatment, it cannot fail by more than \bar{M} times the worst it failed before treatment.” Setting \bar{M}=1 says: the post-treatment deviation is no larger than the biggest pre-treatment deviation. Setting \bar{M}=2 permits a violation twice as large. As \bar{M} \to 0 the restriction collapses to standard DiD (exact parallel trends).

A companion restriction, smoothness \Delta^{SD}(M), instead bounds how much the slope of the differential trend can change between consecutive periods by M — ruling out sharp breaks at exactly the moment of treatment. Both deliver the same deliverable: a robust confidence set.

Placebo and Falsification Tests

Honest DiD bounds the assumption; placebo tests attack it from a different angle, by checking whether your design produces “effects” where there should be none. A design that finds effects in places it shouldn’t is broken.

Three classic falsification tests.

  1. Fake treatment dates. Re-assign a placebo adoption year to the treated regions, before their real adoption (e.g., pretend the program started in 2014 instead of 2017), and re-run the analysis on pre-treatment data only. A non-zero “effect” reveals a pre-trend the event study may have missed.

  2. Untreated (placebo) outcomes. Run the exact same DiD on an outcome the program cannot plausibly affect — say, fall admissions among 15–40 year-olds, or dental-visit rates. A significant effect signals that something other than the program (a regional shock, differential reporting) is driving your result.

  3. Never-treated as fake-treated. Among the control regions, pretend some were treated and estimate a placebo effect. The distribution of these placebo estimates is a permutation benchmark: your real estimate should sit in its tail.

The unifying idea is the breakdown of identification: each placebo turns an untestable assumption into a testable implication. A placebo that should be zero but isn’t is direct evidence that your comparison is contaminated — and unlike the pre-trend test, a failed placebo is strong evidence (rejecting the null here genuinely indicts the design).

Specification Sensitivity

The last layer of diagnostics asks whether the headline number is an artifact of arbitrary modeling choices. A credible result is robust; a fragile one moves around as you make defensible alternative decisions.

  • Levels vs. logs. Modeling fall admissions in levels assumes a constant additive effect (measured in percentage points — an absolute change in the rate); logs assume a proportional effect (a percent change relative to baseline — a very different quantity from percentage points). These are different parallel-trends assumptions — trends can be parallel in levels but not in logs, or vice versa (Roth & Sant’Anna 2023). If the sign or significance flips between them, say so, and justify which scale the assumption is more credible on.

  • Control-group choice. Never-treated regions, not-yet-treated regions, or a matched subset? Each implies a different counterfactual. Report the estimate under each; convergent answers build credibility.

  • Covariate sets. Adjusting for time-varying region characteristics (age structure, läkare per capita, baseline morbidity) can relax parallel trends to conditional parallel trends — but adding “bad controls” affected by treatment reintroduces bias. Show the estimate with and without the adjustment set.

  • Clustering and inference. With few treated clusters, conventional cluster-robust standard errors understate uncertainty. Wild-cluster bootstrap or randomization inference is the honest default.

A specification curve — plotting the estimate across every reasonable combination of these choices — is the most transparent way to report sensitivity. If the bulk of specifications cluster on one side of zero, your result is robust. If they straddle zero, the headline number was a choice, not a finding.

Whichever scale you assume parallel trends on is a substantive choice, not a neutral one.

Math Details: How the Robust Confidence Set Is Formed

From the assumption to the interval

Write the vector of event-study coefficients as \hat{\beta} = (\hat{\beta}_{\text{pre}}, \hat{\beta}_{\text{post}}) with \hat{\beta} \sim \mathcal{N}(\beta, \Sigma), where \Sigma is the estimated variance-covariance matrix from the regression. The post-treatment coefficients decompose as

\beta_{\text{post}} = \underbrace{\tau}_{\text{causal effect}} + \underbrace{\delta_{\text{post}}}_{\text{trend violation}},

and the pre-treatment coefficients are pure violation, \beta_{\text{pre}} = \delta_{\text{pre}}. Read this as: “what we estimate after treatment is the true effect plus whatever parallel-trends violation persists; what we estimate before treatment is the violation alone, with no causal effect mixed in.” Standard DiD sets \delta_{\text{post}} = 0 and reads \tau straight off \hat{\beta}_{\text{post}}. Honest DiD refuses to do that.

The identified set

Fix the restriction \delta \in \Delta(\bar{M}). For the relative-magnitudes restriction with a single pooled post-effect, \Delta^{RM}(\bar{M}) says |\delta_{\text{post}}| \le \bar{M}\,\max_{s}|\delta_{s,\text{pre}}|. Because \tau = \beta_{\text{post}} - \delta_{\text{post}}, the set of effect values consistent with a given \delta_{\text{pre}} is

\mathcal{I}(\bar{M}) = \Big[\, \beta_{\text{post}} - \bar{M}\,\textstyle\max_s|\delta_{s,\text{pre}}|,\;\; \beta_{\text{post}} + \bar{M}\,\max_s|\delta_{s,\text{pre}}| \,\Big].

Read this as: “the true effect could be anywhere within \pm \bar{M} times the worst pre-trend of the point estimate.” This is the identified set — the residual ambiguity that remains even with infinite data, because the assumption alone does not pin \tau down.

Adding sampling uncertainty

We do not observe \beta; we observe \hat\beta with sampling error. The robust confidence set inflates the identified set to account for it. In the simple pooled case this is approximately

\text{CI}_{\text{robust}} = \Big[\, \hat{\beta}_{\text{post}} - \bar{M}\widehat{\Delta}_{\max} - z_{0.975}\,\widehat{\text{se}},\;\; \hat{\beta}_{\text{post}} + \bar{M}\widehat{\Delta}_{\max} + z_{0.975}\,\widehat{\text{se}} \,\Big],

where \widehat{\Delta}_{\max} = \max_s|\hat\beta_{s,\text{pre}}| is the largest observed lead and \widehat{\text{se}} is the standard error of the pooled post-effect. (Rambachan and Roth construct this rigorously via moment inequalities — confidence sets built from “\le”/“\ge” restrictions on parameters rather than the usual equalities — and the fixed-length confidence intervals of Armstrong–Kolesár; the additive form here is the teaching-grade approximation the interactive uses.)

Solving for the breakdown value

The robust CI excludes zero as long as its bound nearest zero stays on the same side. For a negative effect (\hat\beta_{\text{post}} < 0) significance is lost when the upper bound reaches zero:

\hat{\beta}_{\text{post}} + \bar{M}^\ast \widehat{\Delta}_{\max} + z_{0.975}\,\widehat{\text{se}} = 0 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; \bar{M}^\ast = \frac{|\hat{\beta}_{\text{post}}| - z_{0.975}\,\widehat{\text{se}}}{\widehat{\Delta}_{\max}}.

Read this as: “the breakdown value is how much of the point estimate survives after subtracting sampling uncertainty, expressed in units of the worst observed pre-trend.” If |\hat\beta_{\text{post}}| < z_{0.975}\widehat{\text{se}} the estimate was not even significant under exact parallel trends, so \bar{M}^\ast = 0. If \widehat{\Delta}_{\max} \to 0 (perfectly flat pre-trends) the breakdown value diverges — the result tolerates arbitrarily large violations because the data give no reason to expect any.

import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import pyfixest as pf

rng = np.random.default_rng(2017)

# --- Simulate a regional panel: 21 regioner x 9 years ---
n_treated, n_control = 12, 9
years = np.arange(2013, 2022)
adopt = 2017
n_reg = n_treated + n_control

regions = pd.DataFrame({
    "region":  np.arange(n_reg),
    "treated": np.r_[np.ones(n_treated), np.zeros(n_control)].astype(int),
    "alpha":   rng.normal(20, 3, n_reg),
})

panel = regions.merge(pd.DataFrame({"year": years}), how="cross")
panel["rel_time"] = np.where(panel.treated == 1, panel.year - adopt, np.nan)
panel["post"]     = ((panel.treated == 1) & (panel.year >= adopt)).astype(int)
panel["effect"]   = np.where(panel.post == 1, -1.5, 0.0)   # true effect
panel["y"] = (panel.alpha + 0.3 * (panel.year - 2013)
              + panel.effect + rng.normal(0, 1.2, len(panel)))

# Event study via pyfixest's i() operator (mirrors R's fixest, reference k = -1).
# Never-treated regions have rel_time = NaN and are absorbed as pure controls.
es = pf.feols("y ~ i(rel_time, ref=-1) | region + year",
              data=panel, vcov={"CRV1": "region"})
print(es.summary())

# --- Honest-DiD style robust CI (relative-magnitudes), teaching version ---
# i() names the coefficients "rel_time::<k>"; parse the integer k from each.
coefs = es.coef()
ses   = es.se()
k_of  = lambda name: int(name.split("::")[1])
es_k  = coefs.index[coefs.index.str.startswith("rel_time::")]
pre_k  = [name for name in es_k if k_of(name) < -1]   # leads  (k <= -2)
post_k = [name for name in es_k if k_of(name) >= 0]   # lags   (k >=  0)

pre, post = coefs[pre_k], coefs[post_k]

beta_post = post.mean()
se_post   = np.sqrt((ses[post_k] ** 2).sum()) / len(post_k)
max_pre   = pre.abs().max()

def robust_ci(Mbar):
    allow = Mbar * max_pre
    return (beta_post - allow - 1.96 * se_post,
            beta_post + allow + 1.96 * se_post)

for Mbar in [0, 0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0]:
    lo, hi = robust_ci(Mbar)
    print(f"Mbar={Mbar:>4}: robust 95% CI = [{lo:6.2f}, {hi:6.2f}]")

# Breakdown value M* : largest Mbar whose robust CI excludes zero
Mstar = max(0.0, (abs(beta_post) - 1.96 * se_post) / max_pre)
print(f"\nBreakdown value M* = {Mstar:.2f}")
print("Significant for any parallel-trends violation up to "
      f"{Mstar:.2f}x the largest observed pre-trend.")

Exercises

Exercise 1

An event study of the fall-prevention program reports a pooled post-treatment effect of \hat\beta_{\text{post}} = -1.80 with standard error \widehat{\text{se}} = 0.50. The largest absolute lead coefficient (the worst observed pre-trend) is \widehat{\Delta}_{\max} = 0.40.

  1. Is the effect significant at the 5% level under exact parallel trends?
  2. Using the teaching-grade approximation, compute the robust 95% confidence interval at \bar{M} = 1.
  3. Compute the breakdown value \bar{M}^\ast and state its interpretation in one sentence.
Show answer

(a) The naïve 95% CI is -1.80 \pm 1.96 \times 0.50 = [-2.78, -0.82]. It excludes zero, so the effect is significant under exact parallel trends.

(b) At \bar{M}=1 the allowance is \bar{M}\,\widehat{\Delta}_{\max} = 1 \times 0.40 = 0.40. The robust interval is

[-1.80 - 0.40 - 0.98,\; -1.80 + 0.40 + 0.98] = [-3.18,\; -0.42].

It still excludes zero, so the result survives a violation as large as the worst observed pre-trend.

(c) Using \bar{M}^\ast = (|\hat\beta_{\text{post}}| - 1.96\,\widehat{\text{se}})/\widehat{\Delta}_{\max}:

\bar{M}^\ast = \frac{1.80 - 1.96 \times 0.50}{0.40} = \frac{1.80 - 0.98}{0.40} = \frac{0.82}{0.40} = 2.05.

Interpretation: the conclusion that the program reduced fall admissions remains significant at the 5% level for any post-treatment parallel-trends violation up to roughly 2.05 times the largest pre-trend deviation observed in the data — a comfortably robust finding.

Exercise 2

A colleague runs a pre-trend F-test on her four lead coefficients, fails to reject (p = 0.62), and writes: “The parallel-trends assumption is satisfied (F-test p = 0.62).” Using the interactive above, set the pre-trend slope to +0.06 and observe the pre-trend card. Then critique your colleague’s sentence and propose a better one.

Show answer

With a slope of +0.06 the leads are visibly tilted — a genuine violation exists — yet the confidence rules still cross zero and the test passes. This is exactly the low-power trap.

The critique. The sentence commits the classic error of treating “failure to reject H_0” as “H_0 is true.” A non-significant pre-trend test is consistent with (i) truly parallel trends, or (ii) a real violation that the test is underpowered to detect. Roth (2022) shows that conditioning the analysis on having passed such a test actually worsens bias and distorts coverage in the surviving estimates, because passing selects for samples where noise happened to flatten the leads. A small undetectable pre-trend can imply a large post-treatment bias.

A better sentence. “The lead coefficients are individually and jointly insignificant, but the pre-trend test is underpowered. We therefore report Honest-DiD robust confidence sets: the effect remains significant for any violation up to \bar{M}^\ast = [\text{value}] times the largest observed pre-trend, and we report placebo tests on a non-target outcome.” This replaces an unjustified claim of truth with a quantified statement of robustness.

Exercise 3

Design three falsification tests for the fall-prevention DiD. For each, state (i) exactly what you would do, (ii) what result would support the design, and (iii) what result would indict it.

Show answer

Test 1 — Fake treatment date. (i) Re-code adoption to 2015 (two years early) and run the event study on pre-2017 data only. (ii) Supports: the fake-post coefficient is small and insignificant — no divergence before the real program. (iii) Indicts: a significant fake effect reveals a pre-existing differential trend the main event study may have masked.

Test 2 — Placebo (untreated) outcome. (i) Run the identical DiD on fall admissions among 15–40 year-olds, whom an elderly-targeted program cannot plausibly affect. (ii) Supports: a null effect on the placebo outcome. (iii) Indicts: a significant effect on the placebo outcome implies a region-time shock (e.g., differential coding, a hospital reorganization) is driving the result, not the program.

Test 3 — Never-treated as fake-treated (permutation). (i) Among the 9 control regions, repeatedly assign a placebo adoption year and estimate the placebo effect, building a distribution of “effects under the null.” (ii) Supports: the real estimate sits in the extreme tail of the placebo distribution (a randomization-inference p-value below 0.05). (iii) Indicts: the real estimate is unremarkable relative to placebos — the design generates “effects” of comparable size by chance.

Exercise 4

Two analysts study the same program. Analyst A models fall admissions in levels and reports \hat\beta_{\text{post}} = -1.5 (p = 0.01). Analyst B models the log rate and reports a -2\% effect with p = 0.20. Both ran clean event studies with flat-looking pre-trends. How can the same data give a significant effect in levels but not in logs, and what should they report?

Show answer

Why they differ. Levels and logs encode different parallel-trends assumptions. The level specification assumes the treated and control regions would have moved together in absolute admissions per 1,000; the log specification assumes they would have moved together in proportional terms. Trends can be parallel on one scale and divergent on the other — as Roth and Sant’Anna (2023) show, parallel trends is not scale-invariant except under strong conditions (e.g., when treatment is as-good-as-randomly assigned). Regions with high baseline admission rates contribute differently to a level regression than to a log regression, so the weighting and the implied counterfactual change with the functional form. A real additive effect concentrated in high-baseline regions can be statistically clear in levels yet diluted into an insignificant proportional effect in logs.

What to report. Neither analyst should cherry-pick. They should (i) report both specifications transparently, (ii) state which scale the parallel-trends assumption is more credible on for this outcome and justify it on substantive grounds (is the program’s mechanism additive — a fixed number of falls averted — or proportional — a percentage reduction?), and (iii) run Honest-DiD on each to compare breakdown values. If the conclusion hinges entirely on the functional form, that fragility is itself the finding and must be disclosed — the headline number was a modeling choice, not a robust effect.

Key takeaways

  • The event study is the central DiD diagnostic, but the reference period is a modeling choice — always state which period is omitted and check conclusions are not an artifact of re-baselining.
  • A passing pre-trend test is weak evidence: failing to reject is not evidence for the null, the test is underpowered against the most biasing trends, and pre-testing itself distorts coverage.
  • Honest DiD replaces “assume parallel trends” with “bound its failure”: the breakdown value \bar{M}^\ast states how large a violation (in units of the worst observed pre-trend) the conclusion can absorb before significance dies.
  • A failed placebo test (fake dates, untreated outcomes, never-treated permutations) is strong evidence against the design — unlike a passed pre-trend test.
  • Robust findings survive defensible specification changes; a specification curve that straddles zero means the headline number was a choice, not a finding.
