Hypothesis Testing

Null hypotheses, p-values, and the logic of statistical tests

In this section, you will learn to

  • Frame a null and alternative hypothesis and explain the proof-by-contradiction logic of a statistical test
  • Compute a t-statistic and translate it into a p-value, and interpret that p-value as P(\text{data} \mid H_0) rather than P(H_0 \mid \text{data})
  • Distinguish Type I from Type II error, and explain how \alpha, effect size, and sample size jointly determine power
  • Diagnose common p-value misinterpretations, including why “p = 0.05” is not a 5% false-discovery rate once the base rate of real effects is low
  • Distinguish statistical from practical significance and recognize the multiple-testing problem in large Swedish registry studies

A New Blood Pressure Drug on Trial

In 2019, a Swedish region launched a randomized trial across 14 vårdcentraler (primary care centers) in Västra Götaland. The question was whether a new antihypertensive drug reduced systolic blood pressure more effectively than the existing standard treatment. Two hundred patients with diagnosed hypertension were randomly assigned: 100 received the new drug, 100 received the standard. After six months, the researchers measured each patient’s systolic blood pressure and computed the difference in group means.

The new-drug group had an average systolic blood pressure 4.2 mmHg lower than the standard-treatment group. Is that a real effect, or could random chance explain the difference? After all, even if the two drugs were identical, the 100 patients who happened to be assigned to the new drug might have had slightly lower blood pressure simply because of who ended up in which group.

This is the question that hypothesis testing was built to answer. It does not tell us whether the drug works — that is a clinical judgment that requires weighing effect sizes, side effects, and costs. What it tells us is whether the observed data are surprising enough to cast doubt on the assumption that nothing happened at all.

The Logic: Proof by Contradiction

Hypothesis testing follows the logic of reductio ad absurdum — proof by contradiction. The procedure is:

  1. Assume that the treatment has no effect (the “null hypothesis”).
  2. Compute how likely it would be to see data as extreme as what we observed, given that assumption.
  3. If the data are very unlikely under the assumption, conclude that the assumption is probably wrong.

This is the same logic you use in everyday reasoning. If a friend claims they flipped a fair coin and got heads 20 times in a row, you do not conclude “what an unlikely event!” You conclude “that coin is probably not fair.” The hypothesis test formalizes this intuition.

The spine of every test: assume H_0, ask how surprising the data are under it (the p-value), and reject only when that surprise clears the threshold \alpha. “Fail to reject” is not “accept” — it simply means the data did not contradict H_0 strongly enough.

The Null and Alternative Hypotheses

The null hypothesis H_0 is the default assumption of no effect. In a clinical trial comparing a new drug to standard care:

H_0: \mu_{\text{new}} - \mu_{\text{standard}} = 0

How to read this: “H-naught states that the population mean blood pressure under the new drug equals the population mean under the standard drug — that is, the true treatment effect is zero.”

The alternative hypothesis H_1 (or H_a) is the claim we are testing for. In a two-sided test:

H_1: \mu_{\text{new}} - \mu_{\text{standard}} \neq 0

How to read this: “H-one states that the treatment effect is not zero — the new drug changes blood pressure in some direction.”

In a one-sided test, we might instead write H_1: \mu_{\text{new}} - \mu_{\text{standard}} < 0 (the new drug lowers blood pressure), reflecting a directional prediction. We return to one-sided vs. two-sided tests below.

A critical point: the null hypothesis is never proven true. We either reject it (the data are inconsistent with it) or fail to reject it (the data are not inconsistent enough). Failing to reject is not the same as confirming — it may simply mean we did not have enough data.

The Test Statistic

To evaluate how surprising the data are under H_0, we need a single number that summarizes the evidence. This is the test statistic.

For comparing two group means, the standard choice is the t-statistic:

t = \frac{\bar{Y}_1 - \bar{Y}_0}{\text{SE}(\bar{Y}_1 - \bar{Y}_0)}

How to read the t-statistic

t = \frac{\text{estimated effect} - \text{null value}}{\text{standard error of the estimate}}

The numerator is what we observed minus what we expected under H_0 (which is zero). The denominator is the standard error — a measure of how much the estimate would vary across repeated experiments due to random sampling alone.

In words: the t-statistic counts the number of standard errors between our estimate and the null value. A t of 2.0 means the estimate is 2 standard errors away from zero. A t of 0.5 means the estimate is well within the range of normal sampling variation under H_0.

For two independent groups with sample sizes n_1 and n_0 and sample standard deviations s_1 and s_0:

\text{SE} = \sqrt{\frac{s_1^2}{n_1} + \frac{s_0^2}{n_0}}

How to read this: “The standard error equals the square root of the sum of each group’s variance divided by its sample size.” Larger samples and less variable outcomes both produce smaller standard errors, making it easier to detect real effects.

The P-Value

The t-statistic tells us how far our estimate is from the null. The p-value translates that distance into a probability.

The p-value is the probability of observing a test statistic at least as extreme as the one we calculated, assuming that H_0 is true.

p = P(|T| \geq |t_{\text{obs}}| \mid H_0 \text{ is true})

How to read this: “The p-value equals the probability that the absolute value of T is greater than or equal to the absolute value of our observed t, given that the null hypothesis is true.”

  • A small p-value (e.g., p = 0.002) means: “If the null were true, it would be very unlikely to see data this extreme. The data are hard to reconcile with no effect.”
  • A large p-value (e.g., p = 0.43) means: “If the null were true, data like ours would be quite common. We have no strong reason to doubt the null.”

The Decision Rule

The conventional decision rule is:

  • If p < \alpha, reject H_0.
  • If p \geq \alpha, fail to reject H_0.

The threshold \alpha is called the significance level and is conventionally set to 0.05. This means we are willing to accept a 5% chance of incorrectly rejecting a true null hypothesis.

The 0.05 threshold is a convention, not a law of nature. There is nothing sacred about p = 0.05. A result with p = 0.049 is not fundamentally different from one with p = 0.051. Treating the threshold as a bright line — publishing the first and filing the second in a drawer — has contributed to widespread distortions in the scientific literature.

Ronald Fisher, who popularized the p-value, never intended it as a rigid decision boundary. He wrote of it as a measure of evidence, to be interpreted in context alongside effect sizes, prior knowledge, and study design. The mechanical “p < 0.05 therefore significant” ritual is a later invention that Fisher himself would likely have rejected.

In Swedish medical research, Socialstyrelsen (the National Board of Health and Welfare) and SBU (the Swedish Agency for Health Technology Assessment) increasingly emphasize confidence intervals and effect sizes alongside p-values when evaluating clinical evidence.

Two Types of Errors

Every decision made under uncertainty carries the risk of being wrong. There are exactly two ways a hypothesis test can err:

Type I Error (False Positive, \alpha)

Rejecting H_0 when it is actually true. You conclude the drug works when it does not.

\alpha = P(\text{reject } H_0 \mid H_0 \text{ is true})

How to read this: “Alpha equals the probability of rejecting the null hypothesis, given that the null is true.” By setting \alpha = 0.05, we guarantee that if the drug truly has no effect, we will incorrectly declare it effective no more than 5% of the time.

Type II Error (False Negative, \beta)

Failing to reject H_0 when it is actually false. You conclude “insufficient evidence” when the drug really does work.

\beta = P(\text{fail to reject } H_0 \mid H_0 \text{ is false})

How to read this: “Beta equals the probability of failing to reject the null, given that the null is false — that is, given that the drug has a real effect.”

H_0 is true (no effect) H_0 is false (real effect)
Reject H_0 Type I error (\alpha) Correct decision
Fail to reject H_0 Correct decision Type II error (\beta)

There is an inherent trade-off: making \alpha smaller (fewer false positives) makes \beta larger (more false negatives), holding everything else constant. Reducing both simultaneously requires collecting more data.

Statistical Power

Power is the probability of correctly rejecting H_0 when it is false:

\text{Power} = 1 - \beta = P(\text{reject } H_0 \mid H_0 \text{ is false})

How to read this: “Power equals one minus beta — the probability of detecting a real effect when one exists.”

Power depends on three quantities:

  1. The true effect size (\delta): larger effects are easier to detect.
  2. The sample size (n): more data reduces sampling variability.
  3. The significance level (\alpha): a more lenient threshold makes rejection easier.

A common target is power = 0.80, meaning you have an 80% chance of detecting the effect if it exists. Swedish ethics boards (Etikprövningsmyndigheten) typically require a power analysis before approving a clinical trial — running an underpowered study wastes patients’ time and resources by being unlikely to yield a definitive answer.

    q = Math.sqrt(-2 * Math.log(pp));
    return (((((c[0]*q+c[1])*q+c[2])*q+c[3])*q+c[4])*q+c[5]) /
           ((((d[0]*q+d[1])*q+d[2])*q+d[3])*q+1);
  } else if (pp <= phigh) {
    q = pp - 0.5; r = q * q;
    return (((((a[0]*r+a[1])*r+a[2])*r+a[3])*r+a[4])*r+a[5])*q /
           (((((b[0]*r+b[1])*r+b[2])*r+b[3])*r+b[4])*r+1);
  } else {
    q = Math.sqrt(-2 * Math.log(1 - pp));
    return -(((((c[0]*q+c[1])*q+c[2])*q+c[3])*q+c[4])*q+c[5]) /
            ((((d[0]*q+d[1])*q+d[2])*q+d[3])*q+1);
  }
}

// centered at 0; under H1 it is centered at the true effect delta. We use a
// two-sided z-test at level htx_alpha. Everything below is derived from these.
htx_model = {

  const crit = zCrit * se;                        // critical value (in mmHg)
  // Power for a two-sided test = P(reject | H1)
  const lambda = htx_delta / se;                  // noncentrality in z units
  const power = (1 - htx_Phi(zCrit - lambda)) + htx_Phi(-zCrit - lambda);
  const beta = 1 - power;
  return ({ sigma, se, zCrit, crit, lambda, power, beta, alpha: htx_alpha });
}

How to Read the Power Explorer

This is the single most important picture in hypothesis testing. Walk through it slowly:

Step 1: Find the two curves. The slate curve is the sampling distribution of the estimated effect if the null is true — it is centered at zero. The red curve is the sampling distribution if the true effect is \delta — it is centered at \delta. Both have the same width, set by the standard error \sigma\sqrt{2/n}.

Step 2: Find the decision boundary. The dashed vertical lines mark the critical values \pm (critical value). An estimate landing outside these lines makes us reject H_0; an estimate inside makes us fail to reject.

Step 3: Read the shaded areas. The slate tails beyond the critical lines are \alpha — the chance of rejecting when H_0 is actually true (a false positive). The red region is \beta — the part of the alternative curve that falls in the “fail to reject” zone, i.e. a real effect we miss. The green region is power — the part of the alternative curve that correctly lands in the rejection zone.

Step 4: Watch power rise. Increase the true effect \delta: the red curve slides right, more of it clears the critical line, and the green area (power) grows. Watch the separation \lambda = \delta/\text{SE} readout climb — this single number, the distance between the curves in standard-error units, is what drives power. Now reset \delta and increase n instead: both curves get narrower (the standard error shrinks), they separate more cleanly, \lambda rises, and again power climbs. This is exactly the relationship the formula n \approx 2\sigma^2(z_{\alpha/2}+z_\beta)^2/\delta^2 encodes (where z_\beta is the critical value for the desired power).

Step 5: Feel the trade-off. Switch \alpha from 0.05 to 0.01. The critical lines move outward, the slate tails shrink (fewer false positives) — but the green region also shrinks (lower power). Tightening \alpha to avoid Type I errors costs you Type II errors, unless you buy back power with a larger n.

function mulberry32(a) {
  return function() {
    a |= 0; a = a + 0x6D2B79F5 | 0;
    var t = Math.imul(a ^ a >>> 15, 1 | a);
    t = t + Math.imul(t ^ t >>> 7, 61 | t) ^ t;
    return ((t ^ t >>> 14) >>> 0) / 4294967296;
  }
}

function randn(rng) {
  let u1 = rng();
  let u2 = rng();
  return Math.sqrt(-2 * Math.log(u1)) * Math.cos(2 * Math.PI * u2);
}

// t-distribution CDF approximation (two-tailed p-value)
function tTestPValue(tStat, df) {
  // Use the regularized incomplete beta function approximation
  const x = df / (df + tStat * tStat);
  const a = df / 2;
  const b = 0.5;
  // Approximate via normal for large df
  if (df > 100) {
    const z = Math.abs(tStat);
    const p = 2 * (1 - normalCDF(z));
    return Math.max(p, 1e-15);
  }
  // For smaller df, use a series approximation
  const p = incompleteBeta(x, a, b);
  return Math.max(p, 1e-15);
}

function normalCDF(z) {
  const a1 = 0.254829592, a2 = -0.284496736, a3 = 1.421413741;
  const a4 = -1.453152027, a5 = 1.061405429, p = 0.3275911;
  const sign = z < 0 ? -1 : 1;
  z = Math.abs(z) / Math.sqrt(2);
  const t = 1.0 / (1.0 + p * z);
  const y = 1 - (((((a5 * t + a4) * t) + a3) * t + a2) * t + a1) * t * Math.exp(-z * z);
  return 0.5 * (1.0 + sign * y);
}

function incompleteBeta(x, a, b) {
  // Continued fraction for regularized incomplete beta
  if (x === 0 || x === 1) return x;
  const lnBeta = lgamma(a) + lgamma(b) - lgamma(a + b);
  const front = Math.exp(Math.log(x) * a + Math.log(1 - x) * b - lnBeta) / a;
  // Lentz's algorithm
  let f = 1, c = 1, d = 0;
  for (let i = 0; i <= 200; i++) {
    let m = Math.floor(i / 2);
    let numerator;
    if (i === 0) {
      numerator = 1;
    } else if (i % 2 === 0) {
      numerator = (m * (b - m) * x) / ((a + 2 * m - 1) * (a + 2 * m));
    } else {
      numerator = -((a + m) * (a + b + m) * x) / ((a + 2 * m) * (a + 2 * m + 1));
    }
    d = 1 + numerator * d;
    if (Math.abs(d) < 1e-30) d = 1e-30;
    d = 1 / d;
    c = 1 + numerator / c;
    if (Math.abs(c) < 1e-30) c = 1e-30;
    f *= c * d;
    if (Math.abs(c * d - 1) < 1e-10) break;
  }
  return front * (f - 1);
}

function lgamma(x) {
  const cof = [76.18009172947146, -86.50532032941677, 24.01409824083091,
    -1.231739572450155, 0.1208650973866179e-2, -0.5395239384953e-5];
  let y = x, tmp = x + 5.5;
  tmp -= (x + 0.5) * Math.log(tmp);
  let ser = 1.000000000190015;
  for (let j = 0; j < 6; j++) ser += cof[j] / ++y;
  return -tmp + Math.log(2.5066282746310005 * ser / x);
}

// Simulate a single trial
function simulateTrial(delta, n, seed) {
  const rng = mulberry32(seed);
  let sumT = 0, sumC = 0, sumT2 = 0, sumC2 = 0;
  for (let i = 0; i < n; i++) {
    const yc = randn(rng) * 10;  // control: N(0, 10)
    const yt = randn(rng) * 10 + delta;  // treatment: N(delta, 10)
    sumC += yc; sumC2 += yc * yc;
    sumT += yt; sumT2 += yt * yt;
  }
  const meanT = sumT / n, meanC = sumC / n;
  const varT = (sumT2 - n * meanT * meanT) / (n - 1);
  const varC = (sumC2 - n * meanC * meanC) / (n - 1);
  const se = Math.sqrt(varT / n + varC / n);
  const tStat = (meanT - meanC) / se;
  const df = n + n - 2;
  const pVal = tTestPValue(tStat, df);
  return { meanT, meanC, diff: meanT - meanC, se, tStat, pVal, reject: pVal < 0.05 };
}

What P-Values Are NOT

The p-value is one of the most misunderstood quantities in all of science. Here are the most common misinterpretations.

Common misinterpretations of the p-value:

  1. “The p-value is the probability that the null hypothesis is true.” No. The p-value is P(\text{data} \mid H_0), not P(H_0 \mid \text{data}). These are very different things. To get the latter, you would need Bayes’ theorem and a prior probability for H_0 — something the frequentist framework does not provide.

  2. “The p-value is the probability the result is due to chance.” No. The p-value is the probability of seeing data this extreme if the null were true. It does not tell you the probability that chance caused the result.

  3. “A small p-value means the effect is large.” No. With a large enough sample, even a tiny, clinically irrelevant effect will produce a very small p-value. The p-value measures statistical significance (is the evidence against H_0 strong?), not practical significance (is the effect big enough to matter?).

  4. “A large p-value means there is no effect.” No. A large p-value means the data are consistent with H_0, but they may also be consistent with a range of nonzero effects. The study may simply have been underpowered. Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence.

  5. “p = 0.05 means there is a 5% chance the result is a false positive.” No. The false positive rate is 5% across many experiments where the null is true. For any single experiment, the probability the result is a false positive depends on the prior probability that H_0 is true, which is unknown.

The fifth misconception is the most consequential, and the most surprising. The explorer below makes it tangible: it imagines 1000 hypotheses being tested at once, sorts them into true and false positives and negatives, and computes the false discovery proportion — of all the “significant” findings, what fraction are actually false alarms. Watch what happens to that number when real effects are rare.

How to Read the False-Discovery Explorer

Step 1: One thousand hypotheses. Imagine a research field running 1000 tests — 1000 candidate drugs, biomarkers, or quality interventions. Each cell in the grid is one hypothesis. Step 2: Sort the outcomes. Green cells are real effects we correctly detect (true positives); faded green are real effects we miss (false negatives, governed by 1- power). Red cells are nulls we wrongly flag as significant (false positives, governed by \alpha); faded slate are nulls we correctly leave alone (true negatives).

Step 3: Read the headline. The false discovery proportion is the red count divided by all significant results (red + bright green). It answers the question a reader of a published “significant” study actually cares about: given that this result cleared p < \alpha, how likely is it to be a false alarm? Its complement is the positive predictive value (PPV) — the chance the finding is real.

Step 4: Make rare effects rare. Set power to 0.80 and \alpha to 0.05 — a perfectly conventional, “well-designed” study. Now slide the base rate down to 0.10. The false discovery proportion jumps to roughly 36%: more than a third of significant findings are false, even though every individual test has only a 5% false-positive rate. Push the base rate to 0.02 (effects are genuinely rare, as in exploratory screening) and most significant results become false.

Step 5: This is misconception #5 made real. This is precisely why “p = 0.05 means a 5% chance of a false positive” is wrong. The 5% is \alpha — a property of the test under the null. The fraction of your significant findings that are false (the false discovery proportion) depends on the base rate, which no single p-value can tell you. Underpowered studies in low-base-rate fields are a recipe for a literature full of false positives — a key driver of the replication crisis, and a caution for the broad Swedish kvalitetsregister studies that screen many outcomes at once.

One-Sided vs. Two-Sided Tests

A two-sided (or two-tailed) test checks whether the effect differs from zero in either direction:

H_0: \delta = 0 \quad \text{vs.} \quad H_1: \delta \neq 0

The p-value is computed in both tails of the distribution: p = P(|T| \geq |t_{\text{obs}}|).

A one-sided (or one-tailed) test checks for an effect in a specific direction:

H_0: \delta \geq 0 \quad \text{vs.} \quad H_1: \delta < 0

Here the p-value uses only one tail: p = P(T \leq t_{\text{obs}}).

When to use each: Two-sided tests are the default in most research because they guard against effects in unexpected directions. A one-sided test is appropriate only when there is a strong theoretical reason to predict the direction before seeing the data — for example, a pharmacological mechanism that can only lower blood pressure, never raise it. Using a one-sided test after observing the direction of the effect is a form of cheating that doubles the false positive rate.

In Swedish clinical research submitted to Läkemedelsverket (the Medical Products Agency), two-sided tests at \alpha = 0.05 are the regulatory default for efficacy endpoints.

Statistical Significance vs. Practical Significance

Statistical significance is not the same as practical significance. A study using data from Sweden’s Patientregistret (the National Patient Register), which covers the entire population, might find that a treatment lowers systolic blood pressure by 0.3 mmHg with p < 0.001. The effect is statistically significant — the data are very unlikely under H_0 — but clinically meaningless. A 0.3 mmHg reduction would not change any patient’s health outcome.

Conversely, a small pilot study at a single vårdcentral might find a 12 mmHg reduction with p = 0.09. This effect is clinically important but statistically non-significant because the sample was too small to rule out chance. The correct response is not “the drug does not work” but “we need a larger trial.”

Always report and interpret effect sizes alongside p-values. Ask: “Is this effect large enough to matter in clinical practice?” before asking “Is this effect statistically significant?”

This distinction is especially important in Swedish registry research. Because kvalitetsregister like the Swedish Hip Arthroplasty Register (Svenska Höftprotesregistret) or the National Diabetes Register (Nationella Diabetesregistret) cover hundreds of thousands of patients, virtually any nonzero effect — no matter how trivial — will be statistically significant. The p-value becomes uninformative when n is very large. Effect sizes and confidence intervals carry the interpretive weight.

The Multiple Testing Problem

When you test many hypotheses simultaneously, the probability of at least one false positive increases rapidly.

Recall from the Randomization page: a researcher randomizes 100 patients and tests 20 baseline covariates for balance at \alpha = 0.05. Under a correct randomization, the probability of at least one spurious “significant” imbalance is:

P(\text{at least one false positive}) = 1 - (1 - \alpha)^k = 1 - 0.95^{20} \approx 0.64

How to read this: “The probability of at least one false positive equals one minus the probability that all k tests correctly fail to reject, which is (1 - \alpha)^k.”

With 20 tests, you have a 64% chance of finding at least one “significant” result even when nothing is going on. This is the multiple testing problem (also called the multiple comparisons problem).

The curve below shows that 64% as one point on a fast-rising line: the family-wise error rate 1-(1-\alpha)^k climbs toward certainty as the number of tests k grows. The flat green line shows what a Bonferroni correction buys — by shrinking each test’s threshold to \alpha/k, it holds the family-wise error back near \alpha no matter how many tests you run.

Each extra test is another chance for a false alarm, so the uncorrected family-wise error (red) races toward 1; at k = 20 it is the 64% quoted above. Bonferroni (green) tightens each test to \alpha/k, pinning the overall error rate near \alpha — at the cost of power on each individual test.

Common corrections include:

  • Bonferroni correction: Divide \alpha by the number of tests. With 20 tests, each uses \alpha = 0.05/20 = 0.0025. This is conservative — it controls the family-wise error rate (the probability of any false positive) but can be overly strict.
  • Benjamini-Hochberg procedure: Controls the false discovery rate (the expected proportion of false positives among all rejected hypotheses), which is often a more relevant quantity.

In Swedish registry studies that examine many outcomes or subgroups, reporting unadjusted p-values without acknowledging the multiple testing problem is a common methodological weakness.

Math Details: The Distribution of the P-Value Under the Null

Why P-Values Are Uniform Under H_0

A remarkable fact: if H_0 is true, the p-value follows a uniform distribution on [0, 1]. This is not an approximation — it is exact.

Proof sketch. Let T be a continuous test statistic with CDF F_0 under H_0. For a one-sided test, the p-value is p = 1 - F_0(T). Since F_0(T) is a probability integral transform, it follows that F_0(T) \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1) when T \sim F_0. Therefore p = 1 - F_0(T) \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1).

Implication for the significance level. If p \sim \text{Uniform}(0, 1), then:

P(p < \alpha) = \alpha

This is why setting \alpha = 0.05 guarantees a 5% false positive rate. The fraction of p-values falling below any threshold \alpha is exactly \alpha, by definition.

Power as a Function of Effect Size and Sample Size

Under H_1 with true effect \delta and known variance \sigma^2, the z-statistic follows:

Z = \frac{\bar{Y}_1 - \bar{Y}_0}{\sigma\sqrt{2/n}} \sim N\!\left(\frac{\delta}{\sigma\sqrt{2/n}}, 1\right) = N\!\left(\frac{\delta\sqrt{n}}{\sigma\sqrt{2}}, 1\right)

The noncentrality parameter \lambda = \frac{\delta\sqrt{n}}{\sigma\sqrt{2}} governs power. We reject H_0 when |Z| > z_{\alpha/2}, so:

\text{Power} = P(|Z| > z_{\alpha/2} \mid H_1) = 1 - \Phi\!\left(z_{\alpha/2} - \lambda\right) + \Phi\!\left(-z_{\alpha/2} - \lambda\right)

How to read this: Power increases with \lambda, which in turn increases with the effect size \delta and the sample size n, and decreases with the outcome variability \sigma.

For a standard power calculation targeting power = 1 - \beta at significance level \alpha for a two-sided test, the required sample size per group is approximately:

n \approx \frac{2\sigma^2(z_{\alpha/2} + z_\beta)^2}{\delta^2}

How to read this: “The required sample size per group is approximately two times the variance times the square of the sum of the critical values for the desired \alpha and \beta, divided by the squared effect size.”

For \alpha = 0.05 and power = 0.80: z_{0.025} \approx 1.96 and z_{0.20} \approx 0.84, giving (z_{\alpha/2} + z_\beta)^2 \approx (1.96 + 0.84)^2 = 7.84.

# Required sample size for 80% power to detect a 4 mmHg effect
from scipy.stats import norm

def required_n(delta, sigma, alpha=0.05, power=0.80):
    """Sample size per group for a two-sample t-test."""
    z_alpha = norm.ppf(1 - alpha / 2)
    z_beta = norm.ppf(power)
    n = 2 * (sigma ** 2) * (z_alpha + z_beta) ** 2 / delta ** 2
    return int(np.ceil(n))

n_needed = required_n(delta=4, sigma=15)

print(f"To detect a 4 mmHg difference (SD = 15):")
print(f"Required n per group: {n_needed}")

Exercises

Exercise 1

A clinical trial at a Swedish vårdcentral randomizes 60 hypertensive patients (30 per arm) to a new antihypertensive drug vs. placebo. After 12 weeks, the mean systolic blood pressure reduction is 5.8 mmHg in the drug group and 2.1 mmHg in the placebo group. The pooled standard deviation is 12 mmHg.

  1. State the null and alternative hypotheses.
  2. Compute the standard error of the difference in means.
  3. Compute the t-statistic.
  4. The critical value for a two-sided test at \alpha = 0.05 with 58 degrees of freedom is approximately 2.00. Would you reject H_0?
Show answer

(a) H_0: \mu_{\text{drug}} - \mu_{\text{placebo}} = 0 (no difference in blood pressure reduction). H_1: \mu_{\text{drug}} - \mu_{\text{placebo}} \neq 0 (some difference exists).

(b) With equal group sizes (n_1 = n_0 = 30) and pooled SD = 12:

\text{SE} = \sqrt{\frac{s^2}{n_1} + \frac{s^2}{n_0}} = \sqrt{\frac{12^2}{30} + \frac{12^2}{30}} = \sqrt{\frac{144}{30} + \frac{144}{30}} = \sqrt{9.6} \approx 3.10

(c) The observed difference is 5.8 - 2.1 = 3.7 mmHg.

t = \frac{3.7 - 0}{3.10} \approx 1.19

(d) Since |t| = 1.19 < 2.00, we fail to reject H_0. The data do not provide sufficient evidence at the 5% level to conclude that the drug reduces blood pressure more than placebo.

Interpretation: This does not mean the drug has no effect. The observed difference of 3.7 mmHg might be clinically meaningful, but with only 30 patients per arm and SD = 12, the study is underpowered to detect effects of this magnitude. A power calculation would show that detecting a 3.7 mmHg effect with 80% power requires approximately n \approx 2 \times 12^2 \times 7.84 / 3.7^2 \approx 165 patients per group — far more than the 30 enrolled.

Exercise 2

Using the interactive simulator above, set the true effect to 0 and n = 50. Run 1000 trials and record the rejection rate. Now repeat with n = 500. Does the false positive rate change? Explain why or why not.

Show answer

The rejection rate should be approximately 5% in both cases (within simulation variability, you will see something between 3% and 7%).

This is a fundamental property: when H_0 is true, the false positive rate equals \alpha regardless of sample size. Increasing n does not inflate the Type I error rate.

Why? Under H_0, the p-value is uniformly distributed on [0, 1], so P(p < 0.05) = 0.05 exactly, no matter how large n is. A larger sample makes the t-statistic more precise, but if the true effect is exactly zero, the t-statistic is centered at zero and the p-value is uniform. Sample size affects power (the ability to detect real effects) but not the false positive rate (which is controlled by \alpha).

This is precisely what the \alpha level guarantees: the error rate under the null is bounded at \alpha by construction.

Exercise 3

A researcher analyzes data from the Swedish National Diabetes Register (Nationella Diabetesregistret), which includes over 400,000 patients. She compares HbA1c levels between patients using two different insulin brands and finds a difference of 0.02 percentage points with p < 0.001. She concludes the brands differ in efficacy.

Critique this conclusion. What additional information would you want before interpreting this result?

Show answer

The researcher is confusing statistical significance with practical significance. With 400,000 patients, the standard error is extremely small, so even trivially small differences produce tiny p-values. A 0.02 percentage point difference in HbA1c is clinically irrelevant — it is far below the measurement error of the assay itself (typically around 0.2–0.3 percentage points), and orders of magnitude smaller than any threshold that would change clinical management.

Additional information needed:

  1. Effect size and confidence interval. The 95% CI might be something like [0.01, 0.03], which confirms the entire plausible range is clinically meaningless.

  2. Minimum clinically important difference (MCID). For HbA1c, a difference of at least 0.3–0.5 percentage points is typically considered clinically relevant. The observed 0.02 is nowhere near this threshold.

  3. Confounders. This is observational data — patients on different insulin brands may differ systematically in ways that affect HbA1c. Without causal identification (randomization or credible quasi-experimental design), even a larger effect could not be attributed to the insulin brand itself.

  4. Multiple comparisons. If the researcher tested many insulin brands or many outcomes, the p-value should be adjusted.

Key lesson: In large registry studies, the p-value is almost always small. The relevant question shifts from “is the effect nonzero?” (it almost certainly is, with enough data) to “is the effect large enough to matter?” This requires clinical judgment, not a significance threshold.

Exercise 4

A Swedish regional health authority (region) tests a quality improvement initiative across 15 vårdcentraler. For each center, they measure 8 different quality indicators (waiting times, patient satisfaction, readmission rates, etc.) before and after the initiative, and test whether each indicator improved. They find that 2 out of 8 indicators show p < 0.05.

  1. If the initiative had no effect on any indicator, how many significant results would you expect by chance?
  2. What is the probability of observing at least 2 significant results under the null?
  3. Apply the Bonferroni correction. Do either of the results survive?
Show answer

(a) Under the null (no effect on any indicator), the expected number of significant results at \alpha = 0.05 is:

E[\text{significant results}] = k \times \alpha = 8 \times 0.05 = 0.4

So we would expect about 0.4 false positives — less than one, but not zero.

(b) The number of significant results under the null follows a Binomial(8, 0.05) distribution:

P(X \geq 2) = 1 - P(X = 0) - P(X = 1)

P(X = 0) = 0.95^8 = 0.6634

P(X = 1) = \binom{8}{1} \times 0.05 \times 0.95^7 = 8 \times 0.05 \times 0.6983 = 0.2793

P(X \geq 2) = 1 - 0.6634 - 0.2793 = 0.0573

There is about a 5.7% chance of observing 2 or more significant results purely by chance — not overwhelmingly unlikely.

(c) The Bonferroni-corrected significance level is \alpha^* = 0.05 / 8 = 0.00625. Unless one or both of the significant p-values is below 0.00625, neither result survives the correction. If, for instance, the two p-values were 0.012 and 0.038, both would fail the corrected threshold.

Conclusion: Two significant results out of eight tests, without adjustment, provides only weak evidence that the initiative had any real effect. The region should be cautious about concluding success based on unadjusted p-values from multiple tests.

Key takeaways

  • A hypothesis test assumes the null, then asks how surprising the data would be under it; the p-value is that surprise, and a small p-value casts doubt on H_0 rather than proving H_1.
  • The 0.05 threshold is a convention, not a law of nature; p = 0.049 and p = 0.051 carry essentially the same evidence.
  • Lowering \alpha trades fewer false positives for more false negatives; only more data buys down both, raising power 1 - \beta.
  • “p < 0.05 means a 5% chance the finding is false” is wrong — the false discovery proportion depends on the base rate of real effects, which no single p-value reveals.
  • With huge registry samples almost any nonzero effect is statistically significant, so effect sizes, confidence intervals, and multiple-testing corrections must carry the interpretive weight.
