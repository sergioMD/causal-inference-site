Survival Analysis: Hazards and the Kaplan-Meier Curve

Modeling time-to-event with censoring

In this section, you will learn to

  • Recognize a time-to-event (duration) outcome and explain why right-censoring makes OLS and logit the wrong tools
  • Define and connect the three core functions — the survival function S(t), the hazard h(t), and the cumulative hazard H(t) — and read the identity S(t)=\exp(-H(t)) aloud
  • Construct the Kaplan-Meier product-limit estimator by hand from a risk set and an event count
  • Read a median survival time off a KM step curve and interpret two-group differences
  • Compare two survival curves with the log-rank test and fit a KM curve in R (survival) and Python (lifelines)

The Question Behind a Duration

A vårdcentral (primary care center) in Region Uppsala discharges heart-failure patients and wants to know one thing: how long until they come back? The outcome is not “did the patient get readmitted, yes or no” — it is time to readmission, measured in days. A new nurse-led follow-up program (sjuksköterskeledd uppföljning) is being piloted against standard care, and the verksamhetschef (clinic manager) needs to know whether the program keeps patients out of hospital longer.

Two features make this outcome different from anything we have modeled so far. First, the response is a duration T \ge 0 — strictly positive, right-skewed, never normally distributed. Second, and decisively, the study ends before every patient has the event. When the 18-month follow-up window closes, many patients still have not been readmitted. For them we do not know T. We know only that T exceeds the time we observed them. This is right-censoring, and it is the defining problem of survival analysis.

Throw away the censored patients and you bias the result toward the sickest, fastest-failing cases. Code a censored patient as “no event” and run a logit and you pretend a patient followed for 30 days is the same as one followed for 500. Run OLS on the observed times and the censored durations enter as if they were completed — systematically too short. None of the tools from earlier modules handle a half-observed duration correctly. We need machinery built for it.

A subject is right-censored at time c_i if the study ends, or the subject is lost to follow-up, before the event occurs. We then observe the pair

\big(\tilde{T}_i,\ \delta_i\big), \qquad \tilde{T}_i = \min(T_i,\,c_i), \qquad \delta_i = \mathbb{1}\{T_i \le c_i\}.

Read this as: “we observe the follow-up time \tilde T_i, which is the smaller of the true event time T_i and the censoring time c_i, together with an event indicator \delta_i that equals 1 if the event was observed and 0 if the subject was censored.” A censored row (\delta_i = 0) is not missing data — it carries real information: this patient survived at least \tilde T_i days.

Three Functions, One Distribution

Survival analysis describes the distribution of T through three equivalent lenses. Each answers a different clinical question, and they convert into one another by calculus.

The survival function

The survival function is the probability of not yet having had the event by time t:

S(t) = P(T > t) = 1 - F(t),

where F is the cumulative distribution function of T.

Read this as: “S of t is the probability that the event time T exceeds t.” It starts at S(0)=1 (everyone is event-free at the start) and decreases monotonically toward 0. For our patients, S(180) is the probability of still being readmission-free six months after discharge.

The hazard function

The hazard function is the instantaneous event rate at time t, given survival up to t:

h(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{P(t \le T < t + \Delta t \mid T \ge t)}{\Delta t} = \frac{f(t)}{S(t)},

where f(t) = -S'(t) is the density of T.

Read this as: “the hazard at t is the chance the event happens in the next instant, conditional on having made it to t, per unit of time.” It is a rate, not a probability — it can exceed 1. The conditioning on T \ge t is what makes the hazard the natural object for censored data: at each moment we ask only about the patients still at risk.

The cumulative hazard

The cumulative hazard accumulates the hazard over time:

H(t) = \int_0^t h(u)\,du.

Read this as: “H of t is the total hazard experienced from 0 up to t.” It is not a probability — it rises without bound. Its slope at any point is the hazard h(t), so a steepening cumulative-hazard curve signals a rising risk.

How they lock together

These three functions are one distribution wearing three hats. The link is a short, beautiful identity. Because h(u) = f(u)/S(u) = -S'(u)/S(u) = -\frac{d}{du}\log S(u), integrating from 0 to t gives

H(t) = \int_0^t h(u)\,du = -\log S(t) \qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad \boxed{\,S(t) = \exp\!\big(-H(t)\big)\,}.

Read the boxed identity as: “the survival probability equals e raised to the negative cumulative hazard.” Know any one of S, h, H, and you know the other two. The derivation in full is in the Math Details below.

The hazard is the modeling workhorse. We describe results with S(t) (patients understand “60% are still readmission-free at one year”), but we model with h(t). The reason is censoring: the hazard conditions on the risk set — the patients still under observation and event-free — so a censored subject simply leaves the risk set at \tilde T_i without contaminating the estimate. The whole next page, the Cox Proportional Hazards model, is a regression on the hazard for exactly this reason.

The Kaplan-Meier Estimator

We rarely want to assume a parametric shape for S(t). The Kaplan-Meier (KM) estimator — also called the product-limit estimator (Kaplan & Meier, 1958) — is a nonparametric estimate of S(t) that handles right-censoring exactly right.

The idea is to chain together conditional survival probabilities. Order the distinct event times t_1 < t_2 < \cdots < t_k. At each event time t_j, let n_j be the risk set (the number of subjects still under observation and event-free just before t_j) and d_j the number of events at t_j. The probability of surviving through t_j given survival up to it is \big(1 - d_j/n_j\big). Surviving past t means surviving every event time up to t, so we multiply:

The Kaplan-Meier product-limit estimator is

\hat{S}(t) = \prod_{j:\,t_j \le t} \left(1 - \frac{d_j}{n_j}\right),

where d_j is the number of events at distinct event time t_j and n_j is the size of the risk set just before t_j.

Read this as: “\hat S(t) is the product, over all event times t_j at or before t, of one minus the event-to-risk ratio at t_j.” Each factor knocks the curve down by the fraction of the current risk set that had the event — producing a step function that drops only at observed event times and stays flat in between.

The genius is in how censoring enters. A censored subject is not an event, so it never creates a drop. But it does shrink the risk set n_j for every later event time — the subject silently leaves the pool at \tilde T_i. That is exactly the right bookkeeping: we never claim the censored patient had the event, but we stop counting them as “at risk” once we lose sight of them.

Kaplan-Meier requires non-informative (independent) censoring. Why a censored patient leaves must be unrelated to their hazard. If sicker patients drop out of follow-up faster — so censoring predicts the event — the risk set is no longer representative of the survivors, and \hat S(t) is biased. Administrative censoring (the study window simply closing) is the safe case; informative dropout (patients deteriorating and disappearing from the registry) is the dangerous one.

// A tiny seeded PRNG *factory* so "Resample cohort" gives fresh-but-reproducible
// draws without ever reading+writing a mutable. mulberry32 is a standard 32-bit
// generator. We build a NEW generator from an integer seed on each call, so the
// cohort is a pure function of (seed, censoring rate): moving the censoring
// slider re-censors the SAME patients rather than reshuffling them.
svkm_makeRng = function (seed) {
  let a = (seed * 2654435761 + 12345) >>> 0;
  return function () {
    a |= 0; a = (a + 0x6D2B79F5) | 0;
    let t = Math.imul(a ^ (a >>> 15), 1 | a);
    t = (t + Math.imul(t ^ (t >>> 7), 61 | t)) ^ t;
    return ((t ^ (t >>> 14)) >>> 0) / 4294967296;
  };
}
// Draw a censored cohort for one group. True event times T are exponential with
// the group's hazard rate; administrative censoring time C is the smaller of a
// uniform "lost to follow-up" draw (governed by svkm_censRate) and the study
// horizon. We observe min(T, C) and the event indicator δ = 1{T ≤ C}.
//
// Each patient consumes EXACTLY three rng() draws in a fixed order (event-time,
// loss-test, loss-time), regardless of censRate. So changing the censoring
// slider re-censors the SAME underlying patients with the SAME event times T,
// rather than reshuffling the whole stream — the behaviour the prose promises.
svkm_drawGroup = function (rate, n, horizon, censRate, rng) {
  const out = [];
  for (let i = 0; i < n; i++) {
    const uEvent = Math.max(1e-9, rng());   // 1) event-time uniform
    const uLoss  = rng();                    // 2) loss-test uniform
    const uWhen  = rng();                    // 3) loss-time uniform
    // Inverse-CDF sample of an exponential event time (days).
    const T = -Math.log(uEvent) / rate;
    // With probability censRate the patient is lost early (uniform on the
    // window); otherwise they are only censored if they outlive the horizon.
    const C = uLoss < censRate ? uWhen * horizon : horizon;
    const time  = Math.min(T, C);
    const event = T <= C ? 1 : 0;
    out.push({ time, event });
  }
  return out;
}

// ── The Kaplan-Meier product-limit estimator. ──
// Returns the step points {t, S} plus, at each distinct EVENT time, the risk
// set n_j and event count d_j — exactly the table the prose describes.
svkm_km = function (data) {
  // Distinct times sorted ascending; at each we count events and censorings.
  const byTime = d3.rollups(
    data,
    rows => ({
      d: d3.sum(rows, r => r.event),          // events at this time
      c: d3.sum(rows, r => 1 - r.event)       // censorings at this time
    }),
    r => r.time
  ).sort((a, b) => a[0] - b[0]);

  let atRisk = data.length;
  let S = 1;
  const steps = [{ t: 0, S: 1, n: atRisk, d: 0 }];   // anchor at (0, 1)
  const table = [];

  for (const [t, { d, c }] of byTime) {
    const n = atRisk;                          // risk set JUST BEFORE t
    if (d > 0) {
      S = S * (1 - d / n);                     // product-limit factor
      steps.push({ t, S, n, d });
      table.push({ t, n, d, c, factor: 1 - d / n, S });
    }
    atRisk -= (d + c);                          // events AND censorings leave
  }
  return { steps, table };
}

  const hit = km.steps.find(s => s.S <= 0.5);

}

How to Read the Kaplan-Meier Chart

Work through these in order; each isolates one idea.

Step 1: Read the step curves. Each curve starts at \hat S(0) = 1 (top-left: everyone is readmission-free at discharge) and steps down at each observed readmission. It is flat between events — nothing happened to change our estimate. The red curve (nurse-led program) sits above the slate curve (standard care): at any day t, a larger share of program patients are still readmission-free. Higher and farther-right is better.

Step 2: Find the medians. The grey dashed line at \hat S = 0.5 is the median level. Where each curve first crosses below it, drop straight down to the x-axis (the colored dashed verticals): that is the median survival time — the day by which half the group has been readmitted. With the defaults, the program’s median is roughly 220 days versus about 125 for standard care, an advantage of around three months. The cards beneath the chart report the exact numbers.

Step 3: Spot the censoring ticks. The small “+” marks on each curve are censored patients — the study window closed, or they were lost to follow-up, before any readmission. Crucially, a “+” does not make the curve drop. Censoring removes a patient from the risk set without ever being counted as an event. This is the bookkeeping that OLS and logit get wrong.

Step 4: Turn up the censoring. Drag Censoring rate toward 0.6. More “+” marks appear and the curves become choppier (each remaining event lands on a smaller risk set, so each drop is larger); at high censoring a median may read “not reached” — the curve never falls to 0.5 within the window. The curve does not collapse, because KM uses the information censored patients do provide (they survived at least as long as their tick).

The bold last column is the height of the red curve at that step.

Why Not OLS or Logit?

The familiar tools fail on a censored duration in three distinct ways:

  • OLS on the observed times treats every censored \tilde T_i as a completed duration. Because censored times are systematically shorter than the true (unobserved) event times, the mean is biased downward, and the bias grows with the censoring rate.
  • Logit on an “event happened” indicator throws away when the event happened and pretends a patient followed for 30 days carries the same information as one followed for 500. Two cohorts with identical 18-month readmission rates can have wildly different timing — exactly what a clinic cares about.
  • Dropping censored patients (“complete-case”) keeps only the fast-failing subjects, biasing every estimate toward shorter survival.

Kaplan-Meier sidesteps all three by working with the risk set at each event time, conditioning away the patients we can no longer see.

Comparing Two Groups: The Log-Rank Test

The eye says the red curve is higher — but is the gap real or sampling noise? The log-rank test is the standard nonparametric test of the null hypothesis H_0:\ S_1(t) = S_2(t) for all t (the two groups have identical survival).

The logic is a stratified comparison. At each distinct event time t_j, build a 2\times 2 table of (group \times event). Under H_0, the d_j events at t_j should split between the groups in proportion to their risk sets. So the expected events in group 1 is

e_{1j} = d_j\,\frac{n_{1j}}{n_j},

read as “the events at t_j times group 1’s share of the total risk set.” Summing observed-minus-expected across all event times and standardizing by the (hypergeometric) variance v_j gives the log-rank statistic

\chi^2_{\text{LR}} = \frac{\Big(\sum_j (d_{1j} - e_{1j})\Big)^2}{\sum_j v_j}, \qquad v_j = \frac{n_{1j}\,n_{2j}\,d_j\,(n_j - d_j)}{n_j^2\,(n_j - 1)},

which under H_0 is approximately \chi^2 with 1 degree of freedom. A large statistic (small p-value) says the curves differ by more than chance. The log-rank test is most powerful when the hazards are proportional — the assumption that motivates the Cox model on the next page.

The log-rank test detects a difference, not its size. A small p tells you the survival curves are not identical; it does not say how much longer the program keeps patients out of hospital. For that you need an effect measure — a median difference, a survival difference at a fixed horizon, or a hazard ratio from the Cox model. And because the test pools across all event times, two curves that cross (program better early, worse late) can yield a non-significant log-rank statistic even when both groups differ sharply.

Math Details: The S–h–H relationships and the product-limit formula

From hazard to survival

Start from the definition of the hazard as a conditional density:

h(t) = \frac{f(t)}{S(t)}.

Since f(t) = -S'(t) (the density is the negative slope of the survival function, because S = 1 - F and f = F'),

h(t) = \frac{-S'(t)}{S(t)} = -\frac{d}{dt}\log S(t).

Integrate both sides from 0 to t, using S(0) = 1 so \log S(0) = 0:

\int_0^t h(u)\,du = -\big[\log S(t) - \log S(0)\big] = -\log S(t).

The left side is the cumulative hazard H(t), so H(t) = -\log S(t), and exponentiating gives the central identity

S(t) = \exp\!\big(-H(t)\big). \qquad \checkmark

For a constant hazard h(t) = \lambda (the exponential model used to simulate the interactive), H(t) = \lambda t and S(t) = e^{-\lambda t} — the memoryless special case. The median solves e^{-\lambda t} = 0.5, i.e. t_{\text{med}} = \log 2 / \lambda.

The product-limit estimator as a discrete-hazard product

Partition follow-up at the distinct event times t_1 < \cdots < t_k. Write survival past t as a chain of conditional survivals:

S(t) = P(T > t) = \prod_{j:\,t_j \le t} P(T > t_j \mid T > t_{j-1}).

The discrete conditional hazard at t_j — the chance of failing exactly at t_j given you reached it — is estimated by the observed fraction \hat h_j = d_j / n_j, where n_j is the risk set just before t_j and d_j the events at t_j. The conditional survival through t_j is therefore 1 - \hat h_j = 1 - d_j/n_j. Substituting,

\hat S(t) = \prod_{j:\,t_j \le t} \left(1 - \frac{d_j}{n_j}\right).

This is the Kaplan-Meier estimator. Censored observations enter only through n_j: a subject censored in the interval [t_{j-1}, t_j) is counted in the risk set up to its censoring time and then removed, so it lowers every subsequent n_{j'} but never contributes a d_{j'}. The variance of \hat S(t) is given by Greenwood’s formula,

\widehat{\operatorname{Var}}\big(\hat S(t)\big) = \hat S(t)^2 \sum_{j:\,t_j \le t} \frac{d_j}{n_j\,(n_j - d_j)},

which is what survival and lifelines use to draw the pointwise confidence bands around the step curve.

A tiny worked product-limit

Five patients, times (days): 30, 60^{+}, 90, 120, 150^{+}, where ^{+} marks a censored time. The two event times are 30, 90, 120.

t_j n_j d_j 1 - d_j/n_j \hat S(t_j)
30 5 1 4/5 = 0.800 0.800
90 3 1 2/3 = 0.667 0.533
120 2 1 1/2 = 0.500 0.267

At t = 90 the risk set is only 3, not 4: the patient censored at 60^{+} has already left the pool. The censoring lowered n_j but caused no drop. \checkmark

Computing Kaplan-Meier and the Log-Rank Test

Both panels fit KM curves for the two groups, report median survival, and run a log-rank test — the idiomatic survival-analysis workflow in each language. R uses the survival package (survfit, survdiff); Python uses lifelines (KaplanMeierFitter, logrank_test).

  • Python
# install.packages("survival")
library(survival)

# Heart-failure cohort: time = days to readmission (or censoring),
# event = 1 if readmitted, 0 if censored; group = program vs standard.
set.seed(2024)
n <- 80
sim <- function(rate, horizon = 540, cens = 0.25) {
  T <- rexp(n, rate = rate)                       # true event times
  C <- ifelse(runif(n) < cens, runif(n, 0, horizon), horizon)
  data.frame(time = pmin(T, C), event = as.integer(T <= C))
}
prog <- transform(sim(1 / 320), group = "Program")
stnd <- transform(sim(1 / 180), group = "Standard")
hf   <- rbind(prog, stnd)

# The survival OBJECT pairs the follow-up time with the event indicator.
surv_obj <- Surv(hf$time, hf$event)

# Kaplan-Meier estimate by group.
fit <- survfit(surv_obj ~ group, data = hf)
print(fit)                       # n, events, and MEDIAN survival per group
summary(fit, times = c(180, 365))  # Ŝ(t) at 6 and 12 months, with 95% CI

# Log-rank test of S_program(t) = S_standard(t) for all t.
lr <- survdiff(surv_obj ~ group, data = hf)
print(lr)                        # observed vs expected events + chi-square, p

# Plot the step curves with censoring ticks and confidence bands.
plot(fit, col = c("#990000", "#3d5a6c"), lwd = 2, mark.time = TRUE,
     xlab = "Days since discharge", ylab = expression(hat(S)(t)))
legend("topright", c("Program", "Standard"),
       col = c("#990000", "#3d5a6c"), lwd = 2, bty = "n")
# pip install lifelines
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
from lifelines import KaplanMeierFitter
from lifelines.statistics import logrank_test

rng = np.random.default_rng(2024)
n, horizon, cens = 80, 540, 0.25

def sim(rate):
    T = rng.exponential(scale=1 / rate, size=n)            # true event times
    C = np.where(rng.random(n) < cens, rng.uniform(0, horizon, n), horizon)
    return pd.DataFrame({"time": np.minimum(T, C),
                         "event": (T <= C).astype(int)})

prog = sim(1 / 320).assign(group="Program")
stnd = sim(1 / 180).assign(group="Standard")
hf = pd.concat([prog, stnd], ignore_index=True)

# Kaplan-Meier per group; print median survival and Ŝ at fixed horizons.
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
ax = plt.subplot()
for name, color in [("Program", "#990000"), ("Standard", "#3d5a6c")]:
    sub = hf[hf.group == name]
    kmf = KaplanMeierFitter(label=name)
    kmf.fit(sub["time"], event_observed=sub["event"])
    kmf.plot_survival_function(ax=ax, color=color, ci_show=True)
    print(f"{name}: median survival = {kmf.median_survival_time_:.0f} d")
    print(kmf.survival_function_at_times([180, 365]).to_string())

# Log-rank test of S_program(t) == S_standard(t) for all t.
res = logrank_test(prog["time"], stnd["time"],
                   event_observed_A=prog["event"],
                   event_observed_B=stnd["event"])
print(f"log-rank chi2 = {res.test_statistic:.2f}, p = {res.p_value:.4f}")

In both languages the median survival is markedly higher for the program group, and the log-rank test returns a small p-value — the curves differ by more than sampling noise. The next page replaces this two-group comparison with a regression on the hazard that adjusts for patient covariates.

Exercises

Exercise 1

A small relapse study at a vårdcentral follows six patients after a smoking-cessation program. Days to relapse (a ^{+} marks a right-censored time — still abstinent when last seen): 40,\ 40,\ 90^{+},\ 120,\ 200^{+},\ 260.

  1. List the distinct event times and, for each, the risk set n_j and event count d_j. (b) Compute the Kaplan-Meier estimate \hat S(t) at each event time. (c) What is the estimated median time to relapse?
Show answer

(a) Order all six times: 40, 40, 90^{+}, 120, 200^{+}, 260. The distinct event times (drop the censored 90^{+} and 200^{+}) are 40, 120, 260.

  • t_1 = 40: just before t=40 all 6 are at risk, so n_1 = 6; two patients relapse, d_1 = 2.
  • t_2 = 120: patients used up so far are the two events at 40 and the censoring at 90^{+} (3 total), so n_2 = 6 - 3 = 3; one relapse, d_2 = 1.
  • t_3 = 260: by now we have lost the events at 40 and 120 and the censorings at 90^{+} and 200^{+} (5 total), so n_3 = 6 - 5 = 1; one relapse, d_3 = 1.

(b) Apply the product-limit formula \hat S(t) = \prod (1 - d_j/n_j):

  • \hat S(40) = 1 - 2/6 = 4/6 = 0.667.
  • \hat S(120) = 0.667 \times (1 - 1/3) = 0.667 \times 0.667 = 0.444.
  • \hat S(260) = 0.444 \times (1 - 1/1) = 0.444 \times 0 = 0.

(c) The median is the first time \hat S(t) \le 0.5. Since \hat S(40)=0.667 and \hat S(120)=0.444, the curve first drops to/below 0.5 at t = 120. The estimated median time to relapse is 120 days. Note the censored patient at 90^{+} never caused a drop but did shrink the risk set from 6 to 3 by t=120 — exactly the KM bookkeeping.

Exercise 2

A colleague proposes analyzing time-to-readmission by (i) coding “readmitted within 18 months” as a 0/1 outcome and running a logistic regression, and (ii) for patients who were readmitted, running OLS of the readmission time on covariates. State one distinct problem with each approach and explain how Kaplan-Meier / survival methods avoid it.

Show answer

(i) The logit on a binary “readmitted within 18 months” indicator discards the timing. It treats a patient readmitted on day 20 and one readmitted on day 530 as identical “events,” and a patient censored at day 60 (lost to follow-up) is coded the same as one genuinely event-free at 18 months. Two programs with the same 18-month readmission rate but very different timing look identical. Survival methods keep the full duration and condition on the risk set at each time, so when the event happens is the object of analysis.

(ii) The OLS-on-the-readmitted approach conditions on having the event, which is selection on the outcome. It silently drops every censored patient, and the patients who were readmitted within the window are disproportionately the fast-failing ones — so the regression describes a biased subsample and underestimates true survival. Kaplan-Meier (and Cox) instead use the censored observations: a censored patient contributes “survived at least \tilde T_i” to every risk set up to \tilde T_i, rather than being thrown away.

Exercise 3

For a constant-hazard (exponential) model with hazard h(t) = \lambda:

  1. Write H(t) and S(t). (b) Derive the median survival time. (c) Standard care has \lambda_S = 1/180 per day and the program has \lambda_P = 1/320 per day. Compute and compare the two medians, and state the corresponding hazard ratio.
Show answer

(a) The cumulative hazard is H(t) = \int_0^t \lambda\,du = \lambda t, so by the identity S(t) = e^{-H(t)} = e^{-\lambda t}.

(b) The median t_{\text{med}} solves S(t_{\text{med}}) = 0.5: e^{-\lambda t_{\text{med}}} = 0.5 \;\Longrightarrow\; -\lambda t_{\text{med}} = \log 0.5 \;\Longrightarrow\; t_{\text{med}} = \frac{\log 2}{\lambda} \approx \frac{0.693}{\lambda}.

(c) Standard care: t_{\text{med}}^{S} = 0.693 \times 180 \approx 125 days. Program: t_{\text{med}}^{P} = 0.693 \times 320 \approx 222 days. The program nearly doubles the median readmission-free time (an advantage of about 97 days, roughly three months). The hazard ratio is \lambda_P / \lambda_S = (1/320)/(1/180) = 180/320 = 0.5625 — the program carries about 56% of the standard-care readmission hazard at every instant. (These are the population values the interactive simulates; the sampled medians fluctuate around them.)

Exercise 4

In the interactive, push the censoring rate toward 0.6. (a) Why does each remaining step in the curve become larger as censoring rises? (b) Why might the median read “not reached”? (c) Explain why the curve nonetheless does not simply collapse to a biased estimate the way a complete-case analysis would.

Show answer

(a) Each downward step is the factor 1 - d_j/n_j. As censoring removes patients from the risk set, n_j shrinks, so a single event (d_j = 1) on a small risk set — say n_j = 4 — knocks the curve down by 1/4 = 25\%, versus 1/40 early on. Fewer at-risk patients means each event moves the estimate more, which is why high-censoring curves look choppy and their later steps are large.

(b) The median is the first time \hat S(t) \le 0.5. With heavy early censoring, many patients leave the risk set before any event drives the curve down to 0.5; the curve can flatten out above 0.5 and simply end at the last follow-up time without ever crossing it. The median is then undefined within the observed window — correctly reported as “not reached” rather than guessed.

(c) Kaplan-Meier uses the censored observations: a patient censored at \tilde T_i is counted in every risk set up to \tilde T_i, contributing the genuine information “this patient was event-free for at least \tilde T_i days.” A complete-case analysis instead deletes those patients entirely, keeping only the fast-failing ones and biasing survival downward. KM stays (approximately) unbiased under non-informative censoring precisely because the censored rows still shape the risk sets — they are information, not missingness.

Key takeaways

  • Time-to-event outcomes are durations T \ge 0 defined by right-censoring: for many subjects we observe only \tilde T_i = \min(T_i, c_i) and an event indicator \delta_i, knowing only that the true duration exceeds the follow-up time.
  • The survival function S(t)=P(T>t), hazard h(t)=f(t)/S(t), and cumulative hazard H(t)=\int_0^t h are one distribution; they lock together via S(t)=\exp(-H(t)).
  • The Kaplan-Meier estimator \hat S(t)=\prod_{t_j\le t}(1 - d_j/n_j) is a nonparametric step curve that drops only at events and lets censored patients shrink the risk set without ever counting as events.
  • OLS, logit, and complete-case deletion all mishandle censored durations — biasing toward shorter survival or discarding the timing entirely — which is why we use survival-specific tools.
  • The log-rank test compares two survival curves under H_0:\ S_1=S_2 by accumulating observed-minus-expected events across the risk sets; it flags a difference but not its size.

Kaplan-Meier and the log-rank test answer a two-group question nonparametrically, but real cohorts differ in age, comorbidity, sex, and vårdcentral — and we want to adjust for them and report a single effect measure.
