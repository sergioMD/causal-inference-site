Propensity Scores

The balancing score and how to use it

The Curse That Matching Inherits

On the previous page, Matching Methods, we learned to estimate a treatment effect by pairing each treated unit with control units that look like it on the observed covariates X. For a Swedish vårdcentral (primary care center) evaluating its structured diabetes coordination program (Diabetessamordning), “looks like it” might mean: same age band, same sex, same baseline HbA1c, same body-mass index, same number of comorbidities, same neighbourhood deprivation index, same distance to the nearest clinic. Match a treated patient to controls who share all of those, average the within-pair differences, and — under conditional independence — you have an estimate of the program’s effect on the patients who enrolled.

The trouble is arithmetic. Suppose we want to match exactly on just seven covariates, each coarsened into a handful of bins. With, say, five age bands, two sexes, four HbA1c categories, three BMI categories, four comorbidity counts, three distance bands, and five deprivation deciles, the covariate space already has 5 \times 2 \times 4 \times 3 \times 4 \times 3 \times 5 = 7{,}200 distinct cells. A region with a few thousand diabetes patients will leave most of those cells empty, or holding a single treated patient with no control who matches exactly. This is the curse of dimensionality: as the number of covariates grows, exact matches become impossible, and even approximate matches force us to throw information away.

What if there were a way to collapse all seven covariates — or seventy — into a single number, such that matching on that one number were just as good, for the purpose of removing bias, as matching on the entire vector X? That number exists. It is the propensity score, and the theorem that licenses its use is one of the most elegant results in all of causal inference.

In this section, you will learn to

  • Define the propensity score e(X) = P(D=1 \mid X) and explain why it is a balancing score.
  • State and read the Rosenbaum–Rubin theorem, and understand why conditioning on the scalar e(X) suffices to remove bias under conditional independence.
  • Estimate e(X) with a logit model and use it to match, stratify, or weight in Swedish primary-care data.
  • Diagnose overlap (common support) from overlapping propensity-score histograms.
  • Recognize the King–Nielsen “propensity-score paradox” and when it bites.

The Propensity Score

The propensity score is the probability of receiving treatment given the observed covariates:

e(X_i) = P(D_i = 1 \mid X_i = x).

Read this as: “e of X_i is the probability that patient i enrolls in the program, given everything we have measured about them.” It is a single number between 0 and 1 — a patient with e(X_i) = 0.8 is, conditional on their covariates, the kind of patient who enrolls four times out of five; a patient with e(X_i) = 0.1 rarely enrolls.

Notice that the propensity score is a property of the covariates, not of the outcome. It says nothing about HbA1c. It only summarizes how a patient’s measured characteristics translate into a probability of treatment. In an observational study we never see the true function e(\cdot) — we have to estimate it. But for the moment, suppose a benevolent oracle hands us the true e(X_i) for every patient. The remarkable claim is that this one number is all we need.

Two assumptions carry over from the matching page; we restate them because everything below depends on them.

Conditional Independence (Unconfoundedness, CIA). Given the observed covariates, treatment is as good as randomly assigned:

\big(Y_i(0),\, Y_i(1)\big) \perp\!\!\!\perp D_i \mid X_i.

Read this as: “Within the group of patients who share the same covariate vector X, who actually enrolled is unrelated to what their outcomes would have been.” There is no unmeasured confounder driving both enrollment and HbA1c.

Overlap (Common Support). Every covariate profile has a chance of appearing in both arms:

0 < e(X_i) < 1 \quad \text{for all } X_i.

Read this as: “No type of patient is guaranteed to enroll, and none is guaranteed to stay out.” If some kind of patient always enrolls (e = 1), there is no comparable control for them and their effect is unidentified.

The Balancing-Score Theorem

Here is the result that makes propensity scores indispensable. It comes in two parts, both due to Rosenbaum and Rubin (1983).

Rosenbaum–Rubin, Part 1 (the balancing property). The propensity score is a balancing score: conditional on e(X), treatment status is independent of the covariates,

D \perp\!\!\!\perp X \mid e(X).

Read this as: “Among patients who share the same propensity score, the treated and the untreated have the same distribution of covariates.” Even though high-e patients differ from low-e patients, within a slice of constant e the treated and control groups look alike on every component of X — same average age, same average HbA1c, same everything.

This is genuinely surprising. The covariate vector X might be twenty-dimensional, yet the scalar e(X) “carries” all the information about treatment assignment that X contains. Slice the data by e, and inside each slice the treatment looks randomized with respect to the covariates. That is exactly the situation a matched or stratified design wants to engineer — and it can be engineered along one axis instead of twenty.

The second part connects the balancing property to bias removal.

Rosenbaum–Rubin, Part 2 (unconfoundedness given the score). If treatment is unconfounded given X, then it is also unconfounded given the propensity score alone:

\big(Y(0),\, Y(1)\big) \perp\!\!\!\perp D \mid X \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \big(Y(0),\, Y(1)\big) \perp\!\!\!\perp D \mid e(X).

Read this as: “If adjusting for the full covariate vector X would suffice to remove bias, then adjusting for the single number e(X) suffices too.” The high-dimensional adjustment problem collapses to a one-dimensional one.

The practical payoff is enormous. To estimate the average treatment effect on the treated (ATT) — our estimand, the population quantity we are trying to learn (as opposed to the estimator, the formula we compute, or the estimate, the number it returns) —

\text{ATT} = E\big[\,Y_i(1) - Y_i(0) \mid D_i = 1\,\big],

we no longer need to find, for each enrolled patient, a control patient identical on all of X. We only need a control patient with the same propensity score. By Part 2, conditioning on e(X) is enough; by Part 1, matching on e(X) automatically balances every covariate. Seven thousand cells become one continuous dimension we can stratify into five or ten bins.

The score does not create comparability — it reveals it. The theorem assumes CIA holds given the measured X. If a patient’s motivation, diet, or an unrecorded comorbidity drives both enrollment and HbA1c, that confounder is absent from X, absent from e(X), and absent from your adjustment. A propensity score built from the wrong covariates balances the wrong things perfectly. The score is a dimension-reduction device, not a substitute for measuring confounders.

The whole machinery exists to close one picture. The covariate X (baseline severity) sits behind both enrollment D and the outcome Y, opening a back-door path D \leftarrow X \rightarrow Y — a non-causal route that makes the naive comparison confound the program with severity. (A back-door path is any route from D to Y that starts with an arrow into D.) Conditioning on X — equivalently, on the scalar e(X) by Rosenbaum–Rubin — blocks that path while leaving the causal arrow D \rightarrow Y untouched. Toggle the adjustment below and watch the red back-door go grey.

Estimating the Score: Logistic Regression

In practice the oracle is unavailable and we estimate e(X). The default workhorse is logistic regression, which models the log-odds of enrollment as a linear function of the covariates:

\log\!\left(\frac{e(X_i)}{1 - e(X_i)}\right) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_{1i} + \beta_2 X_{2i} + \cdots + \beta_k X_{ki}.

Read this as: “The log-odds of enrolling is a weighted sum of the patient’s covariates.” Solving for e gives the familiar logistic (sigmoid) curve e(X_i) = 1 / \big(1 + e^{-(\beta_0 + \beta'X_i)}\big), which is guaranteed to land between 0 and 1 — as a probability must.

The S-curve below shows that squashing in action: the unbounded linear predictor (log-odds, -\infty to +\infty) on the horizontal axis is mapped to a bounded probability on the vertical axis. A log-odds of 0 maps to \hat e = 0.5; large positive log-odds saturate toward 1, large negative toward 0. One example patient is marked.

The fitted \hat e(X_i) is each patient’s estimated probability of enrolling. We then use these estimates one of three ways:

  1. Matching on the score. Pair each treated patient with the control patient (or k controls) whose \hat e is closest. This is one-dimensional nearest-neighbour matching.
  2. Stratification (subclassification). Sort patients into strata of the score — a classic choice is quintiles — and compare treated vs. control outcomes within each stratum, then average across strata weighting by stratum size. Cochran (1968) showed that five strata remove roughly 90% of the bias from a continuous confounder.
  3. Weighting. Reweight units by functions of \hat e so the weighted treated and control samples are balanced. This is inverse-probability weighting, the subject of the next page.

A crucial point about model selection: we tune the propensity model to achieve covariate balance, not to maximize predictive accuracy. A score that predicts treatment perfectly (\hat e near 0 or 1 for everyone) is a disaster — it means there is no overlap and no comparable controls. The goal is balance, not classification skill.

The two thumbnails below make the trade-off visible. On the left, a modest classifier (low AUC — the share of treated/control pairs the score ranks correctly) leaves the two score distributions heavily overlapping: plenty of comparable controls at every score. On the right, a near-perfect classifier (AUC \approx 1) drives the treated to \hat e \approx 1 and the controls to \hat e \approx 0 — spectacular prediction, but the two distributions barely touch, so almost no patient has a comparable counterpart. Excellent prediction is poor overlap.

// Two synthetic score distributions: "good overlap" (low separation) vs

ps_overlapData = {
  let a = 7777;
  const unif = () => {
    a |= 0; a = a + 0x6D2B79F5 | 0;

    t = t + Math.imul(t ^ t >>> 7, 61 | t) ^ t;
    return ((t ^ t >>> 14) >>> 0) / 4294967296;
  };
  const norm = () => Math.sqrt(-2 * Math.log(unif())) * Math.cos(2 * Math.PI * unif());

  return rows;
}

See It: From Covariates to the Balancing Score

The balance panel reports the standardized mean difference (SMD) — the gap between the treated and control means of a covariate, divided by their pooled standard deviation, \text{SMD} = (\bar X_t - \bar X_c)\,/\,s_{\text{pooled}}. Dividing by the spread makes it unitless, so the same |\text{SMD}| < 0.1 rule of thumb for “well balanced” applies to age, HbA1c, or any covariate at once.

// Returns intercept b0 and slope b1; we then attach a fitted score ehat
// to every patient. Pure in-browser numerics — no external solver.
maps_logit = {
  const data = maps_cohort;
  let b0 = 0, b1 = 0;
  for (let iter = 0; iter < 25; iter++) {
    // Accumulate gradient g and Hessian H of the log-likelihood.
    let g0 = 0, g1 = 0, h00 = 0, h01 = 0, h11 = 0;
    for (const r of data) {
      const eta = b0 + b1 * r.x;
      const p = 1 / (1 + Math.exp(-eta));
      const w = p * (1 - p);
      const resid = r.d - p;
      g0 += resid;
      g1 += resid * r.x;
      h00 += w;
      h01 += w * r.x;
      h11 += w * r.x * r.x;
    }
    // Newton step:  b <- b + H^{-1} g   (H is the negative Hessian here).
    const det = h00 * h11 - h01 * h01;
    if (Math.abs(det) < 1e-10) break;
    const db0 = (h11 * g0 - h01 * g1) / det;
    const db1 = (-h01 * g0 + h00 * g1) / det;
    b0 += db0;
    b1 += db1;
    if (Math.abs(db0) + Math.abs(db1) < 1e-8) break;
  }
  return { b0, b1 };
}

// Each returned record carries a `weight` and a `stratum` so the balance
// computation downstream is a single weighted formula for all three modes.
maps_adjusted = {
  const treated = maps_scored.filter(r => r.d === 1);
  const control = maps_scored.filter(r => r.d === 0);

  if (maps_adjust === "Raw (no adjustment)") {
    return maps_scored.map(r => ({ ...r, weight: 1, stratum: 0 }));
  }

  if (maps_adjust === "1:1 match on score") {
    // Greedy nearest-neighbour matching of each treated unit to its closest
    // control on ehat, WITHOUT replacement, WITH a caliper. The caliper is the
    // Austin (2011) standard, 0.2 × sd(ê): a treated unit whose nearest control
    // is farther than the caliper is DROPPED (no acceptable match exists). This
    // is what makes matching actually balance — without a caliper the treated
    // group is never pruned, so its sicker mean is retained and the covariate
    // SMD barely moves. With the caliper, every retained pair lives inside the
    // common support, so the covariate collapses into balance; the price is that
    // more treated units go unmatched as overlap shrinks (see maps_matchInfo).
    const allE = maps_scored.map(r => r.ehat);
    const mE = d3.mean(allE);
    const sdE = Math.sqrt(d3.mean(allE, e => (e - mE) ** 2));
    const caliper = 0.2 * sdE;
    const pool = control.map(c => ({ ...c, used: false }));
    const out = [];
    for (const t of treated) {
      let best = null, bestGap = Infinity;
      for (const c of pool) {
        if (c.used) continue;
        const gap = Math.abs(c.ehat - t.ehat);
        if (gap < bestGap) { bestGap = gap; best = c; }
      }
      if (best && bestGap <= caliper) {
        best.used = true;
        out.push({ ...t, weight: 1, stratum: 0 });
        out.push({ ...best, weight: 1, stratum: 0 });
      }
    }
    return out;
  }

  // Stratify on score quintiles. Within each quintile we keep all units but
  // weight them so each stratum contributes in proportion to its treated count
  // (an ATT-style stratification). Strata with no controls are dropped.
  const sorted = [...maps_scored].sort((a, b) => a.ehat - b.ehat);
  const nq = 5;
  const out = [];
  for (let q = 0; q < nq; q++) {
    const lo = Math.floor((q * sorted.length) / nq);
    const hi = Math.floor(((q + 1) * sorted.length) / nq);
    const slice = sorted.slice(lo, hi);
    const tr = slice.filter(r => r.d === 1);
    const ct = slice.filter(r => r.d === 0);
    if (tr.length === 0 || ct.length === 0) continue;
    for (const r of tr) out.push({ ...r, weight: 1, stratum: q });
    // Controls reweighted so the stratum's control mass equals its treated count.
    const wc = tr.length / ct.length;
    for (const r of ct) out.push({ ...r, weight: wc, stratum: q });
  }
  return out;
}
// Weighted mean helper for the covariate-balance diagnostic.

  const W = d3.sum(rows, r => r.weight);
  return W > 0 ? d3.sum(rows, r => r.weight * acc(r)) / W : NaN;

// Weighted variance helper.

  const W = d3.sum(rows, r => r.weight);
  if (W <= 0) return NaN;
  const m = maps_wmean(rows, acc);
  return d3.sum(rows, r => r.weight * (acc(r) - m) ** 2) / W;

  const vc = maps_wvar(ct, r => r.x);

  return sp > 0 ? (mt - mc) / sp : 0;
}

How to Read the Three Panels

Work through the interactive in order; each panel isolates one link in the chain.

Step 1: See the imbalance (Panel A). With Selection strength at its default, look at the raw distribution of baseline severity X. The red (enrolled) histogram sits to the right of the slate (standard-care) histogram: enrolled patients are sicker on average. This is the confounding the whole machinery must defeat. A naive HbA1c comparison would credit the program with — or blame it for — this baseline gap.

Step 2: Watch the score separate (Panel B). The estimated propensity score \hat e(X) is, by construction, a monotone transform of X here, so the same imbalance reappears as two histograms on the [0,1] axis. The region where the two histograms overlap is the common support — the patients for whom comparable units of the other arm exist. Now drag Selection strength higher: the two propensity distributions pull apart, the red (enrolled) mass piles up near \hat e = 1 and the slate (standard-care) near \hat e = 0, and the overlap region shrinks. This is overlap failing before your eyes.

Step 3: Rebalance on the scalar (Panel C). Switch Adjustment from “Raw” to “1:1 match on score.” Panel C reports the standardized mean difference (SMD) of X — the headline balance diagnostic, where |\text{SMD}| < 0.1 (the shaded green band) is the conventional threshold for “well balanced.” The raw bar is long and red (outside the green band); the adjusted bar collapses into the green band. You balanced the covariate X by matching on the single number \hat e — the Rosenbaum–Rubin balancing property made visible. (The match uses a caliper of 0.2 \times \text{sd}(\hat e): a treated patient with no control inside that distance is left unmatched, which is exactly what forces the retained pairs into the common-support region.) Try “Stratify on score (quintiles)” for the same effect via subclassification — within each quintile the control group is reweighted to match the treated count, so the comparison targets the treated (an ATT-style stratification).

Step 4: Break it. Push Selection strength toward 3 and watch overlap in Panel B nearly vanish. Now the two adjustments fail in different ways, and the verdict line under Panel C reports each. Under matching, the retained pairs stay inside the green band — but read the verdict: the caliper has to discard a growing share of treated patients (often well over half) because they have no comparable control. You bought balance by throwing away the very patients you wanted to learn about. Under stratification, no units are dropped, so the imbalance instead leaks back into the bar: the adjusted SMD climbs out of the green band as the within-quintile groups stop being comparable. Either way, no method can manufacture comparisons the data do not contain — the score diagnoses the lack of overlap, it does not cure it.

Step 5: Resample. Press Draw a new cohort a few times. The exact bars jitter with sampling noise, but the qualitative story is stable: raw imbalance is large and grows with selection, while the adjustment pulls it back — matching by discarding non-overlapping treated, stratification by averaging within quintiles. The lesson is structural, not an artefact of one draw.

A Caution: The Propensity-Score Paradox

Propensity-score matching in particular — as opposed to stratification or weighting — carries a subtle trap identified by King and Nielsen (2019). The intuition is this: matching on the propensity score tries to make the two groups resemble a completely randomized experiment, in which covariates are balanced only on average. But we usually have something better available — the ideal of a fully blocked (exactly matched) experiment, in which covariates are balanced unit by unit.

The PSM paradox (King & Nielsen, 2019). When you prune units to improve propensity-score balance, you can increase imbalance on the covariates themselves. As you discard units whose scores are far apart, the remaining matched set can still differ on X in ways the scalar score does not see (two patients can share \hat e = 0.5 while one is old-and-mild and the other young-and-severe). Past a point, tightening the score-match degrades covariate balance, model dependence, and bias — the opposite of the goal.

Practical guidance. Always check covariate balance directly (as Panel C does), not just score balance. Prefer methods that target the covariates — coarsened exact matching, Mahalanobis matching, or stable inverse-probability weighting — and treat the propensity score as one tool among several, not the destination. The score is superb for diagnosing overlap and for weighting; for matching, watch the covariate SMDs, not the score gaps.

This caution does not overturn the Rosenbaum–Rubin theorem — the theorem is about the true score and exact conditioning. The paradox is about the finite-sample behaviour of approximate matching on an estimated score. Both are true at once: the score is the right object to condition on in theory, and naive matching on it can misbehave in practice.

Math Details: Proof Sketch of the Balancing Property

Goal

We show Part 1 of Rosenbaum–Rubin: the propensity score e(X) is a balancing score, meaning

D \perp\!\!\!\perp X \mid e(X).

By a standard characterization, this conditional independence holds if and only if the conditional probability of treatment given both X and e(X) does not actually depend on X once e(X) is fixed:

P\big(D = 1 \mid X,\, e(X)\big) = P\big(D = 1 \mid e(X)\big).

Step 1: The left-hand side collapses

Since e(X) is a deterministic function of X, knowing X already tells you e(X) — the second conditioning variable is redundant. Hence

P\big(D = 1 \mid X,\, e(X)\big) = P\big(D = 1 \mid X\big) = e(X),

where the last equality is simply the definition of the propensity score.

Read this as: “Given the full covariate vector, the chance of treatment is e(X) by definition; adding e(X) to the conditioning set tells us nothing new.”

Step 2: The right-hand side

Now compute the probability of treatment given only the score. Use the law of total expectation (the tower property), averaging the treatment indicator over all covariate profiles that share the same score value:

P\big(D = 1 \mid e(X)\big) = E\big[\,D \mid e(X)\,\big] = E\big[\,E[D \mid X] \mid e(X)\,\big] = E\big[\,e(X) \mid e(X)\,\big] = e(X).

Read this as: “Average the per-patient treatment probability e(X) over everyone with a given score; but everyone with that score has the same e(X), so the average is just that value.” The inner expectation E[D \mid X] = e(X) is again the definition; conditioning a constant on itself returns the constant.

Step 3: Compare

Both sides equal e(X):

P\big(D = 1 \mid X,\, e(X)\big) = e(X) = P\big(D = 1 \mid e(X)\big).

So P(D=1 \mid X, e(X)) does not depend on X beyond what e(X) already encodes, which is exactly the statement D \perp\!\!\!\perp X \mid e(X). \qquad\blacksquare

Part 2 follows quickly

For unconfoundedness given the score, assume CIA given X: \big(Y(0), Y(1)\big) \perp\!\!\!\perp D \mid X. We want \big(Y(0), Y(1)\big) \perp\!\!\!\perp D \mid e(X). It suffices to show

P\big(D = 1 \mid Y(0), Y(1),\, e(X)\big) = P\big(D = 1 \mid e(X)\big).

Condition further on X inside the left side and use the tower property:

P\big(D=1 \mid Y(0),Y(1),e(X)\big) = E\big[\,P(D=1 \mid Y(0),Y(1),X) \mid Y(0),Y(1),e(X)\,\big].

By CIA, P(D=1 \mid Y(0),Y(1),X) = P(D=1 \mid X) = e(X) — the potential outcomes drop out. The outer expectation of the constant e(X) (constant given e(X)) is e(X), which by Step 2 equals P(D=1 \mid e(X)). The treatment probability is the same with or without the potential outcomes in the conditioning set, so they are independent given e(X). \qquad\blacksquare

Why a coarser score also balances

Any finer function of X than e(X) is also a balancing score, and e(X) is the coarsest one — the maximal dimension reduction that still balances. This is why a single scalar, rather than the full vector, is enough: e(X) is the smallest summary of X that retains all the information relevant to treatment assignment.

Code: Estimate, Match, and Check Balance

Both implementations simulate a cohort of Swedish diabetes patients, estimate the propensity score with a logit, match 1:1 on it, and — the step that matters most — check covariate balance before and after. The data-generating process puts the true ATT at -0.50 percentage points of HbA1c. (HbA1c is itself reported as a percentage, so a percentage-point change is an absolute move on that scale — e.g. from 7.6% to 7.1% — not a 0.50% relative reduction, which would be a different and much smaller number.)

import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
from sklearn.linear_model import LogisticRegression
from scipy.spatial import cKDTree

rng = np.random.default_rng(2019)
n = 2000

# --- Covariates for diabetes patients at a vårdcentral ---
age      = rng.normal(64, 10, n)
hba1c0   = rng.normal(7.6, 1.0, n)
bmi      = rng.normal(29, 4, n)
deprived = rng.binomial(1, 0.35, n)

# Sicker, older, more-deprived patients enroll more (true propensity).
# The -11 intercept calibrates enrollment to ~19% (a voluntary program),
# leaving a large control pool so every treated patient finds a close match.
lp     = -11 + 0.04*age + 0.7*hba1c0 + 0.05*bmi + 0.4*deprived
e_true = 1 / (1 + np.exp(-lp))
enrolled = rng.binomial(1, e_true)

y0 = 0.6*hba1c0 + 0.01*age + 0.02*bmi + rng.normal(0, 0.5, n)
y1 = y0 - 0.50                                   # program lowers HbA1c by 0.50
hba1c1 = np.where(enrolled == 1, y1, y0)         # observed 12-month HbA1c

df = pd.DataFrame(dict(age=age, hba1c0=hba1c0, bmi=bmi,
                       deprived=deprived, enrolled=enrolled, hba1c1=hba1c1))

# --- Naive (confounded) difference in means ---
naive = df.loc[df.enrolled==1,"hba1c1"].mean() - df.loc[df.enrolled==0,"hba1c1"].mean()

# --- Estimate the propensity score (logit) ---
X = df[["age", "hba1c0", "bmi", "deprived"]].to_numpy()
ps = LogisticRegression(max_iter=1000).fit(X, df.enrolled).predict_proba(X)[:, 1]
df["ps"] = ps

# --- 1:1 nearest-neighbour match on the score (ATT: match each treated) ---
treated = df[df.enrolled == 1]
control = df[df.enrolled == 0]
tree = cKDTree(control[["ps"]].to_numpy())
_, idx = tree.query(treated[["ps"]].to_numpy(), k=1)
matched_ctrl = control.iloc[idx]

# Standardized mean difference helper (the balance diagnostic)
def smd(a, b):
    return (a.mean() - b.mean()) / np.sqrt((a.var() + b.var()) / 2)

for col in ["age", "hba1c0", "bmi", "deprived"]:
    before = smd(treated[col], control[col])
    after  = smd(treated[col], matched_ctrl[col])
    print(f"SMD {col:8s}  before={before:+.2f}  after={after:+.2f}")

att = treated["hba1c1"].mean() - matched_ctrl["hba1c1"].mean()
print(f"\nNaive difference: {naive:+.3f}")        # biased toward 0 (selection masks the benefit)
print(f"PS-matched ATT:   {att:+.3f}")            # close to the true -0.50

Both routes recover an ATT near the true -0.50, while the naive difference is badly biased toward zero — the positive selection bias (enrolled patients were sicker at baseline) nearly cancels the program’s true benefit, so the raw comparison makes an effective program look like it does almost nothing. The decisive output is the balance table: every covariate’s standardized mean difference should fall inside \pm 0.1 after matching. If it does not, the match has failed and the ATT cannot be trusted — balance, not the point estimate, is the thing to inspect first.

Exercise 1

A vårdcentral fits a logistic propensity model for enrollment in a fall-prevention program using a single covariate, age (centered). The fitted model is

\log\!\left(\frac{\hat e}{1-\hat e}\right) = -0.5 + 0.08\,(\text{age} - 75).

  1. What is the estimated propensity score for an 80-year-old? (b) For a 70-year-old? (c) A 78-year-old enrolled patient is to be matched 1:1 on the score. Among three available controls aged 72, 79, and 85, which is the propensity-score nearest neighbour?
Show answer

(a) For age 80: linear predictor = -0.5 + 0.08 \times (80 - 75) = -0.5 + 0.40 = -0.10. Then \hat e = 1/(1 + e^{0.10}) = 1/(1 + 1.105) = 0.475.

(b) For age 70: linear predictor = -0.5 + 0.08 \times (70 - 75) = -0.5 - 0.40 = -0.90. Then \hat e = 1/(1 + e^{0.90}) = 1/(1 + 2.460) = 0.289.

(c) Because the score is monotone increasing in age here (the slope 0.08 > 0), nearest on the score is the same as nearest in age. The treated patient is 78. The control ages are 72 (gap 6), 79 (gap 1), 85 (gap 7), so the 79-year-old is the nearest neighbour. (Check via scores: 78 gives LP = -0.5 + 0.24 = -0.26, \hat e = 0.435; 79 gives LP = -0.18, \hat e = 0.455, the closest of the three.) With a single monotone covariate, score-matching and covariate-matching coincide — the divergence the King–Nielsen paradox exploits appears only with multiple covariates.

Exercise 2

State the Rosenbaum–Rubin balancing property in words and explain why it implies that you can balance a 20-dimensional covariate vector by matching on a single number. Then explain why this does not mean you can ignore unmeasured confounders.

Show answer

Balancing property. Among units that share the same propensity score e(X), the distribution of the covariates X is identical for the treated and the untreated: D \perp\!\!\!\perp X \mid e(X). Within a slice of constant score, treatment is unrelated to the covariates — as if randomized with respect to X.

Why one number suffices. The covariate vector X affects treatment assignment only through the scalar e(X) = P(D=1 \mid X); that scalar is a complete summary, with respect to treatment, of everything in X. So slicing the data by e(X) creates strata that are internally balanced on all of X at once. We reduce a 20-dimensional matching problem to a 1-dimensional one without losing the bias-removing power of the full adjustment (Rosenbaum–Rubin Part 2).

Why unmeasured confounders still bite. The theorem assumes conditional independence holds given the covariates that enter X. The propensity score is built only from measured variables, so e(X) contains no information about anything you did not measure. If an unrecorded confounder (motivation, diet, an undiagnosed comorbidity) drives both enrollment and HbA1c, it is missing from X, missing from e(X), and missing from your adjustment. Perfect balance on measured covariates says nothing about balance on unmeasured ones. The score reduces dimension; it cannot substitute for measuring the right confounders.

Exercise 3

Using the interactive, set Selection strength to a large value (near 3) and watch Panel B. (a) Describe what happens to the overlap of the two propensity-score histograms. (b) Explain, referencing the overlap assumption, why no amount of clever matching can rescue the estimate in this regime. (c) Why is propensity-score matching worse than useless here, rather than merely imprecise?

Show answer

(a) As selection strength grows, the red (enrolled) histogram piles up near \hat e = 1 and the slate (standard-care) histogram near \hat e = 0; the region where both arms have mass — the common support — shrinks toward nothing. Almost every enrolled patient has a score for which there is no control patient at a comparable score, and vice versa.

(b) The overlap (common-support) assumption requires 0 < e(X) < 1 for the relevant patients, i.e. that comparable units of the other arm actually exist at each score. When the histograms barely overlap, that assumption is violated empirically: for most treated patients there is simply no control with a similar score to compare against. Matching, stratification, and weighting are all ways of reusing comparable controls; if there are none, there is nothing to reuse. The ATT for the no-overlap patients is not identified.

(c) There are two failure modes, and both are worse than mere imprecision. Without a caliper, matching never refuses to run: it returns whatever the nearest control is, even one with a wildly different score, so bad matches are silently treated as good ones and the estimate carries large, hidden bias dressed up as a clean point estimate. With a caliper (as in the interactive), matching instead silently discards the treated units that have no comparable control — so it keeps balance, but the estimand quietly drifts from “the ATT for all enrolled patients” to “the ATT for the shrinking, unrepresentative subset that happened to overlap.” Either way a poor estimate looks clean, which is more dangerous than a wide confidence interval that announces the problem. The honest move is to report that the effect is unidentified outside the region of common support, and to say how much of the treated group survived matching.

Exercise 4

A colleague tunes their propensity model by maximizing its classification accuracy (AUC), achieving an impressive AUC of 0.98, and reports that the model “almost perfectly predicts who enrolls.” (a) Why is a high AUC a bad sign for a propensity-score analysis? (b) What should they optimize for instead? (c) Briefly describe the King–Nielsen propensity-score paradox and one practical safeguard against it.

Show answer

(a) An AUC near 1 means the score separates treated from control almost perfectly — \hat e is near 1 for the treated and near 0 for the controls. That is precisely the no-overlap situation: the two propensity distributions barely intersect, so there are few comparable units across arms. Excellent prediction of treatment is equivalent to poor overlap, which is fatal for identification. The goal of a propensity model is comparability, not classification.

(b) They should optimize for covariate balance: after matching/weighting on the score, the standardized mean differences of every covariate should fall inside roughly \pm 0.1, and higher moments / interactions should also be balanced. A “worse-predicting” model that yields good balance and ample overlap is far more useful than a near-perfect classifier.

(c) PSM paradox (King & Nielsen, 2019): pruning units to tighten propensity-score balance can increase imbalance on the covariates themselves (two units with the same score can differ on X), so past a point, matching harder on the score raises bias and model dependence rather than lowering them. Safeguard: check covariate balance directly (SMDs on each covariate, as in Panel C), not just score balance; and prefer methods that target the covariates — coarsened exact matching, Mahalanobis matching, or stable inverse-probability weighting — using the score chiefly to diagnose overlap and to weight.

Key takeaways

  • The propensity score e(X) = P(D=1 \mid X) is a balancing score: conditional on it, treatment is independent of the covariates, so matching/stratifying on one number balances the whole vector.
  • Rosenbaum–Rubin: if treatment is unconfounded given X, it is unconfounded given e(X) alone — collapsing high-dimensional adjustment to one dimension.
  • Estimate e(X) with a logit, then match, stratify (quintiles), or weight; tune the model for covariate balance and overlap, never for predictive accuracy.
  • The score reduces dimension; it cannot conjure comparability the data lack (overlap) or adjust for confounders you never measured (CIA).
  • The King–Nielsen paradox warns that matching harder on an estimated score can worsen covariate balance — always check the covariate SMDs directly.
