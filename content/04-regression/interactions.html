Interactions

Heterogeneous effects and how to model them

In this section, you will learn to

  • Build an interaction model y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x + \beta_2 g + \beta_3(x g) + \varepsilon and read each coefficient correctly, recognizing \beta_1 as a conditional slope (the effect at g = 0), not “the effect of x”
  • Interpret interactions across the three canonical cases: continuous-by-binary (two fanning lines), binary-by-binary (a 2\times2 difference-in-differences), and continuous-by-continuous (the marginal-effect line \beta_1 + \beta_3 g)
  • Center regressors before interacting so main effects refer to the average moderator value where you actually have data
  • Compute a marginal effect and its correct standard error using the full coefficient covariance, including the 2g\operatorname{Cov}(\hat\beta_1,\hat\beta_3) term that produces the bow-tie confidence band
  • Diagnose the most common interaction error: reporting a main effect as if it were an average effect when an interaction is present

When One Number Is Not Enough

A Swedish region is reviewing a long-standing policy question: does staffing a vårdcentral (primary care center) with more general practitioners actually improve the quality of chronic disease care? The natural way to phrase this is as a slope. For every additional läkare (GP) per 1000 listed patients, how many percentage points (a percentage point is an additive move in a share — 58% to 61% is +3 percentage points, not “+3 percent”) does the share of well-controlled diabetes patients — those with HbA1c below 52 mmol/mol — rise?

A straightforward regression returns a single number, say “+3 percentage points per extra GP per 1000 patients,” and the region is tempted to print it on a slide and move on. But a sharper analyst at Socialstyrelsen (the National Board of Health and Welfare) asks an uncomfortable question: does that slope hold everywhere? An extra GP in a wealthy suburb of Stockholm, where most patients are already well-controlled and self-managing, may do very little. The same extra GP in a deprived municipality, where patients face language barriers, lower health literacy, and more comorbidity, may do a great deal — or, if the deeper problem is social rather than clinical, perhaps even less.

If the answer is “it depends on the area,” then a single slope is a lie of omission. It averages together a large effect in deprived areas and a small effect in affluent ones, reporting a middling number that describes no actual vårdcentral. The effect of GP staffing on quality is heterogeneous — it varies with a third variable — and the tool for modeling heterogeneity inside a regression is the interaction term.

This is the regression-era return of an idea we met in Module 1. There, treatment effects \tau_i = Y_i(1) - Y_i(0) differed across patients, and the ATE, ATT, and ATU could all disagree precisely because effects were heterogeneous. An interaction is how we let a regression speak about that heterogeneity directly: instead of one effect, we estimate how the effect changes as a moderator changes.

The Interaction Model

Let y be a quality outcome (the share of well-controlled diabetes patients at a vårdcentral), let x be GP staffing (läkare per 1000 listed patients), and let g be a moderator — the variable along which the effect of x might change. We start with g binary: g = 1 if the vårdcentral sits in a deprived area (high on the Care Need Index), and g = 0 otherwise.

The interaction model adds one term — the product x \cdot g — to the additive model:

y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x + \beta_2 g + \beta_3 (x \cdot g) + \varepsilon

How to read this: “The outcome equals an intercept, plus a slope on staffing, plus a shift for deprived areas, plus a term that lets the staffing slope itself differ between deprived and affluent areas, plus noise.” That last term is the whole point. Without \beta_3 (x \cdot g), the model forces x to have the same slope in both groups; with it, the slope is allowed to bend.

The cleanest way to understand the coefficients is to substitute the two values of g and read off the resulting lines.

The two regression lines. Plug in g = 0 (affluent areas):

y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x + \varepsilon

so the intercept is \beta_0 and the slope is \beta_1.

Plug in g = 1 (deprived areas):

y = (\beta_0 + \beta_2) + (\beta_1 + \beta_3)\, x + \varepsilon

so the intercept is \beta_0 + \beta_2 and the slope is \beta_1 + \beta_3.

This gives the careful reading of each coefficient:

  • \beta_1 is the slope of x when g = 0 — the effect of an extra GP in affluent areas. It is not “the effect of x.”
  • \beta_3 is how much the slope changes per unit of g — the difference in slopes between deprived and affluent areas. It is the interaction effect.
  • \beta_2 is the gap between groups at x = 0 — the vertical distance between the two lines where staffing is zero. With binary g it shifts the whole deprived line up or down.
  • \beta_0 is the predicted outcome for an affluent vårdcentral with x = 0.

The figure below makes that four-coefficient reading geometric before you touch any slider: each \beta is one visible feature of the two lines. \beta_0 is where the affluent line starts; \beta_2 is the vertical gap between the lines at x = 0; \beta_1 is the slope of the affluent (lower) line; and the deprived (upper) line is steeper by exactly \beta_3, so its slope is \beta_1 + \beta_3.

A static map of the four coefficients onto the two lines. Slate = affluent (g=0); red = deprived (g=1); the ochre double-arrow is \beta_2, the between-group gap measured at x=0. Once an interaction is present, \beta_1 governs only the slate line; the red line is steeper by exactly \beta_3.

The single most common error in all of applied regression lives in that list: reading \beta_1 as “the effect of x.” Once an interaction is in the model, \beta_1 is a conditional slope — the effect of x at the specific value g = 0. If you want “the effect of x for a deprived vårdcentral,” you must add \beta_1 + \beta_3. We will return to this mistake, because it is everywhere.

Three Canonical Cases

Interactions come in three flavors depending on the types of x and g. The algebra above is identical in all three; only the interpretation of “per unit of g” changes.

Case 1: Continuous x by binary g — two lines, different slopes

This is the GP-staffing example. The model produces literally two lines on a shared scatter: one for affluent areas (slope \beta_1), one for deprived areas (slope \beta_1 + \beta_3). A non-zero \beta_3 makes the lines fan out — they are no longer parallel. This is the case the interactive below brings to life, and the one you will meet most often in policy work.

Case 2: Binary x by binary g — a 2 \times 2 table of means

Now both regressors are 0/1. Suppose x is enrollment in a structured diabetessamordning (diabetes coordination program), and g is whether the patient is young (under 60) or older. The regression y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x + \beta_2 g + \beta_3 (x g) + \varepsilon is then exactly a saturated model (one with enough parameters to fit every group mean exactly, leaving no functional-form assumption to test) of four cell means:

x = 0 (no program) x = 1 (program)
g = 0 (older) \beta_0 \beta_0 + \beta_1
g = 1 (younger) \beta_0 + \beta_2 \beta_0 + \beta_1 + \beta_2 + \beta_3

The interaction \beta_3 is the difference-in-differences of the cell means:

\beta_3 = \underbrace{\big[(\beta_0 + \beta_1 + \beta_2 + \beta_3) - (\beta_0 + \beta_2)\big]}_{\text{program effect for the young}} - \underbrace{\big[(\beta_0 + \beta_1) - \beta_0\big]}_{\text{program effect for the old}}

How to read this: “The interaction equals the program’s effect among the young minus the program’s effect among the old.” If \beta_3 \neq 0, the program helps one age group more than the other. This is not an accident of phrasing — it is the exact algebraic skeleton of the difference-in-differences estimator we build in Module 6, where the two “groups” become treated vs. control units and the two “values of x” become before vs. after. An interaction in a 2 \times 2 regression is a difference-in-differences.

The four cell means and the two within-group “gaps” they define are easier to see than to read off a table. In the figure, each pair of bars is one age group going from no program to program; the bracket above each pair is that group’s program effect. The interaction \beta_3 is simply how much taller the young bracket is than the old one — the difference of the two differences.

Case 3: Continuous x by continuous g — the marginal-effect formula

When g is also continuous (say, the area’s deprivation score rather than a deprived/affluent label), there are no longer two lines but a continuum of slopes. The effect of x is found by partial differentiation:

\frac{\partial\, E[y \mid x, g]}{\partial x} = \beta_1 + \beta_3\, g

How to read this: “The marginal effect of x on y is not a constant — it is a straight line in g.” At g = 0 the marginal effect is \beta_1; each one-unit increase in the moderator g adds \beta_3 to it. This single equation unifies all three cases: in Case 1, g takes only the values 0 and 1, so the marginal effect takes only the values \beta_1 and \beta_1 + \beta_3 — the two slopes. The interactive’s second view plots exactly this line.

Centering: Making Main Effects Mean Something

Here is a subtle but practical point that trips up nearly everyone. Recall \beta_1 is “the slope of x when g = 0” and \beta_2 is “the gap between groups at x = 0.” Those interpretations are only useful if g = 0 and x = 0 are meaningful, in-sample values.

For binary g, g = 0 is a real category (affluent), so \beta_1 is interpretable. But x = 0 means “a vårdcentral with zero GPs,” which is absurd — no real center has zero staffing, and the data carry no information there. So \beta_2, the between-group gap at zero staffing, is an extrapolation far outside the data. Worse, in a continuous-by-continuous model, \beta_1 (the slope of x at g = 0) may also refer to a deprivation score of exactly zero that no area attains.

Center your regressors before interacting them. Replace x with \tilde{x} = x - \bar{x} and (if continuous) g with \tilde{g} = g - \bar{g}, then fit

y = \gamma_0 + \gamma_1 \tilde{x} + \gamma_2 \tilde{g} + \gamma_3 (\tilde{x}\,\tilde{g}) + \varepsilon.

Centering does not change \gamma_3 — the interaction is invariant to shifts of the regressors. What it changes is the meaning of the main effects: \gamma_1 becomes the slope of x at the average moderator value, and \gamma_2 becomes the group gap at the average staffing level. Both are now evaluated at the center of the data, where you actually have evidence, instead of at an arbitrary — often impossible — zero.

Centering also tames a cosmetic-but-real nuisance: the raw x and the product x g are mechanically correlated, which inflates the standard errors and the variance inflation factors (a VIF measures how much a regressor’s collinearity with the others swells its standard error) of the main effects (though never of the interaction itself). Centering largely removes that correlation and makes the main-effect estimates more stable and more readable. It is close to a free lunch; the only cost is remembering that coefficients now refer to deviations from the mean.

The whole case for centering is geometric: \beta_2 and \beta_1 are defined at x = 0, and for real vårdcentraler x = 0 (zero GPs) sits far outside the data. The two panels below make that visible. On the left, raw staffing: the lines must be extended a long way left to reach x = 0, so the gap \beta_2 read there is an extrapolation into empty space. On the right, centered staffing (\tilde x = x - \bar x): the new zero is the mean, which sits inside the data cloud, so \gamma_2 is the gap measured where you actually have evidence.

p2_centering = {
  const W = Math.min(620, width), H = 250;
  const panelW = (W - 24) / 2;          // two side-by-side panels
  const m = { l: 40, r: 12, t: 24, b: 38 };
  const b0 = 60, b1 = 6, b2 = -14, b3 = 0;   // illustrative; parallel-ish lines
  const xbar = 0.85;
  const dataLo = 0.35, dataHi = 1.40;

  const mk = (offset, mode) => {
    const g = d3.create("svg:g").attr("transform", `translate(${offset},0)`);
    const xmin = mode === "raw" ? 0 : (dataLo - xbar) - 0.05;
    const xmax = mode === "raw" ? 1.55 : (dataHi - xbar) + 0.05;

How to Read the Interaction Visualizer

Step 1: Start with no interaction. Drag \beta_3 to 0. The two fitted lines become exactly parallel — both have slope 2.5. An extra GP buys the same +2.5 percentage points whether the vårdcentral is affluent or deprived. The vertical distance between the lines is governed entirely by \beta_2; the effect of staffing is identical across groups. This is the additive model, with no heterogeneity.

Step 2: Turn on the interaction. Drag \beta_3 up to +6. The red (deprived) line now tilts upward to a slope of 2.5 + 6 = 8.5, while the slate (affluent) line is unchanged at 2.5. The lines fan out: an extra GP does far more in deprived areas. This is exactly the policy story Socialstyrelsen suspected — staffing is a stronger lever where clinical need is concentrated. Notice that \beta_1 = 2.5 has not moved; it was, and remains, the slope for affluent areas only.

Step 3: Watch the gap separate from the slopes. Now slide \beta_2 from -8 up to +10. The whole red line slides vertically, but the two slopes never change. This visually proves that \beta_2 (the group gap) and \beta_3 (the slope difference) are separate parameters: one moves the line up and down, the other tilts it. Confusing the two — “deprived areas score lower, so staffing must matter less there” — is a classic error. Level and slope are independent.

Step 4: Switch to the marginal-effect view. Choose Marginal effect of staffing. Now the horizontal axis is the moderator g and the vertical axis is the marginal effect \partial y / \partial x = \beta_1 + \beta_3 g — a single straight line. Its height at g = 0 is \beta_1 (the dark dot, affluent); its height at g = 1 is \beta_1 + \beta_3 (deprived). The shaded band is the 95% confidence interval for the marginal effect. Note its bow-tie shape: it is narrowest near the middle and widest at the ends, because the variance \mathrm{Var}(\beta_1) + g^2\mathrm{Var}(\beta_3) + 2g\,\mathrm{Cov}(\beta_1,\beta_3) is a quadratic in g. Where the band crosses the dashed zero line, the effect of staffing is no longer statistically distinguishable from zero — the interaction lets you say for which areas the policy demonstrably works. To see this happen, drag \beta_3 to a strong negative value (e.g. -6): the marginal-effect line tilts down until its band actually crosses zero at high g, marking the deprivation level beyond which the policy stops demonstrably working.

The Mistake Everyone Makes

It is worth stating the central pitfall as a standalone warning, because it survives in published papers, policy briefs, and conference talks.

Do not interpret a main effect as an average effect when an interaction is present. In the model y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x + \beta_2 g + \beta_3 (x g) + \varepsilon, the coefficient \beta_1 is the effect of x at g = 0, not the average effect of x across the population. If you report “an extra GP raises quality by 2.5 points” while \beta_3 = 6, you have described affluent areas and silently ignored that the effect is 8.5 in deprived ones.

The mirror-image mistake is forgetting the interaction term when computing a marginal effect. The effect of x for a deprived vårdcentral is \beta_1 + \beta_3 = 8.5, not \beta_1 = 2.5. Analysts who pull a single coefficient off a regression table and call it “the effect” — without checking whether that variable also appears inside an interaction — routinely report numbers that are off by the entire \beta_3.

A useful discipline: whenever a variable appears in an interaction, never speak of “its effect” without naming the value of the moderator at which you are evaluating it. “The effect of staffing in affluent areas is 2.5; in deprived areas it is 8.5; at the average area it is…” — and if you centered, the centered main effect gives you that last number directly.

Math Details: The Marginal Effect and Its Standard Error

The marginal effect is a linear combination of coefficients

For the model E[y \mid x, g] = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x + \beta_2 g + \beta_3 x g, differentiate with respect to x:

\theta(g) \;\equiv\; \frac{\partial\, E[y \mid x, g]}{\partial x} \;=\; \beta_1 + \beta_3\, g .

This is a linear combination of the estimated coefficients with weights that depend on g: weight 1 on \hat\beta_1 and weight g on \hat\beta_3. We can write it as \theta(g) = \mathbf{a}(g)^\top \boldsymbol\beta with \mathbf{a}(g) = (0,\, 1,\, 0,\, g)^\top.

Variance of a linear combination

For any fixed weight vector \mathbf{a}, the variance of \mathbf{a}^\top \hat{\boldsymbol\beta} is \mathbf{a}^\top \mathbf{V} \mathbf{a}, where \mathbf{V} = \widehat{\mathrm{Var}}(\hat{\boldsymbol\beta}) is the estimated coefficient covariance matrix. Expanding for our two non-zero weights:

\mathrm{Var}\big(\hat\theta(g)\big) \;=\; \mathrm{Var}(\hat\beta_1) \;+\; g^2\, \mathrm{Var}(\hat\beta_3) \;+\; 2g\, \mathrm{Cov}(\hat\beta_1, \hat\beta_3).

How to read this: “The variance of the marginal effect equals the variance of the main slope, plus g-squared times the variance of the interaction, plus twice g times the covariance between them.” The third term is the one analysts forget: \hat\beta_1 and \hat\beta_3 are estimated from the same data and are almost always correlated, so you cannot get the marginal-effect SE by combining the two reported standard errors independently. You need the full covariance, which is why dedicated marginal-effects routines exist.

Why the confidence band has a bow-tie shape

The variance is a quadratic function of g: \mathrm{Var}(\hat\theta(g)) = A + Bg + Cg^2 with A = \mathrm{Var}(\hat\beta_1), B = 2\,\mathrm{Cov}(\hat\beta_1,\hat\beta_3), C = \mathrm{Var}(\hat\beta_3). Because C > 0, this parabola opens upward and is minimized at

g^\star = -\frac{B}{2C} = -\frac{\mathrm{Cov}(\hat\beta_1,\hat\beta_3)}{\mathrm{Var}(\hat\beta_3)} .

The marginal effect is estimated most precisely at g^\star and less precisely as g moves away in either direction — the bow-tie. When the regressors are centered, \mathrm{Cov}(\hat\beta_1,\hat\beta_3) \approx 0, so g^\star \approx 0 and the band is narrowest at the mean moderator value, which is usually exactly where you have the most data. Centering thus aligns “most precise” with “most representative.”

The bow-tie band is just the square root of an upward parabola. Plotting \mathrm{Var}(\hat\theta(g)) directly — with the same illustrative variances used by the interactive above — shows where the precision is best: at the bottom of the parabola, g^\star = -B/2C. The band’s waist sits exactly above that vertex.

The variance is a quadratic in g (it opens upward because C = \mathrm{Var}(\hat\beta_3) > 0). Its vertex g^\star = -B/2C — the ochre point — is the moderator value where the marginal effect is pinned down most tightly. Take the square root of this curve and reflect it about the marginal-effect line and you get the bow-tie band of the interactive above.

The pointwise confidence interval

A 95\% pointwise confidence interval for the marginal effect at moderator value g is

\hat\theta(g) \;\pm\; t_{0.975,\, n-k}\,\sqrt{\mathrm{Var}\big(\hat\theta(g)\big)} ,

where k is the number of estimated coefficients. The set of g for which this interval excludes zero is the region of moderator values where the effect of x is statistically significant — a far more honest summary than a single asterisk on \hat\beta_1.

Exercise 1

A region fits the model (quality in percentage points, staffing in GPs per 1000 patients):

\widehat{\text{quality}} = 55 + 3\,(\text{staffing}) - 6\,(\text{deprived}) + 5\,(\text{staffing} \times \text{deprived})

  1. What is the effect of one more GP per 1000 patients in an affluent area?
  2. What is the effect of one more GP per 1000 patients in a deprived area?
  3. Predict quality for an affluent vårdcentral with staffing = 1.0 and for a deprived one with staffing = 1.0.
  4. An analyst reports “staffing raises quality by 3 points.” What is wrong with that statement?
Show answer

(a) In an affluent area, \text{deprived} = 0, so the interaction term vanishes and the slope of staffing is \beta_1 = 3 percentage points per GP/1000.

(b) In a deprived area, \text{deprived} = 1, so the slope is \beta_1 + \beta_3 = 3 + 5 = 8 percentage points per GP/1000.

(c) Affluent (\text{deprived}=0, staffing =1.0): 55 + 3(1.0) - 6(0) + 5(1.0 \times 0) = 58. Deprived (\text{deprived}=1, staffing =1.0): 55 + 3(1.0) - 6(1) + 5(1.0 \times 1) = 55 + 3 - 6 + 5 = 57.

(d) The statement reads the main effect \beta_1 = 3 as if it were the effect of staffing for everyone. But \beta_1 = 3 is the slope only in affluent areas (\text{deprived}=0). In deprived areas the effect is 8. Because \beta_3 = 5 \neq 0, no single number describes the staffing effect; the analyst has reported the affluent-area effect and mislabeled it as universal.

Exercise 2

A diabetes coordination program is evaluated with a binary \times binary model: x = 1 if enrolled, g = 1 if the patient is younger than 60. The fitted cell means of the outcome (share reaching their HbA1c target, in percent) are:

Not enrolled (x=0) Enrolled (x=1)
Older (g=0) 40 52
Younger (g=1) 50 74
  1. Recover \beta_0, \beta_1, \beta_2, \beta_3 from the table.
  2. Interpret \beta_3. Does the program help younger or older patients more?
  3. Show that \beta_3 equals the difference-in-differences of the cell means.
Show answer

Using the saturated mapping (older / not-enrolled is the reference cell):

(a) - $_0 = $ older, not enrolled = 40. - $_1 = $ (older, enrolled) - \beta_0 = 52 - 40 = 12. - $_2 = $ (younger, not enrolled) - \beta_0 = 50 - 40 = 10. - $_3 = $ (younger, enrolled) - \beta_0 - \beta_1 - \beta_2 = 74 - 40 - 12 - 10 = 12.

(b) \beta_3 = 12 > 0. The program’s effect is larger for younger patients. Concretely, the program raises the target-reaching share by \beta_1 = 12 points among the old and by \beta_1 + \beta_3 = 24 points among the young — twice as much.

(c) Difference-in-differences (program effect within each age group, then differenced):

\underbrace{(74 - 50)}_{\text{program effect, young} \,=\, 24} - \underbrace{(52 - 40)}_{\text{program effect, old} \,=\, 12} = 24 - 12 = 12 = \beta_3. \;\checkmark

Equivalently, differencing across age within each enrollment status: (74 - 52) - (50 - 40) = 22 - 10 = 12. The diff-in-diff is symmetric — it does not matter which variable you difference first.

Exercise 3

In a continuous-by-continuous model, g is a deprivation score and the fit is \hat y = \beta_1 x + \beta_3 (x g) + \cdots with \hat\beta_1 = 2.0, \hat\beta_3 = 4.0, and estimated variances/covariance \mathrm{Var}(\hat\beta_1) = 0.25, \mathrm{Var}(\hat\beta_3) = 1.00, \mathrm{Cov}(\hat\beta_1, \hat\beta_3) = -0.20.

  1. Compute the marginal effect of x at g = 1.5.
  2. Compute the standard error of that marginal effect.
  3. Is the marginal effect at g = 1.5 significantly different from zero at the 5% level (use a critical value of 1.96)?
Show answer

(a) Marginal effect:

\hat\theta(1.5) = \hat\beta_1 + \hat\beta_3 (1.5) = 2.0 + 4.0(1.5) = 2.0 + 6.0 = 8.0 .

(b) Using \mathrm{Var}(\hat\theta(g)) = \mathrm{Var}(\hat\beta_1) + g^2\mathrm{Var}(\hat\beta_3) + 2g\,\mathrm{Cov}(\hat\beta_1,\hat\beta_3) at g = 1.5:

\mathrm{Var} = 0.25 + (1.5)^2(1.00) + 2(1.5)(-0.20) = 0.25 + 2.25 - 0.60 = 1.90 .

\mathrm{SE} = \sqrt{1.90} \approx 1.378 .

(c) The test statistic is z = \hat\theta / \mathrm{SE} = 8.0 / 1.378 \approx 5.80, far above 1.96. The 95% CI is 8.0 \pm 1.96(1.378) = [5.30, 10.70], which excludes zero. So yes, the marginal effect at g = 1.5 is significantly different from zero. Note that ignoring the covariance term would have given \mathrm{Var} = 0.25 + 2.25 = 2.50 and \mathrm{SE} \approx 1.58 — an overstatement here, because the negative covariance actually reduces the variance. This is exactly why you must use the full covariance matrix rather than combining standard errors independently.

Exercise 4

Using the interactive visualizer, set \beta_3 = 0 and any value of \beta_2, then switch to the Marginal effect of staffing view. Describe the shape of the marginal-effect line and explain it. Then set \beta_3 = 10 and explain what changes and why the confidence band still bows outward.

Show answer

With \beta_3 = 0: the marginal-effect line is flat (horizontal) at height \beta_1 = 2.5. Since \partial y/\partial x = \beta_1 + \beta_3 g = 2.5 + 0\cdot g = 2.5 for every g, the effect of staffing does not depend on the moderator at all — this is the no-heterogeneity case. The two regression lines in the other view are parallel, consistent with a constant marginal effect. Changing \beta_2 does nothing to this view, because \beta_2 governs the group level, not the staffing slope.

With \beta_3 = 10: the marginal-effect line now slopes steeply upward, from \beta_1 = 2.5 at g = 0 to \beta_1 + \beta_3 = 12.5 at g = 1. The effect of an extra GP grows as we move toward deprived areas.

Why the band still bows: the band’s half-width is 1.96\sqrt{\mathrm{Var}(\hat\beta_1) + g^2\mathrm{Var}(\hat\beta_3) + 2g\,\mathrm{Cov}(\hat\beta_1,\hat\beta_3)}, a quadratic in g that does not depend on the value of \hat\beta_3 — only on its variance. So regardless of whether \beta_3 = 0 or \beta_3 = 10, the band is narrowest near the variance-minimizing moderator value and widens toward the extremes. The interaction strength tilts the line; the precision of the estimates shapes the band, and the two are governed by different quantities.

Key takeaways

  • An interaction term \beta_3(x g) lets the slope on x depend on a moderator; without it, the model forces a single effect across all groups and silently averages away heterogeneity.
  • Once an interaction is in the model, \beta_1 is the slope of x only at g = 0; the effect in the other group is \beta_1 + \beta_3, and \beta_2 (the group gap) is a separate parameter from \beta_3 (the slope difference) — level and slope move independently.
  • A binary-by-binary interaction is exactly a difference-in-differences of four cell means — the algebraic skeleton of the DiD estimator built later.
  • Centering regressors leaves \beta_3 unchanged but makes the main effects refer to the average moderator and average x, where the data actually live, and stabilizes the estimates.
  • The marginal-effect standard error needs the full covariance — \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_1) + g^2\operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_3) + 2g\operatorname{Cov}(\hat\beta_1,\hat\beta_3) — which you cannot reconstruct from two reported standard errors, and which gives the band its bow-tie shape.
