Omitted Variable Bias

The bias formula and its implications for research design

In this section, you will learn to

  • Distinguish the true (long) model from the short model and explain why omitting a relevant confounder pushes it into the error term, violating OLS’s key assumption
  • Derive the omitted-variable-bias formula \operatorname{plim}\hat\alpha_1 = \beta_1 + \beta_2\delta and read the bias as a product of two interpretable pieces
  • Predict the direction of bias from the signs of \beta_2 and \delta alone, and classify the result as attenuation, amplification, or wrong-sign bias
  • Diagnose why “control for everything” is wrong advice, since variables with \beta_2 = 0 or \delta = 0 add no bias and some controls (colliders, mediators) create it
  • Recognize OVB as the algebraic twin of confounding (an open backdoor path) and of selection bias

A Tempting Regression

A Swedish region wants to know whether staffing matters for diabetes care. Across its 160 vårdcentraler (primary care centers), it has two numbers for each center: the staffing intensity — distriktssköterskor (district nurses) per 1,000 listed patients — and the share of enrolled Type 2 diabetes patients who reached the HbA1c target of below 53 mmol/mol last year. The analyst runs the obvious regression: target-attainment on nurse staffing. The slope comes back positive and large. More nurses, better control. The slide deck practically writes itself.

But there is a problem hiding in plain sight. Vårdcentraler are not randomly assigned their staffing levels. Centers in affluent, well-resourced catchment areas tend to attract and retain more staff and serve patients who are easier to bring to target — patients with more education, stable housing, fewer competing health problems, and better health literacy. Centers in deprived areas struggle on both fronts. The catchment area’s socioeconomic deprivation — captured in Sweden by indices like the Care Need Index (CNI) — affects both the regressor (staffing) and the outcome (target attainment), but it never appears in the regression.

When a variable like this is left out, the slope on the variable that is included absorbs part of the omitted variable’s influence. The estimate is biased. This page derives exactly how much, in which direction, and what it implies for how you should design and read regressions. It is the single most important failure mode in all of applied regression — and, as we will see, it is just confounding wearing a regression’s clothes.

The Setup: A True Model and a Short One

Suppose the world is genuinely described by a model with two regressors:

y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i + \beta_2 w_i + \varepsilon_i, \qquad E[\varepsilon_i \mid x_i, w_i] = 0

Here y_i is the outcome (diabetes target attainment at center i), x_i is the regressor we care about (nurse staffing), and w_i is a second variable (area deprivation). The error \varepsilon_i is mean-independent of both regressors — this is what makes it the true or long model: if we could run it, OLS would consistently recover \beta_1, the causal effect of staffing holding deprivation fixed.

The trouble is that the analyst omits w_i — perhaps because the CNI data were not linked, or because nobody thought of it — and instead estimates the short model:

y_i = \alpha_0 + \alpha_1 x_i + u_i

The short regression has its own slope \alpha_1 and its own error u_i = \beta_2 w_i + \varepsilon_i. Notice what has happened: the omitted term \beta_2 w_i has been swept into the short-model error. Read this as: “the leftover error in the short regression contains everything the true model attributed to deprivation, plus the genuine noise.” If deprivation is correlated with staffing — and it is — then this new error is correlated with the regressor, OLS’s key assumption fails, and the slope is no longer estimating \beta_1.

Notation at a glance. This page juggles several Greek letters across two models; here is the whole cast in one place.

Symbol Lives in Plain meaning
\beta_1 true (long) model the effect we want: x on y, holding w fixed
\beta_2 true (long) model effect of the omitted variable w on the outcome y
\varepsilon_i true (long) model genuine noise (well-behaved: mean-independent of x,w)
\alpha_1 short model the slope you actually estimate when w is omitted
u_i short model the short-model error =\beta_2 w_i + \varepsilon_i (contaminated)
\delta auxiliary regression slope of the omitted w on the included x: \operatorname{Cov}(x,w)/\operatorname{Var}(x)
\beta_2\delta the result the bias: \operatorname{plim}\hat\alpha_1-\beta_1

Omitted Variable Bias (OVB) is the difference between what the short regression’s slope converges to and the true coefficient on the included regressor:

\text{Bias}(\hat\alpha_1) = \operatorname{plim} \hat\alpha_1 - \beta_1.

How to read this: “the bias is the gap between the number the short-regression slope settles down to as the sample grows (\operatorname{plim}\hat\alpha_1) and the true effect \beta_1 we wanted.” The notation \operatorname{plim} is the probability limit — the value an estimator converges to in large samples; read it loosely as the estimator’s long-run, settled-down value. (We use \operatorname{plim} rather than the plain average E[\hat\alpha_1] because the slope is a ratio of sample quantities, which only behaves cleanly once the sample is large.) OVB is an asymptotic statement — “asymptotic” just means “in the limit as the sample grows” — so it does not vanish by collecting more data. It is a flaw in what you are estimating, not in how precisely you estimate it.

The OVB Formula

The central result of this page is a single, compact equation. Let \delta be the slope coefficient you would get by regressing the omitted variable w on the included variable x — an “auxiliary” regression:

w_i = \gamma_0 + \delta\, x_i + r_i.

Then the probability limit of the short-regression slope is:

\boxed{\;\operatorname{plim} \hat\alpha_1 \;=\; \beta_1 \;+\; \beta_2 \cdot \delta\;}

and therefore the bias is exactly \beta_2 \cdot \delta.

Read this aloud: “the slope you get from the short regression equals the true effect of x, plus the effect of the omitted variable on the outcome (\beta_2) times the slope of the omitted variable on the included one (\delta).” The short slope is contaminated by the part of w’s influence that travels through x.

There is a clean algebraic identity for \delta in terms of moments:

\delta = \frac{\operatorname{Cov}(x, w)}{\operatorname{Var}(x)}.

Read this as: “\delta is the covariance between the included and omitted variables, scaled by the variance of the included variable” — precisely the formula for a simple-regression slope of w on x.

The mnemonic. Memorize the bias as a product of two pieces:

\text{Bias} \;=\; \underbrace{\beta_2}_{\substack{\text{effect of omitted}\\ \text{on the outcome}}} \;\times\; \underbrace{\delta}_{\substack{\text{relationship of omitted}\\ \text{with the included}}}

“Bias equals (effect of the omitted variable on the outcome) times (the relationship of the omitted variable with the included regressor).” Both pieces must be nonzero for bias to exist. If the omitted variable has no effect on y (\beta_2 = 0), or is uncorrelated with x (\delta = 0), then leaving it out costs you nothing — you may lose precision, but the slope on x is still unbiased. This is why “control for everything” is wrong advice: a variable that is unrelated to x, or unrelated to y, does not bias \hat\alpha_1 whether you include it or not.

The Sign of the Bias: A 2×2 Table

Because the bias is a product of two terms, its sign is determined by the signs of \beta_2 and \delta via ordinary multiplication of signs. There are four cases:

Sign of \beta_2
(omitted → outcome)		 Sign of \delta
(omitted ↔︎ included)		 Sign of Bias (\beta_2\delta) Effect on \hat\alpha_1
+ + + Biased upward (too positive)
- - + Biased upward (too positive)
+ - - Biased downward (too negative)
- + - Biased downward (too negative)

Read this table as: “when the two terms share a sign, the bias is positive and the short slope overshoots the truth; when they have opposite signs, the bias is negative and the short slope undershoots.” This is just the rule “same signs multiply to plus, opposite signs to minus.” You do not need data to predict the direction of bias — you only need to reason about the signs of two relationships, both of which are usually settled by domain knowledge.

“Upward” and “downward” describe movement along the number line, not magnitude. Upward bias on a negative true effect can push the estimate toward zero (attenuation) or even past zero into positive territory (a wrong-sign estimate). Always translate “the bias is positive” into “the estimate is more positive than the truth,” then ask separately what that does to the magnitude and sign. We unpack this next.

Attenuation, Amplification, and Wrong-Sign Bias

Knowing the sign of the bias is not the same as knowing what it does to your conclusion. Three qualitatively different things can happen, depending on whether the bias pushes the estimate toward zero, away from zero, or clean across it.

  • Attenuation (bias toward zero). The bias has the opposite sign to \beta_1, shrinking the estimate’s magnitude. A real effect looks weaker than it is; you may wrongly conclude “no effect.”
  • Amplification (bias away from zero). The bias has the same sign as \beta_1, inflating the magnitude. A modest effect looks impressive; you overstate the policy’s value.
  • Wrong-sign bias. The bias is larger in magnitude than \beta_1 and points the other way, so \operatorname{plim}\hat\alpha_1 ends up on the opposite side of zero. The estimate says “helps” when the truth is “harms,” or vice versa. This is the most dangerous case, and it is exactly what we saw in the naive-comparison example back in Module 1.

“Upward” (more positive) and “downward” (more negative) describe direction on the line, not magnitude. The same upward bias amplifies a positive truth, but on a negative truth it would instead attenuate or flip it. Always read the bias as a move along this line first, then ask what it does to size and sign.

See It: The OVB Sandbox

Below is a simulated regional dataset. The true model is fixed: a genuine, modest positive effect of nurse staffing on target attainment, \beta_1 = 1.5 (one extra nurse per 1,000 patients raises the target-attainment rate by 1.5 percentage points — i.e. from, say, 55% to 56.5%, an additive move on the rate itself, not a 1.5% relative increase — holding deprivation fixed). The plot shows the simulated centers, the short regression line (staffing only) and the long regression line (staffing controlling for deprivation), and the readouts report the true \beta_1, the biased short slope, and the bias.

ovb_beta0 = 55      // baseline target-attainment rate (%)
ovb_beta1 = 1.5     // TRUE effect of staffing (held fixed)
ovb_nCenters = 160
ovb_xMean = 6       // avg nurses per 1,000 patients
ovb_xSD = 1.6
ovb_wSD = 2.2
// Simulate the centers. w is generated as δ·(x - x̄) plus its own residual,
// so the auxiliary regression of w on x has population slope ≈ δ.
ovb_data = {
  ovb_reseed; // reactive dependency: clicking "Resample" redraws
  const rnorm = d3.randomNormal.source(d3.randomLcg(Math.random()));
  const xN = rnorm(0, ovb_xSD);
  const wN = rnorm(0, ovb_wSD);
  const eN = rnorm(0, ovb_noise);
  const rows = [];
  for (let i = 0; i < ovb_nCenters; i++) {
    const x = ovb_xMean + xN();                       // staffing
    const w = ovb_delta * (x - ovb_xMean) + wN();     // deprivation, trends with x
    const y = ovb_beta0 + ovb_beta1 * x + ovb_beta2 * w + eN();
    rows.push({ x, w, y });
  }
  return rows;
}
// Simple-regression slope + intercept of ykey on xkey
ovb_slope = function (rows, xkey, ykey) {
  const xb = d3.mean(rows, d => d[xkey]);
  const yb = d3.mean(rows, d => d[ykey]);
  let sxy = 0, sxx = 0;
  for (const r of rows) {
    sxy += (r[xkey] - xb) * (r[ykey] - yb);
    sxx += (r[xkey] - xb) * (r[xkey] - xb);
  }
  const slope = sxy / sxx;
  return { slope, intercept: yb - slope * xb };
}
// Long-model slope on x via Frisch–Waugh–Lovell: partial w out of x and y.
ovb_longSlopeX = function (rows) {
  const wb = d3.mean(rows, d => d.w);
  const xb = d3.mean(rows, d => d.x);
  const yb = d3.mean(rows, d => d.y);
  let swx = 0, sww = 0, swy = 0;
  for (const r of rows) {
    const wc = r.w - wb;
    swx += wc * (r.x - xb);
    swy += wc * (r.y - yb);
    sww += wc * wc;
  }
  const dxw = swx / sww;   // slope of x on w
  const dyw = swy / sww;   // slope of y on w
  let num = 0, den = 0;
  for (const r of rows) {
    const xres = (r.x - xb) - dxw * (r.w - wb);
    const yres = (r.y - yb) - dyw * (r.w - wb);
    num += xres * yres;
    den += xres * xres;
  }
  return num / den;
}
ovb_fit = {
  const short = ovb_slope(ovb_data, "x", "y");   // y ~ x only
  const aux   = ovb_slope(ovb_data, "x", "w");   // w ~ x  (estimates δ)
  const longB = ovb_longSlopeX(ovb_data);        // y ~ x + w  (recovers β₁)
  return {
    shortSlope: short.slope,
    shortInt: short.intercept,
    longSlope: longB,
    auxDelta: aux.slope,
    bias: short.slope - longB,           // empirical bias
    predBias: ovb_beta2 * ovb_delta      // formula prediction β₂·δ
  };
}

How to Read the OVB Sandbox

Step 1: Establish the truth. The slate dashed line is the long regression — staffing controlling for deprivation. Its slope hovers near the true \beta_1 = 1.5 regardless of how you set the sliders, because the long model includes w (it will wobble more when you raise the noise slider, since it is estimated, not assumed). This is the line you wish you could draw. The point color encodes deprivation: slate points are low-deprivation centers, red points high-deprivation (more deprived = redder = harder to treat).

Step 2: Watch the biased line. The solid red line is the short regression — staffing only, deprivation ignored. Read off the “Short slope” and “True β₁” cards and compare. The gap between them is the bias.

Step 3: Confirm the formula. Compare the “Bias (empirical)” card (the actual short-minus-long gap in this sample) with the “Formula β₂·δ” card (the product of your two sliders). They track each other closely — the formula is not an approximation, it is the population truth, and the finite sample matches it up to sampling noise. Click Resample centers a few times to watch the empirical bias jitter around the formula’s prediction.

Step 4: Manufacture amplification, then attenuation. Set \beta_2 = -2.5 (deprivation hurts outcomes) and \delta = -0.5 (more-deprived areas have slightly fewer nurses). The bias \beta_2\delta is positive and small; since the truth is positive, the short slope is inflated — amplification. Now flip to \delta = +0.5: the bias turns negative and shrinks the slope toward zero — attenuation.

Step 5: Flip the sign. Set \beta_2 = -3 and \delta = +1.5. Now the bias is strongly negative (-4.5), far larger in magnitude than the true +1.5. The short slope goes negative and the “Wrong sign!” banner appears: the regression now claims that more nurses are associated with worse diabetes control. Nothing about the data-generating effect of nurses changed — you have simply let a confounder masquerade as the signal. This is the OVB nightmare, and it is the everyday reality of naive cross-sectional regressions.

Step 6: Kill the bias. Set \delta = 0 (deprivation uncorrelated with staffing). The bias collapses to zero for any value of \beta_2, and the two lines coincide. Then set \delta back and instead set \beta_2 = 0: again the bias vanishes. Both factors must be present for OVB to exist.

Reading the Swedish Example Through the Formula

Return to the regional analyst. The included regressor is nurse staffing x; the omitted variable is area deprivation w (think of w as a CNI-style index, higher = more deprived). To sign the bias, we reason about the two pieces.

  • \beta_2 (deprivation → outcome). Holding staffing fixed, more deprived catchment areas have lower diabetes target attainment: poorer health literacy, more comorbidity, less continuity of care. So \beta_2 < 0.
  • \delta (deprivation ↔︎ staffing). Do more-deprived areas have more or fewer nurses per patient? In Sweden, capitation formulas (and CNI itself) are explicitly designed to channel more resources to high-need areas — so one might expect \delta > 0. But recruitment and retention are chronically harder in deprived and rural catchments, which pushes effective staffing down, suggesting \delta < 0. The sign is genuinely contested and depends on the region.

This is exactly why OVB is a design problem, not a computational one. Consider both scenarios:

Scenario A — compensatory funding wins (\delta > 0). Then \text{Bias} = \beta_2 \cdot \delta = (-)\cdot(+) < 0. The short regression is biased downward: it understates the benefit of nurses, and could even show a negative slope (more nurses, worse outcomes) purely because the nurse-rich centers are also the deprivation-rich, hard-to-treat centers. A naive read would wrongly conclude “staffing does not help.”

Scenario B — recruitment difficulty wins (\delta < 0). Then \text{Bias} = (-)\cdot(-) > 0. The short regression is biased upward: it overstates the benefit of nurses, because the nurse-rich centers are also the affluent, easy-to-treat centers. A naive read would over-credit staffing for an outcome that deprivation was driving.

In both cases the fix is the same: measure deprivation (link the CNI index) and put it in the regression. What changes is the wrong story you would have told had you not.

OVB Is Confounding

If the bias mnemonic feels familiar, it should. On the DAG Basics page we drew a common cause W pointing into both the treatment X and the outcome Y:

X \leftarrow W \rightarrow Y.

A common cause opens a backdoor path X \leftarrow W \rightarrow Y that carries non-causal association from X to Y. Omitting W from the regression leaves that backdoor open, and the spurious association leaks into the slope on X. The OVB formula is the algebraic counterpart of the graphical backdoor criterion:

  for (const e of edges) {

    if (!s || !t) continue;

The backdoor path X \leftarrow W \rightarrow Y is drawn in red: its two edges carry \delta (deprivation → staffing) and \beta_2 (deprivation → attainment). Their product \beta_2\delta is exactly the bias the open path injects into the slope on X. The green edge X \rightarrow Y is the causal effect \beta_1 you want. Controlling for W blocks the red path and leaves the green one alone.

  • \delta \neq 0 corresponds to the edge W \rightarrow X (deprivation influences staffing).
  • \beta_2 \neq 0 corresponds to the edge W \rightarrow Y (deprivation influences outcomes).
  • Their product \beta_2\delta is the bias the open backdoor injects.
  • “Control for W” (the backdoor adjustment) is the same act as “include w in the regression.”

It is also the same phenomenon as the selection bias decomposition from Module 1. There, the naive treatment–control difference equaled the ATT plus a bias term E[Y(0)\mid D=1] - E[Y(0)\mid D=0] — the two groups differed at baseline. With a binary regressor x = D, that baseline difference is \beta_2\delta: a confounder that makes the treated group systematically different in Y(0) is just a variable omitted from the regression. Selection bias, confounding, and omitted variable bias are three dialects of one idea: an open path between regressor and outcome that does not run through the causal arrow you care about.

Math Details: Deriving the OVB Formula via FWL and Projection

Derivation 1: Direct, from the normal equations

The short-regression slope is the simple-regression formula applied to y on x:

\hat\alpha_1 = \frac{\widehat{\operatorname{Cov}}(x, y)}{\widehat{\operatorname{Var}}(x)}.

Substitute the true model y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i + \beta_2 w_i + \varepsilon_i into the covariance in the numerator. Covariance is linear and ignores additive constants:

\operatorname{Cov}(x, y) = \beta_1 \operatorname{Cov}(x, x) + \beta_2 \operatorname{Cov}(x, w) + \operatorname{Cov}(x, \varepsilon).

Because the long model satisfies E[\varepsilon \mid x, w] = 0, we have \operatorname{Cov}(x, \varepsilon) = 0. Also \operatorname{Cov}(x,x) = \operatorname{Var}(x). Divide through by \operatorname{Var}(x):

\frac{\operatorname{Cov}(x,y)}{\operatorname{Var}(x)} = \beta_1 + \beta_2 \cdot \frac{\operatorname{Cov}(x,w)}{\operatorname{Var}(x)} = \beta_1 + \beta_2 \delta.

Taking probability limits (sample moments converge to population moments by the law of large numbers):

\operatorname{plim}\hat\alpha_1 = \beta_1 + \beta_2 \delta. \qquad \blacksquare

Derivation 2: Via Frisch–Waugh–Lovell

The FWL theorem says the long-model coefficient \beta_1 equals the slope from regressing the part of y orthogonal to w on the part of x orthogonal to w. Write \tilde{x} and \tilde{y} for those residuals, so that \beta_1 = \operatorname{Cov}(\tilde y, \tilde x)/\operatorname{Var}(\tilde x).

Decompose x into its projection onto w plus a residual: x = \kappa_0 + \delta_{xw}\, w + \tilde{x}, where \delta_{xw} = \operatorname{Cov}(x,w)/\operatorname{Var}(w) is the slope of x on w and \tilde{x} is orthogonal to w. (Note \delta_{xw} regresses x on w and so divides by \operatorname{Var}(w); the auxiliary slope \delta from earlier regresses w on x and divides by \operatorname{Var}(x) — do not conflate them.) The short slope regresses y on the whole of x — residual and projection together — rather than on \tilde{x} alone. The piece of x that lives along w drags the omitted effect \beta_2 into the slope.

Concretely, from the true model \operatorname{Cov}(w,y) = \beta_2\operatorname{Var}(w) + \beta_1\operatorname{Cov}(x,w). Substituting this back while expanding \operatorname{Cov}(x,y)/\operatorname{Var}(x) collapses to the same destination:

\operatorname{plim}\hat\alpha_1 = \beta_1 + \beta_2\,\frac{\operatorname{Cov}(x,w)}{\operatorname{Var}(x)} = \beta_1 + \beta_2\delta.

The FWL reading of OVB: the short regression fails to partial w out of x. The bias \beta_2\delta is precisely the influence of w that FWL would have scrubbed away. Controlling for a variable and “netting it out by FWL” are the same operation; omitting it leaves \beta_2\delta stuck to your slope.

The multivariable case

With several included regressors \mathbf{X} and an omitted set \mathbf{W}, the result generalizes to matrix form:

\operatorname{plim}\hat{\boldsymbol\alpha} = \boldsymbol\beta_X + \mathbf{\Delta}\,\boldsymbol\beta_W,

where \mathbf{\Delta} = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{W} stacks the auxiliary regressions of each omitted variable on the full set of included ones. Read this as: “each included coefficient is biased by the omitted effects, routed through how the omitted variables project onto the included ones.” A subtle consequence: omitting one variable can bias the coefficient on a different, perfectly measured regressor, because \mathbf{\Delta} mixes them. There is no such thing as a locally contained omission.

Code: Monte Carlo Confirmation

The code below fixes the true model, simulates many regional datasets, and confirms that the average short-regression slope lands on \beta_1 + \beta_2\delta while the long regression recovers \beta_1. We use \beta_1 = 1.5, \beta_2 = -2.5, and generate deprivation as w = \delta\,(x - \bar{x}) + \text{noise} with \delta = 1.4, so the predicted bias is \beta_2\delta = -2.5 \times 1.4 = -3.5 and the expected short slope is 1.5 - 3.5 = -2.0 — a wrong-sign estimate.

  • Python
library(fixest)  # fast OLS; base lm() works identically

set.seed(2024)

# --- True parameters (the world) ---
beta0 <- 55; beta1 <- 1.5; beta2 <- -2.5
delta <- 1.4
n      <- 160
x_mean <- 6; x_sd <- 1.6; w_sd <- 2.2; e_sd <- 4

sim_once <- function() {
  x <- rnorm(n, x_mean, x_sd)                       # nurse staffing
  w <- delta * (x - x_mean) + rnorm(n, 0, w_sd)     # deprivation, correlated with x
  y <- beta0 + beta1 * x + beta2 * w + rnorm(n, 0, e_sd)
  short <- coef(feols(y ~ x))["x"]                  # omit w
  long  <- coef(feols(y ~ x + w))["x"]             # include w
  aux   <- coef(feols(w ~ x))["x"]                 # auxiliary slope (estimates delta)
  c(short = unname(short), long = unname(long), aux = unname(aux))
}

R   <- 5000
out <- replicate(R, sim_once())

cat("Predicted bias  beta2 * delta  :", beta2 * delta, "\n")
cat("Predicted short slope          :", beta1 + beta2 * delta, "\n\n")
cat("Mean short slope (E[alpha1])   :", round(mean(out["short", ]), 3), "\n")
cat("Mean long slope  (recovers b1) :", round(mean(out["long",  ]), 3), "\n")
cat("Mean auxiliary   (recovers d)  :", round(mean(out["aux",   ]), 3), "\n")
cat("Mean empirical bias            :", round(mean(out["short", ] - out["long", ]), 3), "\n")

import numpy as np
import statsmodels.api as sm

rng = np.random.default_rng(2024)

# --- True parameters (the world) ---
beta0, beta1, beta2 = 55.0, 1.5, -2.5
delta = 1.4
n = 160
x_mean, x_sd, w_sd, e_sd = 6.0, 1.6, 2.2, 4.0

def ols_slope_on_first(y, *cols):
    X = sm.add_constant(np.column_stack(cols))
    return sm.OLS(y, X).fit().params[1]   # slope on the first predictor

def sim_once():
    x = rng.normal(x_mean, x_sd, n)                       # nurse staffing
    w = delta * (x - x_mean) + rng.normal(0, w_sd, n)     # deprivation, correlated
    y = beta0 + beta1 * x + beta2 * w + rng.normal(0, e_sd, n)
    short = ols_slope_on_first(y, x)         # omit w
    long_ = ols_slope_on_first(y, x, w)      # include w -> slope on x
    aux   = ols_slope_on_first(w, x)         # auxiliary slope (estimates delta)
    return short, long_, aux

R = 5000
res = np.array([sim_once() for _ in range(R)])
short, long_, aux = res[:, 0], res[:, 1], res[:, 2]

print(f"Predicted bias  beta2 * delta  : {beta2 * delta}")
print(f"Predicted short slope          : {beta1 + beta2 * delta}\n")
print(f"Mean short slope (E[alpha1])   : {short.mean():.3f}")
print(f"Mean long slope  (recovers b1) : {long_.mean():.3f}")
print(f"Mean auxiliary   (recovers d)  : {aux.mean():.3f}")
print(f"Mean empirical bias            : {(short - long_).mean():.3f}")

Exercise 1

A health economist regresses annual cost per patient (y, in SEK) on the number of läkare (physicians) per 1,000 listed patients (x) across vårdcentraler, omitting the average age of the listed population (w). Suppose the true model has \beta_2 = +900 SEK per year of average age (older populations cost more), and centers serving older populations also tend to staff more physicians, so \delta = +0.4.

  1. What is the bias on the physician coefficient? (b) If the true effect of physicians on cost is \beta_1 = +1{,}200 SEK, what does the short regression report on average? (c) Is this attenuation, amplification, or wrong-sign?
Show answer

(a) \text{Bias} = \beta_2 \cdot \delta = 900 \times 0.4 = +360 SEK. The bias is positive.

(b) \operatorname{plim}\hat\alpha_1 = \beta_1 + \beta_2\delta = 1{,}200 + 360 = +1{,}560 SEK.

(c) The true effect is positive (+1{,}200) and the bias is also positive (+360), so the estimate is pushed away from zero — amplification. Exercise 2

A region studies whether longer telefontid (daily phone-line hours) at a vårdcentral reduces avoidable emergency-room visits (y, lower is better). It regresses ER visits on phone hours (x), omitting an urban/rural indicator (w = 1 if rural). The true effect of phone hours is \beta_1 = -3 visits per extra hour (more phone access reduces ER use). Rural centers have more avoidable ER visits for reasons unrelated to phones (distance, fewer alternatives), so \beta_2 = +20. Rural centers also tend to offer longer phone hours to compensate for distance, so \delta = +0.5.

  1. Compute the bias and the expected short slope. (b) Classify the bias. (c) What wrong conclusion might the region reach, and how would they fix it?
Show answer

(a) \text{Bias} = \beta_2\delta = 20 \times 0.5 = +10. Expected short slope = \beta_1 + \beta_2\delta = -3 + 10 = +7.

(b) The true effect is negative (-3), the bias is positive (+10) and larger in magnitude, so \operatorname{plim}\hat\alpha_1 = +7 lands on the opposite side of zero — wrong-sign bias.

(c) The short regression reports a positive slope: centers with longer phone hours appear to have more ER visits, suggesting phone access is useless or even harmful. The truth is the reverse — phone access helps, but it is concentrated in rural centers that have high ER use for other reasons. The omitted urban/rural status confounds the comparison. Exercise 3

A colleague argues: “To be safe against omitted variable bias, I always add every variable I can get my hands on to the regression.” Give two distinct reasons this blanket strategy is misguided, referencing the OVB formula. Identify one type of variable that you should specifically avoid controlling for.

Show answer

Reason 1 — harmless omissions exist. The OVB formula is a product \beta_2\delta. A variable that is unrelated to the regressor of interest (\delta = 0) or has no effect on the outcome (\beta_2 = 0) contributes zero bias whether or not it is included. Adding such variables cannot remove a bias that was not there; it only spends degrees of freedom and can inflate the standard error of \hat\alpha_1 (especially if the added variable is correlated with x, raising variance through multicollinearity). “Control for everything” trades a non-existent bias reduction for a real precision loss.

Reason 2 — some controls create bias. Not every variable is a confounder. Controlling for a collider (a variable caused by both x and y, or by x and an unobserved cause of y) opens a non-causal path and induces bias where none existed. Controlling for a mediator (a variable on the causal pathway x \rightarrow m \rightarrow y) removes part of the very effect you are trying to estimate, biasing \hat\alpha_1 toward zero. The OVB formula assumes w is a confounder (X \leftarrow W \rightarrow Y); it does not license adding arbitrary variables.

Variable to avoid: a collider, or any post-treatment variable (mediators, downstream outcomes of the treatment). The correct strategy is to control for variables that block backdoor paths — common causes — and to leave colliders and mediators alone. This requires a causal model (a DAG), not a “throw everything in” reflex.

Exercise 4

Using the OVB sandbox, set \beta_2 = -2.5 (deprivation hurts outcomes, fixed by Sweden’s clinical reality) and explore \delta. (a) For which sign of \delta is the short regression biased downward? (b) Find the value of \delta that makes the short slope approximately zero, and interpret what that means substantively. (c) The true \beta_1 is +1.5. Solve algebraically for the \delta that drives the short slope to exactly zero.

Show answer

(a) With \beta_2 = -2.5 < 0, the bias \beta_2\delta is negative when \delta > 0. So a positive auxiliary slope — more-deprived areas having more nurses (compensatory funding) — biases the short regression downward. Substantively: the nurse-rich centers are also the hard-to-treat centers, so the raw staffing–outcome relationship is dragged down.

(b)–(c) The short slope is zero when \beta_1 + \beta_2\delta = 0, i.e.

\delta = -\frac{\beta_1}{\beta_2} = -\frac{1.5}{-2.5} = 0.6.

Setting \delta \approx 0.6 in the sandbox makes the red short line approximately flat. The interpretation is striking: a genuinely beneficial nurse effect (\beta_1 = +1.5) is exactly cancelled in the raw data by the confounding pull of deprivation, because the extra nurses are deployed precisely where outcomes are worst. An analyst running the short regression would report “staffing has no association with diabetes control” and might cut nurse budgets — a policy error driven entirely by the omitted CNI index. Push \delta above 0.6 and the slope turns negative (wrong sign); pull it below 0.6 and the benefit partially reappears.

Key takeaways

  • Omitting a relevant confounder sweeps its influence into the short-model error; if that confounder is correlated with the regressor, OLS no longer estimates the true effect.
  • The bias is exactly \beta_2\delta: the effect of the omitted variable on the outcome, times the slope of the omitted variable on the included one. Both pieces must be nonzero for bias to exist.
  • OVB is asymptotic — it does not shrink with more data. It is a flaw in what you estimate, not how precisely you estimate it.
  • Same signs multiply to upward bias, opposite signs to downward bias; “upward” means more positive on the number line, which can attenuate, amplify, or flip the sign of a true effect.
  • OVB, confounding, and selection bias are three dialects of one idea: an open non-causal path between regressor and outcome. The fix is to control for confounders — not to throw every variable into the regression, since colliders and mediators create bias.

What Comes Next

Omitted variable bias shows what happens when a relevant regressor is missing. But even when every relevant variable is present, a model can still be wrong if it imposes the assumption that effects are constant across subgroups — that nurses help affluent and deprived centers by the same amount. The next page, Interactions, relaxes that assumption: it introduces product terms that let the slope on one variable depend on the value of another, turning a single average effect into a family of effects — and revealing the heterogeneity that a main-effects-only regression silently averages away.
