Volatility: ARCH and GARCH

Modeling time-varying variance

In this section, you will learn to

  • Recognize volatility clustering — the bunching of calm and turbulent periods — and explain why it means the conditional variance is predictable even when the mean is not
  • Write the ARCH(q) model, in which today’s variance depends on recent squared shocks, and read its equation aloud
  • Generalize to the GARCH(p,q) model, where variance also depends on its own lags, and interpret the GARCH(1,1) recursion term by term
  • Compute the persistence \alpha + \beta and the unconditional variance \omega/(1-\alpha-\beta), and state the stationarity condition \alpha + \beta < 1
  • Use a fitted GARCH model to forecast next-period risk and trace the multi-step variance forecast back to the long-run level

The Question Behind a Risk Forecast

A regional health authority — Region Stockholm — tracks the week-over-week percentage change in unplanned acute primary-care visits to its vårdcentraler (primary care centers). Most weeks the change is small and unremarkable: a quiet winter week looks much like the one before it. But when an influenza wave breaks, or a heat wave hits, or a new respiratory virus circulates, the series erupts: several consecutive weeks of large swings, up and down, before settling back to calm. The average weekly change is essentially zero and stubbornly unpredictable — you cannot forecast next week’s direction. Yet something else is plainly forecastable: how turbulent next week will be. After a violent week, another violent week is likely; after a placid stretch, calm tends to persist.

This is volatility clustering, and it is everywhere: in financial returns (Engle’s original 1982 motivation), in inflation and unemployment by region, in sjukfrånvaro (sickness absence) rates, and in health-utilisation shocks like the one above. A model that assumes constant variance — the workhorse assumption behind ordinary least squares and the VAR of the previous page — gets the level of risk badly wrong: it understates risk in turbulent periods and overstates it in calm ones. The ARCH and GARCH models fix this by letting the conditional variance evolve over time, driven by the size of recent shocks and by its own recent history.

This page builds that machinery from the ground up. The payoff is a model whose mean may be pure noise but whose variance is genuinely predictable — and a one-step recursion you can iterate forward to forecast risk.

What a Constant-Variance Model Misses

Write the series of interest — the demeaned weekly change — as r_t. A standard model decomposes it into a predictable mean \mu_t plus an unpredictable shock \varepsilon_t:

r_t = \mu_t + \varepsilon_t, \qquad \varepsilon_t = \sigma_t \, z_t, \qquad z_t \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} (0, 1).

Read this as: “the observation at time t equals its conditional mean \mu_t plus a shock \varepsilon_t; the shock is a time-varying scale \sigma_t multiplied by a standardized innovation z_t that has mean zero and variance one.” The innovations z_t (often standard Normal) carry no memory — they are i.i.d. white noise. All of the predictable structure in the size of the shocks is loaded into \sigma_t, the conditional standard deviation (the conditional volatility), with \sigma_t^2 the conditional variance.

A constant-variance model sets \sigma_t^2 = \sigma^2, the same number every week. That is exactly what volatility clustering violates. Below, two series share the same unconditional variance and the same zero mean, but one has constant variance and the other is GARCH. Watch where the turbulence lives.

The mean can be noise while the variance is signal. It is entirely possible — and typical in finance and in health-utilisation shocks — that \mu_t is unforecastable (next week’s direction is a coin flip) while \sigma_t^2 is highly forecastable (next week’s turbulence is predictable from this week’s). Volatility modeling is the art of extracting that second, second-moment signal. It does not help you predict the sign of the next move; it helps you size the risk around it.

ARCH: Variance Built from Recent Shocks

Robert Engle’s insight (1982, Nobel 2003) was to let the conditional variance respond to the squared recent shocks. A big shock last week — in either direction — raises the variance this week.

The ARCH(q) model (AutoRegressive Conditional Heteroskedasticity) specifies the conditional variance as a constant plus a weighted sum of the q most recent squared shocks:

\sigma_t^2 \;=\; \omega \;+\; \alpha_1 \varepsilon_{t-1}^2 \;+\; \alpha_2 \varepsilon_{t-2}^2 \;+\; \cdots \;+\; \alpha_q \varepsilon_{t-q}^2,

with \omega > 0 and \alpha_j \ge 0 to keep the variance positive.

Read this as: “the variance at time t equals a baseline \omega plus \alpha_1 times last period’s squared shock, plus \alpha_2 times the squared shock two periods ago, and so on.” Because shocks enter squared, a large move of either sign — a spike or a plunge in acute visits — pushes next period’s variance up. The variance is “conditional” because it depends on the recently observed shocks; it is “heteroskedastic” because it changes over time.

The squaring is what produces clustering: one large \varepsilon_{t-1} inflates \sigma_t^2, which makes a large \varepsilon_t more likely, which inflates \sigma_{t+1}^2, and so on — turbulence begets turbulence until the squared shocks happen to come in small and the variance decays back toward \omega.

The practical snag is that capturing the long, slow persistence seen in real data requires many lags q — a long, fiddly ARCH(8) or ARCH(12) with many parameters to estimate. The GARCH generalization gets the same persistence with far fewer parameters.

GARCH: Variance with a Memory of Itself

Tim Bollerslev’s GARCH (1986) adds lagged variance terms. The variance now remembers not just recent shocks but its own recent level — a parsimonious way to encode long memory.

The GARCH(p,q) model (Generalized ARCH) adds p lags of the conditional variance to the ARCH(q) terms:

\sigma_t^2 \;=\; \omega \;+\; \sum_{j=1}^{q} \alpha_j \,\varepsilon_{t-j}^2 \;+\; \sum_{k=1}^{p} \beta_k \,\sigma_{t-k}^2,

with \omega > 0, \alpha_j \ge 0, \beta_k \ge 0.

Read this as: “today’s variance equals a baseline \omega, plus a weighted sum of recent squared shocks (the ARCH part), plus a weighted sum of recent variances (the GARCH part).” The \alpha terms inject fresh news; the \beta terms carry old variance forward.

By far the most-used member is GARCH(1,1) — one lag of each:

\boxed{\;\sigma_t^2 \;=\; \omega \;+\; \alpha\,\varepsilon_{t-1}^2 \;+\; \beta\,\sigma_{t-1}^2\;}

Read this aloud as: “the conditional variance today equals a constant baseline \omega, plus \alpha times last period’s squared shock, plus \beta times last period’s variance.” Three forces, three plain meanings:

  • \omega (omega) is the baseline — the floor the variance is pulled toward in quiet times.
  • \alpha (alpha) is the shock impact (the news or reaction coefficient) — how violently the variance jumps in response to a fresh surprise \varepsilon_{t-1}^2.
  • \beta (beta) is the persistence (the memory coefficient) — how much of yesterday’s variance carries into today regardless of today’s news.

A model with high \beta has a long memory: a shock’s effect on variance fades slowly over many periods. A model with high \alpha reacts sharply but, if \beta is low, forgets quickly. Real financial and health-utilisation series almost always show high persistence: \alpha + \beta close to 1, with \beta much larger than \alpha (a typical daily-returns fit is \alpha \approx 0.08, \beta \approx 0.90).

GARCH models the variance, not the mean — fit the mean first. GARCH assumes the shocks \varepsilon_t are serially uncorrelated in their level; the clustering is purely in their size. If the series still has predictable mean structure (trend, seasonality in acute visits, day-of-week effects, an AR/MA component), you must remove it — fit an ARMA mean model, or demean and deseasonalize — before fitting GARCH to the residuals. Feeding raw, autocorrelated levels into GARCH contaminates the squared-residual inputs and produces persistence estimates that reflect leftover mean dynamics rather than genuine volatility clustering. Always test the residuals (an ARCH-LM test) for remaining ARCH effects after fitting, and the standardized residuals for remaining autocorrelation.

Persistence and the Long-Run Variance

The single most important quantity you read off a GARCH(1,1) fit is the persistence, \alpha + \beta. It governs both the long-run (unconditional) variance and how fast shocks die out.

For a GARCH(1,1) model with \alpha + \beta < 1, the unconditional (long-run) variance — the average variance the process reverts to — is

\bar{\sigma}^2 \;=\; \frac{\omega}{1 - \alpha - \beta}.

Read this as: “the long-run variance equals the baseline \omega divided by one minus the persistence.” The closer \alpha + \beta is to 1, the larger the denominator’s amplification: a small baseline \omega is divided by a tiny number, producing a large average variance and shocks that take a very long time to decay.

This formula makes the GARCH(1,1) recursion easy to interpret as mean reversion in variance. Subtracting \bar\sigma^2 from both sides of the recursion (derivation in the Math Details) gives

\sigma_t^2 - \bar\sigma^2 \;=\; \alpha\,(\varepsilon_{t-1}^2 - \bar\sigma^2) \;+\; \beta\,(\sigma_{t-1}^2 - \bar\sigma^2).

Read this as: “today’s deviation of variance from its long-run level is a weighted blend (\alpha and \beta) of last period’s squared-shock deviation and last period’s variance deviation.” Whatever pushes the variance above \bar\sigma^2 decays at rate \alpha + \beta per period — the variance always drifts home, just slowly when persistence is high.

Covariance stationarity requires \alpha + \beta < 1. Only then does a finite unconditional variance \bar\sigma^2 = \omega/(1-\alpha-\beta) exist and the process revert to it. The boundary cases are diagnostic, not decorative:

  • \alpha + \beta = 1 is the IGARCH (integrated GARCH) knife-edge: shocks to variance are permanent (an analogue of a unit root in the mean), \bar\sigma^2 is undefined, and the variance wanders without a long-run anchor.
  • \alpha + \beta > 1 is explosive: the conditional variance diverges, which is economically nonsensical for a return or a utilisation rate.

Estimated persistence of, say, 0.985 is normal and stationary; an estimate that pins against 1 is a warning that the long-run variance is barely identified.

See It: Simulate a GARCH(1,1) Series

The lab below simulates a GARCH(1,1) path so you can watch volatility clustering form. Drag \alpha (shock impact) and \beta (persistence) and observe two things at once: the series of weekly changes (top), where turbulence visibly bunches, and the conditional-volatility band \pm 2\sigma_t (the shaded ribbon), which fans wide right after a large shock and slowly contracts during calm.   const omega = tsgarch_omega;
  const a = tsgarch_alpha;
  const b = tsgarch_beta;

  // Initialize at the long-run variance when it exists, else at the baseline.
  const var0 = tsgarch_persistence < 1 ? tsgarch_targetVar : omega / 0.02;
  let sig2 = var0;
  let eps = Math.sqrt(sig2) * Z();

  const rows = [];
  for (let t = 0; t < n; t++) {
    // GARCH(1,1) recursion: σ²_t = ω + α ε²_{t-1} + β σ²_{t-1}
    sig2 = omega + a * eps * eps + b * sig2;
    const sig = Math.sqrt(sig2);
    eps = sig * Z();                    // this week's shock
    rows.push({
      t,
      r: eps,                           // demeaned weekly % change
      sigma: sig,
      upper: 2 * sig,                   // ±2σ conditional band
      lower: -2 * sig,
      sig2
    });
  }
  return rows;
}

How to Read the GARCH Simulation

Work through these in order; each isolates one idea.

Step 1: Read the two charts. The top chart is the simulated series (red) — the weekly demeaned change in acute visits — with the shaded \pm 2\sigma_t conditional band behind it. The bottom chart plots the conditional volatility \sigma_t directly. The ochre dashed lines mark the long-run level \bar\sigma to which everything reverts. Notice immediately: the red series rarely pierces its own band, because the band is the current scale of the shocks.

Step 2: Spot the clustering. Find a week with an unusually large change in the top chart. The band widens there and stays wide for several weeks afterward before tapering — and the bottom chart shows a matching wave in \sigma_t. Calm stretches show a thin band and a low, flat \sigma_t. That alternation of wide and thin regions is volatility clustering.

Step 3: Crank up persistence \beta. Drag \beta from 0.85 toward 0.98. The clusters grow longer: a single shock now keeps the band wide for many weeks, because each period carries forward 98\% of the previous variance. Watch the “Shock half-life” tile climb — it is how many weeks a variance shock takes to half-decay.

Step 4: Crank up shock impact \alpha. Return \beta to 0.85 and push \alpha toward 0.4. Now the band becomes spiky: it jumps violently the instant a large shock lands, but (with modest \beta) snaps back fast. High \alpha buys reactivity; high \beta buys memory. Real data usually wants a little of the first and a lot of the second.

Step 5: Approach the boundary. Increase \beta (or \alpha) until the persistence readout \alpha + \beta turns red at 1. The “Long-run \bar\sigma” tile reads “-” and the status flips to non-stationary: there is no fixed level for the band to revert to, and the variance wanders. This is the IGARCH knife-edge from the Assumption card.

Step 6: Resample. Press Draw a new random path to see a fresh realization at the same \alpha, \beta. The specific shocks change, but the character — how long clusters last, how wide the band gets — is governed entirely by your two sliders. That is the whole point: GARCH parameters shape the distribution of turbulence, not any one week.

Forecasting Risk

Once \alpha, \beta, \omega are estimated, the GARCH(1,1) recursion is the forecast. Standing at time T with the realized shock \varepsilon_T and variance \sigma_T^2 in hand, the one-step-ahead variance forecast is simply the recursion evaluated forward:

\hat\sigma^2_{T+1} \;=\; \omega \;+\; \alpha\,\varepsilon_{T}^2 \;+\; \beta\,\sigma_{T}^2 .

For horizons beyond one step we no longer know the future shocks, but their expectation is the variance itself (E[\varepsilon_{T+h}^2] = \sigma_{T+h}^2), so the multi-step forecast collapses to a clean geometric-decay formula (derived in the Math Details):

\boxed{\;\hat\sigma^2_{T+h} \;=\; \bar\sigma^2 \;+\; (\alpha+\beta)^{\,h-1}\bigl(\hat\sigma^2_{T+1} - \bar\sigma^2\bigr)\;}

Read this as: “the variance forecast h steps out equals the long-run variance \bar\sigma^2 plus the current deviation from it, shrunk by the persistence raised to the power h-1.” Whatever the current turbulence, the forecast decays geometrically back to the long-run level at rate \alpha + \beta per step. High persistence ⇒ slow reversion ⇒ today’s turbulence matters far into the future. This term-structure of variance is exactly what risk managers and capacity planners want: how long will elevated risk last?

Estimating GARCH in Practice

GARCH parameters are estimated by maximum likelihood (the MLE machinery from Module 11), assuming a distribution for the standardized innovations z_t — Normal, or a fatter-tailed Student-t to match the heavy tails of real shocks. Both panels fit a GARCH(1,1) to a return-like series, report \hat\omega, \hat\alpha, \hat\beta, the implied persistence, and a one-step variance forecast.

# install.packages("rugarch")
library(rugarch)

set.seed(2024)

## ---- Simulate a GARCH(1,1) series to fit (e.g. weekly acute-visit shocks) ----
true_spec <- ugarchspec(
  variance.model = list(model = "sGARCH", garchOrder = c(1, 1)),
  mean.model     = list(armaOrder = c(0, 0), include.mean = FALSE),
  fixed.pars     = list(omega = 2e-5, alpha1 = 0.08, beta1 = 0.90)
)
sim <- ugarchpath(true_spec, n.sim = 1500)
r   <- as.numeric(fitted(sim))            # the simulated series

## ---- Fit GARCH(1,1) by maximum likelihood ----
fit_spec <- ugarchspec(
  variance.model = list(model = "sGARCH", garchOrder = c(1, 1)),
  mean.model     = list(armaOrder = c(0, 0), include.mean = FALSE),
  distribution.model = "norm"             # try "std" for Student-t tails
)
fit <- ugarchfit(fit_spec, data = r)

cp <- coef(fit)
cat(sprintf("omega = %.2e  alpha = %.3f  beta = %.3f\n",
            cp["omega"], cp["alpha1"], cp["beta1"]))

persistence <- cp["alpha1"] + cp["beta1"]
uncond_var  <- cp["omega"] / (1 - persistence)
cat(sprintf("persistence alpha+beta = %.3f\n", persistence))
cat(sprintf("long-run variance       = %.2e  (vol = %.3f)\n",
            uncond_var, sqrt(uncond_var)))

## ---- Forecast conditional variance 10 steps ahead ----
fc <- ugarchforecast(fit, n.ahead = 10)
print(sigma(fc)^2)                        # variance forecasts; reverts to long-run
# pip install arch
import numpy as np
from arch import arch_model
from arch.univariate import GARCH, ConstantMean, Normal

rng = np.random.default_rng(2024)

# ---- Simulate a GARCH(1,1) series (e.g. weekly acute-visit shocks) ----
omega, alpha, beta = 2e-5, 0.08, 0.90
n = 1500
eps = np.zeros(n)
sig2 = omega / (1 - alpha - beta)         # start at the long-run variance
for t in range(1, n):
    sig2 = omega + alpha * eps[t-1]**2 + beta * sig2
    eps[t] = np.sqrt(sig2) * rng.standard_normal()
r = eps * 100                             # scale to "percent" — helps the optimizer

# ---- Fit GARCH(1,1) by maximum likelihood ----
am = arch_model(r, mean="Zero", vol="GARCH", p=1, q=1, dist="normal")
res = am.fit(disp="off")
print(res.params[["omega", "alpha[1]", "beta[1]"]])

a, b = res.params["alpha[1]"], res.params["beta[1]"]
persistence = a + b
uncond_var = res.params["omega"] / (1 - persistence)
print(f"persistence alpha+beta = {persistence:.3f}")
print(f"long-run variance       = {uncond_var:.4f}  (vol = {np.sqrt(uncond_var):.3f})")

# ---- Forecast conditional variance 10 steps ahead ----
fc = res.forecast(horizon=10, reindex=False)
print(fc.variance.iloc[-1].values)        # 10-step variance path -> long-run level

Both fits recover parameters close to the truth (\hat\alpha \approx 0.08, \hat\beta \approx 0.90, persistence \approx 0.98) and a 10-step variance forecast that decays geometrically toward the long-run level \bar\sigma^2 = \omega/(1-\alpha-\beta). When you fit your own series, always read off the persistence first: it is the one number that tells you whether the model is sensible (below 1) and how sticky the volatility is.

Math Details: The unconditional variance, mean-reversion form, and multi-step forecast

Unconditional variance

Start from the GARCH(1,1) recursion and take unconditional expectations of both sides. Write \bar\sigma^2 = E[\sigma_t^2] for the (assumed constant) unconditional variance. A key fact links the squared shock to the variance: since \varepsilon_t = \sigma_t z_t with E[z_t^2] = 1 and z_t independent of \sigma_t,

E[\varepsilon_t^2] = E[\sigma_t^2 z_t^2] = E[\sigma_t^2]\,E[z_t^2] = \bar\sigma^2 .

Now take expectations of \sigma_t^2 = \omega + \alpha\varepsilon_{t-1}^2 + \beta\sigma_{t-1}^2:

\bar\sigma^2 = \omega + \alpha\,\bar\sigma^2 + \beta\,\bar\sigma^2 = \omega + (\alpha+\beta)\,\bar\sigma^2 .

Solve for \bar\sigma^2:

\bar\sigma^2 (1 - \alpha - \beta) = \omega \;\Longrightarrow\; \bar\sigma^2 = \frac{\omega}{1-\alpha-\beta}.

This is finite and positive iff \alpha + \beta < 1 (with \omega > 0). If \alpha + \beta \ge 1 the geometric series implicit in repeatedly substituting the recursion does not converge, and no finite unconditional variance exists — the stationarity condition. \checkmark

Mean-reversion form

Substitute \omega = \bar\sigma^2(1-\alpha-\beta) back into the recursion:

\sigma_t^2 = \bar\sigma^2(1-\alpha-\beta) + \alpha\varepsilon_{t-1}^2 + \beta\sigma_{t-1}^2.

Subtract \bar\sigma^2 from both sides and group:

\sigma_t^2 - \bar\sigma^2 = \alpha(\varepsilon_{t-1}^2 - \bar\sigma^2) + \beta(\sigma_{t-1}^2 - \bar\sigma^2).

The variance’s deviation from its long-run level is a weighted combination of last period’s squared-shock deviation and variance deviation — mean reversion at rate \alpha + \beta.

Multi-step variance forecast

Condition on information at time T. For one step, the shock \varepsilon_T and variance \sigma_T^2 are known, so

\hat\sigma^2_{T+1} = E_T[\sigma^2_{T+1}] = \omega + \alpha\varepsilon_T^2 + \beta\sigma_T^2 .

For two steps, \varepsilon_{T+1} is not known, but E_T[\varepsilon_{T+1}^2] = E_T[\sigma_{T+1}^2 z_{T+1}^2] = \hat\sigma^2_{T+1}. Hence

\hat\sigma^2_{T+2} = \omega + \alpha\,\hat\sigma^2_{T+1} + \beta\,\hat\sigma^2_{T+1} = \omega + (\alpha+\beta)\,\hat\sigma^2_{T+1}.

In general, for h \ge 2, \hat\sigma^2_{T+h} = \omega + (\alpha+\beta)\hat\sigma^2_{T+h-1}. Subtract the fixed point \bar\sigma^2 = \omega/(1-\alpha-\beta) from both sides to linearize the recursion:

\hat\sigma^2_{T+h} - \bar\sigma^2 = (\alpha+\beta)\bigl(\hat\sigma^2_{T+h-1} - \bar\sigma^2\bigr).

This is a first-order geometric recursion in the deviation; iterating it down to h=1 gives the closed form

\hat\sigma^2_{T+h} = \bar\sigma^2 + (\alpha+\beta)^{\,h-1}\bigl(\hat\sigma^2_{T+1} - \bar\sigma^2\bigr).

As h \to \infty and with \alpha+\beta < 1, (\alpha+\beta)^{h-1} \to 0, so \hat\sigma^2_{T+h} \to \bar\sigma^2: the forecast reverts to the long-run variance, the faster the lower the persistence. \checkmark

Exercises

Exercise 1

A GARCH(1,1) model fitted to monthly regional sjukfrånvaro (sickness-absence) shocks gives \hat\omega = 0.0004, \hat\alpha = 0.07, \hat\beta = 0.88.

  1. Compute the persistence. (b) Compute the unconditional variance and the long-run volatility. (c) Is the process covariance-stationary? Justify.
Show answer

(a) Persistence = \hat\alpha + \hat\beta = 0.07 + 0.88 = 0.95.

(b) Unconditional variance \bar\sigma^2 = \dfrac{\hat\omega}{1-\hat\alpha-\hat\beta} = \dfrac{0.0004}{1-0.95} = \dfrac{0.0004}{0.05} = 0.008. Long-run volatility \bar\sigma = \sqrt{0.008} \approx 0.0894, i.e. about 8.9\% per month.

(c) Yes. Covariance stationarity requires \alpha + \beta < 1, and 0.95 < 1, so a finite long-run variance exists and the process reverts to it. The persistence is high but safely below the boundary.

Exercise 2

Explain, in your own words, why squaring the shocks in the ARCH/GARCH variance equation is what produces volatility clustering, and why this would fail if shocks entered linearly (un-squared).

Show answer

The conditional variance must be a measure of the size of recent shocks, irrespective of sign — a big plunge in acute visits is just as much “turbulence” as a big spike. Squaring (or taking absolute values) treats +0.10 and -0.10 identically: both contribute 0.01 to next period’s variance, so any large move of either sign raises future variance. That positive feedback — large \varepsilon_{t-1}^2 \Rightarrow large \sigma_t^2 \Rightarrow a large \varepsilon_t is more likely \Rightarrow large \sigma_{t+1}^2 — is precisely the bunching of turbulent periods.

If shocks entered linearly (\sigma_t^2 = \omega + \alpha\varepsilon_{t-1}), then a large negative shock would lower the variance, possibly making it negative (which is impossible for a variance), and positive and negative shocks would cancel on average rather than accumulate. The model would describe the level of the series, not the size of its swings, and could not generate clustering.

Exercise 3

Using the same fit as Exercise 1 (\bar\sigma^2 = 0.008, \alpha+\beta = 0.95), suppose the current one-step-ahead variance forecast is \hat\sigma^2_{T+1} = 0.020 (the region is in a turbulent stretch, well above the long-run level).

  1. Forecast the variance 3 steps ahead. (b) Roughly how many steps until the forecast has reverted halfway back to \bar\sigma^2?
Show answer

(a) Use \hat\sigma^2_{T+h} = \bar\sigma^2 + (\alpha+\beta)^{h-1}(\hat\sigma^2_{T+1} - \bar\sigma^2) with h=3:

\hat\sigma^2_{T+3} = 0.008 + (0.95)^{2}\,(0.020 - 0.008) = 0.008 + 0.9025 \times 0.012 = 0.008 + 0.01083 = 0.01883.

So about 0.0188 — still elevated, having decayed only modestly from 0.020 toward 0.008.

(b) The deviation from \bar\sigma^2 shrinks by the factor (\alpha+\beta) each step. The half-life solves (0.95)^{m} = 0.5, i.e. m = \dfrac{\ln 0.5}{\ln 0.95} = \dfrac{-0.6931}{-0.0513} \approx 13.5 steps. With persistence 0.95, turbulence takes roughly 13–14 months to half-fade — a long, sticky shock, exactly what high persistence means.

Exercise 4

In the simulation, set \alpha = 0.10, \beta = 0.85 and note the persistence and the length of the volatility clusters. Then raise \beta to 0.97. (a) What happens to the persistence readout and the “Shock half-life” tile? (b) What happens to the length of the calm/turbulent stretches in the chart, and why? (c) Push \beta to 0.92 with \alpha at 0.08 and explain what the status flag would show if you nudged \beta to 0.93.

Show answer

(a) At \alpha=0.10, \beta=0.85 the persistence is 0.95 and the half-life is \ln(0.5)/\ln(0.95)\approx 13.5 weeks. Raising \beta to 0.97 lifts persistence to \alpha+\beta = 0.10+0.97 = 1.07 — above 1 — so the readout turns red and the status flips to non-stationary; the half-life tile reads \infty because variance shocks no longer decay.

(b) Even just below the boundary (say \beta = 0.88, persistence 0.98), the calm and turbulent stretches grow markedly longer: each period carries forward 98\% of the previous variance, so a shock keeps the band wide for many weeks before reverting. Higher persistence = longer, smoother clusters.

(c) At \alpha=0.08, \beta=0.92 the persistence is exactly 1.00 — the IGARCH knife-edge — so the status flag reads non-stationary and the long-run \bar\sigma tile shows “-”. Nudging \beta to 0.93 gives persistence 1.01 > 1, firmly non-stationary (explosive): the long-run variance \omega/(1-\alpha-\beta) is undefined.

Key takeaways

  • Many series — financial returns, inflation, sjukfrånvaro, health-utilisation shocks — show volatility clustering: calm and turbulent periods bunch, so the conditional variance is time-varying and predictable even when the mean is not.
  • ARCH(q) makes the variance a constant plus a weighted sum of recent squared shocks; GARCH(p,q) adds lagged variances, capturing long persistence with few parameters.
  • The GARCH(1,1) recursion \sigma_t^2 = \omega + \alpha\varepsilon_{t-1}^2 + \beta\sigma_{t-1}^2 has three readable parts: baseline \omega, shock impact \alpha, and persistence \beta.
  • Persistence \alpha+\beta governs everything: the unconditional variance \bar\sigma^2 = \omega/(1-\alpha-\beta) exists only when \alpha+\beta<1 (covariance stationarity), and shocks decay at rate \alpha+\beta per step.
  • Forecasts iterate the recursion forward, decaying geometrically to the long-run variance: \hat\sigma^2_{T+h} = \bar\sigma^2 + (\alpha+\beta)^{h-1}(\hat\sigma^2_{T+1} - \bar\sigma^2).
