Unit Roots and Cointegration

Random walks, spurious regression, and long-run equilibrium

In this section, you will learn to

  • Define a unit-root (random-walk) process and explain why its shocks are permanent and its variance grows without bound
  • Recognise the spurious regression trap: two independent I(1) series can show a high R^2 and a large t-statistic purely by chance
  • Test for a unit root with the (augmented) Dickey–Fuller test and read its non-standard critical values, and contrast it with the KPSS test
  • Define cointegration — a stationary linear combination of non-stationary series — and write the error-correction model (ECM) it implies
  • Run the Engle–Granger two-step procedure and interpret the speed-of-adjustment coefficient in R and Python

When Differencing Is Not Optional

The previous page, Stationarity and ARIMA, drew a sharp line between series that revert to a fixed mean and series that do not. This page lives entirely on the wrong side of that line. Consider two regional health series that any analyst at Sveriges Kommuner och Regioner (SKR) might pull from a database: the monthly cost per capita of primary care in Region Skåne and in Region Västra Götaland, both in SEK and both running since 2006. Plot either one and you see the hallmark of a non-stationary series: no fixed level to return to, a wandering trend, and variance that fans out the longer you look.

The temptation is irresistible. Regress one cost series on the other, and you will almost certainly get a tight fit — an R^2 above 0.9, a t-statistic in the double digits, a confident story about how the two regions’ spending move together. The problem is that you would get exactly the same impressive output if the two series had nothing to do with each other — if each were an independent random walk generated by a separate random-number stream. This is the spurious regression problem, and it is one of the most important cautionary tales in applied econometrics.

This page builds the machinery to tell the difference between a real long-run relationship and a statistical mirage. The key concept is the unit root: a single number, \phi = 1, that flips a well-behaved autoregression into a process whose shocks never die. We will see how to test for it, why ordinary least squares (OLS) breaks when it is present, and the one redeeming case — cointegration — in which a regression of one I(1) series on another is not spurious but genuinely informative.

The Random Walk: A Process with a Unit Root

Start from the first-order autoregression you met last page, y_t = \phi\, y_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t, with white-noise shocks \varepsilon_t \sim \text{i.i.d.}(0, \sigma^2). Everything hinges on the single coefficient \phi. When |\phi| < 1 the series is stationary and mean-reverting. The knife-edge case \phi = 1 is the random walk — a process with a unit root.

y_t = y_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t.

Read this as: “today’s value equals yesterday’s value plus a fresh random shock.” There is no pull back toward any centre; wherever the series happens to be, the next step is just that level plus noise. Unrolling the recursion from a starting value y_0 makes the consequence stark:

y_t = y_0 + \sum_{s=1}^{t} \varepsilon_s.

Read this as: “the level at time t is the starting point plus the running sum of every shock that has ever hit the series.” Because the shocks accumulate rather than decay, a random walk is the integral of white noise — hence the name integrated of order one, written I(1): it must be differenced once, \Delta y_t = y_t - y_{t-1} = \varepsilon_t, to become stationary white noise, which is I(0).

A series y_t has a unit root (is I(1)) if its first difference \Delta y_t is stationary but the level y_t is not. The canonical case is the random walk y_t = y_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t, the AR(1) with \phi = 1.

Two properties define the behaviour of a random walk started at y_0:

\mathbb{E}[y_t] = y_0, \qquad \operatorname{Var}(y_t) = t\,\sigma^2.

Read these as: “the expected level stays at the starting point, but the variance grows linearly with time — it is t times the shock variance.” The mean looks innocent; the exploding variance is the real signature. The series drifts arbitrarily far from y_0 as t grows, and any single shock \varepsilon_s shifts every future value by the same amount, forever.

The contrast with a stationary AR(1) is total. In a stationary process a shock’s influence decays geometrically as \phi^k \to 0, the variance settles at the constant \sigma^2/(1-\phi^2), and the series forgets its past. In a random walk the shock’s influence is permanent (its coefficient is 1^k = 1 for all horizons), the variance diverges, and the series remembers everything. A useful variant adds a drift term, y_t = \mu + y_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t, which lays a deterministic trend y_0 + \mu t underneath the wandering — this is the realistic model for a steadily rising cost series.

Permanent vs. transitory shocks. This distinction is not a technicality — it is the whole reason unit roots matter for policy. If regional primary-care cost is stationary, a one-off budget overrun is transitory: the series reverts and next year looks normal. If it is a random walk, that same overrun is permanent: it raises the entire future path by its full size. Whether a shock fades or sticks is the difference between “we had a bad month” and “we have a new baseline.”

The Spurious Regression Trap

Here is the danger. Take two random walks, x_t and y_t, generated from completely independent shock streams — there is no causal or statistical link between them by construction. Now run the OLS regression

y_t = \alpha + \beta\, x_t + u_t.

Classical regression intuition says: since x and y are unrelated, \hat\beta should be near zero, its t-statistic should be small, and R^2 should be near zero. All three expectations are wrong. Granger and Newbold (1974) showed by simulation — and Phillips (1986) proved asymptotically — that for independent I(1) series, \hat\beta does not converge to zero, the t-statistic diverges (it grows without bound as the sample lengthens, so you reject “no relationship” almost always), and R^2 converges to a non-degenerate random variable that is often large. The residuals u_t are themselves I(1) and wildly autocorrelated, which destroys the usual standard-error formulas: they are far too small, so every t-statistic is inflated.

The interactive below lets you manufacture this illusion on demand and watch it recur. Each “redraw” generates two fresh, independent random walks and reports the regression. You will see large t-statistics and high R^2 values appear over and over — pure noise dressed up as a strong relationship — and you can toggle to stationary series to confirm the illusion vanishes.

// Generate two INDEPENDENT series from separate standard-normal streams.
// I(1): cumulative sums of white noise (random walks).
// I(0): the white noise itself (stationary). Both are independent by
// construction, so any apparent x-y relationship is pure chance.
tsunit_data = {
  tsunit_draw;                         // depend on the button so "Redraw" works
  const T = tsunit_T;
  const rndX = d3.randomNormal(0, 1);
  const rndY = d3.randomNormal(0, 1);
  const ex = d3.range(T).map(rndX);    // shocks for x
  const ey = d3.range(T).map(rndY);    // shocks for y (independent stream)
  const stationary = tsunit_kind.startsWith("Two stationary");
  let xc = 0, yc = 0;
  const rows = [];
  for (let t = 0; t < T; t++) {
    if (stationary) {
      rows.push({ t, x: ex[t], y: ey[t] });
    } else {
      xc += ex[t];                     // running sum => random walk
      yc += ey[t];
      rows.push({ t, x: xc, y: yc });
    }
  }
  return rows;
}
// OLS of y on x with the (naive, classical) t-statistic on the slope and R^2.
// For I(1) data these classical statistics are exactly the ones that mislead:
// the residuals are themselves I(1), so the reported SE is far too small.
tsunit_fit = {
  const d = tsunit_data, n = d.length;
  const mx = d3.mean(d, r => r.x), my = d3.mean(d, r => r.y);
  let sxx = 0, sxy = 0, syy = 0;
  for (const r of d) {
    sxx += (r.x - mx) ** 2;
    sxy += (r.x - mx) * (r.y - my);
    syy += (r.y - my) ** 2;
  }
  const beta = sxy / sxx;
  const alpha = my - beta * mx;
  let sse = 0;
  for (const r of d) { const e = r.y - alpha - beta * r.x; sse += e * e; }
  const sigma2 = sse / (n - 2);
  const seBeta = Math.sqrt(sigma2 / sxx);
  const tStat = beta / seBeta;
  const r2 = 1 - sse / syy;
  return { alpha, beta, tStat, r2, n };
}

How to Read the Spurious-Regression Lab

Work through these in order; each isolates one piece of the illusion.

Step 1: Read the two series (top panel). The slate line is x (a stand-in for Region Västra Götaland’s cost) and the red line is y (Region Skåne’s). They are generated from independent random-number streams — by construction there is no link. Yet with the default “Two random walks” setting they often appear to drift together or apart in long swings. That visual co-movement is the seed of the trap.

Step 2: Read the scatter (bottom panel). Each dot is one month (x_t, y_t); the ochre line is the OLS fit. For two random walks the cloud frequently lines up along a clear slope, and the fit looks convincing — exactly the picture you would publish if you believed the regression.

Step 3: Read the verdict tiles. The t-statistic and R^2 quantify the illusion. With random walks you will routinely see |t| well above 1.96 (conventionally “significant at 5%”) and R^2 above 0.5 — both flagged red, because here they are spurious: the truth is \beta = 0 and unrelated series.

Step 4: Redraw, and redraw again. Click Redraw. Two brand-new independent random walks are drawn each time. Watch the large t-statistics and high R^2 values keep coming back. The spurious significance is not a one-off fluke; it recurs draw after draw. A real “5%” test would reject only about 5% of the time — this one rejects far more often.

Step 5: Switch to stationary and compare. Flip the toggle to “Two stationary series”. Now the scatter is a formless blob, R^2 collapses toward zero, and |t| stays below 1.96 on most draws (green verdict). This is what correct behaviour looks like when the data are I(0). The contrast is the whole lesson: the unit root, not any real link, manufactures the significance.

Step 6: Lengthen the sample. Increase T with the slider and keep redrawing the random walks. Counter to all intuition, longer samples make the problem worse for I(1) data: the t-statistic tends to grow, not shrink. More data cannot save a spurious regression — only correctly handling the unit root can.

Testing for a Unit Root: Dickey–Fuller and KPSS

Since a unit root is the source of the trouble, the first step in any time-series analysis is to test for it. The workhorse is the Dickey–Fuller (DF) test. Rewrite the AR(1) by subtracting y_{t-1} from both sides:

\Delta y_t = (\phi - 1)\, y_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t \;=\; \rho\, y_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t, \qquad \rho \equiv \phi - 1.

Read this as: “the change in y regresses on the lagged level, with slope \rho = \phi - 1.” The unit-root case \phi = 1 is now simply \rho = 0. So the hypotheses are

H_0:\ \rho = 0 \ \ (\text{unit root, non-stationary}) \qquad\text{vs.}\qquad H_1:\ \rho < 0 \ \ (\text{stationary, mean-reverting}).

You run the regression, take the t-statistic on \hat\rho, and compare it to a critical value. But here is the catch that trips up everyone the first time: under H_0 the regressor y_{t-1} is itself a random walk, so the usual t-distribution does not apply. The statistic follows the non-standard Dickey–Fuller distribution, whose critical values are more negative than the normal ones. With a constant in the regression, the 5% DF critical value is about -2.86, not -1.65 — you need stronger evidence to reject the unit root than a textbook t-table would suggest.

The augmented Dickey–Fuller (ADF) test fits

\Delta y_t = \rho\, y_{t-1} + \sum_{j=1}^{p} \gamma_j\, \Delta y_{t-j} + (\text{optional constant, trend}) + \varepsilon_t,

and tests H_0:\rho = 0 (unit root) against H_1:\rho < 0 (stationary). The lagged differences \Delta y_{t-j} “augment” the plain DF test to soak up any autocorrelation in \varepsilon_t so the test statistic stays valid.

Read this as: “regress the change in y on the lagged level plus enough lagged changes to whiten the errors.” A large negative ADF statistic (below the critical value) rejects the unit root in favour of stationarity. Because the statistic is non-standard, you must use Dickey–Fuller critical values, never normal-t ones.

The ADF test has a complementary partner that flips the null on its head. The KPSS test (Kwiatkowski, Phillips, Schmidt & Shin, 1992) takes

H_0:\ y_t \text{ is stationary} \qquad\text{vs.}\qquad H_1:\ y_t \text{ has a unit root}.

Because the null is stationarity, a large KPSS statistic (above its critical value) rejects stationarity and points to a unit root. Running ADF and KPSS together is good practice: ADF rejecting (stationary) and KPSS not rejecting (stationary) gives you a confident I(0) verdict, while ADF not rejecting and KPSS rejecting gives you a confident I(1) verdict. When they disagree, the series is in a grey zone — often a sign of a near-unit-root or a structural break.

Low power against persistent stationary series. The ADF test is notoriously bad at distinguishing a true unit root (\phi = 1) from a highly persistent but stationary process (\phi = 0.97). In finite Swedish macro samples — say 15 years of monthly data — it often fails to reject the unit root even when the truth is mean-reverting. Do not read “ADF did not reject” as proof of a unit root; read it as “the data cannot rule one out.” Always pair it with KPSS, inspect the plot, and consider the economics of whether shocks should be permanent.

Cointegration: When a Spurious-Looking Regression Is Real

Everything so far warns you off regressing one I(1) series on another. There is exactly one situation where it is not only allowed but illuminating: cointegration. Two non-stationary series are cointegrated if some linear combination of them is stationary — they wander individually, but they wander together, tethered by a long-run equilibrium they cannot stray far from.

Return to the two regional cost series. Each is I(1) on its own. But both are funded by the same national framework, face the same wage agreements, and serve demographically similar populations, so economic logic says their difference should not drift apart without bound: if Skåne’s per-capita cost ran 30% above Västra Götaland’s indefinitely, budget pressure and policy would pull them back. If that is true, then although y_t and x_t are each I(1), the spread y_t - \beta x_t is I(0) — stationary. That is cointegration.

Two series x_t and y_t, each I(1), are cointegrated if there exists a constant \beta (the cointegrating coefficient) such that the linear combination

z_t = y_t - \alpha - \beta\, x_t

is stationary, i.e. z_t \sim I(0). The vector (1, -\beta) is the cointegrating vector, and z_t is the equilibrium error — the gap between y_t and its long-run level \alpha + \beta x_t.

Read this as: “the two series share a stochastic trend; subtracting \beta times one from the other cancels that common trend and leaves a stationary residual.” Cointegration is the precise statement that two random walks are not independent — they are bound together in the long run.

This is the redemption of the spurious regression. When x and y are cointegrated, the OLS regression y_t = \alpha + \beta x_t + u_t is not spurious: the residual \hat u_t is stationary, \hat\beta is consistent (in fact super-consistent, converging at rate T rather than \sqrt{T}), and the regression estimates the genuine long-run relationship. The whole art is telling the two cases apart, and the Engle–Granger procedure does exactly that.

The Engle–Granger two-step procedure.

  1. Estimate the long-run relationship. Regress y_t on x_t by OLS and save the residuals \hat z_t = y_t - \hat\alpha - \hat\beta x_t.
  2. Test the residuals for a unit root. Apply an ADF test to \hat z_t. If the residuals are stationary (reject the unit root), x and y are cointegrated and step 1 estimated a real long-run equilibrium. If the residuals are I(1), the regression is spurious.

Caveat on critical values. Because \hat z_t are estimated residuals (the regression already minimised their variance), the ADF statistic in step 2 needs Engle–Granger critical values, which are more negative than the standard ADF ones. Use the table your software reports for the cointegration test, not the plain ADF table.

The Error-Correction Model

Cointegration has a profound consequence first proved in the Granger Representation Theorem: if two series are cointegrated, their dynamics can always be written as an error-correction model (ECM). The ECM ties short-run changes to long-run equilibrium:

\Delta y_t = \underbrace{\gamma\, z_{t-1}}_{\text{error correction}} \;+\; \underbrace{\textstyle\sum_j \theta_j\, \Delta x_{t-j} + \sum_j \psi_j \Delta y_{t-j}}_{\text{short-run dynamics}} \;+\; \varepsilon_t, \qquad z_{t-1} = y_{t-1} - \alpha - \beta x_{t-1}.

Read this as: “the change in y this period equals a fraction \gamma of last period’s equilibrium error, plus short-run effects of recent changes, plus noise.” The single most important term is \gamma z_{t-1}. For the system to be stable, \gamma must be negative (typically -1 < \gamma < 0): when y sat above its long-run level last period (z_{t-1} > 0), the term \gamma z_{t-1} is negative, pushing \Delta y_t down and correcting the gap. The coefficient \gamma is the speed of adjustment: \gamma = -0.25 means the system closes a quarter of any equilibrium gap each month. The ECM is the bridge to the next page — it is a constrained vector autoregression, and the unrestricted version is exactly the VAR we turn to next.

Math Details: Why OLS on independent I(1) series is spurious, and the ECM

Why the spurious regression diverges

Let x_t and y_t be independent random walks, x_t = \sum_{s\le t}\eta_s and y_t = \sum_{s\le t}\varepsilon_s, with \eta and \varepsilon mutually independent white noise. Consider the OLS slope

\hat\beta = \frac{\sum_t (x_t - \bar x)(y_t - \bar y)}{\sum_t (x_t - \bar x)^2}.

For a stationary regressor, \frac1T \sum_t (x_t-\bar x)^2 \to \operatorname{Var}(x) (a constant) and \frac1T\sum_t(x_t-\bar x)(y_t-\bar y)\to \operatorname{Cov}(x,y)=0 by the law of large numbers, so \hat\beta \to 0. For I(1) regressors this completely breaks. The sample moments do not converge to constants; instead, after scaling by T^2, they converge to random integrals of Brownian motion:

\frac{1}{T^2}\sum_t (x_t - \bar x)^2 \ \xrightarrow{d}\ \sigma_\eta^2 \int_0^1 \tilde W_x(r)^2\,dr, \qquad \frac{1}{T^2}\sum_t (x_t-\bar x)(y_t-\bar y)\ \xrightarrow{d}\ \sigma_\eta\sigma_\varepsilon \int_0^1 \tilde W_x(r)\,\tilde W_y(r)\,dr,

where \tilde W_x, \tilde W_y are independent demeaned Brownian motions (the continuous-time limits of the two random walks). The ratio — the limit of \hat\beta — is therefore a non-degenerate random variable, not zero: \hat\beta = O_p(1), so each sample produces a different, often large, \hat\beta that never settles down. Worse, because the residuals \hat u_t are themselves I(1), the residual sum of squares \sum_t \hat u_t^2 grows like T^2, so the error-variance estimate \hat\sigma^2 = \tfrac{1}{T-2}\sum_t \hat u_t^2 grows like T. The reported standard error is then \widehat{\operatorname{se}}(\hat\beta) = \sqrt{\frac{\hat\sigma^2}{\sum_t (x_t-\bar x)^2}} = \sqrt{\frac{O_p(T)}{O_p(T^2)}} = O_p\!\big(T^{-1/2}\big), shrinking toward zero even though the true spread of \hat\beta stays of order 1. Dividing a non-vanishing \hat\beta by a standard error that collapses like T^{-1/2} gives a t-statistic of order \sqrt{T}, which diverges: |t| = \hat\beta/\widehat{\operatorname{se}}(\hat\beta) = O_p(\sqrt{T}) \to \infty. This is exactly what you watched in Step 6 of the lab — more data inflates, rather than deflates, the spurious t. The root cause is that the true errors u_t = y_t - \alpha - \beta x_t are I(1) and massively autocorrelated, violating the classical assumption that justifies the OLS standard-error formula.

The cointegrated case is the opposite

If instead z_t = y_t - \alpha - \beta x_t is stationary, the regression residuals are I(0), \sum_t (x_t-\bar x)^2 still grows like T^2 but now the numerator concentrates around the true relationship, and \hat\beta \to \beta at the fast rate T (super-consistency). The pathology vanishes precisely because the residual is stationary — which is why Engle–Granger step 2 tests exactly that residual.

Deriving the ECM from a cointegrated system

Suppose the true long-run relationship is y_t = \alpha + \beta x_t + z_t with z_t\sim I(0), and write a general first-order dynamic regression

y_t = \mu + \phi_1 y_{t-1} + \delta_0 x_t + \delta_1 x_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t.

Subtract y_{t-1} from both sides and add and subtract \delta_0 x_{t-1}:

\Delta y_t = \mu + (\phi_1 - 1) y_{t-1} + \delta_0 \Delta x_t + (\delta_0 + \delta_1) x_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t.

Factor the level terms by (\phi_1 - 1) to expose the equilibrium error:

\Delta y_t = \delta_0 \Delta x_t + (\phi_1 - 1)\Big[\, y_{t-1} - \tfrac{\mu}{1-\phi_1} - \tfrac{\delta_0+\delta_1}{1-\phi_1} x_{t-1}\Big] + \varepsilon_t.

The bracket is exactly the equilibrium error z_{t-1} = y_{t-1} - \alpha - \beta x_{t-1} with \alpha = \mu/(1-\phi_1) and the long-run multiplier \beta = (\delta_0+\delta_1)/(1-\phi_1). Writing \gamma = \phi_1 - 1 gives the ECM

\Delta y_t = \delta_0\,\Delta x_t + \gamma\, z_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t.

Stability requires |\phi_1| < 1, i.e. \gamma = \phi_1 - 1 \in (-2, 0) — the speed of adjustment is negative, so deviations from equilibrium are pulled back. This algebra is the Granger Representation Theorem in its simplest form: cointegration \Leftrightarrow an ECM with \gamma \ne 0.

Running the Tests in Practice

Both panels below simulate two cointegrated cost series (so we know the truth), then run the full workflow: ADF unit-root tests on each series, the Engle–Granger cointegration test, and the error-correction model. The R panel uses urca (Pfaff’s unit-root and cointegration library); the Python panel uses statsmodels.tsa.

  • R
  • Python
# install.packages(c("urca", "dynlm"))   # if needed
library(urca)
library(dynlm)

set.seed(2024)
T <- 240                                   # 20 years of monthly data

## ---- Simulate two COINTEGRATED regional cost series (SEK per capita) ----
# Common stochastic trend = a shared random walk; both regions load on it.
common <- cumsum(rnorm(T, sd = 12))        # shared I(1) trend
x <- 3200 + common + rnorm(T, sd = 20)     # Västra Götaland
# y tracks x in the long run (beta = 1.05) with a STATIONARY equilibrium error:
y <- 150 + 1.05 * x + arima.sim(list(ar = 0.5), n = T, sd = 25)

## ---- Step 0: confirm each series has a unit root (ADF with drift) ----
adf_x <- ur.df(x, type = "drift", selectlags = "AIC")
adf_y <- ur.df(y, type = "drift", selectlags = "AIC")
summary(adf_x)        # test-stat vs DF critical values (≈ -2.88 at 5%)
# Fail to reject H0: both x and y are I(1).

## ---- Step 1: estimate the long-run (cointegrating) relationship ----
lr <- lm(y ~ x)
beta_hat <- coef(lr)["x"]
cat("Long-run beta:", round(beta_hat, 3), "(true = 1.05)\n")
z <- residuals(lr)                          # equilibrium error

## ---- Step 2: Engle–Granger — ADF on residuals (no drift; mean is 0) ----
eg <- ur.df(z, type = "none", selectlags = "AIC")
summary(eg)           # compare to ENGLE–GRANGER critical values, not plain DF
# Large negative stat => reject unit root in z => x and y are cointegrated.

## ---- Step 3: the error-correction model ----
d_y <- diff(y); d_x <- diff(x); z_lag <- z[-T]
ecm <- lm(d_y ~ d_x + z_lag)
summary(ecm)
cat("Speed of adjustment gamma:", round(coef(ecm)["z_lag"], 3), "\n")
# gamma should be negative: each month closes |gamma| of the equilibrium gap.
import numpy as np
import statsmodels.api as sm
from statsmodels.tsa.stattools import adfuller, coint

rng = np.random.default_rng(2024)
T = 240                                      # 20 years of monthly data

# ---- Simulate two COINTEGRATED regional cost series (SEK per capita) ----
common = np.cumsum(rng.normal(0, 12, T))     # shared I(1) trend
x = 3200 + common + rng.normal(0, 20, T)     # Västra Götaland

# Stationary AR(1) equilibrium error so y and x are cointegrated (beta = 1.05).
u = np.zeros(T)
e = rng.normal(0, 25, T)
for t in range(1, T):
    u[t] = 0.5 * u[t-1] + e[t]
y = 150 + 1.05 * x + u                        # Skåne

# ---- Step 0: confirm each series is I(1) (ADF with constant) ----
for name, s in (("x", x), ("y", y)):
    stat, p, *_ = adfuller(s, regression="c", autolag="AIC")
    print(f"ADF {name}: stat={stat:.2f}, p={p:.3f}  -> "
          f"{'I(1): fail to reject unit root' if p > 0.05 else 'stationary'}")

# ---- Steps 1 & 2: Engle–Granger cointegration test in one call ----
# coint() runs the long-run OLS and ADF-tests its residuals with the correct
# Engle–Granger critical values automatically.
eg_stat, eg_p, eg_crit = coint(y, x)
print(f"\nEngle–Granger: stat={eg_stat:.2f}, p={eg_p:.3f}")
print("Cointegrated" if eg_p < 0.05 else "Not cointegrated (spurious risk)")

# ---- Step 1 explicitly: long-run beta and the equilibrium error ----
X = sm.add_constant(x)
lr = sm.OLS(y, X).fit()
beta_hat = lr.params[1]
z = lr.resid                                  # equilibrium error
print(f"\nLong-run beta: {beta_hat:.3f} (true = 1.05)")

# ---- Step 3: the error-correction model ----
dy, dx, z_lag = np.diff(y), np.diff(x), z[:-1]
W = sm.add_constant(np.column_stack([dx, z_lag]))
ecm = sm.OLS(dy, W).fit()
gamma = ecm.params[2]
print(f"Speed of adjustment gamma: {gamma:.3f}  "
      f"(negative => error-correcting)")

In both languages the workflow tells one coherent story: each cost series is I(1) (ADF fails to reject a unit root), but the Engle–Granger test rejects a unit root in the residuals, so the series are cointegrated; the long-run \hat\beta recovers the true 1.05, and the ECM’s \hat\gamma comes out negative — the two regions’ costs drift apart in the short run but are pulled back toward their shared equilibrium each month.

Exercises

Exercise 1

A random walk y_t = y_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t starts at y_0 = 0 with \varepsilon_t \sim \text{i.i.d.}(0,\sigma^2) and \sigma^2 = 9.

  1. Write y_t as a sum of shocks. (b) Give \mathbb{E}[y_t] and \operatorname{Var}(y_t) for general t, and evaluate the variance at t = 100. (c) A single shock \varepsilon_{20} arrives. By how much does it change y_{50}? How does this differ from a stationary AR(1) with \phi = 0.5?
Show answer

(a) Unrolling the recursion, y_t = y_0 + \sum_{s=1}^{t}\varepsilon_s = \sum_{s=1}^{t}\varepsilon_s (since y_0 = 0).

(b) Taking expectations, \mathbb{E}[y_t] = \sum_{s=1}^t \mathbb{E}[\varepsilon_s] = 0. Because the shocks are independent, variances add: \operatorname{Var}(y_t) = \sum_{s=1}^t \operatorname{Var}(\varepsilon_s) = t\sigma^2. At t = 100: \operatorname{Var}(y_{100}) = 100 \times 9 = 900, so the standard deviation is \sqrt{900} = 30. The series wanders ever wider with time.

(c) In the random walk, \varepsilon_{20} enters the sum for every y_t with t \ge 20 with coefficient 1, so y_{50} changes by exactly \varepsilon_{20} — the shock is permanent (and would still shift y_{1000} by the same amount). In a stationary AR(1) with \phi = 0.5, the effect of \varepsilon_{20} on y_{50} is \phi^{50-20} = 0.5^{30} \approx 9 \times 10^{-10} — essentially zero. The shock is transitory and has decayed away.

Exercise 2

In the spurious-regression lab, set the data-generating process to “Two random walks”, T = 300, and click Redraw ten times, jotting down each |t|-statistic. Then switch to “Two stationary series” and do the same. (a) Roughly what fraction of the random-walk draws give |t| > 1.96? What fraction should a valid 5% test give? (b) Explain why the stationary draws behave correctly while the random-walk draws do not.

Show answer

(a) For the random walks you will typically see |t| > 1.96 on the large majority of draws — commonly 70–90%, often with |t| in the double digits. A valid 5% test should reject the true null (\beta = 0) only about 5% of the time. The random-walk regression rejects roughly 15–18 times too often: its true size is far above its nominal 5%. For the stationary series, the rejection rate is close to the correct 5%.

(b) The stationary regressor satisfies the classical OLS assumptions: the errors u_t are I(0) and (here) independent, so the reported standard error is valid and the t-statistic follows (approximately) its textbook distribution. The random-walk regression violates the central assumption: when x and y are independent I(1) series, the true regression error u_t = y_t - \alpha - \beta x_t is itself I(1) and strongly autocorrelated. OLS does not “know” this. The slope \hat\beta never converges — its true spread stays of order 1 — yet the reported standard error collapses toward zero (like T^{-1/2}), so OLS divides a non-vanishing \hat\beta by a standard error that is far too small, inflating |t| to order \sqrt{T} and faking significance. More data makes it worse, not better.

Exercise 3

You run an ADF test (with a constant) on Region Skåne’s monthly primary-care cost and obtain a test statistic of -1.42. The 5% Dickey–Fuller critical value is -2.88. You then run a KPSS test and get a statistic of 0.71 against a 5% critical value of 0.46. (a) What does each test conclude? (b) What is your overall verdict for the series? (c) Why would it be a mistake to compare the ADF statistic to the standard normal critical value of -1.65?

Show answer

(a) ADF: the null is a unit root. The statistic -1.42 is not more negative than -2.88, so we fail to reject the unit root — the ADF test is consistent with non-stationarity. KPSS: the null is stationarity. The statistic 0.71 exceeds 0.46, so we reject stationarity — also pointing to a unit root.

(b) The two tests agree: ADF cannot reject a unit root and KPSS rejects stationarity. This is the confident I(1) verdict. The series should be differenced before any regression, and a level-on-level regression against another series risks being spurious unless cointegration is established.

(c) Under the unit-root null the regressor y_{t-1} is itself non-stationary, so the t-statistic on \hat\rho does not follow the standard normal (or Student-t) distribution — it follows the non-standard Dickey–Fuller distribution, whose quantiles are shifted markedly to the left. Using -1.65 would make you reject the unit root far too easily (here, -1.42 > -1.65 would still fail to reject, but in borderline cases the error flips the conclusion). Always use Dickey–Fuller critical values for an ADF statistic.

Exercise 4

Two cointegrated regional waiting-time series satisfy the estimated ECM \Delta y_t = 0.40\,\Delta x_t - 0.20\, z_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t, \qquad z_{t-1} = y_{t-1} - 5 - 0.9\, x_{t-1}, where y and x are average days to a primary-care appointment in two regions. (a) Interpret the coefficient -0.20. (b) Last month y_{t-1} = 18 and x_{t-1} = 12. Compute the equilibrium error z_{t-1} and the error-correction contribution to \Delta y_t. Which way does it push y, and why does that make sense? (c) What would \gamma = 0 imply?

Show answer

(a) The -0.20 is the speed of adjustment: each month the system closes 20% of any gap between y and its long-run level 5 + 0.9x. Its sign is negative, as required for stability — deviations are corrected rather than amplified. The implied half-life of a deviation is \ln(0.5)/\ln(1-0.20) \approx 3.1 months.

(b) The long-run level for y given x_{t-1}=12 is 5 + 0.9 \times 12 = 15.8 days. The equilibrium error is z_{t-1} = 18 - 15.8 = +2.2 days: y sits above its equilibrium (this region’s waits are longer than the long-run relationship predicts). The error-correction contribution is -0.20 \times 2.2 = -0.44 days. It pushes \Delta y_t down, shortening y’s wait next month — exactly the self-correcting behaviour we want, pulling the above-equilibrium series back toward its long-run level.

(c) If \gamma = 0, the error-correction term vanishes: deviations from the long-run relationship are never corrected, the equilibrium error z_t would itself be I(1), and the two series would not be cointegrated. A non-zero \gamma is precisely what distinguishes a genuine long-run equilibrium from two series that merely happen to be non-stationary.

Key takeaways

  • A unit-root (random-walk) process y_t = y_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t has permanent shocks and variance t\sigma^2 that grows without bound; it is I(1) and must be differenced once to become stationary.
  • Regressing one independent I(1) series on another is spurious: R^2 and |t| are large by chance and the t-statistic diverges with sample size, because the residuals are themselves I(1) and the OLS standard error is far too small.
  • Test for a unit root with the (augmented) Dickey–Fuller test, using its non-standard critical values; pair it with KPSS (whose null is stationarity) for a robust I(0) vs. I(1) verdict, and beware the ADF’s low power against persistent stationary series.
  • Two I(1) series are cointegrated if a linear combination z_t = y_t - \alpha - \beta x_t is stationary; then the level regression is not spurious and \hat\beta is super-consistent.
  • Engle–Granger tests cointegration by ADF-testing the regression residuals (with Engle–Granger critical values); cointegration always implies an error-correction model whose negative speed-of-adjustment \gamma pulls the system back to equilibrium.
