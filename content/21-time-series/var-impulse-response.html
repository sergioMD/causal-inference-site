VAR and Impulse Responses

Dynamics among several series

In this section, you will learn to

  • Write a vector autoregression (VAR) that models several time series jointly, each as a linear function of lags of all of them
  • Test for Granger causality and state precisely what “X Granger-causes Y” does and does not mean
  • Trace an impulse-response function (IRF) by reading the VAR’s companion matrix and taking its powers
  • Explain why identifying a structural shock requires an extra assumption (variable ordering / Cholesky) in one sentence
  • Estimate an IRF the flexible, regression-based way using Jordà local projections, and say when you would prefer it

When One Equation Is Not Enough

Region Skåne’s primärvård (primary care) directorate watches two monthly series side by side: the budgetanslag (operating funding) flowing to its vårdcentraler, measured as deviation from trend in millions of SEK, and the average waiting time for a routine appointment, in days. The policy hunch is dynamic and two-directional. A funding shock this month should shorten waiting times over the coming months as clinics hire and open slots — but with a lag. And waiting times feed back: a month of long queues triggers next month’s emergency funding top-up.

A single regression of waiting time on funding cannot capture this. The two series move together, each responding to its own past and to the other’s past, and a shock to either one ripples through both for months. We need a model of the system, not of one equation in isolation. That model is the vector autoregression.

The VAR, introduced to macroeconomics by Christopher Sims (1980), treats every series as endogenous: each variable is regressed on lags of itself and of every other variable. No variable is privileged as “the” outcome. The payoff is the impulse-response function — a clean answer to the question a policymaker actually asks: if I inject one shock today, what happens to each series over the next two years?

The VAR(1): Two Equations, One System

Stack the two series at month t into a vector. Let y_{1t} be the funding deviation (MSEK) and y_{2t} the waiting-time deviation (days), both written as deviations from their long-run means so we can drop the intercept. A first-order vector autoregression, or VAR(1), writes each variable as a linear function of last month’s values of both, plus a fresh shock:

\begin{aligned} y_{1t} &= a_{11}\,y_{1,t-1} + a_{12}\,y_{2,t-1} + \varepsilon_{1t},\\ y_{2t} &= a_{21}\,y_{1,t-1} + a_{22}\,y_{2,t-1} + \varepsilon_{2t}. \end{aligned}

Read the first line aloud as: “funding this month equals a_{11} times funding last month, plus a_{12} times waiting time last month, plus a funding shock \varepsilon_{1t}.” The coefficient a_{11} is funding’s own persistence; a_{12} is how last month’s queue pushes this month’s funding (the political feedback). Symmetrically in the second line, a_{22} is the inertia of waiting times and a_{21} is how last month’s funding moves this month’s queue.

It is far tidier in matrix form. Collect \mathbf{y}_t = (y_{1t}, y_{2t})', the coefficient matrix \mathbf{A}, and the shock vector \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_t:

\mathbf{y}_t = \mathbf{A}\,\mathbf{y}_{t-1} + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_t, \qquad \mathbf{A} = \begin{pmatrix} a_{11} & a_{12} \\ a_{21} & a_{22} \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_t \sim (\mathbf{0}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}).

Read this as: “the vector \mathbf{y} at time t equals the matrix \mathbf{A} times the vector at t-1, plus a vector of mean-zero shocks with covariance \boldsymbol{\Sigma}.” Everything you know about a scalar AR(1) carries over — it is the same equation y_t = a\,y_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t with numbers promoted to matrices.

A vector autoregression of order p, VAR(p), models a k-vector of series \mathbf{y}_t as

\mathbf{y}_t = \mathbf{c} + \mathbf{A}_1 \mathbf{y}_{t-1} + \mathbf{A}_2 \mathbf{y}_{t-2} + \cdots + \mathbf{A}_p \mathbf{y}_{t-p} + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_t,

where each \mathbf{A}_\ell is a k\times k coefficient matrix, \mathbf{c} is an intercept vector, and \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_t is a vector of serially uncorrelated shocks with covariance \boldsymbol{\Sigma} = \mathrm{E}[\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_t\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_t']. Each equation has the same right-hand-side regressors (the stacked lags), so the VAR can be estimated equation by equation with ordinary least squares.

For the VAR to be well-behaved we assume:

  1. Stationarity (stability). All eigenvalues of the companion matrix lie strictly inside the unit circle (|\lambda| < 1). Equivalently, shocks die out and the IRF decays to zero. For our VAR(1) this is just “the eigenvalues of \mathbf{A} have modulus below 1.” If a series has a unit root, difference it first or model the system as a VECM.
  2. No serial correlation in \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_t. The lag order p is chosen large enough (via AIC/BIC) that the residuals are white noise. Left-over autocorrelation means the dynamics are mis-specified.
  3. Linearity and parameter stability. The matrices \mathbf{A}_\ell are constant over the sample — no structural break in how funding and queues interact.

Granger Causality: Does One Series Help Predict Another?

Before tracing shocks, ask a humbler question: does the funding series carry information about future waiting times beyond what the queue’s own past already tells us? This is Granger causality (Granger, 1969).

In our VAR(1), waiting time is y_{2t} = a_{21}y_{1,t-1} + a_{22}y_{2,t-1} + \varepsilon_{2t}. Funding (y_1) Granger-causes waiting time (y_2) precisely when the coefficient on lagged funding is non-zero, a_{21}\neq 0. With more lags, the test is a joint F/Wald test that all lags of y_1 in the y_2 equation are zero:

H_0:\; a_{21}^{(1)} = a_{21}^{(2)} = \cdots = a_{21}^{(p)} = 0 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \text{$y_1$ does \emph{not} Granger-cause $y_2$.}

Read this as: “the null hypothesis is that every lag of funding has a zero coefficient in the waiting-time equation; rejecting it means past funding improves the forecast of future waiting times.”

Granger causality is predictive, not structural. “y_1 Granger-causes y_2” means past y_1 helps forecast y_2 — nothing more. It can fail to be true causation (a third variable, like a regional reform, drives both) and it can miss true causation (if markets are forward-looking, queues may already price in anticipated funding, so the past adds nothing). It is also directional and can run both ways: funding may Granger-cause queues and queues Granger-cause funding. Treat it as a Granger-predictability test and reserve “cause” for designs with identifying assumptions.

The Impulse-Response Function

Now the centerpiece. Hit the system with a one-time unit shock today — say \varepsilon_{1,0}=1 (a one-MSEK funding surprise), with no further shocks — and let the VAR run. The impulse-response function is the path each variable traces out over horizons h = 0, 1, 2, \dots as that single shock propagates and decays.

For the VAR(1) the algebra is beautiful. Start from \mathbf{y}_0 = \boldsymbol{\delta} (the shock) and set all later shocks to zero. Then \mathbf{y}_1 = \mathbf{A}\boldsymbol{\delta}, \mathbf{y}_2 = \mathbf{A}^2\boldsymbol{\delta}, and in general:

\mathrm{IRF}(h) = \mathbf{A}^h\,\boldsymbol{\delta}.

Read this as: “the response at horizon h is the coefficient matrix raised to the h-th power, times the initial shock vector.” The IRF is literally the powers of \mathbf{A}. Because the system is stable, \mathbf{A}^h \to \mathbf{0}, so every response eventually decays back to zero — shocks are temporary. The off-diagonal entries of \mathbf{A} are what let a funding shock show up in the waiting-time path: cross-effects are why we bothered with a system at all.

The IRF is the model’s policy headline. A regression coefficient answers “how much does y move with x, holding lags fixed for one period?” The IRF answers the question a director actually asks: “if I add one MSEK today, by how much — and for how long — do queues fall over the next two years?” It bundles the entire dynamic chain (the direct effect, the feedback, the persistence) into a single readable curve, horizon by horizon.

The identification problem in one sentence

There is a catch. The shocks \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_t are usually correlated across equations (\boldsymbol{\Sigma} is not diagonal), so a “pure” one-MSEK funding shock with nothing happening to queues is not something the data alone can isolate — recovering economically meaningful structural shocks requires an extra assumption, most commonly a recursive ordering imposed through the Cholesky decomposition of \boldsymbol{\Sigma} (e.g., “funding can move within the month but queues react only with a lag”), which is exactly what a structural VAR (SVAR) formalizes.

See It: A 2-Variable VAR and Its Impulse Responses

The lab below is the VAR(1) above. You set the off-diagonal spillover coefficient a_{21} — how strongly last month’s funding shock bleeds into this month’s waiting time — and the persistence of each series. Then you inject a one-time shock into one variable and watch the IRF: the response paths of both series, horizon by horizon, computed exactly as \mathbf{A}^h\boldsymbol{\delta}.

// 2x2 matrix-vector product, used to step the system forward one period.
tsvar_matvec = (M, v) => [
  M[0][0] * v[0] + M[0][1] * v[1],
  M[1][0] * v[0] + M[1][1] * v[1]
]
// Stability check via the eigenvalues of A. For a 2x2 matrix the eigenvalues
// solve λ² − (trace)λ + det = 0; the system is stable iff BOTH have modulus < 1.
// We report the spectral radius (largest modulus) so the reader sees how close
// to the unit circle the dynamics sit.
tsvar_spectralRadius = {
  const a = tsvar_A[0][0], b = tsvar_A[0][1],
        c = tsvar_A[1][0], d = tsvar_A[1][1];
  const tr = a + d, det = a * d - b * c;
  const disc = tr * tr - 4 * det;
  if (disc >= 0) {
    const s = Math.sqrt(disc);
    return Math.max(Math.abs((tr + s) / 2), Math.abs((tr - s) / 2));
  } else {
    // Complex pair λ = (tr ± i√|disc|)/2; modulus = √det.
    return Math.sqrt(Math.abs(det));
  }
}
// Number of horizons to trace the impulse response over (h = 0..H months).
tsvar_H = 24
// The impulse response: start from the unit shock δ and apply A repeatedly,
// IRF(h) = Aʰ δ. We tabulate h = 0..H for both series.
tsvar_irf = {
  const delta = tsvar_shockVar.startsWith("Funding") ? [1, 0] : [0, 1];
  const rows = [];

    rows.push({ h, series: "Waiting time (y₂)",  value: v[1] });
    v = tsvar_matvec(tsvar_A, v);   // advance: v ← A v
  }
  return rows;
}

How to Read the Impulse-Response Chart

Work through these in order; each isolates one moving part of the VAR.

Step 1: Read the two curves. The red curve is the response of funding (y_1) and the slate curve the response of waiting time (y_2), traced over horizons h=0 (the month of the shock) to h=24. Height is the deviation from baseline caused by the single shock at h=0. Both curves start from the shock vector and then evolve as \mathbf{A}^h\boldsymbol{\delta}.

Step 2: See the impact. With the shock on Funding, at h=0 the red curve jumps to 1 (the unit shock) and the slate curve sits at 0 — the queue has not reacted yet. That one-period lag is the whole point of a dynamic model: cross-effects take time.

Step 3: Grow the spillover. Drag spillover a_{21} more negative. Now the slate waiting-time curve dips below zero starting at h=1: the funding shock works its way into the queue and shortens it (negative response = fewer days). Make a_{21} positive instead and the queue lengthens — the sign of one off-diagonal coefficient flips the entire policy story.

Step 4: Tune persistence. Raise persistence a_{11} toward 0.95. The red curve now decays slowly — a funding surprise lingers for many months — which prolongs the slate response too, because the queue keeps being fed by still-elevated funding. Persistence stretches the IRF horizontally.

Step 5: Break stability. Push both persistence sliders high and open up a_{12} and a_{21} so they reinforce. Watch the stability banner: when the spectral radius reaches 1, it turns red and the curves stop decaying — the system has a unit root and the IRF no longer settles. This is the stationarity assumption failing in real time.

Local Projections: IRFs Without the VAR

The VAR gets its IRF “for free” as \mathbf{A}^h\boldsymbol{\delta}, but that elegance has a cost: every horizon’s response is forced to be consistent with one estimated \mathbf{A}, so a mis-specified \mathbf{A} contaminates the IRF at all horizons. Jordà (2005) proposed a refreshingly direct alternative: estimate the response at each horizon h with its own regression.

A local projection estimates the response of y at horizon h by regressing the future value y_{t+h} on the current shock (or impulse variable) x_t, controlling for lags:

y_{t+h} = \alpha_h + \beta_h\, x_t + \sum_{\ell=1}^{p}\boldsymbol{\gamma}_{h\ell}'\,\mathbf{w}_{t-\ell} + u_{t+h}, \qquad h = 0, 1, 2, \dots

The impulse response at horizon h is the sequence of slopes \widehat\beta_0, \widehat\beta_1, \widehat\beta_2, \dots — one coefficient from one regression per horizon. Here \mathbf{w}_{t-\ell} are control lags and u_{t+h} is the error.

Read the recipe as: “to get the response at horizon h, regress y measured h months ahead on today’s shock; the coefficient \beta_h is the IRF at that horizon, and you run a separate regression for each h.” You are not iterating a matrix — you are reading off a coefficient directly at each lead.

Local projections trade efficiency for robustness. Because each horizon is its own regression, an error in one does not propagate to the others, and the method handles non-linearities, state-dependence, and instrumented shocks (LP-IV) with ordinary regression machinery. The price is noisier estimates at long horizons and wider confidence bands. Rule of thumb: a stable, well-specified small system favors the VAR’s efficiency; messy dynamics, a clean external shock, or a desire to avoid imposing the whole \mathbf{A} favor local projections.

e = rng.multivariate_normal([0, 0], cov, size=n)
fund = np.zeros(n); wait = np.zeros(n)
for t in range(1, n):
    # True VAR(1): funding persists; funding shortens next month's queue (a21<0)
    fund[t] = 0.60 * fund[t-1] + 0.00 * wait[t-1] + e[t, 0]
    wait[t] = -0.35 * fund[t-1] + 0.70 * wait[t-1] + e[t, 1]
Y = pd.DataFrame({"funding": fund, "waiting": wait})

# ---- Choose lag length, then fit the VAR by OLS ----
model = VAR(Y)
print(model.select_order(8).summary())     # AIC/BIC pick p
fit = model.fit(1)
print(fit.summary())

# ---- Granger causality: does funding help predict waiting time? ----
print(fit.test_causality("waiting", ["funding"], kind="f"))

# ---- Impulse response: a funding shock → both series, 24 months out ----
irf = fit.irf(24)
irf.plot(orth=True)        # orth=True → Cholesky-identified (recursive) SVAR

# ---- Same IRF via Jordà local projection (one regression per horizon) ----
H = 24
betas = []
X = sm.add_constant(np.column_stack([Y["funding"].shift(1),
                                     Y["waiting"].shift(1)]))
for h in range(H + 1):
    y_h = Y["waiting"].shift(-h)                 # waiting time h months ahead
    d = pd.concat([y_h, pd.DataFrame(X, index=Y.index)], axis=1).dropna()
    # Design columns are [const, funding_{t-1}, waiting_{t-1}]; the funding
    # impulse coefficient is at position 1 (position 0 is the intercept).
    beta = sm.OLS(d.iloc[:, 0], d.iloc[:, 1:]).fit().params.iloc[1]  # coef on fund_{t-1}
    betas.append(beta)
print("LP IRF of waiting time to a funding impulse:", np.round(betas, 3))

Both routes deliver the same object — the response of each series to a shock, horizon by horizon. The VAR squeezes it from one estimated \mathbf{A}; the local projection reads it off horizon-specific regressions. On clean, stationary data they agree closely; they diverge when the VAR is mis-specified, which is exactly when the local projection’s robustness earns its keep.

Math Details: The Companion Form and the IRF as Powers of the Matrix

Why a VAR(1) is all you ever need: the companion form

A VAR(p) looks more complicated than a VAR(1), but it is a VAR(1) in disguise. Take a VAR(2) in k variables, \mathbf{y}_t = \mathbf{A}_1\mathbf{y}_{t-1} + \mathbf{A}_2\mathbf{y}_{t-2} + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_t. Stack the current and lagged vectors into a 2k-dimensional state \mathbf{z}_t = (\mathbf{y}_t',\,\mathbf{y}_{t-1}')'. Then

\underbrace{\begin{pmatrix}\mathbf{y}_t \\ \mathbf{y}_{t-1}\end{pmatrix}}_{\mathbf{z}_t} = \underbrace{\begin{pmatrix}\mathbf{A}_1 & \mathbf{A}_2 \\ \mathbf{I}_k & \mathbf{0}\end{pmatrix}}_{\mathbf{F},\ \text{the companion matrix}} \underbrace{\begin{pmatrix}\mathbf{y}_{t-1} \\ \mathbf{y}_{t-2}\end{pmatrix}}_{\mathbf{z}_{t-1}} + \begin{pmatrix}\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_t \\ \mathbf{0}\end{pmatrix}.

The bottom block is the bookkeeping identity \mathbf{y}_{t-1}=\mathbf{y}_{t-1}. So every VAR(p) is a VAR(1) in the stacked state, \mathbf{z}_t = \mathbf{F}\mathbf{z}_{t-1} + \boldsymbol{\eta}_t. Stability is the condition that all eigenvalues of \mathbf{F} have modulus <1, and everything below for the VAR(1) applies to \mathbf{F}.

The IRF as powers of \mathbf{A}

For a stable VAR(1), \mathbf{y}_t = \mathbf{A}\mathbf{y}_{t-1} + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_t, substitute backwards repeatedly:

\mathbf{y}_t = \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_t + \mathbf{A}\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_{t-1} + \mathbf{A}^2\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_{t-2} + \cdots = \sum_{j=0}^{\infty} \mathbf{A}^j\,\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_{t-j}.

This is the vector moving-average (VMA) representation: today’s vector is a weighted sum of all past shocks, with weight \mathbf{A}^j on the shock j periods ago. The convergence of the sum requires \mathbf{A}^j\to\mathbf{0}, i.e. stability. The IRF reads the weights off directly:

\frac{\partial \mathbf{y}_{t+h}}{\partial \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_t'} = \mathbf{A}^h \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \mathrm{IRF}(h) = \mathbf{A}^h\,\boldsymbol{\delta},

the response at horizon h to a shock \boldsymbol{\delta} today. Each column of \mathbf{A}^h is the response of all variables to a unit shock in one variable.

A worked closed form via eigen-decomposition

If \mathbf{A} = \mathbf{V}\boldsymbol{\Lambda}\mathbf{V}^{-1} with eigenvalues \lambda_1,\lambda_2 on the diagonal of \boldsymbol{\Lambda}, then \mathbf{A}^h = \mathbf{V}\boldsymbol{\Lambda}^h\mathbf{V}^{-1}, and since \boldsymbol{\Lambda}^h = \mathrm{diag}(\lambda_1^h,\lambda_2^h) the IRF is a sum of geometric decays \lambda_1^h and \lambda_2^h. Take the interactive’s default lower-triangular case a_{12}=0:

\mathbf{A} = \begin{pmatrix} 0.6 & 0 \\ -0.35 & 0.7 \end{pmatrix}.

Its eigenvalues are the diagonal entries \lambda_1 = 0.6 and \lambda_2 = 0.7 (triangular matrix). For a funding shock \boldsymbol{\delta} = (1,0)', the funding response is pure a_{11}^h = 0.6^h, and the waiting-time response can be derived in closed form as

\mathrm{IRF}_2(h) = a_{21}\,\frac{a_{22}^{\,h} - a_{11}^{\,h}}{a_{22}-a_{11}} = -0.35\cdot\frac{0.7^{\,h} - 0.6^{\,h}}{0.7 - 0.6}.

Check the first horizons against the chart: h=0 gives 0 (queue has not reacted), h=1 gives -0.35\cdot(0.7-0.6)/0.1 = -0.35, and h=2 gives -0.35\cdot(0.49-0.36)/0.1 = -0.455. The response builds to a trough and then decays as the slower root 0.7^h dominates — exactly the hump-shaped slate curve in the lab. The spectral radius is \max(0.6,0.7)=0.7<1, so it converges to zero. \checkmark

Exercises

Exercise 1

A two-variable VAR(1) for Region Östergötland links monthly sjukfrånvaro (sick leave, y_1, % deviation) and the number of unfilled vårdcentral vacancies (y_2, deviation) with coefficient matrix

\mathbf{A} = \begin{pmatrix} 0.5 & 0.2 \\ 0.0 & 0.4 \end{pmatrix}.

  1. Is the system stable? (b) Does sick leave Granger-cause vacancies? Do vacancies Granger-cause sick leave? (c) Compute the response of sick leave to a unit vacancy shock \boldsymbol{\delta}=(0,1)' at horizons h=0,1,2.
Show answer

(a) \mathbf{A} is upper-triangular, so its eigenvalues are the diagonal entries 0.5 and 0.4. Both have modulus <1, so the system is stable (spectral radius 0.5); shocks decay.

(b) Sick-leave Granger-causes vacancies iff a_{21}\neq 0. Here a_{21}=0, so sick leave does not Granger-cause vacancies. Vacancies Granger-cause sick leave iff a_{12}\neq 0; here a_{12}=0.2\neq 0, so vacancies do Granger-cause sick leave. Causality is one-directional.

(c) \mathrm{IRF}(h)=\mathbf{A}^h\boldsymbol{\delta} with \boldsymbol{\delta}=(0,1)'.

  • h=0: \mathbf{A}^0\boldsymbol{\delta} = \boldsymbol{\delta} = (0,1)', so sick-leave response =0.
  • h=1: \mathbf{A}\boldsymbol{\delta} = (0.5\cdot0 + 0.2\cdot1,\; 0\cdot0 + 0.4\cdot1)' = (0.2,\,0.4)', so sick-leave response =0.2.
  • h=2: \mathbf{A}^2\boldsymbol{\delta} = \mathbf{A}(0.2,0.4)' = (0.5\cdot0.2 + 0.2\cdot0.4,\; 0.4\cdot0.4)' = (0.18,\,0.16)', so sick-leave response =0.18.

The sick-leave response rises to 0.20 at h=1 then decays toward zero. \checkmark

Exercise 2

Explain, in your own words, why “funding Granger-causes waiting times” is not the same as “extra funding will cause waiting times to fall.” Give one Swedish primary-care scenario where Granger causality would mislead a policymaker.

Show answer

Granger causality is a statement about forecasting: past funding improves the prediction of future waiting times beyond the queue’s own history. It says nothing about a controlled intervention. Two ways it misleads:

  1. Common cause. A regional reform may simultaneously raise funding and reform triage so queues fall. Funding then predicts shorter queues (Granger-causes them) without causing them — the reform drives both. Pouring money in without the reform might do nothing.

  2. Anticipation / reverse information flow. Suppose the region announces a funding rule that tops up any clinic whose queue lengthens. Then long queues today predict more funding next month, so queues Granger-cause funding — but the queue is not “causing” the budget in any policy-actionable sense; it is triggering a pre-committed rule.

Scenario: A director sees that funding Granger-causes shorter queues and concludes “if I add 10 MSEK, queues will fall.” But in the data, funding rose in years when a national digital-triage rollout also happened. The triage rollout is the true cause; the standalone 10 MSEK, without triage, leaves queues unchanged. Granger causality flagged predictability, not a usable lever.

Exercise 3

You have a clean, externally identified one-time funding shock (a quasi-random central-government grant that hit some regions and not others). Would you estimate the IRF with a VAR or with a Jordà local projection? Give two reasons, and state one drawback of your choice.

Show answer

Prefer a local projection (LP-IV style). Reasons:

  1. The shock is already identified externally. You do not need the VAR’s Cholesky ordering to recover a structural shock — you can plug the known grant directly in as x_t and read \beta_h off each horizon. This sidesteps the contestable ordering assumption entirely.

  2. Robustness to mis-specification. Each horizon is its own regression, so an error in the short-run dynamics does not propagate to long-horizon responses the way a wrong \mathbf{A} would contaminate every power \mathbf{A}^h. LP also accommodates non-linear or state-dependent responses (e.g., the grant working differently in understaffed regions).

Drawback: Local projections are less efficient — the horizon-by-horizon estimates are noisier and the confidence bands are wider, especially at long horizons, because they do not borrow strength across horizons the way the VAR’s single \mathbf{A} does. The IRF can also look jagged rather than smooth.

Exercise 4

In the interactive, set the shock on Funding, a_{11}=0.6, a_{22}=0.7, a_{12}=0, and a_{21}=-0.35 (the defaults). (a) Using the closed form from the Math Details, predict the waiting-time response at h=3 and confirm it against the chart tooltip. (b) Now raise a_{22} to 0.9. Qualitatively, what happens to when the waiting-time response bottoms out and how long it takes to settle? Why?

Show answer

(a) The closed form is \mathrm{IRF}_2(h) = -0.35\cdot\dfrac{0.7^{h}-0.6^{h}}{0.7-0.6}. At h=3:

0.7^3 = 0.343,\quad 0.6^3 = 0.216,\quad \mathrm{IRF}_2(3) = -0.35\cdot\frac{0.343-0.216}{0.1} = -0.35\cdot 1.27 = -0.4445.

So the waiting-time response at h=3 is about -0.444 days — hover the slate curve at h=3 and the tooltip reads \approx -0.444. \checkmark Compare it to the h=2 value of -0.455: since -0.444 is less negative (shallower) than -0.455, the curve has already passed its trough. The trough sits exactly at h=2 (-0.455), and by h=3 the response is beginning to recover toward zero.

(b) Raising a_{22} to 0.9 makes the queue’s own inertia much larger. The dominant root becomes 0.9 instead of 0.7, so (i) the trough occurs later (the response keeps deepening for more months before the decay wins) and (ii) the curve takes far longer to settle back to zero, since the response now decays like 0.9^h rather than 0.7^h — a much slower geometric decline. Persistence stretches the IRF out in time. The “settles below 0.05” tile jumps to a much larger horizon.

Key takeaways

  • A VAR models several series jointly: each variable is a linear function of lags of all of them, \mathbf{y}_t = \mathbf{A}_1\mathbf{y}_{t-1}+\cdots+\mathbf{A}_p\mathbf{y}_{t-p}+\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_t, estimable equation-by-equation by OLS.
  • Granger causality tests whether past y_1 improves the forecast of y_2 (a_{21}\neq0); it is predictive, directional, and not the same as structural causation.
  • The impulse-response function is the powers of the coefficient matrix, \mathrm{IRF}(h)=\mathbf{A}^h\boldsymbol{\delta}; a stable system (all eigenvalues inside the unit circle) has IRFs that decay back to zero.
  • Turning correlated reduced-form shocks into a structural shock needs an extra assumption — a recursive ordering via the Cholesky decomposition (SVAR) is the standard choice.
  • Jordà local projections estimate the IRF with one regression per horizon: more robust to mis-specification and external shocks, but noisier and less efficient than the VAR.
