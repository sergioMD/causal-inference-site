Stationarity and ARIMA

The building blocks of time-series modeling

In this section, you will learn to

  • Define (weak) stationarity — constant mean, constant variance, and an autocovariance that depends only on the lag — and explain why it is the precondition for valid time-series inference
  • Read the autocorrelation function (ACF) and partial autocorrelation function (PACF) as diagnostic fingerprints that distinguish AR, MA, and ARMA dynamics
  • Write the AR(p), MA(q), and ARMA(p,q) models, and state the stationarity condition |\phi| < 1 for the AR(1)
  • Use differencing to remove a trend, turning a non-stationary series into a stationary one and giving the ARIMA(p,d,q) model, with a note on seasonality
  • Fit an ARIMA model and produce a forecast in both R (forecast/fable) and Python (statsmodels SARIMAX)

The Question Behind Every Time Series

Region Uppsala publishes the number of besök i primärvården (primary-care visits) at its vårdcentraler every month. The verksamhetschef (operations manager) staring at the dashboard does not want a single average — she wants to know what next month will look like, and how confident she can be. January is always busy; July empties out as families leave for semester. Visits this month are tied to visits last month: a flu wave does not vanish in thirty days. The observations are not a cloud of independent draws — they are a chain, ordered in time, where each value leans on the ones before it.

This ordering and this leaning are the whole subject. A cross-section asks “how do units differ from each other?” A time series asks “how does one unit evolve through time, and what does its past tell us about its future?” The dependence between today and yesterday — serial dependence — is exactly the structure we exploit to forecast, and exactly the structure that breaks the independence assumptions behind every standard error you have met so far.

This page builds the machinery from the ground up. We first pin down the one property that makes a time series learnable — stationarity — then read its memory off the autocorrelation function, then assemble the AR, MA, and ARIMA models that turn that memory into forecasts. The next page confronts the case where stationarity fails outright: the random walk, unit roots, and the spurious regressions they cause.

What Makes a Time Series Learnable

A time series is a sequence \{y_t\}_{t=1}^{T} indexed by time. We only ever observe one realization of it — one history of Uppsala’s primary-care visits, not a thousand parallel Uppsalas. To learn anything about the underlying process from that single path, the process must hold still long enough for its past to be informative about its future. That “holding still” is stationarity.

A series \{y_t\} is (weakly) stationary if its first two moments do not change over time:

  1. Constant mean: E[y_t] = \mu for all t.
  2. Constant variance: \operatorname{Var}(y_t) = \gamma_0 for all t.
  3. Lag-only autocovariance: \operatorname{Cov}(y_t, y_{t-k}) = \gamma_k depends only on the gap k, never on the date t.

Read condition 3 as: “the covariance between the series now and the series k periods ago is the same number \gamma_k whether ‘now’ is January 2019 or January 2024 — only the distance k matters, not where you are in calendar time.”

Weak stationarity (also called covariance stationarity) is what we need in practice; it asks only that the mean, variance, and autocovariances be stable, not that the whole distribution be frozen. A series that drifts upward, or whose swings grow wider over the years, violates it. Most raw economic and health series — visit counts that grow with the population, prices that inflate, sick-leave (sjukfrånvaro) that trends with the labor market — are not stationary as recorded, which is precisely why differencing (below) earns its place.

Why stationarity is not optional. With one realization, our only hope of estimating \mu is to average across time: \hat\mu = \frac1T\sum_t y_t. That average estimates a single, well-defined \mu only if E[y_t] is the same at every t. If the mean drifts, the time average chases a moving target and converges to nothing meaningful. Stationarity is what lets a single path stand in for the whole ensemble — it is the time-series analogue of “i.i.d. sampling” that makes averages, variances, and standard errors interpretable.

Memory: The Autocorrelation Function

Serial dependence is measured by how strongly y_t correlates with its own past. Scaling the autocovariance \gamma_k by the variance \gamma_0 gives a unit-free correlation.

The autocorrelation function (ACF) at lag k is the correlation between the series and its own k-step-lagged self:

\rho_k \;=\; \frac{\operatorname{Cov}(y_t,\,y_{t-k})}{\operatorname{Var}(y_t)} \;=\; \frac{\gamma_k}{\gamma_0}, \qquad \rho_0 = 1.

Read this as: “the lag-k autocorrelation is the covariance between y_t and y_{t-k} divided by the variance — a number in [-1, 1] telling you how much of today’s deviation from the mean is shared with the deviation k months ago.” At lag 0 a series is perfectly correlated with itself, so \rho_0 = 1 always.

The partial autocorrelation function (PACF) at lag k measures the correlation between y_t and y_{t-k} after removing the influence of the intervening lags y_{t-1}, \dots, y_{t-k+1}. It is the “direct” link at lag k, net of everything in between. The two functions together are the diagnostic fingerprint of a model:

Model ACF PACF
AR(p) tails off (decays geometrically) cuts off after lag p
MA(q) cuts off after lag q tails off
ARMA(p,q) tails off tails off

Read the table as: an AR process leaves a sharp PACF (it has exactly p direct lags) and a smoothly decaying ACF; an MA process is the mirror image. This duality is the single most useful thing the ACF/PACF buys you — it turns “which model?” into “look at two pictures.”

The Building Blocks: AR, MA, ARMA

Autoregression: AR(p)

An autoregressive model says the present is a linear function of its own recent past plus a fresh shock. The order-1 case, AR(1), is the workhorse:

y_t = c + \phi\, y_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t, \qquad \varepsilon_t \sim \text{white noise}(0,\sigma^2).

Read this as: “this month’s visits equal a constant c, plus a fraction \phi of last month’s visits, plus an unpredictable shock \varepsilon_t.” The coefficient \phi is the persistence: a flu surge of size \varepsilon this month carries \phi of itself into next month, \phi^2 into the month after, and so on — an exponentially fading echo. The general AR(p) just adds more lags: y_t = c + \phi_1 y_{t-1} + \dots + \phi_p y_{t-p} + \varepsilon_t.

The AR(1) stationarity condition. The AR(1) is (weakly) stationary if and only if

|\phi| < 1.

Read this as: “the persistence coefficient must be strictly inside the unit interval (-1, 1).” If |\phi| < 1, shocks decay and the series reverts to its mean \mu = c/(1-\phi). At \phi = 1 the process becomes a random walk — shocks never die, the variance grows without bound, and stationarity fails. That boundary case is the unit root of the next page. For higher-order AR(p), the analogous condition is that all roots of the characteristic polynomial lie outside the unit circle.

Moving average: MA(q)

A moving-average model says the present is a weighted sum of the current and recent shocks — not of past values:

y_t = \mu + \varepsilon_t + \theta_1 \varepsilon_{t-1} + \dots + \theta_q \varepsilon_{t-q}.

Read this as: “this month equals a baseline \mu plus today’s shock plus echoes of the last q shocks, weighted by \theta_1, \dots, \theta_q.” An MA(q) has memory of exactly q periods: a shock affects the series for q months and then is gone entirely. This is why its ACF cuts off after lag q — there is simply no correlation beyond the window of remembered shocks.

Putting them together: ARMA(p,q)

Real series often need both — some persistence in levels (AR) and some short-lived shock structure (MA). The combined model is

y_t = c + \underbrace{\phi_1 y_{t-1} + \dots + \phi_p y_{t-p}}_{\text{AR}(p)\ \text{part}} + \varepsilon_t + \underbrace{\theta_1 \varepsilon_{t-1} + \dots + \theta_q \varepsilon_{t-q}}_{\text{MA}(q)\ \text{part}}.

ARMA(p,q) is the flexible engine of stationary time-series modeling: a small handful of parameters can reproduce a wide range of ACF/PACF shapes. But it assumes the series is already stationary. When it is not, we difference first.

From ARMA to ARIMA: Differencing

Most raw series trend. Uppsala’s monthly visit count grows as the population grows; it is not mean-stationary. The fix is differencing: instead of modeling the level, model the change.

The first difference of a series is

\Delta y_t = y_t - y_{t-1}.

A series that becomes stationary after differencing d times is integrated of order d, written I(d). Modeling an ARMA(p,q) on the d-times-differenced series gives the ARIMA(p,d,q) model: AutoRegressive Integrated Moving Average.

Read ARIMA(p,d,q) as: “difference the series d times to make it stationary, then fit p autoregressive lags and q moving-average lags to the result.” A linear trend is removed by d=1; a quadratic trend by d=2. Most economic and health series need d=0 or d=1.

Do not over-difference. Differencing a series that is already stationary injects an artificial -1 autocorrelation at lag 1 (an MA(1) unit root) and inflates the variance of your forecasts. Difference only as much as a unit-root test (next page) and the ACF tell you to. The mantra: difference to remove a trend, never reflexively. Equally, under-differencing leaves a trend in the residuals and the model’s standard errors are not trustworthy.

Seasonality. Monthly health data carries a yearly rhythm: every January spikes, every July dips. A seasonal difference y_t - y_{t-12} removes a fixed monthly pattern, and the SARIMA(p,d,q)(P,D,Q)_s model adds seasonal AR/MA terms at the seasonal lag s (here s = 12). For Swedish monthly primary-care data, a seasonal component is almost always required — a non-seasonal ARIMA will leave a tell-tale spike at lag 12 in the residual ACF.

How to Read the AR(1) Simulator

Work through these in order; each isolates one idea.

Step 1: Start at white noise. Drag persistence \phi all the way to 0. The top series jitters around its mean \mu with no memory at all — each month is an independent draw. The ACF below is a single spike of height 1 at lag 0 and essentially zero everywhere else. This is the “no serial dependence” baseline: a time series with nothing to forecast beyond its mean.

Step 2: Add persistence. Raise \phi to about 0.6. The series now wanders — runs of above-average months and runs of below-average months appear, because each value pulls the next toward itself. The ACF stems decay geometrically: \rho_1 = \phi, \rho_2 = \phi^2, and so on. Read the “Shock half-life” tile: at \phi = 0.6 a flu surge has faded to half its size in roughly 1.4 months.

Step 3: Approach the random walk. Push \phi toward 0.98. The series becomes a slow, smooth drift that can stay far from its mean for years; the ACF barely decays at all over 18 lags. The “Regime” tile turns red and the half-life explodes toward infinity — shocks essentially never die. This is the edge of stationarity: at \phi = 1 exactly, the process is a random walk and the mean-reverting pull vanishes entirely (the subject of the next page).

Step 4: Toggle the MA term. Turn on Add an MA(1) term \theta and set \theta to +0.4. The series gains one extra period of short-run smoothing layered on top of the AR persistence — consecutive months are stitched a little tighter because they share the previous shock. Set \theta negative and consecutive months instead tend to zig-zag, partly cancelling. This is the MA part of ARMA: memory carried by shocks, not by levels.

Step 5: Resample. Click Draw a new random sample a few times at a fixed \phi. The individual path changes every time — it is one realization of a random process — but its character (smoothness, wandering, the decay rate of the ACF) is governed entirely by \phi, not by the particular shocks. That invariance across redraws is exactly what stationarity guarantees: the structure is stable even though the path is not.

Forecasting Intuition

Once an ARIMA model is fitted, forecasting is mechanical: feed the model its own recent values (and shocks) and roll forward. For the AR(1), the h-step-ahead forecast is

\hat y_{T+h} = \mu + \phi^{h}\,(y_T - \mu).

Read this as: “the forecast h months out equals the long-run mean \mu, plus the current deviation y_T - \mu shrunk by \phi^h.” Because |\phi| < 1, the factor \phi^h \to 0 as the horizon h grows: all forecasts decay back to the mean, fast if \phi is small, slowly if \phi is near 1. This is the single most important forecasting intuition for stationary series — the distant future is just the unconditional mean, and the forecast interval widens to the unconditional variance. A non-stationary (random-walk) series behaves entirely differently: its best forecast is the last value and the interval fans out without bound, which is exactly why distinguishing the two cases (next page) matters so much in practice.

Math Details: AR(1) mean, variance, ACF, and the stationarity condition

Start from the AR(1) in deviation form. Write \tilde y_t = y_t - \mu so the constant drops out:

\tilde y_t = \phi\,\tilde y_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t, \qquad E[\varepsilon_t] = 0,\ \operatorname{Var}(\varepsilon_t)=\sigma^2,\ \varepsilon_t \perp \tilde y_{t-1}.

Mean

Take expectations of the level equation y_t = c + \phi y_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t. Under stationarity E[y_t] = E[y_{t-1}] = \mu, so

\mu = c + \phi\mu \;\Longrightarrow\; \mu = \frac{c}{1-\phi},

which requires \phi \ne 1. (At \phi = 1 the mean is undefined — a first signal of non-stationarity.)

Variance

Take the variance of \tilde y_t = \phi \tilde y_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t. Because \varepsilon_t is independent of \tilde y_{t-1}, the cross term vanishes:

\operatorname{Var}(\tilde y_t) = \phi^2 \operatorname{Var}(\tilde y_{t-1}) + \sigma^2.

Under stationarity \operatorname{Var}(\tilde y_t) = \operatorname{Var}(\tilde y_{t-1}) = \gamma_0, so

\gamma_0 = \phi^2 \gamma_0 + \sigma^2 \;\Longrightarrow\; \gamma_0 = \frac{\sigma^2}{1 - \phi^2}.

For this to be a finite, positive variance we need 1 - \phi^2 > 0, i.e. |\phi| < 1. This is the stationarity condition. As \phi \to 1, \gamma_0 \to \infty: the variance blows up, the series can wander arbitrarily far from its mean, and weak stationarity fails.

Autocovariance and ACF

Multiply \tilde y_t = \phi \tilde y_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t by \tilde y_{t-k} (for k \ge 1) and take expectations. Since \varepsilon_t is uncorrelated with the past \tilde y_{t-k}:

\gamma_k = E[\tilde y_t \tilde y_{t-k}] = \phi\, E[\tilde y_{t-1}\tilde y_{t-k}] = \phi\,\gamma_{k-1}.

This recursion \gamma_k = \phi\,\gamma_{k-1} unwinds to \gamma_k = \phi^k \gamma_0. Dividing by \gamma_0 gives the autocorrelation function:

\boxed{\;\rho_k = \frac{\gamma_k}{\gamma_0} = \phi^{\,k}\;}, \qquad k = 0, 1, 2, \dots

The ACF decays geometrically at rate \phi — exactly the envelope drawn in the simulator. When 0 < \phi < 1 it decays monotonically; when -1 < \phi < 0 it alternates sign while decaying in magnitude. The geometric decay (rather than a sharp cutoff) is the AR signature in the ACF; the PACF, by contrast, cuts off after lag 1 for an AR(1) because there is exactly one direct lag.

Below we simulate a seasonal monthly primary-care series, inspect its ACF/PACF, let an automatic routine pick the orders, and forecast 12 months ahead with a prediction interval. The R panel uses the forecast/fable ecosystem; the Python panel uses statsmodels SARIMAX.

library(fpp3)        # tsibble + fable + feasts (tidy time series)

clinic |> gg_tsdisplay(difference(visits), plot_type = "partial")

  model(auto = ARIMA(visits))          # searches p,d,q (+ seasonal P,D,Q)

import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from statsmodels.tsa.statespace.sarimax import SARIMAX
from statsmodels.graphics.tsaplots import plot_acf, plot_pacf

# ---- Simulate 10 years of monthly primary-care visits ----------------
rng = np.random.default_rng(2024)
n, mu = 120, 95
t = np.arange(n)
trend  = 0.15 * t                                  # slow upward drift
season = 8 * np.sin(2 * np.pi * t / 12)            # yearly rhythm

# AR(1) noise with phi = 0.6
phi, sigma = 0.6, 6.0
eps = rng.normal(0, sigma, n)
ar = np.zeros(n)
for k in range(1, n):
    ar[k] = phi * ar[k - 1] + eps[k]

visits = mu + trend + season + ar
idx = pd.period_range("2014-01", periods=n, freq="M")
y = pd.Series(visits, index=idx, name="visits")

# ---- Diagnose: ACF and PACF of the first difference -----------------
dy = y.diff().dropna()
fig, ax = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(11, 3))
plot_acf(dy, lags=24, ax=ax[0]); ax[0].set_title("ACF of Δ visits")
plot_pacf(dy, lags=24, ax=ax[1]); ax[1].set_title("PACF of Δ visits")

# ---- Fit a seasonal ARIMA(1,1,0)(0,1,1)[12] by ML -------------------
model = SARIMAX(y, order=(1, 1, 0), seasonal_order=(0, 1, 1, 12),
                enforce_stationarity=True, enforce_invertibility=True)
res = model.fit(disp=False)
print(res.summary())

# ---- Forecast 12 months ahead with a 95% prediction interval --------
fc = res.get_forecast(steps=12)
mean_fc = fc.predicted_mean
ci = fc.conf_int(alpha=0.05)

ax = y.plot(label="observed", color="#990000")
mean_fc.plot(ax=ax, label="forecast", color="#3d5a6c")
ax.fill_between(ci.index, ci.iloc[:, 0], ci.iloc[:, 1],
                color="#3d5a6c", alpha=0.15, label="95% PI")
ax.legend(); ax.set_ylabel("Visits per 1,000 residents")
plt.show()

Both routines diagnose the series the same way — a trend that calls for one regular difference (d=1) and a yearly spike that calls for one seasonal difference (D=1) — and then fit AR/MA terms to the stationary remainder. The forecast decays the seasonal pattern forward while the prediction interval widens with the horizon, exactly as the AR(1) intuition predicts.

Exercises

Exercise 1

A regional series of monthly sjukfrånvaro (sick-leave) rates follows an AR(1): y_t = 4 + 0.8\, y_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t, with \operatorname{Var}(\varepsilon_t) = \sigma^2 = 9.

  1. Is the series stationary? (b) Compute the long-run mean \mu. (c) Compute the variance \gamma_0 and the lag-2 autocorrelation \rho_2.
Show answer

(a) Stationary iff |\phi| < 1. Here \phi = 0.8, so |0.8| < 1 — yes, stationary.

(b) \mu = \dfrac{c}{1-\phi} = \dfrac{4}{1 - 0.8} = \dfrac{4}{0.2} = 20.

(c) Variance: \gamma_0 = \dfrac{\sigma^2}{1-\phi^2} = \dfrac{9}{1 - 0.64} = \dfrac{9}{0.36} = 25.

Lag-2 ACF: \rho_2 = \phi^2 = 0.8^2 = 0.64. (A shock retains 64% of its correlation two months out — high persistence, as expected from \phi = 0.8.)

Exercise 2

You plot the ACF and PACF of a stationary (already-differenced) series of monthly waiting times. The ACF shows a single significant spike at lag 1 and is essentially zero thereafter; the PACF tails off geometrically. Which ARMA model does this fingerprint suggest, and why?

Show answer

This is the classic MA(1) signature. Recall the duality from the diagnostic table:

  • an MA(q) has an ACF that cuts off after lag q and a PACF that tails off;
  • an AR(p) is the mirror image (PACF cuts off, ACF tails off).

Here the ACF cuts off sharply after lag 1 while the PACF decays smoothly — so q = 1 and there is no AR part: MA(1), i.e. y_t = \mu + \varepsilon_t + \theta_1 \varepsilon_{t-1}. The single ACF spike says the series remembers exactly one period of shocks; the geometric PACF is what an MA process always produces when you try to express it through direct lags of the level.

Exercise 3

A colleague fits an ARIMA(2,2,1) to a series of monthly primary-care visits that has only a mild linear upward trend and no curvature. The residual ACF shows a strong negative spike at lag 1. Diagnose the likely problem and propose a fix.

Show answer

The series has a linear trend, which one regular difference (d=1) removes. Choosing d=2 over-differences it: differencing an already-stationary (once-differenced) series injects an artificial MA(1) structure with a coefficient near -1, which shows up as exactly the strong negative lag-1 autocorrelation in the residuals described here.

Fix: reduce the differencing order to d=1 (an ARIMA(p,1,q)). Confirm with a unit-root test (next page) and by checking that the once-differenced series is mean-stationary with no remaining trend. The guiding rule from the Caution card: difference only enough to remove the trend — never reflexively, and a negative lag-1 residual spike is the tell-tale sign you went one difference too far.

Exercise 4

Using the AR(1) forecast formula \hat y_{T+h} = \mu + \phi^h(y_T - \mu), suppose a region’s primary-care visits have \mu = 90, \phi = 0.5, and the most recent observation is y_T = 110. (a) Forecast 1, 2, and 4 months ahead. (b) What value do the forecasts approach as h \to \infty, and why?

Show answer

The current deviation from the mean is y_T - \mu = 110 - 90 = 20.

(a) Apply \hat y_{T+h} = 90 + 0.5^h \times 20:

  • h=1: 90 + 0.5^1 \times 20 = 90 + 10 = \mathbf{100}.
  • h=2: 90 + 0.5^2 \times 20 = 90 + 0.25 \times 20 = 90 + 5 = \mathbf{95}.
  • h=4: 90 + 0.5^4 \times 20 = 90 + 0.0625 \times 20 = 90 + 1.25 = \mathbf{91.25}.

(b) As h \to \infty, \phi^h = 0.5^h \to 0, so \hat y_{T+h} \to \mu = \mathbf{90}. Because |\phi| < 1, the influence of the current value decays geometrically and every long-horizon forecast collapses to the unconditional mean. The series is mean-reverting: the further out you look, the less the present tells you, until the best guess is simply the long-run average.

Key takeaways

  • A time series is a single, time-ordered realization with serial dependence; learning from it requires (weak) stationarity — constant mean, constant variance, and a lag-only autocovariance.
  • The ACF \rho_k = \gamma_k/\gamma_0 measures memory; the ACF/PACF pair is a diagnostic fingerprint — AR processes leave a cutoff PACF and a decaying ACF, MA processes the mirror image.
  • AR(p) models persistence in levels, MA(q) models short-lived shock structure, and ARMA(p,q) combines them; the AR(1) is stationary iff |\phi| < 1, with mean c/(1-\phi), variance \sigma^2/(1-\phi^2), and ACF \phi^k.
  • Differencing d times removes a trend and turns ARMA into ARIMA(p,d,q); seasonal differencing handles yearly rhythms (SARIMA). Over-differencing is a real hazard, flagged by a negative lag-1 residual spike.
  • For a stationary series every forecast decays to the unconditional mean at rate \phi^h, and the prediction interval widens toward the unconditional variance.
