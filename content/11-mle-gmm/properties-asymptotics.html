Properties and Asymptotics of MLE

Consistency, asymptotic normality, and the information matrix

In this section, you will learn to

  • State the three pillars of large-sample MLE theory — consistency, asymptotic normality, and efficiency — and explain what each one promises
  • Compute the Fisher information I(\theta) two equivalent ways (curvature of the log-likelihood and variance of the score) and use its inverse as the asymptotic variance
  • Read standard errors off the observed information (the Hessian at the optimum) and watch them shrink like 1/\sqrt{n}
  • Apply the Cramér–Rao lower bound to argue that the MLE is asymptotically efficient, and use invariance to re-parameterise an estimate for free
  • Propagate uncertainty through a smooth transformation g(\hat\theta) with the delta method

Why the Likelihood Is Only Half the Story

The previous page, The Likelihood Principle, showed you how to find a maximum-likelihood estimate: write down the log-likelihood for your data, then climb to its peak. For a Swedish vårdcentral (primary care center) modelling the count of fall-related emergency visits among its elderly patients with a Poisson model, the peak gives a single number \hat\lambda — the estimated mean number of falls per patient-year.

But a point estimate alone is almost useless for policy. If the regional health authority (regionen) is deciding whether a fall-prevention program is worth its SEK budget, it needs to know not just “the estimated fall rate is 0.42 per patient-year” but “and we are confident it lies between 0.38 and 0.46.” That second statement — the standard error, the confidence interval, the uncertainty — is what turns an estimate into evidence.

Maximum likelihood comes with a remarkable promise: in large samples, the estimator behaves in an almost completely predictable way. It homes in on the truth (consistency), its sampling distribution becomes a tidy bell curve (asymptotic normality), and the width of that bell curve is determined by a single object, the Fisher information, which we can read straight off the curvature of the log-likelihood. This page makes those three promises precise and shows you how to extract a standard error from them.

Let \theta_0 denote the true parameter value and \hat\theta_n the maximum-likelihood estimator from a sample of size n. The three large-sample properties we develop are:

  • Consistency: \hat\theta_n \xrightarrow{p} \theta_0 — read “\hat\theta_n converges in probability to \theta_0”: as the sample grows, the estimate piles up arbitrarily close to the truth.
  • Asymptotic normality: \sqrt{n}\,(\hat\theta_n - \theta_0) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}\!\big(0,\, I_1(\theta_0)^{-1}\big) — read “\sqrt{n} times the estimation error converges in distribution to a Normal with mean zero and variance I_1(\theta_0)^{-1}.” Here I_1(\theta_0) is the Fisher information from a single observation.
  • Efficiency: among all well-behaved estimators, none has a smaller asymptotic variance — the MLE attains the Cramér–Rao lower bound.

The whole argument rests on one quantity, so we build it carefully.

The Score and the Information

Write the log-likelihood of an i.i.d. sample y_1,\dots,y_n as \ell_n(\theta) = \sum_{i=1}^n \log f(y_i \mid \theta). Two derivatives of this function do all the work.

The first derivative is the score, the gradient of the log-likelihood:

s_i(\theta) = \frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\,\log f(y_i \mid \theta), \qquad S_n(\theta) = \sum_{i=1}^n s_i(\theta).

Read this as: “the score for observation i is the slope of its log-density with respect to the parameter, and the total score is the sum over all observations.” The MLE \hat\theta_n is exactly the value where the total score is zero — the top of the hill is flat.

The score has a beautiful property: at the true parameter, its expected value is zero, E_{\theta_0}[s_i(\theta_0)] = 0. On average, the data point you in no particular direction once you are already at the truth. Its spread, however, is informative — and that spread is the Fisher information.

The Fisher information from a single observation can be written two equivalent ways:

I_1(\theta) \;=\; \underbrace{\operatorname{Var}_\theta\!\big[s_i(\theta)\big]}_{\text{variance of the score}} \;=\; \underbrace{-\,E_\theta\!\left[\frac{\partial^2}{\partial\theta^2}\log f(y_i\mid\theta)\right]}_{\text{expected negative curvature}}.

Read this as: “the information equals the variance of the score, and also equals the expected negative second derivative of the log-density.” The first form says information is how much the score wiggles across samples; the second says it is how sharply curved the log-likelihood is at its peak. The equality of these two expressions is the information matrix identity, derived in the Math Details.

Intuition for the curvature form: a log-likelihood that rises to a sharp, narrow peak pins the parameter down tightly — a small move away from \hat\theta costs a lot of fit, so the data are informative. A flat, gently rounded peak leaves many parameter values nearly as plausible — the data are uninformative. Curvature = information = precision.

For n i.i.d. observations the information simply adds up: I_n(\theta) = n\, I_1(\theta). More data, more information, in direct proportion. This additivity is the engine behind the 1/\sqrt{n} shrinkage you will watch in the interactive.

The inverse information is the asymptotic variance. Combining the two displays above, in finite (but large) samples the MLE is approximately distributed as

\hat\theta_n \;\approx\; \mathcal{N}\!\left(\theta_0,\; \frac{1}{n\,I_1(\theta_0)}\right) = \mathcal{N}\!\left(\theta_0,\; I_n(\theta_0)^{-1}\right).

So the standard error is \operatorname{SE}(\hat\theta_n) = 1/\sqrt{n\,I_1(\theta_0)}. It falls like 1/\sqrt{n}: to halve your standard error you need four times the data. Because I_1 is positive, more information always means a smaller standard error.

In practice we do not know \theta_0, so we evaluate the curvature at the estimate using the observed information — minus the Hessian of the log-likelihood at \hat\theta_n:

\hat{I}_n = -\,\frac{\partial^2 \ell_n(\theta)}{\partial\theta^2}\bigg|_{\theta=\hat\theta_n}, \qquad \widehat{\operatorname{SE}}(\hat\theta_n) = \sqrt{\hat{I}_n^{-1}}.

Read this as: “evaluate how sharply the log-likelihood curves down at its peak, take the reciprocal, and the square root is the standard error.” This is literally what every statistics package reports: summary() in R and .summary() in statsmodels invert the Hessian returned by the optimiser. The interactive below shows that this Hessian-based SE tracks the true sampling spread of \hat\theta remarkably well, even at modest n.

See It: The Sampling Distribution Concentrates and Goes Normal

Our running example is a Poisson model of fall-related emergency visits. Each elderly patient i at a Swedish vårdcentral contributes a count y_i \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_0) over one year, where \lambda_0 is the true mean annual fall rate. The MLE is just the sample mean, \hat\lambda = \bar{y}, and for the Poisson the single-observation information is I_1(\lambda) = 1/\lambda (derived below), so the theory predicts

\hat\lambda \;\approx\; \mathcal{N}\!\left(\lambda_0,\; \frac{\lambda_0}{n}\right), \qquad \operatorname{SE}(\hat\lambda) = \sqrt{\lambda_0/n}.

The interactive runs a Monte Carlo experiment: it draws many independent samples of size n, computes \hat\lambda for each, and histograms them. On top it overlays the theoretical Normal curve the asymptotics predict. Slide n and watch two things happen at once: the histogram narrows (consistency) and its shape snaps to the bell curve (asymptotic normality). The dashed marker shows the observed-information standard error from a single representative sample — watch it shrink like 1/\sqrt{n}.

// Poisson sampler via the Knuth algorithm (browser-side randomness).
// Returns one Poisson(lambda) draw.
mleprop_rpois = function (lambda) {
  const L = Math.exp(-lambda);
  let k = 0, p = 1;
  do {
    k++;
    p *= Math.random();
  } while (p > L);
  return k - 1;
}
// The Monte Carlo experiment. Referencing mleprop_draw makes this cell
// recompute on every button click (fresh randomness) without any mutable.
mleprop_mc = {
  mleprop_draw;                       // dependency: resample on click
  const n      = mleprop_n;
  const lam    = mleprop_lambda0;
  const reps   = mleprop_reps;

  const estimates = new Float64Array(reps);
  for (let r = 0; r < reps; r++) {
    let sum = 0;
    for (let i = 0; i < n; i++) sum += mleprop_rpois(lam);
    estimates[r] = sum / n;           // MLE = sample mean
  }
  const arr = Array.from(estimates);
  const mcMean = d3.mean(arr);
  const mcSd   = d3.deviation(arr);
  return { estimates: arr, mcMean, mcSd };
}
// Theoretical large-sample SE from the Fisher information: I1(lambda)=1/lambda,
// so Var(lambda_hat) = lambda0 / n  and  SE = sqrt(lambda0 / n).

How to Read the Monte Carlo

Work through these in order; each isolates one of the three asymptotic promises.

Step 1: Read the picture. The slate-blue bars are a histogram of \hat\lambda across many simulated studies, each with n patients. The Uppsala-red curve is the Normal distribution the asymptotic theory predicts, \mathcal{N}(\lambda_0,\, \lambda_0/n). The ochre dashed line marks the true rate \lambda_0. Notice that the histogram is centred on \lambda_0 — this is consistency: the MLE is (essentially) unbiased and homes in on the truth.

Step 2: Watch it concentrate. Drag sample size n from 10 up toward 1000. The histogram squeezes inward around \lambda_0 — the estimates from different studies agree more and more closely. This is \hat\lambda \xrightarrow{p}\lambda_0 made visual: with enough data, sampling error nearly vanishes.

Step 3: Watch it become Normal. At small n (say 10) with a low rate \lambda_0, the histogram is visibly skewed — counts are discrete and bounded below by zero, so the sampling distribution is lumpy and right-leaning, and the red curve fits imperfectly. Increase n: the lumps smooth out and the histogram snaps onto the red Normal. This is the Central Limit Theorem doing its work inside the MLE — asymptotic normality is an approximation that gets better with n.

Step 4: Confirm the standard error. Compare the three SE tiles. Theory SE is \sqrt{\lambda_0/n} from the Fisher information. Monte Carlo SD is the actual spread of \hat\lambda across replications — the “truth” we are trying to predict. Observed-info SE is what a single study would report by inverting its own Hessian. All three should agree closely, and the agreement improves as n grows. This is the punchline: the curvature of one log-likelihood predicts the spread you would see across infinitely many repeated studies.

Step 5: See the 1/\sqrt{n} law. Note the Theory SE at n=40, then quadruple n to 160. The SE roughly halves, not quarters. Diminishing returns to data are baked into maximum likelihood: precision grows only with the square root of sample size. Click Draw new Monte Carlo a few times to confirm the pattern is not a fluke of one random draw.

Efficiency and the Cramér–Rao Bound

Consistency and normality tell us the MLE lands on the truth with a Normal wobble. Efficiency tells us no estimator wobbles less.

The Cramér–Rao lower bound states that for any unbiased estimator \tilde\theta of a scalar \theta,

\operatorname{Var}(\tilde\theta) \;\ge\; \frac{1}{n\,I_1(\theta)} \;=\; I_n(\theta)^{-1}.

Read this as: “the variance of any unbiased estimator is at least the inverse of the total Fisher information.” The inverse information is a floor below which no unbiased estimator can go. The MLE attains this floor asymptotically, so we call it asymptotically efficient: among all consistent, asymptotically-normal estimators, it has the smallest possible asymptotic variance.

This is why maximum likelihood is the default in so much of econometrics: when the model is correctly specified, you cannot beat it in large samples. For the fall-count example, the sample mean \bar y is the MLE and its variance \lambda_0/n exactly equals I_n(\lambda_0)^{-1} — the Poisson sample mean is fully efficient, and no cleverer unbiased estimator of the fall rate exists.

The asymptotic guarantees are not free. They require regularity conditions: the true parameter lies in the interior of the parameter space (not on a boundary), the log-density is smooth enough to differentiate twice, the support of f(y\mid\theta) does not depend on \theta, and — crucially — the model is correctly specified. If f is the wrong family, the MLE still converges, but to the parameter that minimises the Kullback–Leibler divergence from the truth, and the tidy variance formula I_n^{-1} no longer holds (you then need the “sandwich” robust variance). Treat I_n^{-1} as a best case that assumes you got the model right.

Invariance: Re-Parameterising for Free

A small but enormously convenient property: the MLE is invariant under transformation. If \hat\theta is the MLE of \theta and g is any function, then the MLE of g(\theta) is simply g(\hat\theta).

For the fall-count study, suppose the region cares not about the rate \lambda but about the probability a patient has zero falls in a year, \pi_0 = P(Y=0) = e^{-\lambda}. By invariance, the MLE of that probability is just \hat\pi_0 = e^{-\hat\lambda} — no re-optimisation needed. You maximise once, in whatever parameterisation is convenient, and read off any smooth function of the result.

But invariance only hands you the point estimate. The standard error of \hat\pi_0 is a different question, because squeezing \hat\lambda through the nonlinear map e^{-\lambda} stretches its uncertainty too. That is exactly what the delta method is for.

The Delta Method: Standard Errors for g(\hat\theta)

We have a standard error for \hat\theta. We want one for g(\hat\theta). The delta method linearises g around \theta_0 with a first-order Taylor expansion and propagates the variance through the slope.

The delta method. If \sqrt{n}(\hat\theta - \theta_0) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2) and g is differentiable at \theta_0 with g'(\theta_0)\neq 0, then

\sqrt{n}\,\big(g(\hat\theta) - g(\theta_0)\big) \;\xrightarrow{d}\; \mathcal{N}\!\big(0,\; g'(\theta_0)^2\,\sigma^2\big).

In standard-error terms, \widehat{\operatorname{SE}}\big(g(\hat\theta)\big) = \big|g'(\hat\theta)\big| \cdot \widehat{\operatorname{SE}}(\hat\theta).

Read this as: “the standard error of the transformed estimate is the absolute slope of g at the estimate, times the standard error of the original estimate.” Geometrically, g' is the local magnification factor: where g is steep, a small wobble in \hat\theta becomes a large wobble in g(\hat\theta); where g is flat, uncertainty is compressed.

Apply it to the zero-fall probability \pi_0 = e^{-\lambda}, so g(\lambda) = e^{-\lambda} and g'(\lambda) = -e^{-\lambda}. With \hat\lambda = 2.0 from n=40 patients, the SE of \hat\lambda is \sqrt{\hat\lambda/n} = \sqrt{2.0/40} = \sqrt{0.05} = 0.2236. Then

\hat\pi_0 = e^{-2.0} = 0.1353, \qquad \widehat{\operatorname{SE}}(\hat\pi_0) = \big|-e^{-2.0}\big|\cdot 0.2236 = 0.1353 \times 0.2236 = 0.0303.

So roughly 13.5\% of patients have a fall-free year, with a standard error of about 3.0 percentage points — an approximate 95% interval of [0.076,\,0.195]. We obtained a calibrated uncertainty for a nonlinear function of the parameter without ever fitting a second model.

The delta method has blind spots. Two failure modes recur in applied work and the formula gives no warning when they bite:

  • Vanishing slope. When g'(\theta_0)=0 the first-order term collapses and the leading approximation is degenerate — you must go to a second-order (quadratic) expansion, and the limiting law is no longer Normal. Estimating the variance of a quantity near its own optimum, where the derivative is zero, is the classic trap.
  • Bounded transforms near a boundary. Probabilities live in [0,1], but a symmetric delta-method interval can spill past 0 or 1 — as it nearly does for our \hat\pi_0 at small n. When the transform is bounded or strongly curved, prefer building the interval on a scale where the Normal approximation is better (e.g. the log-odds in Exercise 3) and then transforming the endpoints back. The delta method is a local linearisation; trust it only where g is close to linear over the plausible range of \hat\theta.
Math Details: the information identity and the delta method

Why the score has mean zero

Start from the fact that any density integrates to one: \int f(y\mid\theta)\,dy = 1 for every \theta. Differentiate both sides with respect to \theta and pass the derivative inside the integral (a regularity condition):

0 = \frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\int f\,dy = \int \frac{\partial f}{\partial\theta}\,dy = \int \frac{\partial \log f}{\partial\theta}\, f\,dy = E_\theta\!\left[\frac{\partial \log f}{\partial\theta}\right] = E_\theta[s(\theta)].

The trick in the middle is \partial f/\partial\theta = f \cdot \partial(\log f)/\partial\theta. So the score has expectation zero at the true parameter.

The information matrix identity

Differentiate the identity \int s(\theta)\, f\,dy = 0 once more with respect to \theta:

0 = \frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\int s\, f\,dy = \int \frac{\partial s}{\partial\theta}\, f\,dy + \int s\,\frac{\partial f}{\partial\theta}\,dy = E_\theta\!\left[\frac{\partial s}{\partial\theta}\right] + \int s\cdot s\, f\,dy,

again using \partial f/\partial\theta = f\, s. The second term is E_\theta[s^2]. Since \partial s/\partial\theta = \partial^2 \log f/\partial\theta^2 and E_\theta[s]=0 means E_\theta[s^2]=\operatorname{Var}_\theta(s), we rearrange to

\operatorname{Var}_\theta(s) = E_\theta[s^2] = -\,E_\theta\!\left[\frac{\partial^2 \log f}{\partial\theta^2}\right].

That is the information identity: variance of the score equals expected negative curvature. Both equal I_1(\theta).

Asymptotic normality in one line of intuition

A first-order Taylor expansion of the total score S_n around \theta_0, evaluated at the root \hat\theta where S_n(\hat\theta)=0, gives 0 \approx S_n(\theta_0) + S_n'(\theta_0)(\hat\theta-\theta_0), so

\sqrt{n}(\hat\theta - \theta_0) \approx \frac{\tfrac{1}{\sqrt n}S_n(\theta_0)}{-\tfrac{1}{n}S_n'(\theta_0)}.

The numerator is \sqrt n times a sample average of mean-zero scores, which the CLT sends to \mathcal{N}(0, I_1). The denominator is a sample average of -\partial s/\partial\theta, which the law of large numbers sends to I_1. Slutsky’s theorem combines them into \mathcal{N}(0, I_1 / I_1^2) = \mathcal{N}(0, I_1^{-1}). That is the asymptotic-normality result.

The Fisher information for the Poisson

With f(y\mid\lambda) = e^{-\lambda}\lambda^y / y!, the log-density is \log f = -\lambda + y\log\lambda - \log(y!). The score is s = -1 + y/\lambda and the second derivative is \partial^2 \log f/\partial\lambda^2 = -y/\lambda^2. Taking the negative expectation, and using E[Y]=\lambda,

I_1(\lambda) = -E\!\left[-\frac{Y}{\lambda^2}\right] = \frac{\lambda}{\lambda^2} = \frac{1}{\lambda}.

Hence \operatorname{Var}(\hat\lambda) = 1/(n\,I_1) = \lambda/n — exactly the variance the Poisson sample mean already has, confirming efficiency.

The delta method derivation

Expand g to first order around \theta_0: g(\hat\theta) \approx g(\theta_0) + g'(\theta_0)(\hat\theta - \theta_0). Subtract g(\theta_0) and scale by \sqrt n:

\sqrt n\big(g(\hat\theta)-g(\theta_0)\big) \approx g'(\theta_0)\cdot \sqrt n(\hat\theta-\theta_0).

The right side is a constant g'(\theta_0) times a quantity converging to \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2). Multiplying a Normal by a constant c multiplies its variance by c^2, giving \mathcal{N}\big(0,\, g'(\theta_0)^2\sigma^2\big). The remainder term is O_p(1/\sqrt n) and vanishes asymptotically.

Computing Information-Based and Delta-Method Standard Errors

Both panels fit the Poisson fall-count model by maximum likelihood, extract the standard error by inverting the observed information (the Hessian the optimiser returns), and then propagate that uncertainty through g(\lambda)=e^{-\lambda} with the delta method. The numbers match the worked example above.

  • R
  • Python
library(bbmle)   # mle2: maximum likelihood with a numeric Hessian

set.seed(2024)
# Simulated fall-related ER visits for n elderly patients at a vårdcentral.
n     <- 40
lam0  <- 2.0
y     <- rpois(n, lambda = lam0)

# Negative log-likelihood for a Poisson rate.
nll <- function(lambda) -sum(dpois(y, lambda, log = TRUE))

fit <- mle2(nll, start = list(lambda = 1), method = "BFGS")

lam_hat <- coef(fit)["lambda"]
# Observed-information SE: bbmle inverts the numeric Hessian for us.
se_lam  <- sqrt(diag(vcov(fit)))["lambda"]

cat(sprintf("lambda_hat = %.4f\n", lam_hat))
cat(sprintf("SE(lambda) = %.4f   (theory sqrt(lam_hat/n) = %.4f)\n",
            se_lam, sqrt(lam_hat / n)))

# Invariance: MLE of pi0 = P(Y = 0) = exp(-lambda) is exp(-lambda_hat).
pi0_hat <- exp(-lam_hat)

# Delta method: g(lambda) = exp(-lambda), g'(lambda) = -exp(-lambda).
gprime  <- -exp(-lam_hat)
se_pi0  <- abs(gprime) * se_lam

cat(sprintf("\npi0_hat    = %.4f  (P of a fall-free year)\n", pi0_hat))
cat(sprintf("SE(pi0)    = %.4f   via delta method\n", se_pi0))
cat(sprintf("95%% CI    = [%.4f, %.4f]\n",
            pi0_hat - 1.96 * se_pi0, pi0_hat + 1.96 * se_pi0))
import numpy as np
from scipy import optimize, stats

rng = np.random.default_rng(2024)
# Simulated fall-related ER visits for n elderly patients at a vårdcentral.
n, lam0 = 40, 2.0
y = rng.poisson(lam0, size=n)

# Negative log-likelihood for a Poisson rate.
def nll(theta):
    lam = theta[0]
    if lam <= 0:
        return np.inf
    return -np.sum(stats.poisson.logpmf(y, lam))

# Optimise, then read the inverse-Hessian from the BFGS result.
res = optimize.minimize(nll, x0=[1.0], method="BFGS")
lam_hat = res.x[0]
# res.hess_inv approximates the inverse observed information -> variance.
se_lam = np.sqrt(res.hess_inv[0, 0])

print(f"lambda_hat = {lam_hat:.4f}")
print(f"SE(lambda) = {se_lam:.4f}   (theory sqrt(lam_hat/n) = "
      f"{np.sqrt(lam_hat / n):.4f})")

# Invariance: MLE of pi0 = P(Y = 0) = exp(-lambda) is exp(-lambda_hat).
pi0_hat = np.exp(-lam_hat)

# Delta method: g(lambda) = exp(-lambda), g'(lambda) = -exp(-lambda).
gprime = -np.exp(-lam_hat)
se_pi0 = abs(gprime) * se_lam

print(f"\npi0_hat    = {pi0_hat:.4f}  (P of a fall-free year)")
print(f"SE(pi0)    = {se_pi0:.4f}   via delta method")
print(f"95% CI     = [{pi0_hat - 1.96 * se_pi0:.4f}, "
      f"{pi0_hat + 1.96 * se_pi0:.4f}]")

Both routes report a \hat\lambda near 2 with an SE near \sqrt{\hat\lambda/n}\approx 0.22, and a zero-fall probability near 0.135 with a delta-method SE near 0.030 — exactly the hand computation. The lesson: the optimiser’s Hessian is the observed information, and a single derivative turns that into a standard error for any smooth quantity you care about.

Exercises

Exercise 1

A vårdcentral models the time (in days) between consecutive fall-related ER visits as Y \sim \text{Exponential}(\theta) with density f(y\mid\theta) = \theta e^{-\theta y} for y>0, where \theta is the rate. (a) Find the score and show E_\theta[s]=0. (b) Derive the Fisher information I_1(\theta). (c) Write the large-sample standard error of \hat\theta for a sample of size n.

Show answer

(a) \log f = \log\theta - \theta y, so the score is s(\theta) = \partial\log f/\partial\theta = 1/\theta - y. For the Exponential, E_\theta[Y] = 1/\theta, hence E_\theta[s] = 1/\theta - 1/\theta = 0. \checkmark

(b) The second derivative is \partial^2\log f/\partial\theta^2 = -1/\theta^2, a constant. Therefore

I_1(\theta) = -E_\theta\!\left[-\tfrac{1}{\theta^2}\right] = \frac{1}{\theta^2}.

(Check via the variance form: \operatorname{Var}(s)=\operatorname{Var}(1/\theta - Y)=\operatorname{Var}(Y)=1/\theta^2. \checkmark)

(c) \operatorname{Var}(\hat\theta) = 1/(n\,I_1) = \theta^2/n, so

\operatorname{SE}(\hat\theta) = \sqrt{\theta^2/n} = \frac{\theta}{\sqrt n}.

In practice replace \theta by \hat\theta = 1/\bar y, giving \widehat{\operatorname{SE}}(\hat\theta) = \hat\theta/\sqrt n.

Exercise 2

Continuing Exercise 1, the region reports the mean time between falls, \mu = 1/\theta, rather than the rate. (a) Use invariance to state \hat\mu. (b) Use the delta method to find \operatorname{SE}(\hat\mu) in terms of \theta and n. (c) With n=50 and \hat\theta = 0.05 per day, give \hat\mu and its standard error.

Show answer

(a) By invariance, \hat\mu = 1/\hat\theta.

(b) Here g(\theta) = 1/\theta so g'(\theta) = -1/\theta^2. From Exercise 1, \operatorname{SE}(\hat\theta) = \theta/\sqrt n. The delta method gives

\operatorname{SE}(\hat\mu) = |g'(\theta)|\cdot \operatorname{SE}(\hat\theta) = \frac{1}{\theta^2}\cdot\frac{\theta}{\sqrt n} = \frac{1}{\theta\sqrt n}.

Equivalently, \operatorname{SE}(\hat\mu) = \mu/\sqrt n since \mu = 1/\theta — the same proportional structure as the rate.

(c) \hat\mu = 1/0.05 = 20 days. With \hat\theta = 0.05 and n=50,

\widehat{\operatorname{SE}}(\hat\mu) = \frac{1}{0.05\cdot\sqrt{50}} = \frac{1}{0.05\times 7.071} = \frac{1}{0.3536} = 2.83 \text{ days}.

So the mean gap between falls is about 20 \pm 2.8 days.

Exercise 3

A study of HbA1c control records whether each of n=200 diabetes patients is “at target” (<52 mmol/mol). Let Y\sim\text{Bernoulli}(p) with \hat p = 0.65. (a) Show that the Fisher information for a single Bernoulli is I_1(p)=1/[p(1-p)]. (b) Compute \widehat{\operatorname{SE}}(\hat p). (c) The region instead wants the log-odds \eta = \log\frac{p}{1-p}. Use the delta method to find \widehat{\operatorname{SE}}(\hat\eta).

Show answer

(a) \log f = y\log p + (1-y)\log(1-p). The score is s = y/p - (1-y)/(1-p) and the second derivative is \partial^2\log f/\partial p^2 = -y/p^2 - (1-y)/(1-p)^2. Taking the negative expectation with E[Y]=p:

I_1(p) = \frac{p}{p^2} + \frac{1-p}{(1-p)^2} = \frac1p + \frac{1}{1-p} = \frac{1}{p(1-p)}.

(b) \operatorname{Var}(\hat p) = 1/(n\,I_1) = p(1-p)/n, so

\widehat{\operatorname{SE}}(\hat p) = \sqrt{\frac{0.65\times 0.35}{200}} = \sqrt{\frac{0.2275}{200}} = \sqrt{0.0011375} = 0.0337.

(c) With \eta = \log\frac{p}{1-p}, the derivative is g'(p) = \frac{1}{p(1-p)}. The delta method gives

\widehat{\operatorname{SE}}(\hat\eta) = \frac{1}{p(1-p)}\cdot\widehat{\operatorname{SE}}(\hat p) = \frac{1}{0.2275}\times 0.033727 = 0.1482.

(Keep the unrounded \widehat{\operatorname{SE}}(\hat p)=\sqrt{0.2275/200}=0.033727 here; plugging in the 2-significant-figure 0.034 would inflate the answer.)

A neat shortcut: \operatorname{SE}(\hat\eta) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n\,p(1-p)}} = 1/\sqrt{200\times 0.2275} = 1/\sqrt{45.5} = 0.1482. \checkmark

Exercise 4

Using the Monte Carlo interactive, set \lambda_0 = 1.0 and start at n=10. (a) Describe the shape of the histogram and how well the red Normal fits. (b) Increase n to 250 and describe what changed about both the centre and the shape. (c) Read the Theory SE at n=10 and at n=250; verify the ratio matches what the 1/\sqrt n law predicts.

Show answer

(a) At \lambda_0=1.0 and n=10, the histogram is wide and visibly right-skewed — the discreteness of small Poisson counts and the floor at zero make the sampling distribution of \bar y lumpy and asymmetric. The red asymptotic Normal is a rough approximation: it overshoots into negative-looking territory the data cannot reach and underweights the right tail. This is the regime where “asymptotic” normality has not kicked in yet.

(b) At n=250 the histogram narrows sharply (it now hugs \lambda_0=1 much more tightly — consistency) and becomes symmetric and smooth, matching the red bell curve almost perfectly (the CLT has taken over — asymptotic normality). Both promises are visible at once.

(c) Theory SE =\sqrt{\lambda_0/n}. At n=10: \sqrt{1/10}=\sqrt{0.1}=0.3162. At n=250: \sqrt{1/250}=\sqrt{0.004}=0.0632. The ratio is 0.3162/0.0632 = 5.0. The 1/\sqrt n law predicts the ratio \sqrt{250/10}=\sqrt{25}=5. \checkmark Multiplying the sample by 25 cut the standard error by exactly 5.

Key takeaways

  • In large samples the MLE is consistent (\hat\theta_n\to\theta_0), asymptotically Normal (\sqrt n(\hat\theta_n-\theta_0)\to\mathcal N(0, I_1^{-1})), and efficient (it attains the Cramér–Rao floor).
  • The Fisher information I_1(\theta) is both the variance of the score and the expected negative curvature of the log-likelihood; these are equal by the information identity, and a sharper peak means more information and smaller standard errors.
  • The inverse information is the asymptotic variance: \operatorname{SE}(\hat\theta)=1/\sqrt{n\,I_1} shrinks like 1/\sqrt n, and software reports it by inverting the observed information (the Hessian at the optimum).
  • Invariance gives the point estimate of any smooth function for free, \widehat{g(\theta)}=g(\hat\theta); the delta method gives its standard error, \operatorname{SE}(g(\hat\theta))=|g'(\hat\theta)|\cdot\operatorname{SE}(\hat\theta).
  • All of this assumes correct specification and interior, smooth, support-stable parameters — violate those and the clean I_n^{-1} variance must be replaced by a robust sandwich.

What Comes Next

Maximum likelihood is powerful precisely because it squeezes every drop of information from a fully specified probability model — and that is also its Achilles’ heel: you must commit to a distribution for Y. The next page, Method of Moments and GMM, relaxes that commitment. Instead of a full likelihood, you supply only a handful of moment conditions — statements like “this residual is, on average, uncorrelated with that instrument” — and let the data pin down the parameters. We will see that MLE is a special case of the Generalized Method of Moments (its score equations are moment conditions), that GMM inherits its own asymptotic-normality and efficiency theory, and that the optimal GMM weighting matrix plays the role the Fisher information played here. The information-and-delta-method machinery you just built carries straight over.
