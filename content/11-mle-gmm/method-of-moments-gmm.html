Method of Moments and GMM

Estimation from moment conditions

In this section, you will learn to

  • Apply the classical method of moments by equating sample moments to their population counterparts and solving for the parameter
  • Write a population moment condition E[g(w,\theta)] = 0 and recognise the sample moment vector \bar g_n(\theta) whose root (or near-root) defines the GMM estimator
  • Form the GMM criterion as a weighting-matrix quadratic form in the sample moments, and distinguish the just-identified case (which sets the moments to zero) from the over-identified case (which can only shrink them)
  • Explain why the efficient weighting matrix is the inverse of the moment variance, and compute Hansen’s J-statistic to test the over-identifying restrictions
  • Recognise OLS and 2SLS/IV as special cases of GMM by reading off their moment conditions

When the Model Speaks in Averages

Sweden’s vårdcentraler (primary care centers), run by the country’s 21 regioner (regions), are awash in administrative data: every HbA1c reading, every blood-pressure measurement, every recorded waiting time for a läkare (physician) appointment. Often we do not have a fully specified probability model for these numbers — we are not willing to claim that HbA1c is exactly Gaussian, or that waiting times are exactly exponential. What we are willing to claim is something weaker and more robust: a handful of statements about averages.

For example: “the average HbA1c reduction delivered by the Diabetessamordning (diabetes coordination program) is \mu.” Or: “the instrument — distance to the nearest staffed vårdcentral — is uncorrelated with the unobserved health shock.” Each of these is a statement that some expectation equals a known value (often zero). The previous page, Properties and Asymptotics of MLE, built estimators from a full likelihood. This page builds estimators from these much weaker moment conditions alone — which is exactly why the resulting machinery, the Generalized Method of Moments, has become the unifying language of modern econometrics. OLS, IV, 2SLS, and many panel estimators are all GMM in disguise.

The logic is disarmingly simple, and it is two centuries old.

Classical Method of Moments

Karl Pearson’s method of moments (1894) rests on a single idea: a population moment is an expectation, an expectation is estimated by its sample average, so set the sample average equal to the population formula and solve.

Suppose each patient’s annual HbA1c reading w_i is drawn from some distribution with mean \mu and variance \sigma^2. The first two population moments are

E[w] = \mu, \qquad E[w^2] = \mu^2 + \sigma^2.

Read the second one aloud: “the expected value of the squared reading equals the squared mean plus the variance” — this is just the rearrangement of \operatorname{Var}(w) = E[w^2] - (E[w])^2. We have two unknowns (\mu, \sigma^2) and two moment equations. The method of moments replaces each population moment by the matching sample average,

\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n w_i = \hat\mu, \qquad \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n w_i^2 = \hat\mu^2 + \hat\sigma^2,

and solves. The first equation gives \hat\mu = \bar w; substituting into the second gives \hat\sigma^2 = \tfrac1n\sum_i w_i^2 - \bar w^2, the (biased) sample variance. No likelihood, no distributional assumption beyond “these two moments exist” — just an analogy between population and sample.

The classical method of moments estimator solves the system that equates the first k sample moments to the first k population moments, expressed as functions of the parameter vector \theta = (\theta_1, \dots, \theta_k):

\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n w_i^{\,j} \;=\; E_\theta\!\left[w^{\,j}\right], \qquad j = 1, 2, \dots, k.

With k equations and k unknowns the system is just-identified: generically there is one solution, and it sets every sample-versus-population discrepancy to zero exactly.

This works whenever the number of moment equations equals the number of parameters. But the deep questions of econometrics — instruments, panel orthogonality conditions — routinely hand us more moment equations than parameters. We cannot set all of them to zero at once. We need a principled way to use them all anyway. That is GMM.

From Moments to Moment Conditions

Lars Peter Hansen’s 1982 paper (the work cited in his 2013 Nobel) generalised the method of moments to the case where the moments are arbitrary functions of the data and parameters, and where there may be more of them than parameters.

Fix notation. Let w_i collect all the data for observation i (outcomes, regressors, instruments). Let \theta be a p-vector of parameters. A moment function is a vector-valued function g(w_i, \theta) of length m, chosen so that, at the true parameter \theta_0, its expectation is exactly zero:

E\!\left[g(w_i, \theta_0)\right] = 0.

The m equations E[g(w_i,\theta_0)] = 0 are the population moment conditions (also called orthogonality conditions). They encode everything the model assumes. The sample moment vector is their empirical analogue,

\bar g_n(\theta) \;=\; \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n g(w_i, \theta),

an m-vector that, by the law of large numbers, converges to E[g(w_i,\theta)] for every fixed \theta. The GMM idea is to choose \hat\theta so that \bar g_n(\hat\theta) is as close to the zero vector as possible.

Three regimes follow immediately from comparing the count of moments m to the count of parameters p:

  • Under-identified (m < p): fewer equations than unknowns. The parameter is not pinned down — nothing can save you. Find more moments.
  • Just-identified (m = p): exactly as many equations as unknowns. We can generally solve \bar g_n(\hat\theta) = 0 exactly. Classical method of moments lives here.
  • Over-identified (m > p): more equations than unknowns. We cannot zero out all m sample moments with only p free parameters. We must compromise — and how we compromise is the whole subject of GMM.

A Swedish example makes the over-identified case vivid. Suppose we want one number: the average HbA1c benefit \theta of a fall-prevention-style diabetes program. We have two noisy administrative readouts of that benefit — say, a register-based estimate from Socialstyrelsen and a survey-based estimate from the regions’ own audits. Each gives a moment condition “this readout, minus \theta, has mean zero.” Two moments, one parameter: over-identified. We cannot make both residuals exactly zero with a single \theta. GMM tells us how to weight the two disagreeing signals.

The GMM Criterion

Since we cannot set \bar g_n(\theta) to zero in the over-identified case, we instead minimise a scalar measure of its size. The natural measure is a weighted quadratic form.

Given an m \times m symmetric positive-definite weighting matrix W, the GMM estimator is

\hat\theta_{\text{GMM}} \;=\; \arg\min_{\theta} \; Q_n(\theta), \qquad Q_n(\theta) \;=\; \bar g_n(\theta)^{\!\top}\, W \, \bar g_n(\theta).

Read the criterion aloud: “Q_n of theta equals the sample moment vector transposed, times the weighting matrix, times the sample moment vector.” It is a generalised squared length of \bar g_n(\theta). When all moments are exactly zero, Q_n = 0; the further any moment strays from zero, the larger Q_n, with W deciding how heavily each moment (and each pairing of moments) is penalised.

A few facts make this concrete:

  1. Just-identified case (m = p). Here we can drive \bar g_n(\hat\theta) to the zero vector, so Q_n(\hat\theta) = 0 regardless of W. The weighting matrix is irrelevant when there is nothing to compromise. Method of moments and GMM coincide.

  2. Over-identified case (m > p). Now \bar g_n(\hat\theta) \neq 0 in general — the best we can do is make the weighted combination small. Different W’s give different estimates, because they trade off the moments differently. This is where choosing W well matters, and the next section shows there is a single best choice.

  3. Diagonal W is just weighted least squares on the moments. If W = \operatorname{diag}(w_1, \dots, w_m), then Q_n = \sum_j w_j\, \bar g_{n,j}(\theta)^2: each squared moment residual gets its own weight. Larger w_j means “trust moment j more — pull \theta toward satisfying it.”

See It: Two Disagreeing Moments, One Parameter

The interactive below is the over-identified Swedish scenario in its barest form. One parameter \theta (the program’s true average HbA1c reduction, in percentage points) must satisfy two noisy moment conditions:

g_1(\theta) = \bar a - \theta, \qquad g_2(\theta) = \bar b - \theta,

where \bar a is the register-based readout and \bar b is the survey-based readout. Each is a straight line in \theta, crossing zero at its own target. No single \theta can zero out both lines at once (unless \bar a = \bar b). Drag \theta and watch the weighted objective Q_n(\theta) = w_1 g_1(\theta)^2 + w_2 g_2(\theta)^2 trace out a parabola whose minimum is the GMM estimate. The weighting toggle lets you switch between equal weights and the efficient weights (more weight on the lower-variance, more-reliable register readout).

How to Read the Two-Panel Plot

Work through these steps in order; each isolates one idea about GMM.

Step 1: Read the two moment lines (top panel). The red line is g_1(\theta) = \bar a - \theta, the register-based moment; the slate line is g_2(\theta) = \bar b - \theta, the survey-based moment. Each is a downward-sloping straight line that crosses zero (the hollow dot on the horizontal axis) at its own target — the register at \theta = -0.72, the survey at \theta = -0.44. Because the two targets differ, no vertical line can hit both zeros at once. That is exactly what “over-identified” means: two equations, one unknown, and they disagree.

Step 2: See the residuals you are trying to shrink. The ochre dashed line marks your current \theta. The two faint vertical bars at that \theta are the residuals g_1(\theta) and g_2(\theta) — how far each moment is from being satisfied. GMM cannot make both bars vanish; it can only make a weighted combination of their squares as small as possible.

Step 3: Watch the objective (bottom panel). The red curve is Q_n(\theta) = w_1 g_1(\theta)^2 + w_2 g_2(\theta)^2 — a parabola, because it is a sum of squared lines. The ochre dot rides the curve at your current \theta. The green vertical line and the \hat\theta label mark the minimum: the GMM estimate. Drag the \theta slider until the ochre dot slides down into the green minimum — the readout’s \hat\theta tile turns green when you are there.

Step 4: Toggle the weighting matrix. Flip the control back and forth between Equal weights and Efficient weights (inverse variance) (it starts on efficient). Going from equal to efficient, two things happen at once. First, the objective parabola changes shape. Second — the payoff — the green minimiser \hat\theta slides toward the register target (-0.72). Under equal weights, \hat\theta is the plain average of the two readouts, (-0.72 - 0.44)/2 = -0.58. Under efficient weights, the more precise register readout (variance 0.020) gets about 0.075/0.020 \approx 3.75 times the weight of the noisy survey (variance 0.075), pulling \hat\theta to about -0.66 (precisely -0.661, as the readout tile shows). GMM listens harder to the moment it trusts more.

Step 5: Connect to the formula. With diagonal W, the minimiser is the inverse-variance-weighted mean, \hat\theta = (w_1\bar a + w_2\bar b)/(w_1 + w_2). This is precisely the efficient way to combine two noisy unbiased estimates — the same logic behind meta-analysis and the Kalman filter. The next section shows why “inverse variance” is optimal in general.

The Efficient Weighting Matrix

In the over-identified case the choice of W changes the estimate and its precision. Hansen’s central result identifies the single best choice.

The efficient GMM weighting matrix is the inverse of the variance of the moments. Let

S \;=\; \operatorname{Var}\!\big(g(w_i,\theta_0)\big) \;=\; E\!\big[g(w_i,\theta_0)\,g(w_i,\theta_0)^{\!\top}\big]

be the m \times m covariance matrix of the moment functions at the truth. Setting W = S^{-1} minimises the asymptotic variance of \hat\theta_{\text{GMM}} among all valid weighting matrices. Intuitively: down-weight noisy moments, up-weight precise ones, and account for the correlations between them. A moment with large variance carries little information and should not be allowed to drag the estimate around.

There is a chicken-and-egg problem: S depends on \theta_0, which we do not know. The standard fix is two-step (feasible) efficient GMM:

  1. Step 1. Estimate \hat\theta^{(1)} with any sensible weighting matrix — usually W = I (the identity), which just minimises the unweighted sum of squared moments. This \hat\theta^{(1)} is consistent but not efficient.
  2. Step 2. Use \hat\theta^{(1)} to estimate \hat S = \tfrac1n \sum_i g(w_i, \hat\theta^{(1)}) g(w_i, \hat\theta^{(1)})^{\top}, then re-minimise with W = \hat S^{-1} to get the efficient \hat\theta^{(2)}.

Iterating to convergence gives iterated GMM; using a clever one-step adjustment gives continuously updated GMM (CUE). All three are asymptotically equivalent and efficient.

OLS and 2SLS Are GMM

The reason GMM is the lingua franca of econometrics is that the estimators you already know are special cases. Reading off their moment conditions is the quickest way to internalise the framework.

OLS as GMM. The linear model y_i = x_i^{\top}\beta + u_i assumes the regressors are orthogonal to the error: E[x_i u_i] = 0. Substituting u_i = y_i - x_i^{\top}\beta gives the moment condition

E\!\big[\,x_i\,(y_i - x_i^{\top}\beta)\,\big] = 0.

There are p moments (one per regressor) and p parameters — just-identified. Setting the sample analogue \tfrac1n\sum_i x_i(y_i - x_i^{\top}\hat\beta) = 0 to zero and solving reproduces the normal equations and hence \hat\beta_{\text{OLS}} = (X^{\top}X)^{-1}X^{\top}y exactly. The weighting matrix is irrelevant.

IV / 2SLS as GMM. When a regressor is endogenous (E[x_i u_i] \neq 0) but we have an m-vector of instruments z_i with E[z_i u_i] = 0, the moment condition uses the instruments instead of the regressors:

E\!\big[\,z_i\,(y_i - x_i^{\top}\beta)\,\big] = 0.

  • If m = p (as many instruments as endogenous-plus-exogenous regressors), the system is just-identified: GMM = simple IV, and W does not matter.
  • If m > p (more instruments than parameters), the system is over-identified: we cannot zero out all m moments. Minimising Q_n(\beta) = \bar g_n(\beta)^{\top} W \bar g_n(\beta) with W = (Z^{\top}Z)^{-1} reproduces two-stage least squares exactly; using the efficient W = \hat S^{-1} gives efficient GMM, which beats 2SLS when the errors are heteroskedastic.

This is the link back to the Two-Stage Least Squares page: 2SLS is GMM with a particular, not generally efficient, weighting matrix. The over-identified diabetes example above — two noisy readouts (register and survey) of one HbA1c-benefit parameter — is structurally identical to an over-identified IV problem with two instruments for a single endogenous coefficient: in both cases two moment conditions chase one parameter, and the efficient weighting matrix decides how to reconcile them.

Hansen’s J-Test of Over-identifying Restrictions

The over-identified case hands us a free diagnostic. If we have m > p moments and the model is correct, then all m population moments are genuinely zero, and the efficient GMM estimate should make all m sample moments jointly small. If instead the minimised objective is large — if even after the best compromise the moments stubbornly refuse to be near zero — that is evidence the model (or some instruments) is misspecified.

Hansen’s J-statistic is the minimised efficient-GMM objective, scaled by the sample size:

J \;=\; n \cdot \bar g_n(\hat\theta)^{\!\top}\, \hat S^{-1}\, \bar g_n(\hat\theta) \;=\; n \cdot Q_n(\hat\theta).

Under the null that all moment conditions are valid, J \xrightarrow{d} \chi^2_{m-p} — a chi-square distribution with degrees of freedom equal to the number of over-identifying restrictions (m - p, the count of “extra” moments beyond what is needed to identify \theta). A large J (small p-value) rejects the joint validity of the moment conditions.

Two cautions deserve emphasis:

  • A just-identified model has m - p = 0 degrees of freedom, J \equiv 0, and nothing to test. The J-test only exists when you have spare moments.
  • The J-test is a joint test. A rejection tells you that something is wrong — one or more instruments may be invalid, or the functional form may be misspecified — but it does not tell you which moment is the culprit. A non-rejection is reassuring but not proof: low power, or two bad instruments whose biases cancel, can let a flawed model pass.

In the Swedish over-identified scenario, a significant J would say: “the register and survey readouts of the program’s HbA1c benefit disagree by more than their sampling noise can explain — they are not both measuring the same \theta.” That is a substantive red flag, not a nuisance.

Math Details: Efficient Weighting and the Asymptotic Distribution of the J-Statistic

Asymptotic variance of the GMM estimator

Let G = E\!\big[\partial g(w_i,\theta_0)/\partial\theta^{\top}\big] be the m \times p Jacobian (expected gradient) of the moment function, and S = \operatorname{Var}(g(w_i,\theta_0)) its m\times m covariance. A first-order Taylor expansion of the first-order condition G^{\top} W \bar g_n(\hat\theta) = 0 around \theta_0 gives the sandwich form

\sqrt{n}\,(\hat\theta_{\text{GMM}} - \theta_0) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal N\!\big(0,\; V(W)\big), \qquad V(W) = (G^{\top} W G)^{-1}\, G^{\top} W S W G\, (G^{\top} W G)^{-1}.

Why W = S^{-1} is efficient

Substituting W = S^{-1} collapses the sandwich:

V(S^{-1}) = (G^{\top} S^{-1} G)^{-1} G^{\top} S^{-1} S\, S^{-1} G (G^{\top} S^{-1} G)^{-1} = (G^{\top} S^{-1} G)^{-1}.

One can show V(W) - V(S^{-1}) is positive semi-definite for every valid W (the argument is the matrix analogue of the Gauss–Markov theorem, or equivalently the Cauchy–Schwarz inequality applied to the moment space). So no weighting matrix delivers a smaller asymptotic variance than S^{-1}. This is exactly why the interactive’s “efficient weights” tile uses inverse variances: with independent moments, S is diagonal and S^{-1} = \operatorname{diag}(1/\operatorname{Var} g_1, \dots).

The J-statistic’s chi-square limit

At the efficient estimate, define the standardised moment vector \sqrt{n}\,\hat S^{-1/2}\bar g_n(\hat\theta). Asymptotically \sqrt n\,\bar g_n(\theta_0) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal N(0, S), so \sqrt n\,S^{-1/2}\bar g_n(\theta_0) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal N(0, I_m) — a vector of m independent standard normals. Estimating the p parameters projects this vector onto the (m-p)-dimensional space orthogonal to the columns of S^{-1/2}G. The squared length of a standard normal vector confined to a (m-p)-dimensional subspace is \chi^2_{m-p}. Hence

J = n\,\bar g_n(\hat\theta)^{\top}\hat S^{-1}\bar g_n(\hat\theta) \xrightarrow{d} \chi^2_{m-p}.

The loss of p degrees of freedom is the price of using the data to estimate \theta before testing the moments — the same bookkeeping as residual degrees of freedom in OLS.

Computing GMM

Both implementations encode the over-identified diabetes scenario: one parameter \theta (the average HbA1c reduction) and two noisy moment conditions, estimated by efficient two-step GMM. Each then runs the J-test.

library(gmm)

# --- Two noisy readouts of the SAME program effect θ on HbA1c (pp). ---
# Register readout centred on -0.72, survey readout on -0.44 (they disagree).
# Register is more precise (sd 0.14) than the survey (sd 0.27).
set.seed(2019)
n <- 600
register <- rnorm(n, mean = -0.72, sd = 0.14)
survey   <- rnorm(n, mean = -0.44, sd = 0.27)
dat <- cbind(register, survey)

# Moment function: each readout minus the single parameter θ has mean zero.
#   g1 = register - θ ,  g2 = survey - θ   (2 moments, 1 parameter => over-id)
g <- function(theta, x) {
  cbind(x[, "register"] - theta,
        x[, "survey"]   - theta)
}

# Two-step efficient GMM (identity W in step 1, S^{-1} in step 2).
fit <- gmm(g, x = dat, t0 = c(theta = -0.5), wmatrix = "optimal")

summary(fit)                 # efficient θ̂, SE, and the J-test
cat("\nθ̂ (efficient GMM):", round(coef(fit), 3), "\n")

# Hansen's J-test of the over-identifying restriction (df = m - p = 1).
J <- specTest(fit)
print(J)

# Sanity check: with diagonal optimal W the estimate is the inverse-variance
# weighted mean of the two readouts.
w1 <- 1 / var(register); w2 <- 1 / var(survey)
cat("Inverse-variance weighted mean:",
    round((w1 * mean(register) + w2 * mean(survey)) / (w1 + w2), 3), "\n")
import numpy as np
from statsmodels.sandbox.regression.gmm import GMM
from scipy import stats

# --- Two noisy readouts of the SAME program effect θ on HbA1c (pp). ---
rng = np.random.default_rng(2019)
n = 600
register = rng.normal(-0.72, 0.14, n)   # precise register readout
survey   = rng.normal(-0.44, 0.27, n)   # noisier survey readout
data = np.column_stack([register, survey])

class TwoMomentGMM(GMM):
    # 2 moment conditions, 1 parameter => over-identified (1 restriction).
    def momcond(self, params):
        theta = params[0]
        g1 = self.exog[:, 0] - theta     # register - θ
        g2 = self.exog[:, 1] - theta     # survey   - θ
        return np.column_stack([g1, g2])

model = TwoMomentGMM(endog=np.zeros(n), exog=data, instrument=data,
                     k_moms=2, k_params=1)
res = model.fit(start_params=[-0.5], maxiter=2, optim_method='bfgs')  # 2-step efficient

print(res.summary())
theta_hat = res.params[0]
print(f"\nθ̂ (efficient GMM): {theta_hat:.3f}")

# Hansen's J-test: J = n * Q(θ̂) ~ chi2(m - p) = chi2(1).
J = res.jval                     # minimised efficient objective * n
p_value = stats.chi2.sf(J, df=2 - 1)
print(f"Hansen J = {J:.3f},  p-value = {p_value:.3f}  (df = 1)")

# Sanity check: inverse-variance weighted mean of the two readouts.
w1, w2 = 1 / register.var(ddof=1), 1 / survey.var(ddof=1)
print("Inverse-variance weighted mean:",
      round((w1 * register.mean() + w2 * survey.mean()) / (w1 + w2), 3))

Because the two readouts genuinely disagree by more than sampling noise (they were simulated with means -0.72 and -0.44), the J-test here will reject: the data are telling you the two sources are not measuring the same \theta. Set both means equal and the J-test will stop rejecting — a hands-on demonstration of what the test detects.

Exercises

Exercise 1

A region records waiting times (in days) for a distriktssköterska (district nurse) appointment. You assume waiting times follow an exponential distribution with rate \lambda, for which E[w] = 1/\lambda. In a sample of 400 patients the mean waiting time is \bar w = 8 days. (a) Use the classical method of moments to estimate \lambda. (b) The exponential also satisfies \operatorname{Var}(w) = 1/\lambda^2. If the sample variance is 61 days^2, is the single-parameter exponential model adequate, or do the two moment-based estimates of \lambda disagree?

Show answer

(a) Equate the first sample moment to the population mean: \bar w = 1/\lambda, so \hat\lambda = 1/\bar w = 1/8 = 0.125 per day.

(b) The second moment gives a separate estimate. From \operatorname{Var}(w) = 1/\lambda^2 we get \hat\lambda = 1/\sqrt{61} = 1/7.81 \approx 0.128 per day. The mean-based estimate is 0.125 and the variance-based estimate is 0.128 — very close. The exponential imposes mean = standard deviation (1/\lambda for both): here \bar w = 8 and \sqrt{61} = 7.81, almost equal, so the model is adequate. Had the variance been, say, 400 (sd = 20 \gg 8), the two estimates would clash — the over-dispersion would reject the exponential. This is the method-of-moments precursor to the over-identification idea: a one-parameter model with two moments is over-identified, and disagreement between the moment-based estimates is informal evidence of misspecification.

Exercise 2

In the interactive’s notation, g_1(\theta) = \bar a - \theta and g_2(\theta) = \bar b - \theta, with weights w_1 and w_2. (a) Minimise Q(\theta) = w_1 g_1(\theta)^2 + w_2 g_2(\theta)^2 analytically to find \hat\theta. (b) Plug in the interactive’s numbers (\bar a = -0.72, \bar b = -0.44) under equal weights and under efficient weights (w_1 = 1/0.020, w_2 = 1/0.075). Confirm the efficient estimate is pulled toward the register readout.

Show answer

(a) Differentiate and set to zero:

\frac{dQ}{d\theta} = -2 w_1 (\bar a - \theta) - 2 w_2 (\bar b - \theta) = 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; \hat\theta = \frac{w_1 \bar a + w_2 \bar b}{w_1 + w_2}.

The GMM estimate is the weighted mean of the two targets — the inverse-variance weighted mean when w_j = 1/\operatorname{Var}(g_j).

(b) Equal weights (w_1 = w_2 = 1): \hat\theta = (-0.72 - 0.44)/2 = -0.58.

Efficient weights (w_1 = 1/0.020 = 50, w_2 = 1/0.075 = 13.33):

\hat\theta = \frac{50(-0.72) + 13.33(-0.44)}{50 + 13.33} = \frac{-36.0 - 5.87}{63.33} = \frac{-41.87}{63.33} \approx -0.661.

The efficient estimate -0.661 sits much closer to the precise register readout -0.72 than the equal-weight estimate -0.58 does. Down-weighting the noisy survey is exactly the efficiency gain Hansen’s theorem promises. \checkmark

Exercise 3

A health economist estimates the effect of nurse contact hours on HbA1c using three instruments (distance to the nearest vårdcentral, a same-week appointment-availability index, and a regional staffing ratio) for one endogenous regressor. (a) How many over-identifying restrictions are there? (b) State the moment conditions and the degrees of freedom of the Hansen J-test. (c) The J-test rejects at the 1% level. List two distinct things that could be wrong, and explain why the test cannot tell you which.

Show answer

(a) With m = 3 instruments (moments) and p = 1 structural parameter on the endogenous regressor (ignoring the intercept for clarity, or counting it symmetrically on both sides), the number of over-identifying restrictions is m - p. With one endogenous coefficient and three instruments there are 3 - 1 = 2 over-identifying restrictions.

(b) The moment conditions are E[z_{ij}(y_i - x_i^{\top}\beta)] = 0 for each instrument j = 1, 2, 3 — each instrument must be orthogonal to the structural error. The J-statistic is asymptotically \chi^2_{2} (degrees of freedom = m - p = 2).

(c) Two possibilities: (i) one or more instruments are invalid — e.g., the regional staffing ratio affects HbA1c directly (through overall care quality), violating the exclusion restriction; (ii) the structural model is misspecified — e.g., the true effect of nursing on HbA1c is nonlinear, so the linear moment conditions cannot all hold simultaneously. The J-test is a joint test of all the moment conditions together: it aggregates all m standardised moments into a single number and asks whether that number is too big. A rejection says “the moments are jointly incompatible” but cannot attribute the blame to a specific instrument or to functional form, because every culprit inflates the same statistic.

Exercise 4

Show that in the just-identified case (m = p) the GMM estimate does not depend on the weighting matrix W, and that the Hansen J-statistic is identically zero. Why does this mean OLS (viewed as GMM) offers no over-identification test?

Show answer

When m = p, the sample moment vector \bar g_n(\theta) has the same number of components as \theta has free parameters. Generically the system of equations \bar g_n(\theta) = 0 has a solution \hat\theta that sets every sample moment to exactly zero. At that \hat\theta:

  • The objective is Q_n(\hat\theta) = \bar g_n(\hat\theta)^{\top} W \bar g_n(\hat\theta) = 0^{\top} W\, 0 = 0 for any positive-definite W. Since Q_n \ge 0 always and equals 0 here, \hat\theta minimises Q_n regardless of W. So the weighting matrix is irrelevant — changing W cannot change the minimiser when the minimum value is already zero.

  • The J-statistic is J = n\,Q_n(\hat\theta) = n \cdot 0 = 0 identically. There is nothing to test.

This is precisely OLS’s situation: the moment condition E[x_i(y_i - x_i^{\top}\beta)] = 0 supplies exactly p moments for p coefficients (just-identified), so the sample moments are zeroed out exactly and J \equiv 0. With m - p = 0 over-identifying restrictions, the \chi^2_0 “distribution” is degenerate — there is no spare moment whose validity could be checked against the data. You can only test over-identifying restrictions when you have moments to spare.

Key takeaways

  • The classical method of moments equates sample moments to population moments and solves; GMM generalises this to moment conditions E[g(w,\theta)] = 0 with possibly more moments than parameters.
  • GMM minimises the quadratic form Q_n(\theta) = \bar g_n(\theta)^{\top} W \bar g_n(\theta); in the just-identified case the moments are zeroed exactly and W is irrelevant, while in the over-identified case W governs how disagreeing moments are traded off.
  • The efficient weighting matrix is W = S^{-1}, the inverse of the moment covariance — down-weight noisy moments, up-weight precise ones — delivering the smallest asymptotic variance (G^{\top}S^{-1}G)^{-1}.
  • OLS and 2SLS/IV are GMM with specific moment conditions (E[x u] = 0 and E[z u] = 0); 2SLS uses a non-efficient weighting matrix that efficient GMM improves on under heteroskedasticity.
  • Hansen’s J = n\,Q_n(\hat\theta) \xrightarrow{d} \chi^2_{m-p} tests the joint validity of the over-identifying restrictions; it exists only when m > p and cannot say which moment is at fault.
